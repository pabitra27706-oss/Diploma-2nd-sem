4.1 Introduction to Transformer and Electrical Machines

Transformer

A transformer is a static electrical device that transfers electrical energy from one circuit to another by electromagnetic induction, without changing frequency.

Electrical Machine

An electrical machine is a device that converts energy from one form to another. For example:

  • Generator: Mechanical → Electrical
  • Motor: Electrical → Mechanical
  • Transformer: Electrical → Electrical (same frequency, different voltage/current level)
Energy Conversion Summary
  • Transformer: AC electrical power at one voltage → AC electrical power at another voltage
  • Motor: Electrical energy → Mechanical rotation
  • Generator: Mechanical rotation → Electrical energy

4.2 General Construction and Principle of Different Types of Transformers

What is a Transformer?

Basic Working Idea

A transformer works on the principle of mutual induction. When AC is supplied to the primary winding, it creates alternating magnetic flux in the core. This alternating flux links the secondary winding and induces emf in it.

🔥 Key Point: A transformer works only on AC supply. It does not work on steady DC because DC does not produce changing flux.

Working Principle of Transformer

  1. AC supply is given to the primary winding
  2. Alternating current flows and produces alternating magnetic flux in the core
  3. This flux links both primary and secondary windings
  4. According to Faraday’s law, emf is induced in both windings
  5. If load is connected to secondary, current flows and power is transferred from primary to secondary

Ideal Transformer Concept

Ideal Transformer

An ideal transformer is one which has no losses, no leakage flux and 100% efficiency.

In practical transformers, there are:

  • Copper loss
  • Iron/core loss
  • Leakage flux
  • Voltage drop in windings
Common Mistake

Students often write that transformer changes frequency. This is wrong. A transformer changes voltage and current level, but frequency remains the same.

Solved Example

Example 1

A transformer has 500 turns in primary and 1000 turns in secondary. State whether it is step-up or step-down.

Solution
Primary turns N1 = 500
Secondary turns N2 = 1000
Since N2 > N1, secondary voltage will be higher.

✅ It is a step-up transformer

4.3 EMF Equation and Transformation Ratio of Transformer

EMF Equation of Transformer

Idea Behind EMF Equation

When alternating flux links a winding of N turns, emf is induced according to Faraday’s law. For a sinusoidal flux, the RMS induced emf is:

EMF Equation

E = 4.44 f N φm

  • E = RMS induced emf in volts
  • f = frequency in Hz
  • N = number of turns
  • φm = maximum flux in weber

Therefore, for primary and secondary:

Primary and Secondary EMF

E1 = 4.44 f N1 φm

E2 = 4.44 f N2 φm

Transformation Ratio

Transformation Ratio

The ratio of secondary voltage to primary voltage is called the transformation ratio.

Formula: Transformation Ratio

k = E2E1 = V2V1 = N2N1

For an ideal transformer:

Current Relation

I2I1 = N1N2

V1I1 = V2I2

Cases of Transformation Ratio

k > 1

Secondary voltage is greater than primary voltage.

Step-up transformer

k < 1

Secondary voltage is less than primary voltage.

Step-down transformer

k = 1

Primary and secondary voltages are equal.

Isolation transformer

Turns Ratio, Voltage Ratio and Current Ratio

QuantityRelation
Turns ratioN2/N1
Voltage ratioV2/V1
EMF ratioE2/E1

Solved Examples

Example 2

A transformer has 1000 primary turns and 200 secondary turns. If primary voltage is 220 V, find secondary voltage.

Solution
Given: N1 = 1000, N2 = 200, V1 = 220 V
Use: V2/V1 = N2/N1
V2/220 = 200/1000 = 0.2
V2 = 220 × 0.2 = 44 V

✅ Secondary voltage = 44 V

Example 3

A transformer operates at 50 Hz with 500 turns and maximum flux 0.01 Wb. Find induced emf.

Solution
Given: f = 50 Hz, N = 500, φm = 0.01 Wb
E = 4.44 f N φm
E = 4.44 × 50 × 500 × 0.01
E = 1110 V

✅ Induced emf = 1110 V

4.4 Auto Transformer

Auto Transformer

An auto transformer is a transformer having only one continuous winding, a part of which is common to both primary and secondary circuits.

Basic Idea

In an ordinary two-winding transformer, primary and secondary are electrically isolated from each other. But in an auto transformer, the same winding acts partly as primary and partly as secondary.

Fig. 4.9: Auto transformer construction showing single tapped winding

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Construction of Auto Transformer

  • It has a laminated iron core
  • Only one copper winding is wound on the core
  • The winding has one or more taps
  • A part of the winding is common to both primary and secondary

Working Principle

It works on the same principle of electromagnetic induction as an ordinary transformer.

  1. AC supply is applied to the whole winding or a part of it
  2. Alternating flux is produced in the core
  3. EMF is induced in the tapped section
  4. Output is taken from a suitable tap depending on required voltage

Step-Up and Step-Down Auto Transformer

Step-Up Auto Transformer

Output is taken from a larger number of turns than the input side, so output voltage becomes higher.

Step-Down Auto Transformer

Output is taken from a smaller number of turns than the input side, so output voltage becomes lower.

Fig. 4.10: Step-up and step-down auto transformer connections

Advantages of Auto Transformer

Advantages
  • Requires less copper
  • Smaller size
  • Lighter weight
  • Less cost
  • Higher efficiency
  • Better voltage regulation

Disadvantages of Auto Transformer

Disadvantages
  • No electrical isolation between primary and secondary
  • Not safe where isolation is necessary
  • If common winding breaks, full primary voltage may appear on load side

Applications of Auto Transformer

  • Voltage regulator
  • Variac / laboratory variable AC supply
  • Starting of induction motors
  • Interconnecting systems of nearly equal voltages
  • Booster in transmission lines

4.5 Construction and Working Principle of Motors

Electric Motor

An electric motor is an electrical machine that converts electrical energy into mechanical energy.

Principle of Motor Action

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a mechanical force. This is the principle of motor action.

This force causes rotation, and thus electrical energy is converted into mechanical motion.

Force on Conductor

F = B I l sin θ

  • F = force in newton
  • B = flux density
  • I = current
  • l = active length of conductor
  • θ = angle between conductor and field
🔥 Most Important Concept: Motor works on the principle that a current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a force.

General Working Steps of a Motor

  1. Electrical supply is given to motor winding
  2. Magnetic field is produced
  3. Current-carrying conductors interact with magnetic field
  4. Force is developed on conductors
  5. Torque is produced
  6. Rotor starts rotating and mechanical output is obtained

Meaning of Torque

Torque

Torque is the turning or twisting force that causes the motor shaft to rotate.

Real Examples of Motor Action
  • Ceiling fan rotates because its motor produces torque
  • Water pump lifts water using motor-driven impeller
  • Lathe machine spindle rotates due to motor
  • Mixers, grinders and washing machines work with motors

Solved Numericals

⭐ Exam Strategy: In this unit, numericals mainly come from: EMF equation, turns ratio, voltage ratio, and simple transformer relations.
Numerical 1: Transformation Ratio

A transformer has 400 turns in primary and 100 turns in secondary. Find transformation ratio and state the type of transformer.

Solution
Given: N1 = 400, N2 = 100
k = N2/N1 = 100/400 = 0.25
Since k < 1, it is a step-down transformer.

✅ k = 0.25, Step-down transformer

Numerical 2: Secondary Voltage

A transformer has primary voltage 220 V, primary turns 500 and secondary turns 1000. Find secondary voltage.

Solution
Use: V2/V1 = N2/N1
V2/220 = 1000/500 = 2
V2 = 220 × 2 = 440 V

✅ Secondary voltage = 440 V

Numerical 3: EMF Equation

A transformer has 250 turns, supply frequency 50 Hz and maximum flux 0.02 Wb. Find induced emf.

Solution
E = 4.44 f N φm
E = 4.44 × 50 × 250 × 0.02
E = 1110 V

✅ Induced emf = 1110 V

Numerical 4: Current Ratio

An ideal transformer has primary current 2 A and turns ratio N2/N1 = 4. Find secondary current.

Solution
Given: I1 = 2 A, N2/N1 = 4
For ideal transformer: I2/I1 = N1/N2
I2/2 = 1/4
I2 = 0.5 A

✅ Secondary current = 0.5 A

Long Answer Questions (5–7 Marks Style)

Q 1. Explain the construction and working principle of a transformer.

A transformer is a static electrical device that transfers AC power from one circuit to another by mutual induction. Its main parts are laminated magnetic core, primary winding, secondary winding, insulation, tank, oil and bushings.

When AC is supplied to the primary winding, alternating current flows and produces alternating magnetic flux in the iron core. This flux links both primary and secondary windings. According to Faraday’s law, emf is induced in both windings. If load is connected across the secondary winding, current flows and power is transferred from primary to secondary.

A transformer works only on AC because changing flux is required for induction.

Q 2. Derive the EMF equation of a transformer and explain transformation ratio.

For a transformer with sinusoidal flux, the RMS induced emf in a winding is:

E = 4.44fNφm

Where E is induced emf, f is frequency, N is number of turns and φm is maximum flux.

For primary winding:

E1 = 4.44fN1φm

For secondary winding:

E2 = 4.44fN2φm

So,

E2/E1 = N2/N1

For ideal transformer:

V2/V1 = N2/N1

This is called transformation ratio.

Q 3. Explain auto transformer with advantages, disadvantages and applications.

An auto transformer is a transformer having only one continuous winding with tapping. A part of the winding is common to both primary and secondary.

It works on electromagnetic induction like a normal transformer.

Advantages: less copper, smaller size, lighter weight, lower cost, better efficiency, better voltage regulation.

Disadvantages: no electrical isolation, less safety, full primary voltage may appear on output if winding breaks.

Applications: variac, voltage control, induction motor starting, interconnection of close voltage systems.

Q 4. Explain the working principle of an electric motor and mention its main parts.

An electric motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy. It works on the principle that a current carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a force.

This force acts on the conductors and produces torque, which rotates the rotor.

Main parts of a motor:

  • Stator — stationary part
  • Rotor — rotating part
  • Windings — carry current
  • Shaft — gives mechanical output
  • Bearings — support rotation

Thus the motor gives rotational mechanical output from electrical input.

Q 5. Classify motors and write their applications.

Motors are broadly classified into DC motors and AC motors.

DC motors: Shunt motor, series motor, compound motor.

  • Shunt motor — lathes, fans, blowers
  • Series motor — cranes, hoists, traction
  • Compound motor — elevators, conveyors

AC motors: Single-phase induction motor, three-phase induction motor, synchronous motor.

  • Single-phase motor — fans, refrigerators, washing machines
  • Three-phase motor — pumps, compressors, machine tools
  • Synchronous motor — constant speed drive, PF improvement

Q 6. Compare auto transformer and two-winding transformer.

An auto transformer has a single continuous winding, while a two-winding transformer has separate primary and secondary windings.

Auto transformer uses less copper, is cheaper, smaller and more efficient. However, it has no electrical isolation between input and output and is less safe.

A two-winding transformer is costlier and larger, but it provides electrical isolation and is safer for general power transfer.

Auto transformer is used for voltage control and motor starting, while two-winding transformers are used for transmission and distribution.

Practice Numericals with Toggle Solutions

Q 1. A transformer has N1 = 200 and N2 = 100. Find transformation ratio.

k = N2/N1 = 100/200 = 0.5

Answer: 0.5

Q 2. A transformer has V1 = 220 V and N1 = 500, N2 = 250. Find V2.

V2/V1 = N2/N1 = 250/500 = 0.5

V2 = 220 × 0.5 = 110 V

Answer: 110 V

Q 3. A transformer operates at 50 Hz with 300 turns and maximum flux 0.02 Wb. Find induced emf.

E = 4.44fNφm

= 4.44 × 50 × 300 × 0.02

= 1332 V

Answer: 1332 V

Q 4. A transformer has V1 = 100 V, V2 = 400 V. State the type.

Since V2 > V1, it is a step-up transformer.

Answer: Step-up transformer

Q 5. An ideal transformer has V1 = 200 V, I1 = 4 A and V2 = 100 V. Find I2.

For ideal transformer: V1I1 = V2I2

200 × 4 = 100 × I2

I2 = 800/100 = 8 A

Answer: 8 A

Q 6. A conductor of length 0.4 m carries 5 A in a magnetic field of 0.8 T at 90°. Find force.

F = BIl sinθ = 0.8 × 5 × 0.4 × 1 = 1.6 N

Answer: 1.6 N

Q 7. If N1 = 1000 and N2 = 1000, what type of transformer is it?

Since N1 = N2, voltage ratio is 1.

Answer: Isolation transformer / 1:1 transformer

Q 8. A transformer has N1 = 250, N2 = 500 and I1 = 2 A. Find I2 for ideal transformer.

I2/I1 = N1/N2 = 250/500 = 0.5

I2 = 2 × 0.5 = 1 A

Answer: 1 A

Quick Revision / Unit Summary

Unit 4 Quick Revision
  • A transformer is a static device working on mutual induction.
  • Transformer works on AC only, not DC.
  • Main transformer parts: core, primary winding, secondary winding, insulation, tank, oil.
  • Transformer changes voltage/current level but not frequency.
  • Step-up: N2 > N1, V2 > V1
  • Step-down: N2 < N1, V2 < V1
  • EMF equation: E = 4.44fNφm
  • Transformation ratio: k = V2/V1 = N2/N1
  • Auto transformer has one winding and no electrical isolation.
  • Motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy.
  • Motor principle: current carrying conductor in magnetic field experiences force.
  • Main motor parts: stator, rotor, shaft, winding, bearings.
  • DC series motor gives high starting torque.
  • Three-phase induction motor is the workhorse of industry.
Last-Minute Exam Tips
  • Memorize E = 4.44fNφm
  • Remember transformer does not change frequency
  • Always compare N2 with N1 to identify step-up or step-down
  • For auto transformer, write both advantage and main disadvantage
  • For motor principle, mention force on current carrying conductor
  • In application answers, write specific examples like fan, pump, crane, lathe
Common Mistakes
  • Writing transformer works on DC — wrong
  • Confusing step-up and step-down relations
  • Using wrong turns ratio formula
  • Forgetting that current ratio is inverse of turns ratio
  • Writing auto transformer has two separate windings — wrong
  • Confusing rotor and stator
