Rationale

The phenomena of electricity and magnetism are interconnected. Electric field produces magnetic effect and vice versa. The magnetic field forms a necessary connection between electrical and mechanical energy. All electromechanical devices depend on the magnetic field for interchange of energy. This unit deals with the law governing the magnetic field, magnetization characteristic, emf induction and correlation between simple magnetic and electrical circuits.

Michael Faraday (1791–1867)

The most influential scientist in history, contributed to the study of electromagnetism and electrochemistry. He received very little formal education, but was an excellent experimentalist. His research on the magnetic field around a conductor carrying a direct current established the basis for the concept of the electromagnetic field. The breakthrough came when Faraday found that a changing magnetic field produces an electric field. This relation was later modeled mathematically by James Maxwell and is known as Faraday's law. This led to the discovery of the electric dynamo. The SI unit of capacitance is named farad in his honor.

4.1.2 Current, Voltage, Power & Energy

Current

Electric Current

The fundamental electrical quantity is charge (unit: Coulomb). The smallest charge: electron = −1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C; proton = +1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C. Electric current is the time rate of change of charged particles flowing through a predetermined area.

\[ i = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} \]

Unit: Ampere (A) = 1 Coulomb / 1 second

Voltage (Potential Difference)

Voltage

The work done in moving a unit charge from point a to point b. Unit: Volt (V) = 1 Joule / 1 Coulomb. The potential difference between two points is independent of the path chosen. If voltage of point a is higher than b (Vab), work must be done moving charge from b to a (energy input). Vba = −Vab.

Power and Energy

Power

Defined as work done per unit time.

\[ P = \frac{\text{Work}}{\text{time}} = \frac{\text{Work}}{\text{charge}} \times \frac{\text{charge}}{\text{time}} = V \times I \]

Unit: Watts (W) = Joules/second. If V and I are constant over time t, Energy E = V × I × t joules.

Passive Sign Convention

Power absorbed (positive): Current I flows into the positive terminal of V.

Power delivered (negative): Current I flows out of the positive terminal of V.

4.1.4 Kirchhoff's Laws

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

Since charge cannot be created but must be conserved, the sum of the currents at a node must be zero.

\[ \sum_{k=1}^{N} i_k = 0 \] ...(4.1)

Convention: Current entering a node = negative; current leaving = positive.

\[ i_1 + i_2 + (-i_3) + (-i_4) = 0 \] ...(4.2)

Fig. 4.5: Illustration of KCL — Node A with currents i₁, i₂ (entering), i₃, i₄ (leaving)

🔍 Click to view
Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

No energy is lost or created in an electric circuit. In a closed circuit, the sum of all voltages associated with sources must equal the sum of load voltages, so the net voltage around the circuit is zero.

\[ \sum_{k=1}^{N} V_k = 0 \] ...(4.3)
Reference Node

Any node can be chosen as a reference. The reference voltage is typically assigned zero volts. Other node voltages are measured relative to this reference.

4.2.1 Magnetic Parameters

A current-carrying conductor creates a magnetic field in the surrounding space. The circular closed paths are known as lines of flux. Direction given by the right-hand rule.

Fig. 4.7: Flux lines surrounding current-carrying conductor — concentric circles, denser near conductor

🔍 Click to view
Magnetizing Force (H)

Current flowing through a conductor per unit length of flux line. For circular flux line with radius r:

\[ H = \frac{i}{2\pi r} \quad \text{A/m} \] ...(4.4)

H is independent of material properties.

Flux Density (B)

Depends on material properties:

\[ B = \mu_0 \mu_r H \quad \text{Wb/m}^2 \text{ or Tesla} \] ...(4.5)

Where μ = permeability, μ₀ = 4π×10⁻⁷ (free space), μᵣ = relative permeability. Unit of permeability: Wb/(A·m).

Fig. 4.8: B-H curve of different magnetic materials — ferromagnetic (μf), paramagnetic (μp), free space (μ₀), diamagnetic (μd)

Magnetomotive Force (MMF)

A tightly wound coil with N turns carrying current I increases field intensity N-fold. MMF = N × I (unit: Ampere-turns, AT).

4.2.2 Magnetic Circuits

Flux Density Equations
\[ B = \frac{\mu N I}{l} \] ...(4.6)
\[ B = \frac{\phi}{A} \] ...(4.7)

Equating (4.6) and (4.7):

\[ N I = \phi \cdot \frac{l}{\mu A} \] ...(4.8)

The term \(\frac{l}{\mu A}\) is the reluctance (ℛ) of the magnetic circuit. Thus: MMF = φ × ℛ (analogous to V = I × R).

4.3 Electromagnetic Induction

4.3.1 Faraday's Law

Faraday's Law

A time-varying magnetic flux causes an induced electromotive force (emf):

\[ e = -\frac{d\phi}{dt} \] ...(4.9)

For an N-turn coil:

\[ e = -N\frac{d\phi}{dt} = -\frac{d\lambda}{dt} \] ...(4.10)

Where λ = Nφ = flux linkage of the coil.

Lenz's Law

The negative sign means the induced emf tends to cause a current that opposes the change in flux.

Three Cases of Flux Linkage Change

Case I: Motional EMF

Flux constant, coil moves relative to it. EMF = Blv volts. Direction: Fleming's right-hand rule. Associated with electromechanical energy conversion.

\[ e = Blv \] ...(4.11)

Case II: Transformer EMF

Coil stationary, flux varies (pulsations). No motion, no energy conversion. EMF = −dφ/dt (same as Eq. 4.9). Also called statically induced emf.

Case III: Both

Coil moves through varying flux. Both motional and transformer emf are induced.

4.3.2 Self & Mutual Inductance

Self-Inductance

Self-Inductance (L)

Measures the voltage induced in a coil by the magnetic field created by current flowing in the same coil.

\[ e = N\frac{d\phi}{dt} = N\frac{d\phi}{di}\frac{di}{dt} = L\frac{di}{dt} \] ...(4.12)

Where \(L = N\frac{d\phi}{di}\). For linear B-H: \(L = \frac{N\phi}{i} = \frac{\lambda}{i}\) H.

\[ L = N^2\mu\frac{A}{l} = \frac{N^2}{\mathcal{R}} \] ...(4.13)

Unit: Henry (H). Depends only on geometry, permeability, and number of turns.

Fig. 4.11: Self Inductance — coil with N turns, current i, flux φ linking all turns

Mutual Inductance

Mutual Inductance (M)

When two coils share flux (wound on common core or placed close), a part of flux from one coil links the other, creating magnetic coupling.

\[ M_{12} = N\frac{d\phi_{12}}{dt} = \frac{\lambda_{12}}{i_1} \text{ H} \] ...(4.14)
\[ M_{21} = N\frac{d\phi_{21}}{dt} = \frac{\lambda_{21}}{i_2} \text{ H} \] ...(4.15)

For tightly coupled coil (no flux leakage): M₁₂ = M₂₁ = M. Unit: Henry.

The analysis of a magnetic circuit is analogous to a resistive electric circuit. Kirchhoff's laws apply equally to magnetic circuits.

Solved Problems

Example 4.1.1

For the circuit shown (5V source, 3V and 10V resistors, 3A current source), determine power absorbed by R and power delivered by the current source.

Solution
KVL in mesh-1: 5 − 3 − V₂ = 0 → V₂ = 2V
KVL in mesh-2: V₂ + 10 − V₁ = 0 → V₁ = 2 + 10 = 12V
Power delivered by current source = 12 × 3 = 36 W
Power absorbed by R = 10 × 3 = 30 W
Example 4.2.1

Calculate flux and flux density. A = 0.0001 m², N = 500 turns, I = 0.1A, μᵣ = 1000, core 0.1m × 0.1m square.

Solution
F = NI = 500 × 0.1 = 50 AT
Mean length l = 4 × 0.1 = 0.4 m
\(\mathcal{R} = \frac{l}{\mu_0\mu_r A} = \frac{0.4}{1000 \times 0.0001 \times 4\pi \times 10^{-7}} = 3.18 \times 10^6\) AT/Wb
\(\phi = \frac{F}{\mathcal{R}} = \frac{50}{3.18 \times 10^6} = 1.57 \times 10^{-5}\) Wb
\(B = \frac{\phi}{A} = \frac{1.57 \times 10^{-5}}{0.0001}\) = 0.157 Wb/m²
Example 4.3.1

A bar magnet with total flux 500 × 10⁻⁶ Wb is withdrawn through a 1000-turn coil in 1/10 sec. Find the emf.

Solution
\[e = N\frac{d\phi}{dt} = 1000 \times \frac{500 \times 10^{-6}}{1/10} = 1000 \times 5000 \times 10^{-6}\]
e = 5 V
Example 4.3.2

Find inductance of solenoid: length 1000mm, 3000 turns, cylindrical tube diameter 60mm, μᵣ = 1.

Solution
\(A = \pi\frac{0.060^2}{4} = 2.83 \times 10^{-3}\) m²
\(L = N^2\mu_0\mu_r\frac{A}{l}\)
\[L = 3000^2 \times 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 1 \times \frac{2.83 \times 10^{-3}}{1}\]
L = 32 mH
Example 4.4.1

Iron ring: circumference 140 cm, area 12 cm², 500 turns, 2A current, flux = 1.2 mWb. Find μᵣ.

Solution
F = NI = 500 × 2 = 1000 AT
\(\mathcal{R} = \frac{F}{\phi} = \frac{1000}{1.2 \times 10^{-3}} = 833.33 \times 10^3\)
\(\mathcal{R} = \frac{l}{\mu_0\mu_r A}\) → \(833.33 \times 10^3 = \frac{1.4}{12 \times 10^{-4} \times \mu_r \times 4\pi \times 10^{-7}}\)
μᵣ ≈ 1115

Exercises

A. MCQs

Q 4.1

Sources of steady magnetic field include:

  • a. 1, 3 and 4 only
  • b. 3 and 4 only
  • c. 1, 2, 3 and 4
  • d. 1, 2 and 4 only
Answer: c. 1, 2, 3 and 4

A permanent magnet, charged disc rotating at uniform speed, accelerating charge, and linearly changing electric field all produce magnetic fields.

Q 4.2

Magnetic field by infinite linear current-carrying conductor (A/m):

  • a. H = μI/(2πR)
  • b. H = I/(2πR)
  • c. H = μI/(2r)
  • d. H = I/r
Answer: b. H = I/(2πR)

Magnetic field intensity H = i/(2πr) — independent of material (no μ term).

Q 4.3

Faraday and Lenz's law are summarized by:

  • a. e = LR
  • b. e = L di/dt
  • c. e = −dφ/dt
  • d. None
Answer: c. e = −dφ/dt

Faraday's law states emf = negative rate of change of flux; the negative sign represents Lenz's law.

Q 4.4

An emf is induced in a conductor if it:

  • a. Lies in a magnetic field
  • b. Lies perpendicular to field
  • c. Cuts the magnetic flux
  • d. Moves parallel to field
Answer: c. Cuts the magnetic flux

EMF is induced only when conductor cuts (moves across) magnetic flux lines.

Q 4.5

Reluctance depends on:

  • a. Nature of material
  • b. Length of flux path
  • c. Cross-sectional area
  • d. All the above
Answer: d. All the above

ℛ = l/(μ₀μᵣA) — depends on material (μᵣ), length (l), and area (A).

Q 4.6

Coil 250 turns, 50V DC, resistance 10Ω. MMF developed is:

  • a. 500
  • b. 1250
  • c. 2500
  • d. 250
Answer: b. 1250

I = V/R = 50/10 = 5A. MMF = NI = 250 × 5 = 1250 AT.

B. Subjective Questions

Q1. A magnetic circuit: 150 turns, A = 5×10⁻⁴ m², l = 25×10⁻² m, I = 2A, φ = 0.3×10⁻³ Wb. Find H and μᵣ.

H = NI/l = 150×2/0.25 = 1200 A/m. B = φ/A = 0.3×10⁻³/5×10⁻⁴ = 0.6 T. μᵣ = B/(μ₀H) = 0.6/(4π×10⁻⁷×1200) = 397.9

Q2. For the circuit shown, determine which components absorb/deliver power.

Apply passive sign convention: If current enters the + terminal → element absorbs power. If current exits + terminal → element delivers power. Using KCL/KVL to find unknown voltages/currents, then compute P = VI for each element and check the sign.

Q3. Apply KVL and KCL to find V₁, V₂ and I₁ for the circuit (5V source, 3V & 10V drops, 2Ω resistor, 3A source).

KVL mesh-1: 5 − 3 − V₂ = 0 → V₂ = 2V. KVL mesh-2: V₂ + 10 − V₁ = 0 → V₁ = 12V. I₁ = V₂/(2Ω) = 2/2 = 1A.

Q4. 230V, 100W lamp. Find (i) resistance (ii) current (iii) energy in 8 hrs.

(i) R = V²/P = 230²/100 = 529 Ω (ii) I = P/V = 100/230 = 0.435 A (iii) E = P×t = 100×8 = 800 Wh = 0.8 kWh

Q5. Electromagnetic circuit: Find flux density, sketch flux lines, indicate N and S poles. A = 0.01 m².

Calculate B = φ/A. Flux lines form closed paths through the core. N pole is where flux exits (determined by right-hand rule wrapping around the coil — thumb points to N).

Q6. Magnetic structure: draw analogy, find total reluctance and self-inductance. N=100, μᵣ=3000μ₀, A₁=A₃=100cm², A₂=25cm², l₁=l₃=30cm, l₂=10cm.

ℛ₁ = l₁/(μ₀μᵣA₁), ℛ₂ = l₂/(μ₀μᵣA₂), ℛ₃ = l₃/(μ₀μᵣA₃). For parallel structure: ℛ₂ ∥ ℛ₃ + ℛ₁. Then L = N²/ℛtotal.

Q7. Explain statically induced and dynamically induced emf with one application each.

Statically induced: Coil stationary, flux varies (transformer emf). Application: Transformer. Dynamically induced: Conductor moves through stationary field (motional emf, e=Blv). Application: Generator/Dynamo.

Q8. Compare magnetic circuits and electric circuits.

Refer Table 4.1: EMF↔MMF, Current↔Flux, Resistance↔Reluctance, E-field↔H-field, J↔B. Kirchhoff's laws apply to both. Key difference: flux doesn't "flow" and dissipate energy like current.

Q9. Derive expression for self-inductance of a coil.

From Faraday's law: e = N(dφ/dt) = N(dφ/di)(di/dt) = L(di/dt). So L = N(dφ/di). For linear material: L = Nφ/i = λ/i. Since φ = NI·μA/l, L = N²μA/l = N²/ℛ henrys.

Unit Summary

Key Takeaways
  • The fundamental electrical quantity is charge.
  • Current = time rate of change of charge flow.
  • Voltage = work done in moving a unit charge.
  • Power = work done per unit time = V × I.
  • Circuit terminologies: branch, node, mesh, loop.
  • KCL: sum of currents at a node = 0.
  • KVL: sum of voltages around a closed loop = 0.
  • Current-carrying conductor creates a magnetic field.
  • Flux density: B = μ₀μᵣH.
  • Materials classified as ferromagnetic, paramagnetic, diamagnetic based on μᵣ.
  • Time-varying flux induces emf (Faraday's law).
  • Lenz's law: induced current opposes change in flux.
  • Self-inductance: L = N²/ℛ (henry).
  • Mutual inductance: magnetic coupling between neighboring coils.
  • Magnetic circuit analysis is analogous to resistive electric circuits.
  • Reluctance ∝ mean length, ∝ 1/cross-sectional area.
