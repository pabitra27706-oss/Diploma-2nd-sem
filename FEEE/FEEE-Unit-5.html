5.1 Energy Level Diagrams — Insulators, Conductors & Semiconductors

Quick Review: Atomic Structure & Electrons

Why Do We Start with Atoms?

To understand why copper conducts electricity but rubber doesn't, we need to look at what happens to electrons inside atoms when many atoms come together to form a solid material.

Key facts about atoms:

  • Every atom has a nucleus (protons + neutrons) and electrons orbiting around it
  • Electrons occupy different energy levels (shells) — K, L, M, N...
  • The outermost shell electrons are called valence electrons
  • Valence electrons decide whether a material conducts electricity or not
  • An electron needs extra energy to break free from its atom and become a "free electron" that can carry current
Simple Analogy

Think of electrons as people sitting in a stadium. The valence band is like the seating area — people are bound to their seats. The conduction band is like the open ground/field — once people reach there, they can move freely. The gap between seats and field is the energy gap — a wall they need to jump over!

Energy Band Theory 🔥

What is Energy Band Theory?

When billions of atoms come together to form a solid, the individual energy levels of each atom merge and spread out to form continuous bands of energy called energy bands. This is called Energy Band Theory.

There are three important energy bands:

1. Valence Band (VB)

  • The highest range of energy levels that are occupied by valence electrons at absolute zero temperature (0 K)
  • Electrons in this band are bound to atoms — they cannot move freely
  • This band may be completely or partially filled
  • Electrons here do NOT contribute to electrical conduction

2. Conduction Band (CB)

  • The energy band above the valence band
  • Contains free electrons that can move through the material
  • Electrons in this band are not bound to any atom — they carry current
  • May be empty, partially filled, or overlapping with VB

3. Forbidden Energy Gap (Eg)

  • The energy gap between valence band and conduction band
  • No electron can exist in this gap — it is "forbidden"
  • Measured in electron-Volt (eV)
  • The size of this gap decides whether material is conductor, semiconductor, or insulator
⭐ Most Important Exam Point: The forbidden energy gap (Eg) is the KEY factor that classifies a material as conductor, semiconductor, or insulator. This is the basis of ALL semiconductor physics.

Common Exam Mistake

Students often confuse the temperature behaviour:

  • Conductors: Resistance ↑ as temperature ↑ (conductivity decreases)
  • Semiconductors: Resistance ↓ as temperature ↑ (conductivity increases)
  • Insulators: Remain non-conducting under normal temperature changes

Remember: Semiconductor has negative temperature coefficient of resistance (NTC). Conductor has positive temperature coefficient (PTC).

Why Silicon is Preferred Over Germanium?

Silicon vs Germanium
  1. Larger energy gap: Si (1.12 eV) vs Ge (0.72 eV) — Silicon devices can work at higher temperatures
  2. Lower leakage current: Silicon has much less reverse current than Germanium
  3. Abundance: Silicon is the 2nd most abundant element on Earth (found in sand!)
  4. Stable oxide: SiO₂ (silicon dioxide) forms a natural protective layer — very useful in IC fabrication
  5. Cost: Silicon is cheaper than Germanium
  6. Higher operating temperature: Si can work up to ~200°C; Ge only up to ~80°C

Conclusion: Almost all modern semiconductor devices use Silicon. Germanium is used only in special applications.

5.2 Intrinsic & Extrinsic Semiconductors — P-Type & N-Type

Intrinsic (Pure) Semiconductor 🔥

Definition: Intrinsic Semiconductor

An intrinsic semiconductor is a pure semiconductor material that has no impurity atoms added to it. It is a chemically pure form of semiconductor like pure Silicon or pure Germanium.

Crystal Structure of Intrinsic Semiconductor

Let's understand using Silicon (most common semiconductor):

  • Silicon atom has 14 electrons — electronic configuration: 2, 8, 4
  • It has 4 valence electrons (in the outermost shell)
  • Each Silicon atom shares its 4 valence electrons with 4 neighbouring atoms
  • This sharing creates covalent bonds — each bond has 2 shared electrons
  • This forms a crystal lattice structure — a very organized arrangement

Behaviour at Different Temperatures

At Absolute Zero (0 K = -273°C)

  • All valence electrons are tightly bound in covalent bonds
  • No free electrons — no conduction
  • Semiconductor acts as a perfect insulator
  • Both conduction band is empty and valence band is full

At Room Temperature (300 K = 27°C)

  • Thermal energy causes some covalent bonds to break
  • A freed electron goes to the conduction band — becomes a free electron
  • The vacancy left behind is called a "hole"
  • Each broken bond creates an electron-hole pair (EHP)
  • Both electrons AND holes contribute to current
Definition: Hole

A hole is the vacancy created in the covalent bond when an electron breaks free. It behaves as a positive charge carrier equal in magnitude to the electron charge but opposite in sign. Holes move in the opposite direction to electrons.

Key Properties of Intrinsic Semiconductor

Properties
  1. Number of free electrons = Number of holes
    ne = nh = ni

    Where ni = intrinsic carrier concentration

  2. Total current = electron current + hole current
    Itotal = Ie + Ih
  3. Conductivity is very low — because number of free carriers is small
  4. Conductivity increases with temperature — more bonds break → more EHPs
  5. Not useful for practical devices in pure form — too few carriers
Intrinsic Carrier Concentration

For Silicon at room temperature (300 K):

ni ≈ 1.5 × 1010 per cm³

For Germanium at room temperature (300 K):

ni ≈ 2.5 × 1013 per cm³

Germanium has more intrinsic carriers because its energy gap is smaller (0.72 eV vs 1.12 eV)

Remember — Electron vs Hole Current

Electron current: Electrons move from negative to positive terminal (opposite to conventional current direction)

Hole current: Holes move from positive to negative terminal (same as conventional current direction)

In a semiconductor, BOTH electrons and holes carry current simultaneously!

Concept of Doping 🔥⭐

Definition: Doping

Doping is the process of deliberately adding a small amount of impurity atoms to a pure (intrinsic) semiconductor to increase its electrical conductivity. The impurity added is called a dopant.

Why is Doping Needed?
  • Pure semiconductor has very few charge carriers — not useful for practical applications
  • By adding impurities, we can dramatically increase the number of charge carriers
  • We can control what type of charge carrier is in majority — electrons or holes
  • Doping makes semiconductor practically useful for making diodes, transistors, ICs
  • Even a tiny amount of doping (1 impurity atom per 106 to 108 semiconductor atoms) makes a huge difference!
Definition: Doping Concentration

Doping concentration refers to the number of impurity (dopant) atoms added per unit volume of the semiconductor material. It is usually expressed in atoms/cm³. Typical doping concentrations range from 1014 to 1018 atoms/cm³.

Higher doping concentration → More charge carriers → Higher conductivity

Lower doping concentration → Fewer extra carriers → Lower conductivity

Definition: Extrinsic Semiconductor

A semiconductor that has been doped with impurity atoms is called an extrinsic semiconductor. It has much higher conductivity than an intrinsic semiconductor.

There are two types of extrinsic semiconductors, depending on the type of impurity added:

⭐ Key Rule for Doping:
  • If we add a pentavalent (5 valence electrons) impurity → we get N-Type semiconductor
  • If we add a trivalent (3 valence electrons) impurity → we get P-Type semiconductor

N-Type Semiconductor 🔥⭐

Definition: N-Type Semiconductor

When a pure semiconductor (like Silicon) is doped with a pentavalent impurity (having 5 valence electrons), the resulting semiconductor is called an N-Type semiconductor. The "N" stands for Negative — because the majority charge carriers are electrons (negative charges).

How N-Type is Formed — Step by Step
  1. Take a pure Silicon crystal (4 valence electrons per atom)
  2. Add a small amount of pentavalent impurity — like Phosphorus (P), Arsenic (As), Antimony (Sb), or Bismuth (Bi)
  3. Each impurity atom has 5 valence electrons
  4. It replaces a Silicon atom in the crystal lattice
  5. 4 of its electrons form covalent bonds with 4 neighbouring Silicon atoms
  6. The 5th electron is left over — it is loosely bound and easily becomes a free electron
  7. This extra electron needs very little energy (~0.05 eV) to become free — much less than breaking a covalent bond (~1.12 eV)
  8. Each pentavalent impurity atom donates one free electron → called a Donor atom

Properties of N-Type Semiconductor

Properties of N-Type
  1. Majority carriers: Electrons (e⁻)
  2. Minority carriers: Holes (h⁺) — generated by thermal energy breaking covalent bonds
  3. Dopant type: Pentavalent (Group V elements) — called Donor impurity
  4. Donor examples: Phosphorus (P), Arsenic (As), Antimony (Sb), Bismuth (Bi)
  5. Number of electrons >> Number of holes: ne >> nh
  6. Electrically neutral overall — though it has extra electrons, the donor atom's nucleus has an extra proton too
  7. Fermi level shifts closer to the Conduction Band
  8. Current flow is mainly due to electrons
  9. Adding a donor atom creates a new donor energy level just below the conduction band (about 0.05 eV below CB)

Common Mistake

N-Type does NOT mean it is negatively charged! An N-Type semiconductor is electrically neutral. The "N" simply means the majority carriers are negative charges (electrons). The overall crystal has equal positive and negative charges.

P-Type Semiconductor 🔥⭐

Definition: P-Type Semiconductor

When a pure semiconductor is doped with a trivalent impurity (having 3 valence electrons), the resulting semiconductor is called a P-Type semiconductor. The "P" stands for Positive — because the majority charge carriers are holes (positive charges).

How P-Type is Formed — Step by Step
  1. Take a pure Silicon crystal (4 valence electrons per atom)
  2. Add a small amount of trivalent impurity — like Boron (B), Indium (In), Gallium (Ga), or Aluminium (Al)
  3. Each impurity atom has 3 valence electrons
  4. It replaces a Silicon atom in the crystal lattice
  5. 3 of its electrons form covalent bonds with 3 neighbouring Silicon atoms
  6. The 4th bond remains incomplete — there is a vacancy (missing electron)
  7. This vacancy is a hole — it can accept an electron from a nearby bond
  8. Each trivalent impurity atom accepts one electron → called an Acceptor atom
  9. When a nearby electron fills this hole, it creates a new hole at the position it left — this is how hole current flows

Properties of P-Type Semiconductor

Properties of P-Type
  1. Majority carriers: Holes (h⁺)
  2. Minority carriers: Electrons (e⁻) — from thermal generation
  3. Dopant type: Trivalent (Group III elements) — called Acceptor impurity
  4. Acceptor examples: Boron (B), Indium (In), Gallium (Ga), Aluminium (Al)
  5. Number of holes >> Number of electrons: nh >> ne
  6. Electrically neutral overall — same reasoning as N-Type
  7. Fermi level shifts closer to the Valence Band
  8. Current flow is mainly due to holes
  9. Adding an acceptor atom creates a new acceptor energy level just above the valence band (about 0.05 eV above VB)

P-Type does NOT mean it is positively charged! Like N-Type, a P-Type semiconductor is also electrically neutral. The "P" simply means the majority carriers are positive charges (holes).

Mass Action Law

The Mass Action Law

For any semiconductor (intrinsic or extrinsic) at thermal equilibrium:

ne × nh = ni²

Where:

  • ne = concentration of free electrons (per cm³)
  • nh = concentration of holes (per cm³)
  • ni = intrinsic carrier concentration

Meaning: If you increase the number of one type of carrier (by doping), the number of the other type automatically decreases. The product always equals ni².

For N-Type: ne is large, so nh becomes very small

For P-Type: nh is large, so ne becomes very small

⭐ Exam Tip: The Mass Action Law (ne × nh = ni²) is valid for both intrinsic and extrinsic semiconductors at thermal equilibrium. It is commonly asked in short answer questions.

Two Types of Current in Semiconductors

1. Drift Current

  • Caused by applied electric field (external voltage)
  • Charge carriers (electrons and holes) move in a definite direction due to the field
  • Electrons drift opposite to the electric field
  • Holes drift in the direction of the electric field
  • This is similar to current in conductors

2. Diffusion Current

  • Caused by concentration gradient (unequal distribution of carriers)
  • Carriers move from high concentration region to low concentration region
  • Like perfume spreading in a room — from where it's concentrated to where it's not
  • Does NOT need an external electric field
  • Important in P-N junction formation
Quick Memory Aid

For Dopants:

  • "Donors DONATE electrons" → Pentavalent → N-Type (D for Donor, 5th electron is Donated)
  • "Acceptors ACCEPT electrons" → Trivalent → P-Type (A for Acceptor, creates hole to Accept)

For Element Groups:

  • Group III (3 valence e⁻): Boron, Indium, Gallium, Aluminium → "BIG Al"
  • Group V (5 valence e⁻): Phosphorus, Arsenic, Sb(Antimony), Bismuth → "PAS Bi"

Solved Example

Example 5.1

A Silicon semiconductor is doped with phosphorus so that the electron concentration is ne = 5 × 1016 per cm³. If the intrinsic carrier concentration of Silicon at room temperature is ni = 1.5 × 1010 per cm³, find the hole concentration.

Solution
Given:
Electron concentration, ne = 5 × 1016 per cm³
Intrinsic carrier concentration, ni = 1.5 × 1010 per cm³
Find: Hole concentration, nh = ?
Using Mass Action Law:
ne × nh = ni²
Solving for nh:
nh = ni²ne = (1.5 × 1010)²5 × 1016
Calculating:
nh = 2.25 × 10205 × 1016 = 0.45 × 104
nh = 4.5 × 10³ per cm³ = 4500 holes per cm³

Observation: The hole concentration (4500/cm³) is extremely small compared to electron concentration (5 × 1016/cm³). This confirms it is an N-Type semiconductor where electrons are majority carriers.

Example 5.2

A pure Germanium sample has intrinsic carrier concentration ni = 2.5 × 1013 per cm³. It is doped with Boron with doping concentration NA = 4 × 1015 per cm³. Find the electron and hole concentrations. What type of semiconductor is it?

Solution
Given:
Intrinsic carrier concentration, ni = 2.5 × 1013 per cm³
Boron is a trivalent impurity → Acceptor → P-Type
Acceptor concentration, NA = 4 × 1015 per cm³
For P-Type semiconductor:
Since NA >> ni, the hole concentration is approximately:
nh ≈ NA = 4 × 1015 per cm³
Finding electron concentration using Mass Action Law:
ne = ni²nh = (2.5 × 1013)²4 × 1015
ne = 6.25 × 10264 × 1015 = 1.5625 × 1011 per cm³
nh = 4 × 1015 /cm³ (majority carriers)
ne ≈ 1.56 × 1011 /cm³ (minority carriers)

It is a P-Type semiconductor because holes (nh) >> electrons (ne).

5.3 P-N Junction Diode — Formation & Properties

What is a P-N Junction? 🔥⭐

Definition: P-N Junction

A P-N Junction is formed when a P-Type semiconductor and an N-Type semiconductor are joined together in close contact. The boundary where they meet is called the P-N Junction. This is the most fundamental semiconductor device — the diode.

⭐ Important Note: A P-N junction is NOT made by simply pressing two pieces together. It is formed in a single crystal of semiconductor by doping one half with P-type impurity and the other half with N-type impurity. This ensures continuous crystal lattice.

Formation of P-N Junction — Step by Step 🔥

What Happens When P and N are Joined?

When P-Type and N-Type semiconductors are joined, the following process happens automatically without any external voltage:

Initial Condition:
  • P-side has many holes (majority) and few electrons (minority)
  • N-side has many electrons (majority) and few holes (minority)
  • There is a concentration gradient at the junction
Diffusion of Carriers:
  • Electrons from N-side diffuse (move) towards P-side (high to low concentration)
  • Holes from P-side diffuse towards N-side
  • This movement is called diffusion current
Formation of Immobile Ions:
  • When electrons leave N-side, they leave behind positive donor ions (fixed, cannot move)
  • When holes leave P-side, they leave behind negative acceptor ions (fixed, cannot move)
  • These ions create a region of fixed charges near the junction
Formation of Depletion Region:
  • Near the junction, free carriers (electrons and holes) have recombined and moved away
  • This region is now depleted of free charge carriers
  • It is called the Depletion Region (or Depletion Layer, Space Charge Region)
  • Width is typically very small: ~0.5 μm (micrometres)
Formation of Barrier Potential:
  • The immobile ions create an electric field across the depletion region
  • This field is directed from N-side (positive ions) to P-side (negative ions)
  • This electric field opposes further diffusion of carriers
  • A potential difference develops across the junction — called Barrier Potential (VB)
  • Equilibrium is reached when diffusion current = drift current

Definition: Depletion Region

The depletion region is the narrow zone around the P-N junction that is depleted (empty) of free charge carriers (both electrons and holes). It contains only fixed immobile ions — positive donor ions on the N-side and negative acceptor ions on the P-side.

Definition: Barrier Potential (VB)

The barrier potential (also called built-in potential or contact potential) is the potential difference that develops across the depletion region of a P-N junction due to immobile ions. It opposes further diffusion of majority carriers.

VB for Silicon ≈ 0.7 V   |   VB for Germanium ≈ 0.3 V
🔥 Must Remember Values:
Silicon barrier potential = 0.7 V  |  Germanium barrier potential = 0.3 V
These values are asked in almost every WBSCTE exam!

P-N Junction Diode — Symbol & Terminals

Diode Basics

A diode is a two-terminal semiconductor device made from a P-N junction.

  • Anode (A): Connected to the P-side — the positive terminal
  • Cathode (K): Connected to the N-side — the negative terminal
  • A diode allows current to flow in one direction only — from Anode to Cathode (like a one-way valve)

Biasing of P-N Junction Diode 🔥⭐

Biasing means applying an external voltage (from a battery) to the P-N junction diode. There are two ways to bias a diode:

1. Forward Bias ⭐

Forward Bias Condition

Connection: Positive terminal of battery connected to P-side (Anode) and Negative terminal connected to N-side (Cathode).

What happens:

  1. External voltage pushes holes from P-side towards the junction
  2. External voltage pushes electrons from N-side towards the junction
  3. This opposes the built-in electric field
  4. The depletion region becomes narrower
  5. The barrier potential decreases
  6. When applied voltage > barrier potential (0.7V for Si, 0.3V for Ge), large current flows
  7. The diode is said to be "ON" or conducting

Key Points in Forward Bias:

  • Current flows from Anode to Cathode (conventional current)
  • Resistance is very low (few ohms)
  • Current increases exponentially with voltage after barrier potential is crossed
  • The voltage at which significant current starts = Knee Voltage (≈ VB)

2. Reverse Bias ⭐

Reverse Bias Condition

Connection: Positive terminal of battery connected to N-side (Cathode) and Negative terminal connected to P-side (Anode).

What happens:

  1. External voltage pulls holes in P-side away from the junction
  2. External voltage pulls electrons in N-side away from the junction
  3. This supports the built-in electric field — same direction
  4. The depletion region becomes wider
  5. The barrier potential increases
  6. Almost no current flows — diode is "OFF"
  7. Only a tiny reverse saturation current (I0) flows due to minority carriers — in microamperes (μA) for Si, milliamperes for Ge

Key Points in Reverse Bias:

  • Resistance is very high (megaohms)
  • Only tiny leakage current flows
  • If reverse voltage is increased beyond Breakdown Voltage (VBR), suddenly large current flows — this can damage the diode

V-I Characteristics of P-N Junction Diode 🔥⭐

Definition: V-I Characteristics

The V-I Characteristic of a diode is the graph showing the relationship between the voltage applied across the diode (V) and the current flowing through it (I). It shows how the diode behaves in both forward and reverse bias conditions.

Understanding the V-I Curve

Forward Bias Region (Right side of graph, V > 0):

  1. Very little current flows until voltage reaches the Knee Voltage (Vknee)
  2. Vknee ≈ 0.7 V for Silicon, 0.3 V for Germanium
  3. After Vknee, current increases very rapidly (exponentially)
  4. The curve is steep — small increase in voltage → large increase in current
  5. Forward resistance is very low

Reverse Bias Region (Left side of graph, V < 0):

  1. Only a very small reverse saturation current (I0 or IS) flows
  2. This current remains nearly constant regardless of reverse voltage
  3. I0 is in μA range for Silicon and mA range for Germanium
  4. At Breakdown Voltage (VBR), current suddenly increases sharply — diode breaks down
  5. Reverse resistance is very high

Properties of P-N Junction Diode

Summary of Properties
  1. Unidirectional conduction: Allows current in one direction only (forward bias)
  2. Non-linear device: V-I relationship is not a straight line
  3. Knee voltage: ~0.7 V (Si) and ~0.3 V (Ge) — minimum voltage to turn ON

  5. Reverse resistance: Very high (typically 1–100 MΩ)
  6. Reverse saturation current: Very small (μA for Si, mA for Ge)
  7. Breakdown voltage: Beyond this, reverse current increases rapidly
  8. Temperature sensitive: Current increases with temperature
  9. Acts as a rectifier: Converts AC to DC
  10. Two terminals: Anode (P) and Cathode (N)

Diode Current Equation (Shockley Equation)

Shockley Diode Equation
I = I0 (eV/ηVT − 1)

Where:

  • I = Diode current (Amperes)
  • I0 = Reverse saturation current (Amperes)
  • V = Voltage across the diode (Volts)
  • η (eta) = Ideality factor (1 for Germanium, 2 for Silicon)
  • VT = Thermal voltage = kTq ≈ 26 mV at room temperature (300 K)
  • k = Boltzmann's constant = 1.38 × 10−23 J/K
  • T = Absolute temperature in Kelvin
  • q = Electron charge = 1.6 × 10−19 C
  • e = Euler's number ≈ 2.718
Simplified Understanding
  • Forward bias (V > 0): eV/ηVT is very large → I ≈ I0 × eV/ηVT (exponentially large current)
  • Reverse bias (V < 0): eV/ηVT ≈ 0 → I ≈ −I0 (only saturation current flows)

5.4 PNP and NPN Transistors

What is a Transistor? 🔥⭐

Definition: Transistor

A Transistor (Transfer + Resistor) is a three-terminal semiconductor device that can amplify weak signals and act as an electronic switch. It is formed by sandwiching one type of semiconductor between two layers of another type.

Why Transistor is Important
  • Invented in 1947 by Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley at Bell Labs
  • Replaced vacuum tubes — made electronics smaller, cheaper, more reliable
  • A modern CPU chip contains billions of transistors
  • Two main functions: Amplification and Switching
  • Also called BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor) because both electrons AND holes participate in current flow

Types of BJT Transistors

There are two types of BJT transistors based on construction:

NPN Transistor

  • A thin layer of P-type semiconductor sandwiched between two layers of N-type
  • Structure: N — P — N
  • Majority carriers: Electrons
  • More commonly used — faster because electrons move faster than holes
  • Arrow on emitter points outward (away from base)

PNP Transistor

  • A thin layer of N-type semiconductor sandwiched between two layers of P-type
  • Structure: P — N — P
  • Majority carriers: Holes
  • Less commonly used — slower than NPN
  • Arrow on emitter points inward (towards base)

Working Principle of NPN Transistor 🔥⭐

NPN Transistor — How It Works

Biasing condition for normal (active) operation:

  • Emitter-Base junction: Forward biased (VBE applied)
  • Collector-Base junction: Reverse biased (VCB applied)
Forward biased E-B junction:
  • Electrons from the heavily doped N-type emitter are injected into the base
  • A small number of holes from P-type base cross into emitter (but very few because base is lightly doped)
  • This creates the Emitter Current (IE)
Transit through the thin base:
  • Electrons enter the base region
  • Since base is very thin and lightly doped, only about 2-5% of electrons recombine with holes in the base
  • This recombination creates the small Base Current (IB)
  • The remaining 95-98% of electrons pass through the base
Collection at collector:
  • The reverse biased C-B junction has a strong electric field
  • This field sweeps (pulls) the electrons from the base into the collector
  • These collected electrons constitute the Collector Current (IC)
  • IC is almost equal to IE (about 95-98% of it)

Working Principle of PNP Transistor

PNP Transistor — How It Works

Biasing: Same as NPN but voltage polarities are reversed.

  • E-B junction: Forward biased
  • C-B junction: Reverse biased

Working:

  1. Holes from heavily doped P-type emitter are injected into the N-type base
  2. Base is thin and lightly doped — most holes (95-98%) pass through the base
  3. Only 2-5% of holes recombine with electrons in the base → creates IB
  4. Remaining holes are collected by the P-type collector → creates IC

Key difference from NPN: In PNP, the majority carriers are holes instead of electrons, and all current directions are reversed.

Transistor Current Relations 🔥⭐

Fundamental Current Relation
IE = IC + IB

Where:

  • IE = Emitter current (largest current)
  • IC = Collector current (nearly equal to IE)
  • IB = Base current (very small, typically 2-5% of IE)

Relationship: IE > IC >> IB

Current Gain (α — Alpha) — Common Base Configuration

Alpha (α) — DC Current Gain in CB
α = ICIE
  • α is always less than 1 (typically 0.95 to 0.99)
  • It means 95% to 99% of emitter current reaches the collector
  • α can never be ≥ 1

Current Gain (β — Beta) — Common Emitter Configuration

Beta (β) — DC Current Gain in CE
β = ICIB
  • β is always much greater than 1 (typically 20 to 500)
  • A small base current controls a much larger collector current — this is amplification!
  • This is the most commonly used parameter

Relationship between α and β

α ↔ β Conversion Formulas
β = α1 − α      and      α = β1 + β

These formulas are frequently asked in exams!

🔥 Must Remember:
  • IE = IC + IB
  • α = IC/IE (always < 1)
  • β = IC/IB (always >> 1)
  • β = α/(1−α)  |  α = β/(1+β)

Example 5.3

A transistor has α = 0.98. If the emitter current IE = 10 mA, find the collector current IC, base current IB, and β.

Solution
Given: α = 0.98, IE = 10 mA
Find: IC, IB, β
Finding IC:
α = IC/IE
IC = α × IE = 0.98 × 10 = 9.8 mA
Finding IB:
IE = IC + IB
IB = IE − IC = 10 − 9.8 = 0.2 mA
Finding β:
β = α1 − α = 0.981 − 0.98 = 0.980.02 = 49
Verification: β = IC/IB = 9.8/0.2 = 49 ✓
IC = 9.8 mA, IB = 0.2 mA, β = 49

A transistor in CE configuration has β = 100. If the base current IB = 50 μA, find the collector current and emitter current.

Solution

β = IC/IB

5.4 FET, MOSFET & CMOS

Why FET? — Limitations of BJT

BJT vs FET — Why We Need FET

BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor) has some limitations:

  • BJT is a current-controlled device — needs continuous base current → more power consumption
  • BJT has low input impedance — draws current from the signal source
  • BJT uses both electrons and holes (bipolar) — slower at very high frequencies

FET overcomes these limitations — it is a voltage-controlled device with very high input impedance and uses only one type of carrier (unipolar).

FET — Field Effect Transistor 🔥⭐

Definition: FET

A Field Effect Transistor (FET) is a three-terminal, voltage-controlled, unipolar semiconductor device in which the current flow is controlled by an electric field applied to the gate terminal. It uses only ONE type of charge carrier (either electrons or holes).

Three Terminals of FET

Source (S)

The terminal through which charge carriers enter the channel. Equivalent to the Emitter in BJT.

Drain (D)

The terminal through which charge carriers leave the channel. Equivalent to the Collector in BJT.

Gate (G)

The terminal that controls the current flow through the channel by applying voltage. Equivalent to the Base in BJT.

Types of FET (JFET)

JFET — Junction Field Effect Transistor

There are two types based on the channel material:

  1. N-channel JFET: Channel is N-type → current carried by electrons → more common, faster
  2. P-channel JFET: Channel is P-type → current carried by holes → less common, slower

Working Principle of N-channel JFET:

  1. An N-type semiconductor bar forms the channel between Source and Drain
  2. Two P-type regions are diffused on both sides of the N-channel → these form the Gate
  3. A voltage VDS is applied between Drain and Source → current flows through the channel
  4. A negative voltage VGS is applied at the Gate (reverse biasing the P-N junction)
  5. This creates a depletion region that extends into the channel
  6. More negative VGS → wider depletion region → narrower channel → less current
  7. At a certain VGS (called pinch-off voltage VP), the channel is completely closed → current = 0

Key point: Gate voltage controls drain current — no gate current flows because gate junction is reverse biased!

MOSFET — Metal Oxide Semiconductor FET 🔥⭐

Definition: MOSFET

A MOSFET (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor) is an advanced type of FET where the gate terminal is insulated from the channel by a thin layer of silicon dioxide (SiO₂). This gives it an extremely high input impedance (10¹⁰ to 10¹⁵ Ω). It is the most widely used transistor in modern ICs.

MOSFET Name Explained
  • Metal: The gate electrode (aluminium or polysilicon)
  • Oxide: SiO₂ insulating layer between gate and channel
  • Semiconductor: The silicon substrate forming the channel

Also called IGFET (Insulated Gate FET) because the gate is electrically insulated from the channel.

Types of MOSFET

Two Types of MOSFET

1. Depletion-type MOSFET (D-MOSFET)

  • Has a pre-formed channel between Source and Drain
  • Can work in both depletion mode (VGS reduces current) and enhancement mode (VGS increases current)
  • Normally ON — current flows when VGS = 0
  • Similar to JFET but with insulated gate

2. Enhancement-type MOSFET (E-MOSFET) ⭐

  • Has NO pre-formed channel — channel is created by applying gate voltage
  • Works ONLY in enhancement mode
  • Normally OFF — no current when VGS = 0
  • Channel is formed (induced) when VGS exceeds threshold voltage (Vth)
  • Most commonly used in digital circuits, CMOS technology, and ICs
Working of Enhancement MOSFET (N-channel)
  1. P-type silicon substrate with two N-type regions (Source and Drain)
  2. A thin SiO₂ insulating layer covers the surface between Source and Drain
  3. A metal gate electrode is placed on top of the oxide
  4. When VGS = 0: No channel exists → no current flows (normally OFF)
  5. When VGS > Vth (threshold voltage, typically 1–3V):
  6. The positive gate voltage attracts electrons from the P-substrate towards the surface
  7. These electrons form a thin N-type channel (called inversion layer) between Source and Drain
  8. Now current can flow from Drain to Source through this channel
  9. Higher VGS → wider channel → more current

CMOS — Complementary MOS 🔥⭐

Definition: CMOS

CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) is a technology that uses both N-channel and P-channel Enhancement MOSFETs connected in a complementary (paired) configuration. It is the dominant technology for manufacturing modern digital ICs — from logic gates to microprocessors.

How CMOS Works — CMOS Inverter (NOT Gate)

The simplest CMOS circuit is the CMOS Inverter (NOT gate):

  • One PMOS (P-channel MOSFET) is connected between VDD (power supply) and output
  • One NMOS (N-channel MOSFET) is connected between output and Ground
  • Both gates are connected together as the input

When Input = HIGH (Logic 1):

  • NMOS turns ON, PMOS turns OFF
  • Output is connected to Ground → Output = LOW (0)

When Input = LOW (Logic 0):

  • PMOS turns ON, NMOS turns OFF
  • Output is connected to VDD → Output = HIGH (1)

Key advantage: In either state, one transistor is always OFF. So there is no direct path from VDD to Ground → almost zero static power consumption!

Important Definitions

1. Energy Band

When a large number of atoms are brought together in a solid, the individual energy levels of atoms merge to form continuous ranges of allowed energies called energy bands.

2. Valence Band

The highest range of energy levels that are occupied by valence electrons at absolute zero temperature. Electrons in this band are bound to atoms and do not conduct electricity.

3. Conduction Band

The energy band above the valence band that contains free electrons which can move through the material and conduct electric current.

4. Forbidden Energy Gap (Eg)

The energy difference between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band. No electron can have energy within this gap. Measured in electron-Volt (eV).

5. Semiconductor

A material whose electrical conductivity lies between that of a conductor and an insulator, with an energy gap of approximately 1 eV. Its conductivity increases with temperature. Examples: Silicon, Germanium.

6. Intrinsic Semiconductor

A pure semiconductor without any added impurity atoms. The number of free electrons equals the number of holes (ne = nh = ni).

7. Extrinsic Semiconductor

A semiconductor that has been doped with impurity atoms to increase its conductivity. It can be N-type (doped with pentavalent impurity) or P-type (doped with trivalent impurity).

8. Doping

The process of deliberately adding a controlled amount of impurity atoms to a pure semiconductor to increase its electrical conductivity and create majority charge carriers.

9. Hole

A vacancy created in a covalent bond when an electron breaks free from the bond. It behaves as a positive charge carrier and moves in the direction opposite to electron movement.

10. Depletion Region

The narrow region around the P-N junction that is depleted of free charge carriers. It contains only fixed immobile ions and has a built-in electric field.

11. Barrier Potential

The potential difference that develops across the depletion region of a P-N junction due to immobile ions. It opposes further diffusion of majority carriers. Approximately 0.7 V for Si and 0.3 V for Ge.

12. Forward Bias

The condition when the positive terminal of a battery is connected to the P-side and the negative terminal to the N-side of a diode. The depletion region narrows and large current flows.

13. Reverse Bias

The condition when the positive terminal is connected to the N-side and the negative terminal to the P-side. The depletion region widens and only tiny reverse saturation current flows.

14. Transistor

A three-terminal semiconductor device (Emitter, Base, Collector) that can amplify signals and act as an electronic switch. Made by sandwiching one type of semiconductor between two layers of the other type (NPN or PNP).

15. FET (Field Effect Transistor)

A three-terminal, voltage-controlled, unipolar semiconductor device where the drain current is controlled by the electric field applied at the gate terminal. Terminals: Source, Gate, Drain.

16. MOSFET

A type of FET where the gate is insulated from the channel by a thin SiO₂ layer, providing extremely high input impedance. Most widely used transistor in modern integrated circuits.

17. CMOS

Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor — a technology that uses paired N-channel and P-channel Enhancement MOSFETs in a complementary configuration. It offers extremely low power consumption and is used in virtually all modern digital ICs.

  • b. Very low power consumption

Answer: b. Very low power consumption

Q 5.14

The relation between emitter, collector, and base current in a transistor is:

  • a. IB = IC + IE
  • b. IC = IE + IB
  • c. IE = IC + IB
  • d. IE = IC − IB
Answer: c. IE = IC + IB

Emitter current is the total current entering the transistor. It splits into collector current (major portion, ~95-98%) and base current (small portion, ~2-5%). So IE = IC + IB.

Q 5.15

With increase in temperature, the conductivity of a semiconductor:

  • a. Decreases
  • b. Increases
  • c. Remains constant
  • d. First increases then decreases
Answer: b. Increases

As temperature increases, more covalent bonds break, generating more electron-hole pairs. This increases the number of charge carriers, and hence the conductivity increases. Semiconductors have a negative temperature coefficient of resistance.

Short Answer Questions (2-Mark Style)

Exam Tip

Short answer questions carry 2 marks each. Write 3-4 lines with the key definition or explanation. Include a formula or small diagram if applicable.

Q1. What is forbidden energy gap? Give its value for Silicon and Germanium.

The forbidden energy gap (Eg) is the energy difference between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band. No electron can exist at energy levels within this gap. It determines whether a material is a conductor, semiconductor, or insulator.

For Silicon: Eg = 1.12 eV
For Germanium: Eg = 0.72 eV

Q2. What is doping? Why is it necessary?

Doping is the process of adding a small and controlled amount of impurity atoms to a pure (intrinsic) semiconductor to increase its electrical conductivity.

Necessity: Pure semiconductors have very few charge carriers at room temperature, making them impractical for use in electronic devices. Doping increases the number of charge carriers (electrons or holes) significantly, making the semiconductor useful for making diodes, transistors, and ICs.

Q3. What is a hole in a semiconductor?

A hole is the vacancy created in a covalent bond when a valence electron gains enough energy and breaks free from the bond. It behaves as a positive charge carrier with a charge equal in magnitude to the electron charge (+1.6 × 10−19 C). Holes move in the direction opposite to electron movement (i.e., from positive to negative terminal). In semiconductors, both electrons and holes contribute to electric current.

Q4. State the Mass Action Law for semiconductors.

The Mass Action Law states that for any semiconductor (intrinsic or extrinsic) at thermal equilibrium, the product of the electron concentration (ne) and hole concentration (nh) is always constant and equals the square of the intrinsic carrier concentration (ni).

Formula: ne × nh = ni²

This means if doping increases one type of carrier, the other type automatically decreases.

Q5. What is depletion region in a P-N junction?

The depletion region (also called space charge region) is the narrow zone around the P-N junction that is depleted (empty) of free mobile charge carriers — both electrons and holes. It contains only fixed immobile ions: positive donor ions on the N-side and negative acceptor ions on the P-side. These ions create an internal electric field that opposes further diffusion of carriers. The width of this region is typically about 0.5 μm.

Q6. What is the barrier potential? State its value for Si and Ge diodes.

Barrier potential (also called built-in potential or contact potential) is the potential difference that develops across the depletion region of a P-N junction due to the immobile ions. It opposes the diffusion of majority carriers across the junction.

For Silicon diode: VB ≈ 0.7 V
For Germanium diode: VB ≈ 0.3 V

Q7. Write the relation between α and β of a transistor.

The relationship between α (current gain in CB configuration) and β (current gain in CE configuration) is:

β = α / (1 − α)   and   α = β / (1 + β)

Where: α = IC/IE (always less than 1, typically 0.95-0.99) and β = IC/IB (always much greater than 1, typically 20-500).

Q8. What is CMOS? State its main advantage.

CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) is a technology that uses both N-channel and P-channel Enhancement MOSFETs connected in a complementary (paired) configuration to build digital logic circuits.

Main advantage: Extremely low static power consumption. In any logic state, one of the two complementary transistors is always OFF, so there is no direct path from VDD to ground, resulting in nearly zero static power dissipation. This makes CMOS ideal for battery-powered devices and high-density ICs.

Q9. Why is the base of a transistor made very thin and lightly doped?

The base is made very thin (about 10-25 μm) and lightly doped so that:

  1. Most of the charge carriers injected from the emitter can pass through the base without recombining with base carriers
  2. Only about 2-5% of carriers recombine in the base (creating the small IB)
  3. The remaining 95-98% reach the collector (creating the large IC)

If the base were thick or heavily doped, most carriers would recombine in the base, and the transistor would not amplify — it would just behave like two diodes back to back.

Q10. Differentiate between drift current and diffusion current.

Drift Current: Caused by an applied external electric field. Charge carriers (electrons and holes) are forced to move in a specific direction by the field. It is the dominant current mechanism in conductors.

Diffusion Current: Caused by the concentration gradient of charge carriers. Carriers move from a region of high concentration to low concentration, even without any external field. It is important in P-N junction formation. Similar to perfume spreading in a room.

Q1. Draw and explain the energy band diagrams of conductor, semiconductor, and insulator. Compare them. [7 marks] 🔥

Energy Band Theory: When billions of atoms come together to form a solid, the individual energy levels merge into continuous ranges called energy bands. The three important bands are:

  • Valence Band (VB): Highest energy band occupied by valence electrons at 0K
  • Conduction Band (CB): Energy band above VB containing free electrons
  • Forbidden Energy Gap (Eg): Gap between VB and CB — no electron can exist here

1. Conductor: VB and CB overlap (Eg ≈ 0). Large number of free electrons available. Conductivity is very high. Examples: Cu, Ag, Al.

2. Semiconductor: Small energy gap (Eg ≈ 1 eV). At room temperature, some electrons can jump from VB to CB. Conductivity is moderate and increases with temperature. Examples: Si (1.12 eV), Ge (0.72 eV).

3. Insulator: Very large energy gap (Eg > 3 eV). Electrons cannot cross the gap under normal conditions. Conductivity is negligible. Examples: Diamond (6 eV), Glass, Rubber.

Key comparison points:

  • Conductor: Eg = 0, high conductivity, PTC
  • Semiconductor: Eg ≈ 1 eV, moderate conductivity, NTC
  • Insulator: Eg > 3 eV, negligible conductivity

Q2. Explain the formation of N-Type and P-Type semiconductors. Compare their properties. [7 marks] 🔥

N-Type Semiconductor:

  • Formed by doping a pure semiconductor (Si) with a pentavalent impurity (P, As, Sb)
  • Impurity has 5 valence electrons — 4 form covalent bonds with Si, the 5th electron is left over and becomes free
  • Each impurity atom donates one electron → called Donor impurity
  • Majority carriers: Electrons; Minority carriers: Holes
  • Fermi level shifts towards Conduction Band

P-Type Semiconductor:

  • Formed by doping with a trivalent impurity (B, In, Ga, Al)
  • Impurity has 3 valence electrons — forms only 3 covalent bonds, creating a hole (vacancy) at the 4th bond
  • Each impurity atom accepts an electron → called Acceptor impurity
  • Majority carriers: Holes; Minority carriers: Electrons
  • Fermi level shifts towards Valence Band

[Draw diagram showing trivalent impurity atom with incomplete 4th bond — Fig. 5.9]

Both are electrically neutral overall.

Q3. Explain the formation of P-N junction. Describe the depletion region and barrier potential. [7 marks] 🔥

Formation of P-N Junction:

A P-N junction is formed when P-type and N-type semiconductors are joined together in a single crystal. The following process occurs automatically:

  1. Diffusion: Electrons from N-side diffuse to P-side; Holes from P-side diffuse to N-side (due to concentration gradient)
  2. Recombination: Near the junction, electrons and holes recombine, eliminating free carriers
  3. Immobile ions formed: Electrons leaving N-side leave behind positive donor ions. Holes leaving P-side leave behind negative acceptor ions
  4. Depletion region: The region near the junction becomes depleted (empty) of free carriers. It contains only fixed immobile ions
  5. Electric field: Immobile ions create an internal electric field directed from N-side to P-side
  6. Barrier potential: This field creates a potential difference (VB) that opposes further diffusion
  7. Equilibrium: Diffusion current = Drift current → no net current

Barrier Potential: VB ≈ 0.7 V for Silicon, 0.3 V for Germanium.

Depletion region width: Approximately 0.5 μm.

Q4. Explain forward bias and reverse bias of a P-N junction diode with diagrams. Draw the V-I characteristics. [7 marks] 🔥

Forward Bias:

  • Connection: +ve of battery → P-side (Anode), −ve → N-side (Cathode)
  • External voltage opposes barrier potential → depletion region narrows
  • When V > VB (0.7V for Si), majority carriers cross junction → large current flows
  • Diode is ON, resistance is very low

Reverse Bias:

  • Connection: +ve of battery → N-side (Cathode), −ve → P-side (Anode)
  • External voltage supports barrier potential → depletion region widens
  • Only tiny reverse saturation current (I0) flows due to minority carriers
  • Diode is OFF, resistance is very high
  • At breakdown voltage (VBR), large reverse current flows — can damage diode

Q5. Explain the construction and working principle of an NPN transistor with a neat diagram. Write the current relations. [7 marks] 🔥

Construction:

  • NPN transistor has a thin, lightly doped P-type Base sandwiched between two N-type regions (Emitter and Collector)
  • Emitter: Heavily doped, medium size — emits charge carriers
  • Base: Very thin (~10-25 μm), lightly doped — controls current flow
  • Collector: Moderately doped, largest — collects charge carriers
  • Has two P-N junctions: E-B junction and C-B junction

[Draw NPN transistor construction and circuit symbol — Fig. 5.16]

Working Principle (Active mode):

  • E-B junction: Forward biased → electrons from emitter injected into base (IE)
  • Base is thin & lightly doped → only 2-5% electrons recombine → small IB
  • C-B junction: Reverse biased → strong field sweeps remaining 95-98% electrons into collector → large IC

Current Relations:

  • IE = IC + IB
  • α = IC/IE (0.95 to 0.99)
  • β = IC/IB (20 to 500)
  • β = α/(1−α) and α = β/(1+β)

Q6. What is FET? Explain the construction and working of an N-channel JFET. Compare BJT and FET. [7 marks]

FET (Field Effect Transistor): A three-terminal (Source, Gate, Drain), voltage-controlled, unipolar semiconductor device where the drain current is controlled by the electric field applied at the gate.

Construction of N-channel JFET:

  • An N-type semiconductor bar forms the channel between Source and Drain
  • Two heavily doped P-type regions are diffused on opposite sides of the N-channel to form the Gate
  • The two gate regions are internally connected

[Draw N-channel JFET construction — Fig. 5.20]

Working:

  1. VDS applied → current flows through N-channel from Drain to Source
  2. Negative VGS applied → reverse biases the P-N gate junctions
  3. Depletion regions extend into the channel → channel narrows
  4. More negative VGS → wider depletion → narrower channel → less ID
  5. At pinch-off voltage (VP), channel closes → ID = 0

BJT vs FET comparison (key points):

  • BJT: Current-controlled, Bipolar | FET: Voltage-controlled, Unipolar
  • BJT: Low input impedance | FET: Very high input impedance
  • BJT: Higher noise | FET: Lower noise
  • BJT: More power | FET: Less power consumption
  • BJT: E, B, C terminals | FET: S, G, D terminals

Q7. What is MOSFET? Explain the working of Enhancement-type N-channel MOSFET. What are its applications? [5 marks]

MOSFET (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor FET): A type of FET where the gate is insulated from the channel by a thin SiO₂ (silicon dioxide) layer. This provides extremely high input impedance (10¹⁰ – 10¹⁵ Ω). Also called IGFET (Insulated Gate FET).

Working of Enhancement-type N-channel MOSFET:

  1. P-type silicon substrate has two N-type regions (Source and Drain)
  2. A thin SiO₂ insulating layer is deposited between S and D
  3. A metal gate electrode is placed on the oxide
  4. When VGS = 0 → no channel → no current (normally OFF)
  5. When VGS > Vth (threshold voltage) → positive gate attracts electrons from substrate → an N-type inversion layer (channel) forms between S and D
  6. Current flows through this induced channel → higher VGS → wider channel → more ID

[Draw cross-section of E-MOSFET — Fig. 5.22]

Applications: Digital ICs, Microprocessors, Memory chips, Power supplies, Motor drivers, CMOS logic circuits, Image sensors.

Q8. What is CMOS? Explain the working of a CMOS inverter. State advantages and applications of CMOS technology. [5 marks]

CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor): A technology that uses paired N-channel (NMOS) and P-channel (PMOS) Enhancement MOSFETs in a complementary configuration.

CMOS Inverter (NOT Gate):

  • PMOS connected between VDD and output
  • NMOS connected between output and Ground
  • Both gates connected together as input

When Input = HIGH (1): NMOS ON, PMOS OFF → Output connected to Ground → Output = LOW (0)

When Input = LOW (0): PMOS ON, NMOS OFF → Output connected to VDD → Output = HIGH (1)

In either state, one transistor is always OFF → no direct path from VDD to Ground → nearly zero static power consumption

[Draw CMOS inverter circuit — Fig. 5.24]

Advantages: Very low power, high noise immunity, high packing density, wide voltage range, full voltage swing, fast switching.

Applications: Microprocessors (Intel, AMD), RAM, Flash memory, Logic ICs (74HC series), CMOS image sensors, Microcontrollers, FPGA.

Practice Numerical Problems

Numerical Problem Tips

For numerical problems, always follow these steps: (1) Write "Given" data clearly, (2) Write "To Find", (3) Write the formula, (4) Substitute values, (5) Calculate step by step, (6) Box the final answer with units. Show all intermediate steps — examiners give partial marks for correct steps even if the final answer is wrong.

Num 1. A Silicon crystal is doped with Arsenic. The electron concentration is ne = 8 × 1015 /cm³. If ni (Si) = 1.5 × 1010 /cm³, find the hole concentration and identify the type of semiconductor.

ne = 8 × 1015 /cm³
ni = 1.5 × 1010 /cm³
Arsenic = Pentavalent → Donor → N-Type
Mass Action Law: ne × nh = ni²
nh = ni² / ne
nh = (1.5 × 1010)² / (8 × 1015)
nh = (2.25 × 1020) / (8 × 1015)
nh = 0.28125 × 105
nh = 2.8125 × 104 /cm³ ≈ 28,125 holes/cm³

Type: N-Type semiconductor (ne >> nh)

Num 2. A transistor has β = 150. If IB = 30 μA, calculate IC, IE, and α.

β = 150, IB = 30 μA
β = IC / IB
IC = β × IB = 150 × 30 = 4500 μA = 4.5 mA
IE = IC + IB = 4500 + 30 = 4530 μA = 4.53 mA
α = β / (1 + β) = 150 / (1 + 150) = 150 / 151 = 0.9934
α = IC/IE = 4500/4530 = 0.9934 ✓
IC = 4.5 mA, IE = 4.53 mA, α = 0.9934

Num 3. For a transistor in CB configuration, α = 0.96 and IE = 20 mA. Find IC, IB, and β.

α = 0.96, IE = 20 mA
α = IC / IE
IC = α × IE = 0.96 × 20 = 19.2 mA
IB = IE − IC = 20 − 19.2 = 0.8 mA = 800 μA
β = α / (1 − α) = 0.96 / (1 − 0.96) = 0.96 / 0.04 = 24
β = IC / IB = 19.2 / 0.8 = 24 ✓
IC = 19.2 mA, IB = 0.8 mA, β = 24

Num 4. A Germanium semiconductor is doped with Indium to form P-type with hole concentration nh = 5 × 1016 /cm³. Find the electron concentration. Given ni (Ge) = 2.5 × 1013 /cm³.

nh = 5 × 1016 /cm³ (majority — P-type)
ni = 2.5 × 1013 /cm³
Mass Action Law: ne × nh = ni²
ne = ni² / nh
ne = (2.5 × 1013)² / (5 × 1016)
ne = (6.25 × 1026) / (5 × 1016)
ne = 1.25 × 1010 /cm³
ne = 1.25 × 1010 electrons/cm³ (minority carriers)

Note: nh (5 × 1016) >> ne (1.25 × 1010), confirming P-type.

Num 5. The collector current of a transistor is 4.9 mA and the base current is 100 μA. Find: (i) Emitter current, (ii) β, and (iii) α.

IC = 4.9 mA, IB = 100 μA = 0.1 mA
IE = IC + IB = 4.9 + 0.1 = 5.0 mA
β = IC / IB = 4.9 / 0.1 = 49
α = IC / IE = 4.9 / 5.0 = 0.98
Verify: α = β/(1+β) = 49/50 = 0.98 ✓
IE = 5.0 mA, β = 49, α = 0.98

Num 6. If α of a transistor changes from 0.97 to 0.99, by what factor does β change?

β₁ = α₁ / (1 − α₁) = 0.97 / (1 − 0.97) = 0.97 / 0.03 = 32.33
β₂ = α₂ / (1 − α₂) = 0.99 / (1 − 0.99) = 0.99 / 0.01 = 99
Factor = β₂ / β₁ = 99 / 32.33 = 3.06
β changes by a factor of approximately 3 (from 32.33 to 99)

Observation: A small change in α (from 0.97 to 0.99 — just 0.02 increase) causes β to increase by about 3 times! This shows how sensitive β is to changes in α.
