Band Theory

In solids, energy bands are formed due to overlapping of atomic orbitals.

  • Valence band: Outermost electrons
  • Conduction band: Free electrons for conduction
  • Band gap (Eg): Energy difference between bands

Conductor

No band gap (overlap). Electrons freely move.

Insulator

Large band gap > 5 eV. No conduction.

Semiconductor

Small band gap ~1 eV. (Si: 1.1 eV, Ge: 0.7 eV)

Explanation

Conductor: Valence and conduction bands overlap → free electrons.

Insulator: Large forbidden gap → electrons cannot jump.

Semiconductor: Small gap → at room temp, some electrons gain energy and jump to conduction band.

Intrinsic/Extrinsic · Doping · P-Type · N-Type

Intrinsic Semiconductor

Pure semiconductor (Si, Ge). Equal number of electrons and holes. Conductivity low.

Extrinsic Semiconductor

Doped with impurities to increase conductivity. Two types: N-type and P-type.

Doping Concentration

Doping is adding impurity atoms (pentavalent or trivalent) to intrinsic semiconductor. Concentration: 1016 to 1018 atoms/cm³.

P-Type (Trivalent impurity - Boron)

SiBSi

Holes are majority carriers. Accepts electrons.

N-Type (Pentavalent impurity - Phosphorus)

SiPSi

Electrons are majority carriers. Donates electrons.

Properties
  • P-Type: Holes (positive charge carriers) dominate. Formed by doping with trivalent atoms (B, Al, Ga).
  • N-Type: Electrons dominate. Formed by doping with pentavalent atoms (P, As, Sb).
  • Extrinsic semiconductors are electrically neutral overall.

P-N Junction Diode · Formation · Properties

Formation

When P-type and N-type semiconductors are joined, a P-N junction is formed. At the junction, diffusion occurs: electrons from N side cross to P side, holes from P side cross to N side.

This creates a depletion region (no free carriers) and a built-in potential barrier (~0.7V for Si, ~0.3V for Ge).

Forward Bias

P to +, N to -. Depletion width decreases. Current flows when V > barrier potential.

Reverse Bias

P to -, N to +. Depletion width increases. Very small leakage current (until breakdown).

Diode Properties
  • Allows current only in one direction (rectifier).
  • Used in power supplies, clippers, clampers, voltage regulators (Zener), etc.
  • I-V characteristic: exponential in forward, almost zero in reverse.

Bipolar Junction Transistors: PNP · NPN

PNP Transistor

Two P-type layers sandwiching an N-type base. Emitter (P), Base (N), Collector (P).

  • Majority carriers: holes
  • Arrow on emitter points IN (→P)
  • Applications: Amplifiers, switching circuits (less common than NPN)
NPN Transistor

Two N-type layers sandwiching a P-type base. Emitter (N), Base (P), Collector (N).

  • Majority carriers: electrons
  • Arrow on emitter points OUT (N→)
  • Applications: Most common in amplifiers, switches, digital logic
Common Applications of BJT
  • Amplifiers (audio, RF)
  • Switching circuits (LED drivers, relay drivers)
  • Darlington pairs for high current gain
  • Oscillators

FET · MOSFET · CMOS

FET (Field Effect Transistor)

Voltage-controlled device. Input impedance very high. Types: JFET (junction FET) and MOSFET.

Applications: Low-noise amplifiers, analog switches, buffer amplifiers.

MOSFET

Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor FET. Most common transistor in digital/analog ICs.

Types: Enhancement mode, Depletion mode. N-channel and P-channel.

Applications: Microprocessors, memory, power electronics (switching).

CMOS

Complementary MOS – uses both N-channel and P-channel MOSFETs on same chip.

Very low power consumption (only dissipates during switching).

Applications: Almost all digital ICs (CPUs, RAM, logic gates), microcontrollers.

Quick Check: Which device is used in almost all modern microprocessors and why?

CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) is used because of its extremely low static power consumption, high noise immunity, and scalability. It uses complementary pairs of p-type and n-type MOSFETs.
