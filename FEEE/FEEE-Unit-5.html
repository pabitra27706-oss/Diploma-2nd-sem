5.1 AC Fundamentals

5.1.1 Introduction

Alternating current (AC) or alternating voltage is a sinusoidal varying current or voltage. Almost all electrical power supply systems involve sinusoidal AC current derived from sinusoidal AC voltage. A generator is used to produce AC voltage.

5.1.2 Alternating Quantity

Definition

An alternating quantity changes continuously in magnitude and alternates in direction at regular intervals of time. The sine wave is the ideal form and accepted standard. Waves deviating from the standard are called distorted waves.

Generation of AC Voltage

When a coil is rotated in a magnetic field, an alternating emf is induced. The value depends on: number of turns, strength of magnetic field, and speed of rotation. At positions a and c (parallel to field) → emf = 0. At positions b and d (perpendicular to field) → emf = maximum. EMF varies as a sine function.

Comparison: AC vs DC System

Advantages of AC over DC

  1. AC voltages can be efficiently stepped up/down using transformer
  2. AC motors are cheaper and simpler in construction
  3. Switchgear for AC is simpler
  4. Easier and cheaper generation and transmission

Single-Phase vs Three-Phase

Single-phase: Used for small demands (homes).

Three-phase: Used where heavy load is required (industry). Three overlapping AC cycles, each offset by 120°.

5.1.3 Important AC Terms

1. Cycle

One complete set of positive and negative values of alternating quantity. Spread over 360° or 2π radians.

2. Frequency (f)

Number of cycles that occur in one second. Unit: Hz (cycles/sec). Example: 50 Hz = 50 cycles per second.

3. Time Period (T)

Time taken in seconds to complete one cycle.

\[ T = \frac{1}{f} \] ...(5.1)
4. Amplitude

Maximum value (positive or negative) attained by an alternating quantity. Also called maximum or peak value.

5. Average Value

Arithmetic average over half a cycle (full cycle average = 0). Defined as that steady current which transfers the same charge as the alternating current.

\[ I_{av} = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{0}^{\pi}I_m\sin\omega t\,d(\omega t) = \frac{2I_m}{\pi} = 0.637 I_m \] ...(5.3)
6. RMS Value (Root Mean Square)

That steady current which produces the same amount of heat as the alternating current in the same time. Also called Effective Value. Value measured by an ammeter.

\[ I_{rms} = \frac{I_m}{\sqrt{2}} = 0.707\,I_m \] ...(5.4)
7. Form Factor

Ratio of RMS value to Average value.

\[ \text{Form Factor} = \frac{0.707\,I_m}{0.637\,I_m} = 1.11 \] ...(5.5)
8. Peak Factor (Crest Factor)

Ratio of Maximum value to RMS value.

\[ \text{Peak Factor} = \frac{\text{Maximum value}}{\text{RMS value}} = 1.414 \] ...(5.6)
9. Angular Velocity (ω)

Number of radians covered in one second.

\[ \omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi f \quad \text{rad/sec} \] ...(5.7)
10. Instantaneous Value

Value of the quantity at any instant.

Mathematical Representation

Alternating Voltage & Current Equations
\[ v = V_m\sin\theta = V_m\sin\omega t = V_m\sin 2\pi ft = V_m\sin\frac{2\pi}{T}t \] ...(5.8)
\[ i = I_m\sin 2\pi ft = I_m\sin\omega t = I_m\sin\frac{2\pi}{T}t \] ...(5.9)

From equation 5.8: Peak value = coefficient of sine; Frequency = coefficient of t ÷ 2π.

Example: v = 20 sin 314t → Vm = 20V, f = 314/2π = 50 Hz.

5.1.4 Phase, Phase Difference & Power Factor

Phase

The fraction of the time period which has elapsed since the quantity last passed through zero. Two waves are in phase when they reach maximum and zero values at the same time.

Phase Difference

The relative difference between two alternating quantities, expressed in terms of phase angle. A plus (+) sign denotes lead; minus (−) sign denotes lag.

Leading: Reaches maximum value earlier. Lagging: Reaches maximum value later.

Three-Coil EMF Equations
\[ e_A = E_m\sin\omega t \quad\text{(reference)} \] ...(5.10b)
\[ e_B = E_m\sin(\omega t - \beta) \] ...(5.10a)
\[ e_C = E_m\sin[\omega t - (\alpha+\beta)] \] ...(5.10c)

5.1.5 Phasor

Phasor Representation

A phasor is a vector representing a sinusoidal function. It rotates about the origin with angular speed ω in anti-clockwise direction. Length = magnitude (peak value); arrow = direction. The projection on the vertical axis gives the instantaneous value.

5.1.6 AC in Pure R, L, C Circuits

5.1.6.1 Pure Resistive Circuit

Pure R

V = IR. When v = Vm sin 2πft, then i = Im sin 2πft where Im = Vm/R.

Voltage and current are in phase (phase difference = 0°). Power factor cos φ = 1.

5.1.6.2 Pure Inductive Circuit

Pure L

Current lags voltage by 90° (π/2). i = Im sin(2πft − π/2) = −Im cos 2πft.

Im = Vm/ωL where XL = ωL = 2πfL (Inductive Reactance, in Ω). Power factor cos φ = 0.

Fig. 5.12–5.14: Pure inductive circuit, waveform (I lags V by 90°), phasor (V∠0°, I∠−90°)

🔍 Click to view

5.1.6.3 Pure Capacitive Circuit

Pure C

Current leads voltage by 90° (π/2). i = Im sin(2πft + π/2).

Im = Vm/(1/ωC) where XC = 1/ωC = 1/(2πfC) (Capacitive Reactance, in Ω). Power factor cos φ = 0.

Fig. 5.15–5.17: Pure capacitive circuit, waveform (I leads V by 90°), phasor (I∠90°, V∠0°)

Memory Aid: "CIVIL"

C-I-V-I-L: In a Capacitor, I leads V; In an Inductor (L), V leads I.

5.2.2 R-L Series Circuit

R and L in series — same current flows through both. Taking current as reference: i = Im sin ωt.

Fig. 5.18–5.19: R-L series circuit and waveform (voltage leads current)

🔍 Click to view
R-L Series Formulas
\[ V_R = I_m R\sin\omega t \] ...(5.11)
\[ V_L = I_m\omega L\cos\omega t \] ...(5.12)

Substituting R = Z cos θ, XL = Z sin θ:

\[ v = V_m\sin(\omega t + \theta) \] ...(5.14)

Voltage leads current by angle θ (current lags voltage).

\[ \theta = \tan^{-1}\frac{\omega L}{R} = \tan^{-1}\frac{X_L}{R} \] ...(5.15)
\[ Z = \sqrt{R^2 + \omega^2 L^2} = \sqrt{R^2 + X_L^2} \] ...(5.16)

Total power: P = VI cos θ (V, I are RMS values)

5.2.3 R-C Series Circuit

Fig. 5.20–5.21: R-C series circuit and waveform (current leads voltage by φ)

🔍 Click to view
R-C Series Formulas
\[ Z = \sqrt{R^2 + X_C^2} = \sqrt{R^2 + \frac{1}{\omega^2 C^2}} \] ...(5.17)
\[ \theta = \tan^{-1}\frac{X_C}{R} = \tan^{-1}\frac{1}{\omega CR} \] ...(5.18)
\[ i = I_m\sin(2\pi ft + \phi) \] ...(5.19)

Current leads voltage by angle φ. cos φ = R/Z. Power: P = VI cos φ.

Net Reactance

X = XL − XC (when XL > XC) → inductive circuit, current lags.

X = XC − XL (when XC > XL) → capacitive circuit, current leads.

RLC Series Formulas
\[ Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2} \] ...(5.20)
\[ I_m = \frac{V_m}{Z};\quad \cos\phi = \frac{R}{Z};\quad \phi = \cos^{-1}\frac{R}{Z} \]

Impedance Triangle

Impedance Triangle

A right-angled triangle: Base = Resistance R, Perpendicular = Net Reactance X, Hypotenuse = Impedance Z. Geometrical representation of circuit impedance.

5.2.4 Parallel RLC Circuit

Parallel RLC

R, L, C connected in parallel. Voltage across each element is the same. Current divides depending on impedance. Dual relationship with series RLC.

Total current: \( I_S^2 = I_R^2 + (I_L - I_C)^2 \)

Fig. 5.24–5.25: Parallel RLC circuit and phasor diagram

🔍 Click to view
Admittance
\[ Y = \frac{1}{Z} = \frac{I_S}{V} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{R}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{X_L} - \frac{1}{X_C}\right)^2} \] ...(5.21)

Admittance Y is the reciprocal of impedance. Total admittance = sum of branch admittances.

Resonance

Resonance in Series RLC

Series Resonance

When XL = XC, net reactance = 0. The circuit is in "Electrical Resonance".

\[ f_r = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}} \] ...(5.22)

At resonance: Z = R (minimum impedance), current is maximum, phase angle = 0°.

Resonance in Parallel RLC

Parallel Resonance

At resonant frequency, XL = XC but unlike series circuit: impedance becomes maximum, circuit behaves as purely resistive, power factor = unity.

5.3 Three-Phase Circuits

5.3.2 Advantages of Three-Phase System

Advantages over Single-Phase
  1. Power is about three times that of single-phase
  2. Two types of voltages available: phase and line
  3. Large power can be transmitted
  4. Higher power factor of 3-phase motors
  5. Three-phase currents produce rotating magnetic field (required for AC motors); single-phase produces only pulsating field

5.3.3 Star and Delta Connection

Star (Y) Connection

Three ends shorted together → neutral point N. Remaining 3 ends = supply terminals R, Y, B.

Delta (Δ) Connection

End of one coil joined to start of next. Forms 3 junctions = supply terminals R, Y, B. No neutral point.

5.3.5 Electric Power

Power Formulas
\[ P = V \times I = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R} \] ...(5.29)

5.3.6 Power Triangle

Power Triangle

Right-angled triangle: Base = Active/True Power P (Watts), Perpendicular = Reactive Power Q (VAR), Hypotenuse = Apparent Power S (VA).

\[ \text{Power Factor} = \frac{\text{Active Power}}{\text{Apparent Power}} = \frac{kW}{kVA} = \cos\phi \] ...(5.30)
\[ P = VI\cos\phi \quad\text{(True Power)} \] ...(5.31)

Solved Problems

Example 5.1.1

i = 141.4 sin 314t. Find (i) Im (ii) Frequency (iii) Time Period (iv) Value at t = 5ms.

Solution

i) Im = 141.4 A

ii) ω = 314 → f = 314/(2π) = 50 Hz

iii) T = 1/f = 0.02 sec

iv) i = 141.4 sin(314 × 0.005) = 141.4 sin(1.57) = 141.4 × sin(90°) ≈ 141.4 A (at peak)

Example 5.2.1

R-L series: R = 6Ω, XL = 8Ω, V = 141.4V rms. At instant v = +100V (increasing), find (i) current (ii) VR (iii) VL.

Solution

Z = 6 + j8 = 10∠53.1°. v = 200 sin ωt, i = 20 sin(ωt − 53.1°).

When v = 100V: sin ωt = 0.5 → ωt = 30°.

i) i = 20 sin(30° − 53.1°) = −20 sin 23.1° = −7.85 A

ii) VR = −7.85 × 6 = −47 V

iii) VL max = 20 × 8 = 160V, leads current by 90°, leads voltage by 36.9°. VL = 160 sin(30° + 36.9°) = 147.2 V

Example 5.2.2

5A through non-inductive resistance + choking coil, 250V 50Hz. VR = 125V, Vcoil = 200V. Find impedance, reactance, resistance of coil, power absorbed, total power.

Solution

a) Coil impedance = 200/5 = 40 Ω. Rcoil = 27.5/5 = 5.5 Ω. XL = 198.1/5 = 39.62 Ω.

b) Power by coil = I²R = 5² × 5.5 = 137.5 W

c) Total R = 25 + 5.5 = 30.5 Ω. Total power = 5² × 30.5 = 762.5 W

Example 5.3.1

Star-connected 3-phase: VL = 400V, R = 23Ω per phase. Find IP, IL, cos φ, total power.

Solution

VP = 400/√3 = 230V. IP = 230/23 = 10A. IL = √3 × 10 = 17.3A. cos φ = 1 (resistive).

P = √3 × 400 × 17.3 × 1 = 12 kW

Example 5.3.2

Delta-connected: Z = 8 + j6 per phase, VL = 400V. Find VP, IP, IL, total power.

Solution

VP = VL = 400V. Z = √(64+36) = 10Ω. IP = 400/10 = 40A. IL = √3 × 40 = 69.2A.

cos φ = R/Z = 8/10 = 0.8. P = √3 × 400 × 69.2 × 0.8 = 38.4 kW

Exercises

A. MCQs

Q 5.1

Peak value of sine wave occurs:

  • a. Once at positive max
  • b. Once at negative max
  • c. Twice at positive and negative max
  • d. Twice at positive max
Answer: c. Peak occurs twice per cycle — once positive max and once negative max.

Q 5.2

Imaginary part of impedance is:

  • a. Resistance
  • b. Reactance
  • c. Admittance
  • d. Conductance
Answer: b. Reactance. Z = R + jX where X (reactance) is the imaginary part.

Q 5.3

50 Hz sine wave angular frequency (rad/sec):

  • a. 50/π
  • b. 50/2π
  • c. 50π
  • d. 100π
Answer: d. 100π. ω = 2πf = 2π × 50 = 100π rad/sec.

Q 5.4

i(t) = 100 sin 314t achieves 86.6A after how many seconds from zero?

  • a. 1/300 sec
  • b. 1/600 sec
  • c. 1/1200 sec
  • d. 1/150 sec
Answer: a. 1/300 sec. 86.6 = 100 sin(314t) → sin(314t) = 0.866 → 314t = π/3 → t = π/(3×314) ≈ 1/300 sec.

Q 5.5

If v₁ = A sin ωt and v₂ = B sin(ωt − φ):

  • a. v₁ lags v₂ by φ
  • b. v₂ lags v₁ by φ
  • c. v₂ leads v₁ by φ
  • d. v₁ in phase with v₂
Answer: b. v₂ lags v₁ by φ. The negative angle (−φ) means v₂ reaches its peak later.

Q 5.6

Star connection, IL = 15A. Phase current = ?

  • a. 10 A
  • b. 12.5 A
  • c. 15 A
  • d. 17 A
Answer: c. 15 A. In star connection, IL = IP.

B. Subjective Questions

Q1. Define "Power factor". Value for pure inductive circuit?

Power factor = cos φ = ratio of true power to apparent power = R/Z. For pure inductive circuit, φ = 90°, so cos φ = 0.

Q2. v = 300 sin ωt across pure C (XC = 15Ω). Find: (a) i expression (b) power factor (c) phase angle, power consumed.

(a) Im = Vm/XC = 300/15 = 20A. i = 20 sin(ωt + 90°)

(b) cos φ = 0 (pure capacitive)

(c) Phase angle = 90° (current leads). Power = VI cos φ = 0 W.

Q3. R = 12Ω, L = 0.15H, C = 100μF, series, 100V 50Hz. Find Z, I, phase difference.

XL = 2π×50×0.15 = 47.12Ω. XC = 1/(2π×50×100×10⁻⁶) = 31.83Ω.

X = XL − XC = 15.29Ω. Z = √(12² + 15.29²) = 19.44Ω

I = 100/19.44 = 5.14A. φ = tan⁻¹(15.29/12) = 51.87° (current lags).

Q4. Z₁ = 10 + j15Ω, Z₂ = 6 − j8Ω in parallel, IS = 20A. Find power in each branch.

|Z₁| = √(100+225) = 18.03Ω, |Z₂| = √(36+64) = 10Ω. By current divider: I₁ = IS×Z₂/(Z₁+Z₂), I₂ = IS×Z₁/(Z₁+Z₂). Then P₁ = |I₁|²×R₁, P₂ = |I₂|²×R₂.

Q5. Delta system: VP = 500V, IP = 20A, cos φ = 0.8. Total power?

P = 3 × VP × IP × cos φ = 3 × 500 × 20 × 0.8 = 24,000 W = 24 kW

Unit Summary

Key Takeaways
  • RMS value = steady DC current producing same heat. Effective value = 0.707 × Im.
  • Phase is the relative position measured in time or angle.
  • One complete set of +ve and −ve values = cycle.
  • Phase angle between V and I found by dividing R by Z.
  • Power factor ranges from −1 to 1.
  • 3-phase advantages: ~3× power, rotating magnetic field, less conductor material, higher motor pf.
  • R, C, L can be combined as RC, RL, LC, RLC circuits.
  • Star: IL = IP; VL = √3 VP.
  • Delta: VL = VP; IL = √3 IP.
  • Total 3-phase power: W = √3 VL IL cos φ.
  • Power Triangle: Base = Active (W), Perpendicular = Reactive (VAR), Hypotenuse = Apparent (VA).
