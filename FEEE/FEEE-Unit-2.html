Unit Overview

What This Unit Covers

This unit explains the basic electrical quantities and the fundamentals of magnetic circuits. It is one of the most important units because many later topics such as transformers, motors, generators and inductors are based on these principles.

In simple words, this unit teaches you:

  • How electricity flows and does work
  • How magnetic fields are produced
  • How electricity and magnetism are related
  • How induced emf is generated
  • Why cores suffer hysteresis and eddy current losses
🔥 Why This Unit Matters: Without understanding electric and magnetic circuits, it is impossible to properly understand transformers, motors, generators, relays, chokes and inductors.

2.1 Basic Electrical Quantities: EMF, Current, Potential Difference, Power & Energy

Definition: Electromotive Force (EMF)

Electromotive force (EMF) is the total electrical energy supplied by a source per unit charge to move the charge around a complete circuit.

Formula: EMF

EMF = Work done by sourceCharge moved = W / Q

Unit of EMF = Volt (V)

Simple understanding: EMF is the pushing force of the source, like a pump that pushes water through pipes.

Definition: Electric Current

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor.

Formula: Current

I = Qt

Where:

  • I = Current in ampere (A)
  • Q = Charge in coulomb (C)
  • t = Time in second (s)

Analogy: If charge is like water, current is like the rate of water flow through a pipe.

Definition: Potential Difference (P.D.)

Potential difference between two points is the work done in moving unit positive charge from one point to the other.

Formula: Potential Difference

V = WQ

Unit = Volt (V)

Exam Note: EMF and potential difference have the same unit (volt), but they are not exactly the same. EMF is the total voltage supplied by the source, while potential difference is the voltage drop between two points in a circuit.

Power and Energy

Definition: Electric Power

Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed or produced.

Formula: Electric Power

P = V × I

Also,

P = I²R = V²/R

Unit = Watt (W)

Definition: Electrical Energy

Electrical energy is the total work done by electric power over a period of time.

Formula: Electrical Energy

E = P × t = VIt

Unit = Joule (J)

Practical unit = kilowatt-hour (kWh)

1 kWh = 3.6 × 106 J

Practical Examples
  • A battery provides EMF
  • Current flows through a fan motor when connected to supply
  • The voltage across a bulb is the potential difference across it
  • A 100W bulb consumes power at the rate of 100 joule per second
  • Your electricity bill is based on energy consumption in kWh

Solved Basics

Example 1

If 20 coulomb of charge passes through a conductor in 5 seconds, find the current.

Solution
Given: Q = 20 C, t = 5 s
Use formula: I = Q/t
I = 20/5 = 4 A

✅ Current = 4 A

Example 2

A device operates on 220V and draws 2A current. Find its power.

Solution
Given: V = 220V, I = 2A
P = VI = 220 × 2 = 440 W

✅ Power = 440 W

Example 3

A 1kW heater runs for 3 hours. Find energy consumed in kWh and joules.

Solution
Given: P = 1 kW, t = 3 h
Energy in kWh = P × t = 1 × 3 = 3 kWh
Energy in joule = 3 × 3.6 × 106 = 10.8 × 106 J

✅ Energy = 3 kWh = 10.8 × 106 J

2.2 Magnetic Circuit Fundamentals

What is a Magnetic Circuit?

A magnetic circuit is the closed path followed by magnetic flux. Just as electric current flows in an electric circuit, magnetic flux flows in a magnetic circuit.

Examples: transformer core, motor stator core, relay magnetic path.

Magnetomotive Force (MMF)

Magnetomotive force (MMF) is the force that produces magnetic flux in a magnetic circuit.

Formula: MMF

MMF = N × I

Where:

  • N = Number of turns of coil
  • I = Current through the coil (A)

Unit = Ampere-turn (AT)

Analogy: MMF in magnetic circuit is similar to EMF in electric circuit.

Magnetic Flux (φ)

Magnetic flux is the total number of magnetic lines of force set up in a magnetic circuit.

Unit = Weber (Wb)

Magnetic Flux Density (B)

Magnetic flux density is the flux per unit area normal to the magnetic field.

Formula: Flux Density

B = φ / A

Unit = Weber/m² = Tesla (T)

Magnetic Field Strength (H)

Magnetic field strength is the magnetizing force per unit length of the magnetic path.

Formula: Field Strength

H = NI / l

Unit = Ampere-turn per metre (AT/m) or A/m

Permeability (μ)

Permeability is the property of a material by which it allows magnetic lines of force to pass through it.

Formula: Permeability

μ = B / H

Also,

μ = μ0 × μr

  • μ0 = permeability of free space = 4π × 10-7 H/m
  • μr = relative permeability
Reluctance (ℜ)

Reluctance is the opposition offered by a magnetic circuit to the establishment of magnetic flux.

Formula: Reluctance

ℜ = l / (μA)

Where:

  • l = length of magnetic path (m)
  • μ = permeability of material
  • A = cross-sectional area (m²)

Unit = AT/Wb

Magnetic Ohm's Law

φ = MMF / ℜ = NI / ℜ

🔥 Very Important: In magnetic circuits, the basic relation is: Flux = MMF / Reluctance This is exactly similar to electric law: Current = EMF / Resistance

Magnetic Force

When a magnetic field acts on a moving charge or a current-carrying conductor, a force is produced. This is the basic principle of motors.

Force on a Current Carrying Conductor

When a conductor carrying current is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force.

F = B I l sin θ

  • F = force in newton
  • B = flux density in tesla
  • I = current in ampere
  • l = length of conductor in magnetic field
  • θ = angle between conductor and magnetic field

Leakage Factor

Leakage Factor

Leakage factor is the ratio of total flux produced in a magnetic circuit to the useful flux.

Formula: Leakage Factor

Leakage Factor = Total Flux / Useful Flux

Since some flux leaks into air and does not follow the desired path, leakage factor is always greater than 1.

B-H Curve

B-H Curve

The B-H curve shows the relationship between magnetic flux density B and magnetic field strength H for a magnetic material.

As H increases, B also increases, but after some point the material reaches saturation and B does not increase much even if H increases further.

2.2 Hysteresis Loop, Hysteresis Loss & Eddy Current Loss

Why Magnetic Losses Occur

When magnetic materials like iron are subjected to alternating magnetization, they do not behave perfectly. Some energy is lost in the core in each cycle. These losses mainly are:

  • Hysteresis loss
  • Eddy current loss

These losses are very important in transformers and electrical machines.

Hysteresis Loop

Hysteresis

Hysteresis means lagging. In a magnetic material, magnetic flux density B lags behind the magnetizing force H.

Hysteresis Loop

When a magnetic material is taken through a complete cycle of magnetization, the graph between B and H forms a closed loop called the hysteresis loop.

The hysteresis loop gives important magnetic properties:

  • Retentivity: Ability of material to retain magnetism after removing H
  • Coercivity: Reverse magnetizing force required to reduce residual magnetism to zero
  • Saturation: Condition when increase in H does not produce much increase in B

Fig. 2.5: Hysteresis loop showing saturation, retentivity and coercivity

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
🔥 Exam Important: Area of hysteresis loop represents the hysteresis energy loss per cycle per unit volume of magnetic material.

Hysteresis Loss

Hysteresis Loss

Hysteresis loss is the energy lost in the form of heat in a magnetic material during repeated magnetization and demagnetization due to molecular friction.

Steinmetz Formula

Ph = η Bmax1.6 f V

Where:

  • Ph = hysteresis loss
  • η = Steinmetz coefficient (depends on material)
  • Bmax = maximum flux density
  • f = frequency
  • V = volume of core

How to reduce hysteresis loss?

  • Use magnetic materials with narrow hysteresis loop
  • Use silicon steel in transformers
  • Use soft magnetic materials where frequent magnetization reversal occurs

Eddy Current Loss

Eddy Current

Eddy currents are circulating currents induced inside the body of a magnetic material when it is subjected to a changing magnetic flux.

Eddy Current Loss

Eddy current loss is the heat loss caused by the flow of eddy currents inside the magnetic core.

When alternating flux links an iron core, small circular currents are induced inside the core. These currents cause I²R loss, which heats the core.

Fig. 2.6: Eddy currents in a solid iron core and reduction by lamination

Pe ∝ Bmax2 f2 t2 V

Where t is the thickness of each lamination.

How to reduce eddy current loss?

  • Use thin laminated core instead of solid core
  • Insulate laminations from each other
  • Use high resistivity magnetic materials such as silicon steel

2.3 Electromagnetic Induction: Faraday’s Laws and Lenz’s Law

What is Electromagnetic Induction?

Electromagnetic induction is the phenomenon of production of emf in a conductor or coil whenever the magnetic flux linked with it changes.

This is one of the most important principles in electrical engineering because generators, transformers, inductors, relays and many measuring instruments work on this principle.

Simple idea: If magnetic field changes near a conductor, electricity is produced in that conductor.

🔥 Very Important: No change in magnetic flux → No induced emf.
Change may occur by moving the conductor, moving the magnet, or changing the current producing the magnetic field.

Faraday’s Laws of Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday’s First Law

Whenever the magnetic flux linking a conductor or coil changes, an emf is induced in it.

Faraday’s Second Law

The magnitude of induced emf is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux linkage.

Formula: Induced EMF

e = - N dφdt

For average value,

e = - N ΔφΔt

  • e = induced emf in volt
  • N = number of turns
  • φ = magnetic flux in weber
  • t = time in second
Meaning of Negative Sign

The negative sign in Faraday’s law comes from Lenz’s law. It shows that the induced emf always acts in such a direction that it opposes the cause producing it.

Fig. 2.7: Electromagnetic induction by moving a magnet towards and away from a coil

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Lenz’s Law

Lenz’s Law

Lenz’s law states that the direction of induced emf or induced current is such that it opposes the cause producing it.

Suppose a magnet is moved towards a coil. Then the induced current in the coil will produce a magnetic field that tries to oppose that approach. If the magnet is moved away, the induced current changes direction so that it tries to oppose the separation.

Physical Meaning of Lenz’s Law

Lenz’s law is actually a statement of conservation of energy. If induced current helped the change instead of opposing it, energy would be created from nothing, which is impossible.

Fig. 2.8: Lenz’s law showing direction of induced current for approaching and receding magnet

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Conditions for Inducing EMF

  • There must be a magnetic field
  • There must be a conductor or coil
  • There must be a change in flux linkage

Ways to Change Flux

  • Move conductor in a magnetic field
  • Move magnet relative to coil
  • Change the magnetic field strength
  • Change the area of coil linked with flux
  • Rotate a coil in magnetic field
Applications of Electromagnetic Induction
  • Electric generators
  • Transformers
  • Induction motors
  • Dynamic microphones
  • Induction furnaces
  • Speedometers and measuring instruments
  • Wireless charging systems
Example 6

A coil of 200 turns experiences a change in flux from 0.01 Wb to 0.03 Wb in 0.2 second. Find the average induced emf.

Solution
Given: N = 200, φ₁ = 0.01 Wb, φ₂ = 0.03 Wb, Δt = 0.2 s
Change in flux, Δφ = φ₂ − φ₁ = 0.03 − 0.01 = 0.02 Wb
Using Faraday’s law, e = N(Δφ/Δt)
e = 200 × (0.02/0.2) = 200 × 0.1 = 20 V

✅ Average induced emf = 20 V

2.4 Dynamically Induced EMF, Statically Induced EMF, Self and Mutual Inductance

A. Dynamically Induced EMF

Dynamically Induced EMF

When a conductor moves in a magnetic field and cuts magnetic flux, an emf is induced in it. This emf is called dynamically induced emf.

This is the principle of electric generator.

Formula: Dynamically Induced EMF

e = B l v sin θ

  • B = flux density (T)
  • l = active length of conductor (m)
  • v = velocity of conductor (m/s)
  • θ = angle between direction of motion and magnetic field

When conductor moves perpendicular to field, θ = 90°, so:

e = B l v

B. Statically Induced EMF

Statically Induced EMF

When the conductor or coil remains stationary but the magnetic flux linking it changes, the induced emf is called statically induced emf.

This is the principle of transformer.

Formula: Statically Induced EMF

e = - N dφdt

Statically induced emf is of two types:

  1. Self induced emf
  2. Mutually induced emf

C. Self Inductance

Self Inductance

Self inductance is the property of a coil by which a change in current through it induces an emf in the same coil, opposing that change.

If current in a coil changes, the magnetic flux produced by that coil also changes. This changing flux links the same coil and induces emf in it. This induced emf is called self induced emf.

Formula: Self Induced EMF

e = - L dIdt

Where L is self inductance in henry (H).

Formula: Inductance

L = NφI

Also, flux linkage λ = Nφ, so:

L = λ / I

Unit of Inductance

A coil has inductance of 1 henry if a current changing at the rate of 1 ampere per second induces an emf of 1 volt in it.

D. Mutual Inductance

Mutual Inductance

Mutual inductance is the property by which a change in current in one coil induces emf in another nearby coil.

When current in the primary coil changes, the magnetic flux linked with the secondary coil also changes. Because of that, emf is induced in the secondary coil. This is the principle of transformer action.

Formula: Mutually Induced EMF

e = - M dIdt

Where M is mutual inductance in henry (H).

Formula: Mutual Inductance

M = N2 φ12I1

Example 7

A straight conductor of length 0.5 m moves with velocity 10 m/s at right angle to a magnetic field of flux density 0.8 T. Find induced emf.

Solution
Given: B = 0.8 T, l = 0.5 m, v = 10 m/s
Since motion is at right angle, e = Blv
e = 0.8 × 0.5 × 10 = 4 V

✅ Induced emf = 4 V

Example 8

The current in a 2 H inductor changes from 2 A to 8 A in 3 seconds. Find the average self induced emf.

Solution
Given: L = 2 H, I₁ = 2 A, I₂ = 8 A, Δt = 3 s
Change in current, ΔI = 8 − 2 = 6 A
Average induced emf, e = L(ΔI/Δt)
e = 2 × (6/3) = 2 × 2 = 4 V

✅ Average self induced emf = 4 V

2.5 Energy Stored in Magnetic Circuit

Basic Idea

Whenever current flows through a coil or inductor, a magnetic field is produced around it. This magnetic field stores energy. Therefore, an inductor behaves like an energy storage device.

Energy Stored in Magnetic Field

The work done in establishing current in an inductor is stored as magnetic energy in the magnetic field around it.

Formula: Magnetic Energy

W = 12 L I²

  • W = energy stored in joule
  • L = inductance in henry
  • I = current in ampere

This formula is very important and often asked in short answer, long answer, and numerical form.

Derivation of Energy Stored in an Inductor

Simple Derivation

We know induced emf in an inductor is:

e = L dIdt

Power supplied to inductor:

p = eI = L I dIdt

Energy stored,

W = ∫p dt = ∫ L I dIdt dt = ∫ L I dI

Taking L constant,

W = L ∫ I dI = L I²2

Therefore,

W = 12LI²

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Practical Significance
  • Energy storage in chokes and inductors
  • Switching power supplies
  • Electromagnets and relays
  • Transformers and machines
  • Magnetic field collapse can produce high voltage spikes
Example 9

An inductor of 5 H carries a current of 4 A. Find the energy stored in it.

Solution
Given: L = 5 H, I = 4 A
Use formula: W = ½LI²
W = ½ × 5 × 4² = 0.5 × 5 × 16 = 40 J

✅ Energy stored = 40 J

2.6 Fleming’s Left Hand Rule and Right Hand Rule

Fleming’s Left Hand Rule

Fleming’s Left Hand Rule

If the thumb, forefinger and middle finger of the left hand are stretched mutually perpendicular to each other, then:

  • Forefinger shows direction of magnetic field
  • Middle finger shows direction of current
  • Thumb shows direction of force or motion

This rule is used for motors, where a current carrying conductor placed in magnetic field experiences force.

Fleming’s Right Hand Rule

Fleming’s Right Hand Rule

If the thumb, forefinger and middle finger of the right hand are stretched mutually perpendicular to each other, then:

  • Forefinger shows direction of magnetic field
  • Thumb shows direction of motion of conductor
  • Middle finger shows direction of induced current

This rule is used for generators, where conductor motion in magnetic field produces induced current.

Solved Numericals

Numerical 1: MMF

A coil of 1000 turns carries 1.5 A current. Find the magnetomotive force.

Solution
Given: N = 1000, I = 1.5 A
MMF = NI = 1000 × 1.5 = 1500 AT

✅ MMF = 1500 AT

Numerical 2: Field Strength

A magnetic path of length 0.4 m is wound with 800 turns carrying 2 A current. Find magnetic field strength H.

Solution
Given: N = 800, I = 2 A, l = 0.4 m
H = NI/l = 800 × 2 / 0.4
H = 1600 / 0.4 = 4000 A/m

✅ H = 4000 A/m

Numerical 3: Reluctance

A magnetic core has path length 0.5 m, area 4 × 10-4 m² and permeability 2 × 10-3 H/m. Find reluctance.

Solution
Given: l = 0.5 m, A = 4 × 10-4 m², μ = 2 × 10-3 H/m
ℜ = l/(μA)
ℜ = 0.5 / [(2 × 10-3) × (4 × 10-4)]
ℜ = 0.5 / (8 × 10-7) = 6.25 × 105 AT/Wb

✅ Reluctance = 6.25 × 105 AT/Wb

Numerical 4: Magnetic Flux

A magnetic circuit has MMF = 1200 AT and reluctance = 3 × 105 AT/Wb. Find flux.

Solution
Given: MMF = 1200 AT, ℜ = 3 × 105 AT/Wb
Flux, φ = MMF/ℜ
φ = 1200 / (3 × 105) = 4 × 10-3 Wb

✅ Flux = 4 × 10-3 Wb

Numerical 5: Dynamically Induced EMF

A conductor of length 0.25 m moves at 12 m/s in a magnetic field of 1.2 T at right angle. Find induced emf.

Solution
Given: B = 1.2 T, l = 0.25 m, v = 12 m/s
e = Blv = 1.2 × 0.25 × 12 = 3.6 V

✅ Induced emf = 3.6 V

Numerical 6: Faraday’s Law

A 500-turn coil experiences a change in flux of 0.04 Wb in 0.5 second. Find average induced emf.

Solution
Given: N = 500, Δφ = 0.04 Wb, Δt = 0.5 s
e = N(Δφ/Δt) = 500 × (0.04/0.5)
e = 500 × 0.08 = 40 V

✅ Average induced emf = 40 V

Numerical 7: Self Induced EMF

A 4 H coil carries current changing from 1 A to 5 A in 2 second. Find self induced emf.

Solution
Given: L = 4 H, ΔI = 5 − 1 = 4 A, Δt = 2 s
e = L(ΔI/Δt) = 4 × (4/2) = 8 V

✅ Self induced emf = 8 V

Numerical 8: Energy Stored

A coil of inductance 3 H carries 6 A current. Find energy stored.

Solution
Given: L = 3 H, I = 6 A
W = ½LI² = ½ × 3 × 6²
W = 0.5 × 3 × 36 = 54 J

✅ Energy stored = 54 J

Long Answer Questions (5–7 Marks Style)

Q 1. Explain the important terms used in magnetic circuits.

The important magnetic circuit terms are:

  • MMF: Magnetizing force producing flux. MMF = NI.
  • Magnetic flux: Total magnetic lines of force, unit weber.
  • Flux density: Flux per unit area, B = φ/A, unit tesla.
  • Field strength: Magnetizing force per unit length, H = NI/l.
  • Permeability: Ability of material to carry flux, μ = B/H.
  • Reluctance: Opposition to magnetic flux, ℜ = l/(μA).

The basic magnetic relation is φ = MMF/ℜ, which is similar to Ohm's law in electric circuits.

Q 2. Explain B-H curve, hysteresis loop, hysteresis loss and eddy current loss.

B-H curve shows the relation between magnetic flux density B and magnetic field strength H. As H increases, B increases up to saturation.

Hysteresis loop is the closed curve obtained when a material is taken through a complete cycle of magnetization. It gives retentivity, coercivity and saturation.

Hysteresis loss occurs due to repeated magnetization and demagnetization and is represented by the area of hysteresis loop. It can be reduced by using soft magnetic materials.

Eddy current loss is caused by circulating currents induced inside the magnetic core due to changing flux. It is reduced by using thin laminated cores and high resistivity materials.

Q 3. State and explain Faraday’s laws and Lenz’s law.

Faraday’s First Law: Whenever magnetic flux linking a conductor changes, an emf is induced in it.

Faraday’s Second Law: The magnitude of induced emf is directly proportional to the rate of change of flux linkage.

e = -N(dφ/dt)

Lenz’s Law: The direction of induced emf/current is such that it opposes the cause producing it.

The negative sign in Faraday’s law represents Lenz’s law. These laws form the basis of generators, transformers and inductors.

Q 4. Differentiate between dynamically induced emf and statically induced emf.

Dynamically induced emf is produced when a conductor moves in a magnetic field and cuts flux. Formula: e = Blv sinθ. Example: generator.

Statically induced emf is produced when the conductor is stationary but flux linking it changes. Formula: e = -N(dφ/dt). Example: transformer.

Main differences:

  • Dynamic emf requires motion; static emf does not.
  • Dynamic emf is due to flux cutting; static emf is due to flux change.
  • Generator uses dynamic emf; transformer uses static emf.

Q 5. Explain self inductance and mutual inductance with formulas.

Self inductance is the property of a coil by which change in current through it induces emf in the same coil.

e = -L(dI/dt)

Mutual inductance is the property by which change in current in one coil induces emf in another nearby coil.

e = -M(dI/dt)

Self inductance is used in inductors and choke coils. Mutual inductance is used in transformers. Self inductance involves one coil; mutual inductance involves two coils.

Q 6. Explain Fleming’s Left Hand Rule and Right Hand Rule.

Fleming’s Left Hand Rule: If thumb, forefinger and middle finger of left hand are held mutually perpendicular, then forefinger gives magnetic field direction, middle finger gives current direction and thumb gives force or motion. It is used for motor action.

Fleming’s Right Hand Rule: If thumb, forefinger and middle finger of right hand are held mutually perpendicular, then forefinger gives field direction, thumb gives motion of conductor and middle finger gives induced current direction. It is used for generator action.
