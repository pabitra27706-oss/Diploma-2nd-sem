1.1 Passive Components: Resistors, Capacitors & Inductors

What are Passive Components?

In electrical engineering, all components are classified into two broad categories:

  • Active Components: Components that can supply energy or amplify signals. Examples: Batteries, Transistors, Op-Amps.
  • Passive Components: Components that can only absorb, store, or dissipate energy. They cannot generate or amplify energy on their own. Examples: Resistors, Capacitors, Inductors.

Simple Analogy: Active components are like a water pump (they push water). Passive components are like pipes, tanks, and valves (they control water flow but don't create it).

Definition: Passive Component

A passive component is an electrical component that does not require an external power source to operate and cannot introduce net energy into a circuit. It can only consume (resistor), store temporarily (capacitor, inductor), or release stored energy.

🔥 Exam Important: The three basic passive components are: Resistor (R), Capacitor (C), and Inductor (L). Every electrical circuit is made up of combinations of these three elements along with active sources.

A. Resistor (R)

Definition: Resistor

A resistor is a passive two-terminal component that opposes (resists) the flow of electric current through it. It converts electrical energy into heat energy. Its resistance is measured in Ohms (Ω).

Real-world Analogy: Imagine water flowing through a pipe. If you make the pipe narrower, the water flow reduces. A resistor is like that narrow section — it restricts current flow, just like a narrow pipe restricts water flow.

Ohm's Law — The Most Fundamental Law

Formula: Ohm's Law

V = I × R

Where:

  • V = Voltage across the resistor (in Volts, V)
  • I = Current flowing through the resistor (in Amperes, A)
  • R = Resistance of the resistor (in Ohms, Ω)

Resistance Formula

Formula: Resistance of a Conductor

R = ρ × LA

Where:

  • R = Resistance (Ω)
  • ρ (rho) = Resistivity or specific resistance of the material (Ω·m)
  • L = Length of the conductor (m)
  • A = Cross-sectional area of the conductor (m²)

Power Dissipated in a Resistor

Formulas: Power in a Resistor

P = V × I = I² × R = V²R

Where P = Power dissipated (in Watts, W). A resistor always converts electrical energy into heat.

Series and Parallel Combination of Resistors

Series Connection

Resistors are connected end-to-end. Same current flows through all resistors.

Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...

  • Current is same through all resistors
  • Voltage is divided across each resistor
  • Total resistance increases
  • Rtotal is always greater than the largest individual resistance

Parallel Connection

Resistors are connected across each other. Same voltage across all resistors.

1Rtotal = 1R₁ + 1R₂ + 1R₃ + ...

  • Voltage is same across all resistors
  • Current is divided among resistors
  • Total resistance decreases
  • Rtotal is always less than the smallest individual resistance
Quick Tip: Two Resistors in Parallel

For only two resistors in parallel, use the shortcut formula:

Rtotal = R₁ × R₂R₁ + R₂

This is much faster than the reciprocal method!

B. Capacitor (C)

Definition: Capacitor

A capacitor is a passive two-terminal component that stores electrical energy in an electric field between two conducting plates separated by an insulating material (called a dielectric). Its ability to store charge is called capacitance, measured in Farads (F).

Real-world Analogy: A capacitor is like a small water tank. It fills up (charges) when water flows in, stores the water, and releases it when needed. Similarly, a capacitor stores electrical charge and releases it when the circuit needs it.

Construction of a Capacitor

A basic capacitor consists of:

  1. Two metallic plates (conductors) — called electrodes
  2. Dielectric material (insulator) between the plates — such as air, paper, mica, ceramic, or electrolyte
  3. When voltage is applied, positive charge accumulates on one plate and negative charge on the other

Fig. 1.5: Construction of a parallel plate capacitor showing plates and dielectric

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Capacitance Formula

Formula: Capacitance

C = QV    and    C = ε₀ × εr × Ad

Where:

  • C = Capacitance (Farads, F)
  • Q = Charge stored (Coulombs, C)
  • V = Voltage applied across plates (Volts, V)
  • ε₀ = Permittivity of free space = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m
  • εr = Relative permittivity (dielectric constant) of the dielectric material
  • A = Area of each plate (m²)
  • d = Distance between plates (m)

Energy Stored in a Capacitor

Formula: Energy Stored

E = 12 × C × V² = 12 × Q × V = Q²2C

Where E = Energy stored (in Joules, J)

Capacitors combine OPPOSITE to resistors:

  • Resistors in series → ADD; Capacitors in series → reciprocal ADD
  • Resistors in parallel → reciprocal ADD; Capacitors in parallel → ADD

This is a very common exam trick question!

C. Inductor (L)

Definition: Inductor

An inductor is a passive two-terminal component that stores electrical energy in a magnetic field when electric current flows through it. It is essentially a coil of wire wound around a core. Its property is called inductance, measured in Henry (H).

Real-world Analogy: An inductor is like a heavy flywheel. A flywheel resists changes in rotational speed — once spinning, it wants to keep spinning; when stopped, it resists starting. Similarly, an inductor resists changes in current. It tries to keep the current constant.

Construction of an Inductor

A basic inductor consists of:

  1. Coil of wire — usually copper wire wound in multiple turns
  2. Core — can be air core, iron core, or ferrite core
  3. When current flows through the coil, it creates a magnetic field
  4. This magnetic field stores energy

Inductance Formula

Formula: Inductance of a Coil

L = μ₀ × μr × N² × Al

Where:

  • L = Inductance (Henry, H)
  • μ₀ = Permeability of free space = 4π × 10⁻⁷ H/m
  • μr = Relative permeability of core material
  • N = Number of turns in the coil
  • A = Cross-sectional area of the core (m²)
  • l = Length of the coil (m)

Voltage Across an Inductor

Formula: Inductor Voltage

VL = L × dIdt

The voltage across an inductor is proportional to the rate of change of current through it. If current is constant (DC steady state), VL = 0, meaning inductor acts as a short circuit for DC.

Energy Stored in an Inductor

Formula: Energy Stored

E = 12 × L × I²

Where E = Energy stored (in Joules, J), L = Inductance (H), I = Current (A)

1.2 Types of Signal Waveforms

What is a Signal Waveform?

A signal waveform is the graphical representation of how a voltage or current varies with time. When we plot voltage (or current) on the Y-axis and time on the X-axis, the resulting shape is called the waveform.

Understanding waveforms is essential because different circuits respond differently to different types of signals.

A. DC (Direct Current) vs AC (Alternating Current)

Definition: Direct Current (DC)

Direct Current (DC) is a type of electric current that flows in only one direction and has a constant magnitude over time. The polarity of DC voltage remains the same — positive terminal stays positive and negative terminal stays negative.

Sources of DC: Batteries, Solar cells, DC generators, Rectifier output.

Definition: Alternating Current (AC)

Alternating Current (AC) is a type of electric current that periodically reverses its direction and continuously changes its magnitude over time. The polarity of AC voltage alternates between positive and negative.

Sources of AC: AC generators (alternators), Power stations, Inverters.

B. Voltage Waveform vs Current Waveform

Voltage & Current Waveforms

Both voltage and current can have waveforms. The key difference:

  • Voltage waveform: Shows how voltage (V) varies with time (t). Y-axis = Volts.
  • Current waveform: Shows how current (I) varies with time (t). Y-axis = Amperes.

In a resistive circuit, voltage and current waveforms have the same shape (both are in phase). But in circuits with capacitors or inductors, the current waveform may lead or lag the voltage waveform (phase difference — covered in Unit 3).

C. Periodic vs Non-Periodic Waveforms

Definition: Periodic Waveform

A periodic waveform is one that repeats itself at regular intervals of time. The time taken for one complete repetition (one cycle) is called the Time Period (T).

Mathematically: f(t) = f(t + T), where T is the period.

Definition: Non-Periodic Waveform

A non-periodic (aperiodic) waveform is one that does NOT repeat itself at regular intervals. It has no fixed time period or frequency.

Types of Periodic Waveforms

🔴 Sinusoidal (Sine) Wave

The most fundamental periodic waveform. Smooth and continuous. Used for AC power supply.

v(t) = Vm sin(ωt)

🟢 Square Wave

Alternates between two fixed levels (HIGH and LOW) with sharp transitions. Used in digital electronics as clock signal.

🔵 Triangular Wave

Linearly rises and falls in a symmetrical triangle shape. Used in testing circuits and function generators.

🟡 Sawtooth Wave

Rises linearly and drops sharply (or vice versa). Used in CRT TV scanning and music synthesizers.

1.3 Voltage & Current Sources

Introduction to Sources

Every electrical circuit needs a source of energy to drive current through it. These sources provide the electromotive force (EMF) that pushes electrons through the circuit.

Sources are classified in two ways:

  1. Based on output type: Voltage Source vs Current Source
  2. Based on dependence: Independent Source vs Dependent Source

And each type can be either ideal or non-ideal (practical).

A. Voltage Source

Definition: Voltage Source

A voltage source is a two-terminal device that maintains a fixed voltage across its terminals regardless of the current drawn from it. It provides a constant potential difference.

i. Ideal Voltage Source

Ideal Voltage Source

An ideal voltage source maintains a constant terminal voltage regardless of the load current. No matter how much current the load draws, the voltage stays exactly the same.

  • Internal resistance = 0 Ω (zero)
  • Terminal voltage = EMF (always)
  • Can supply unlimited current (theoretically)
  • Does NOT exist in reality — it is a theoretical concept

Vterminal = Vsource (always, for any current)

ii. Non-Ideal (Practical) Voltage Source

Practical Voltage Source

A practical (non-ideal) voltage source has a small internal resistance (rs) in series with the ideal source. Because of this, the terminal voltage drops when current is drawn.

  • Internal resistance = rs (small but non-zero)
  • Terminal voltage < EMF (when current flows)
  • More current drawn → more voltage drop across rs

Vterminal = Vsource − I × rs

Example: A battery has EMF = 12V and internal resistance rs = 0.5Ω. When 2A current flows: Vterminal = 12 − 2×0.5 = 11V

B. Current Source

Definition: Current Source

A current source is a two-terminal device that maintains a fixed current through it regardless of the voltage across its terminals.

i. Ideal Current Source

Ideal Current Source

An ideal current source supplies a constant current to the circuit regardless of the load resistance or voltage across it.

  • Internal resistance = ∞ (infinite)
  • Output current is always constant
  • Does NOT exist in reality

Ioutput = Isource (always, for any voltage)

ii. Non-Ideal (Practical) Current Source

Practical Current Source

A practical (non-ideal) current source has a large but finite internal resistance (Rs) in parallel with the ideal current source. Some current leaks through this internal resistance.

  • Internal resistance = Rs (large but finite)
  • Load current < Source current (some current diverted through Rs)

Iload = Isource − VRs

D. Independent vs Dependent Sources

Definition: Independent Source

An independent source provides a voltage or current that is NOT affected by any other voltage or current in the circuit. Its value is fixed or varies only with time (not with other circuit variables).

Symbol: Represented by a circle.

Definition: Dependent (Controlled) Source

A dependent source (also called a controlled source) provides a voltage or current whose value depends on (is controlled by) some other voltage or current elsewhere in the circuit.

Symbol: Represented by a diamond (rhombus) shape.

1.3–1.4 Source Transformation

What is Source Transformation?

Source transformation is a technique to convert (transform) a practical voltage source into an equivalent practical current source, or vice versa. The transformed source produces the same voltage and current at its terminals — the rest of the circuit cannot tell the difference!

Analogy: It's like saying the same thing in two different languages. The meaning stays the same, only the form changes. A voltage source with series resistance and a current source with parallel resistance can behave identically at their terminals.

🔥🔥 Most Important Topic for Exam: Source transformation is the most frequently asked numerical problem from Unit 1 in WBSCTE exams. Master this technique thoroughly!

A. Transformation Rules

Voltage Source → Current Source

A practical voltage source Vs with series resistance Rs can be converted to:

An equivalent current source Is with parallel resistance Rs

Is = VsRs    (with Rs in parallel)

Current Source → Voltage Source

A practical current source Is with parallel resistance Rs can be converted to:

An equivalent voltage source Vs with series resistance Rs

Vs = Is × Rs    (with Rs in series)

B. Key Rules to Remember

Rules for Source Transformation
  1. The resistance value Rs remains the same in both forms — it just changes from series (voltage source) to parallel (current source), or vice versa.
  2. Polarity matters! The direction of current in the current source must match the direction that current would flow out of the positive terminal of the voltage source.
  3. Ideal sources CANNOT be transformed:
    • An ideal voltage source (Rs = 0) cannot be converted to a current source (I = V/0 = ∞)
    • An ideal current source (Rs = ∞) cannot be converted to a voltage source (V = I×∞ = ∞)
  4. Only independent sources can be transformed (not dependent sources in simple analysis)
  5. The terminal behavior is identical — the load connected to either form sees the same voltage and current
Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • Mistake 1: Forgetting to change series resistance to parallel (or vice versa)
  • Mistake 2: Getting the current direction wrong in the transformed source
  • Mistake 3: Trying to transform an ideal source (Rs = 0 or ∞)
  • Mistake 4: Changing the value of Rs during transformation — it stays the SAME!

C. Step-by-Step Method

How to Perform Source Transformation
Identify the source type — Is it a voltage source with series resistance, or a current source with parallel resistance?
Note the values — Write down Vs (or Is) and Rs.
Apply the formula:
  • Voltage → Current: Is = Vs / Rs
  • Current → Voltage: Vs = Is × Rs
Change connection type: Series resistance ↔ Parallel resistance.
Check polarity/direction: Current arrow should point from − to + terminal through the source (or in the direction that Vs would push current).
Redraw the circuit with the new equivalent source.

D. Solved Examples on Source Transformation

Example 1: Voltage Source to Current Source

Problem: Convert a 20V voltage source with 5Ω series resistance into an equivalent current source.

Solution
Given: Vs = 20V, Rs = 5Ω
Apply formula: Is = Vs / Rs
Is = 20 / 5 = 4A
The equivalent is a 4A current source with 5Ω resistance in parallel.

✅ Equivalent: 4A current source with 5Ω parallel resistance

Example 2: Current Source to Voltage Source

Problem: Convert a 3A current source with 10Ω parallel resistance into an equivalent voltage source.

Solution
Given: Is = 3A, Rs = 10Ω
Apply formula: Vs = Is × Rs
Vs = 3 × 10 = 30V
The equivalent is a 30V voltage source with 10Ω resistance in series.

✅ Equivalent: 30V voltage source with 10Ω series resistance

Example 3: Finding Load Current Using Source Transformation

Problem: A 12V battery with internal resistance 2Ω is connected to a 4Ω load resistor. Find the load current using source transformation.

Solution
Given: Vs = 12V, rs = 2Ω, RL = 4Ω
Method 1 (Direct):
I = Vs / (rs + RL) = 12 / (2 + 4) = 12/6 = 2A
Method 2 (Source Transformation):
Convert voltage source to current source:
Is = Vs / rs = 12/2 = 6A
Now we have 6A current source with 2Ω in parallel with 4Ω load.
Using current divider:
IL = Is × RsRs + RL = 6 × 22 + 4 = 6 × 26 = 2A

✅ Load current IL = 2A (Both methods give same answer — confirming source transformation works!)

When to Use Source Transformation?
  • When a circuit has mixed sources (some voltage, some current) — transform all to one type for easier analysis
  • When you need to simplify a complex circuit before applying Ohm's law
  • When combining parallel voltage sources or series current sources becomes difficult
  • It is often used as an intermediate step in Norton's and Thevenin's theorem (covered in advanced courses)

A. Resistor Based Numericals

Numerical 1: Ohm's Law

A resistor of 10Ω is connected across a 20V supply. Find the current through the resistor and power dissipated.

Solution
Given: R = 10Ω, V = 20V
Using Ohm's law, I = V/R = 20/10 = 2A
Power, P = V × I = 20 × 2 = 40W

✅ Current = 2A, Power dissipated = 40W

Numerical 2: Series Resistors

Three resistors of 2Ω, 3Ω and 5Ω are connected in series across a 20V supply. Find (i) total resistance, (ii) total current, (iii) voltage across each resistor.

Solution
Total series resistance: RT = 2 + 3 + 5 = 10Ω
Total current: I = V / RT = 20 / 10 = 2A
Voltage across 2Ω resistor: V₁ = I × R₁ = 2 × 2 = 4V
Voltage across 3Ω resistor: V₂ = 2 × 3 = 6V
Voltage across 5Ω resistor: V₃ = 2 × 5 = 10V
Check: V₁ + V₂ + V₃ = 4 + 6 + 10 = 20V ✔

✅ RT = 10Ω, I = 2A, Voltages = 4V, 6V, 10V

Numerical 3: Parallel Resistors

Two resistors of 6Ω and 3Ω are connected in parallel across a 12V source. Find the equivalent resistance and current through each branch.

Solution
Equivalent resistance: 1RT = 16 + 13 = 16 + 26 = 36 = 12
So, RT = 2Ω
Current through 6Ω branch: I₁ = V / R₁ = 12 / 6 = 2A
Current through 3Ω branch: I₂ = 12 / 3 = 4A
Total current = I₁ + I₂ = 2 + 4 = 6A

✅ Equivalent resistance = 2Ω, Branch currents = 2A and 4A

B. Capacitor Based Numericals

Numerical 4: Charge Stored in a Capacitor

A capacitor of 10μF is connected across 100V. Find the charge stored.

Solution
Given: C = 10μF = 10 × 10⁻⁶ F, V = 100V
Using Q = C × V
Q = 10 × 10⁻⁶ × 100 = 1000 × 10⁻⁶ C
Q = 10⁻³ C = 1 mC

✅ Charge stored = 1 mC

Numerical 5: Energy Stored in a Capacitor

A 20μF capacitor is charged to 50V. Find the energy stored in it.

Solution
Given: C = 20μF = 20 × 10⁻⁶ F, V = 50V
Formula: E = ½CV²
E = ½ × 20 × 10⁻⁶ × (50)²
E = 0.5 × 20 × 10⁻⁶ × 2500
E = 10 × 10⁻⁶ × 2500 = 25000 × 10⁻⁶ J
E = 25 × 10⁻³ J = 0.025 J

✅ Energy stored = 0.025 J

C. Inductor Based Numericals

Numerical 6: Energy Stored in an Inductor

An inductor of 2H carries a current of 3A. Find the energy stored in the magnetic field.

Solution
Given: L = 2H, I = 3A
Formula: E = ½LI²
E = ½ × 2 × (3)²
E = 1 × 9 = 9J

✅ Energy stored = 9 J

D. Source Transformation Numericals

Numerical 7: Practical Voltage Source Transformation

Convert a 24V practical voltage source with internal resistance 6Ω into its equivalent practical current source.

Solution
Given: Vs = 24V, Rs = 6Ω
Formula: Is = Vs/Rs
Is = 24/6 = 4A
Equivalent source = 4A current source with 6Ω in parallel

✅ Equivalent current source = 4A with 6Ω parallel resistance

Numerical 8: Practical Current Source Transformation

Convert a 5A current source with 8Ω parallel resistance into an equivalent voltage source.

Solution
Given: Is = 5A, Rs = 8Ω
Formula: Vs = Is × Rs
Vs = 5 × 8 = 40V
Equivalent source = 40V voltage source with 8Ω in series

✅ Equivalent voltage source = 40V with 8Ω series resistance

Numerical 9: Resistor Color Code

Find the resistance value of a resistor having color bands: Brown, Black, Red, Gold.

Solution
Brown = 1, Black = 0
So first two digits = 10
Red multiplier = ×100
Resistance = 10 × 100 = 1000Ω = 1kΩ
Gold tolerance = ±5%

✅ Resistance = 1kΩ ±5%

Important Definitions

Passive Component

A component that cannot generate energy but can only absorb, store, or dissipate it. Examples: resistor, capacitor, inductor.

Resistor

A passive component that opposes the flow of electric current and dissipates electrical energy in the form of heat.

Resistance

The property of a conductor by which it opposes the flow of electric current. Its SI unit is ohm (Ω).

Capacitor

A passive two-terminal component that stores electrical energy in an electric field between two plates separated by a dielectric.

Capacitance

The ability of a capacitor to store charge per unit voltage. It is measured in farads (F).

Inductor

A passive two-terminal component that stores electrical energy in the form of a magnetic field when current flows through it.

Inductance

The property of a coil due to which it opposes any change in current flowing through it. It is measured in henry (H).

Direct Current (DC)

Electric current that flows only in one direction and generally remains constant in magnitude.

Alternating Current (AC)

Electric current that changes its magnitude and reverses its direction periodically with time.

Periodic Waveform

A waveform that repeats itself after equal intervals of time.

Non-Periodic Waveform

A waveform that does not repeat itself after regular intervals of time.

Voltage Source

A source that maintains a constant voltage across its terminals.

Current Source

A source that maintains a constant current through the circuit.

Ideal Voltage Source

A voltage source having zero internal resistance and constant terminal voltage irrespective of load current.

Ideal Current Source

A current source having infinite internal resistance and constant output current irrespective of terminal voltage.

Practical Voltage Source

A voltage source having a small internal resistance in series with the ideal voltage source.

Practical Current Source

A current source having a large internal resistance in parallel with the ideal current source.

Independent Source

A source whose value does not depend on any other voltage or current in the circuit.

Dependent Source

A source whose value depends on some other voltage or current elsewhere in the circuit.

Source Transformation

The process of converting a practical voltage source into an equivalent practical current source, or vice versa, without changing the external circuit behavior.

Short Answer Questions (2 Marks Style)

Q 1. Define passive components.

Passive components are electrical components that cannot generate energy or amplify a signal. They only absorb, store, or dissipate energy. Examples: resistor, capacitor, and inductor.

Q 2. What is the function of a resistor?

A resistor opposes the flow of electric current in a circuit and dissipates electrical energy as heat. It is used for current limiting, voltage division, and biasing circuits.

Q 3. What is capacitance?

Capacitance is the ability of a capacitor to store electric charge per unit voltage. Mathematically, C = Q/V. Its unit is farad (F).

Q 4. State two differences between DC and AC.

(1) DC flows in one direction only, whereas AC changes direction periodically.
(2) DC has zero frequency, whereas AC has a definite frequency such as 50Hz.

Q 5. What is an ideal voltage source?

An ideal voltage source is a source that maintains constant terminal voltage regardless of load current. Its internal resistance is zero.

Q 6. What is source transformation?

Source transformation is the process of converting a practical voltage source into an equivalent practical current source, or vice versa, without changing the behavior seen by the external circuit.

Q 7. What is a dependent source?

A dependent source is a source whose voltage or current depends on some other voltage or current in the circuit. It is represented by a diamond symbol.

Q 8. What is the unit of inductance?

The SI unit of inductance is henry (H).

Q 9. Why are electrolytic capacitors polarized?

Electrolytic capacitors are polarized because their construction depends on a chemical dielectric layer formed for one direction of voltage only. Reverse connection may damage the capacitor.

Q 10. Write the formula for energy stored in an inductor.

The energy stored in an inductor is E = ½LI².

Long Answer Questions (5–7 Marks Style)

Q 1. Explain resistor, capacitor and inductor with construction, function and applications.

Resistor: A resistor is a passive component that opposes current flow. It is made of resistive material like carbon or metal film. Its unit is ohm. It dissipates electrical energy as heat. Applications: current limiting, voltage division, biasing, heating elements.

Capacitor: A capacitor consists of two conducting plates separated by a dielectric. It stores charge and electrical energy in an electric field. Its unit is farad. Applications: filtering, coupling, decoupling, timing circuits, motor starting.

Inductor: An inductor is a coil of wire wound on air/iron/ferrite core. It stores energy in magnetic field and opposes change in current. Its unit is henry. Applications: chokes, filters, transformers, tuning circuits.

Conclusion: These three are the most basic passive components used in all electrical and electronic circuits.

Q 2. Distinguish between DC and AC. Draw their waveforms.

DC: Direct current flows only in one direction and generally remains constant in magnitude. It has zero frequency. Example: battery output.

AC: Alternating current changes both magnitude and direction periodically with time. It has a definite frequency such as 50Hz in India. Example: mains supply.

Differences:

  • DC is unidirectional; AC is bidirectional.
  • DC has constant polarity; AC polarity changes periodically.
  • DC frequency = 0Hz; AC frequency = 50Hz or 60Hz.
  • DC is used in electronics; AC is used in power distribution.

Waveforms: DC waveform is a straight horizontal line, while AC waveform is sinusoidal. (In exam, draw a straight line for DC and sine curve for AC.)

Q 3. Explain ideal and practical voltage sources and current sources.

Ideal Voltage Source: Maintains constant voltage for any current drawn. Internal resistance is zero.

Practical Voltage Source: Consists of an ideal voltage source in series with a small internal resistance. Terminal voltage falls when load current increases. Formula: VT = Vs − Irs.

Ideal Current Source: Maintains constant current irrespective of terminal voltage. Internal resistance is infinite.

Practical Current Source: Consists of an ideal current source with a large resistance connected in parallel. Some current is lost through this internal resistance.

Comparison: Ideal sources are theoretical; practical sources exist in real life.

Q 4. Explain independent and dependent sources. Mention different types of dependent sources.

Independent Source: A source whose value does not depend on any other circuit quantity. It may be voltage or current source. It is shown by a circle symbol. Example: battery.

Dependent Source: A source whose value depends on some other voltage or current in the circuit. It is shown by a diamond symbol. It is used to model transistors, FETs and op-amps.

Types of dependent sources:

  • VCVS — Voltage Controlled Voltage Source
  • VCCS — Voltage Controlled Current Source
  • CCVS — Current Controlled Voltage Source
  • CCCS — Current Controlled Current Source

These are very important in circuit analysis and electronic device modeling.

Q 5. What is source transformation? Explain with necessary formula and example.

Definition: Source transformation is the conversion of a practical voltage source into an equivalent practical current source, or vice versa, without changing terminal behavior.

Voltage source to current source:
A voltage source Vs in series with Rs is equivalent to a current source Is in parallel with the same resistance Rs.

Is = Vs/Rs

Current source to voltage source:
A current source Is in parallel with Rs is equivalent to a voltage source Vs in series with the same resistance Rs.

Vs = IsRs

Example: 20V source with 5Ω series resistance → equivalent current source = 20/5 = 4A with 5Ω parallel resistance.

Use: It simplifies circuit analysis and is often used in solving numericals.

Q 6. Explain resistor color code with an example.

Resistor color code is used to indicate the resistance value and tolerance by means of colored bands on the resistor body.

For a 4-band resistor:

  • 1st band = first digit
  • 2nd band = second digit
  • 3rd band = multiplier
  • 4th band = tolerance

Color values: Black 0, Brown 1, Red 2, Orange 3, Yellow 4, Green 5, Blue 6, Violet 7, Grey 8, White 9.

Example: Red-Violet-Orange-Gold

  • Red = 2
  • Violet = 7
  • Orange = ×1000
  • Gold = ±5%

So resistance = 27 × 1000 = 27000Ω = 27kΩ ±5%.

  • Write units with every final answer.
  • Do not confuse series and parallel formulas.

  • For color code, first two bands are digits, third is multiplier, fourth is tolerance.
  • Capacitor stores energy in electric field; inductor stores energy in magnetic field.

  • Using resistor series formula for capacitors — wrong.
  • Forgetting to convert μF into F during numerical calculations.
  • Drawing wrong internal resistance placement in practical sources.
  • Using wrong current direction after source transformation.
