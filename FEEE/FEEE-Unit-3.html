Unit Overview

This unit introduces Alternating Current (AC) circuits, which are used in home supply, industries, motors, transformers and power systems. Unlike DC, AC changes both magnitude and direction with time.

3.1 Basic AC Quantities

Alternating Quantity

An alternating quantity is a voltage or current that changes its magnitude and direction periodically with time.

The most common AC waveform is the sinusoidal waveform.

General Sinusoidal Equation

v = Vm sin ωt

i = Im sin ωt

  • Vm = maximum or peak voltage
  • Im = maximum or peak current
  • ω = angular frequency in rad/s
  • t = time in second

Fig. 3.1: Sinusoidal AC waveform showing positive half cycle, negative half cycle and one complete cycle

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Cycle

Cycle

One complete set of positive and negative alternation of an AC waveform is called one cycle.

Time Period

Time Period (T)

The time taken by an AC waveform to complete one cycle is called the time period.

Unit = second (s)

Frequency

Frequency (f)

The number of cycles completed in one second is called the frequency.

Unit = Hertz (Hz)

Frequency-Time Period Relation

f = 1T

T = 1f

⭐ Important: In India, the AC mains supply frequency is 50 Hz. That means 50 complete cycles per second.

Angular Speed / Angular Frequency

Angular Frequency (ω)

Angular frequency is the angular displacement of a sinusoidal waveform per second.

Unit = rad/s

Formula: Angular Frequency

ω = 2πf = 2πT

Amplitude / Maximum Value

Amplitude

The maximum value of an alternating quantity measured from zero axis is called the amplitude or peak value.

Instantaneous Value

Instantaneous Value

The value of alternating voltage or current at any instant of time is called its instantaneous value.

RMS Value 🔥

RMS Value

The RMS (Root Mean Square) value of an alternating quantity is the equivalent DC value that produces the same heating effect in a resistor as the AC quantity.

Formula: RMS Value of Sine Wave

Vrms = Vm√2 = 0.707 Vm

Irms = Im√2 = 0.707 Im

Why RMS is Important

When we say household AC supply is 230 V, we mean 230 V RMS, not peak voltage. The peak value is about 325 V.

Average Value

Average Value

The average value of an AC quantity is the arithmetic mean of all instantaneous values over a half cycle.

For a complete sine wave, average value is zero because positive and negative halves cancel each other.

Formula: Average Value of Sine Wave

Vavg = 2Vmπ = 0.637 Vm

Iavg = 2Imπ = 0.637 Im

Form Factor

Form Factor

Form factor is the ratio of RMS value to average value of an AC waveform.

Formula: Form Factor

Form Factor = RMS ValueAverage Value

For sine wave:

Form Factor = 0.7070.637 = 1.11

Peak Factor / Crest Factor

Peak Factor

Peak factor is the ratio of maximum value to RMS value of an AC waveform.

Formula: Peak Factor

Peak Factor = Maximum ValueRMS Value

For sine wave:

Peak Factor = 10.707 = 1.414

Fig. 3.2: AC waveform showing peak value, RMS value and average value

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Phase and Phase Difference 🔥

Phase

Phase indicates the angular position of a sinusoidal quantity with respect to a reference point.

Phase Difference

The angular difference between two sinusoidal quantities of same frequency is called phase difference.

If two waveforms reach their maximum and zero values at the same instant, they are in phase. If one reaches earlier or later, they are out of phase.

In Phase

Two quantities are in phase when phase difference is 0°.

Leading

If one waveform reaches its value earlier, it is said to lead.

Lagging

If one waveform reaches its value later, it is said to lag.

Fig. 3.3: Phase difference between two sine waves showing leading and lagging

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Impedance

Impedance (Z)

Impedance is the total opposition offered by an AC circuit to the flow of current. It includes both resistance and reactance.

Unit = Ohm (Ω)

Formula: Impedance

Z = V / I

For series RL or RC type circuits:

Z = √(R² + X²)

🔥 Very Important: In AC, resistance alone is not enough. Inductor and capacitor also oppose current, and total opposition is called impedance.

Solved Examples on AC Basics

Example 1

An AC waveform has frequency 50 Hz. Find its time period and angular frequency.

Solution
Given: f = 50 Hz
Time period, T = 1/f = 1/50 = 0.02 s
Angular frequency, ω = 2πf = 2 × 3.14 × 50 = 314 rad/s

✅ T = 0.02 s, ω = 314 rad/s

Example 2

The maximum value of AC voltage is 100 V. Find its RMS and average value.

Solution
Given: Vm = 100 V
Vrms = 0.707 × 100 = 70.7 V
Vavg = 0.637 × 100 = 63.7 V

✅ RMS value = 70.7 V, Average value = 63.7 V

Example 3

Find form factor and peak factor of a sinusoidal waveform.

Solution
For sine wave, RMS = 0.707 of peak and average = 0.637 of peak
Form factor = 0.707 / 0.637 = 1.11
Peak factor = 1 / 0.707 = 1.414

✅ Form factor = 1.11, Peak factor = 1.414

3.2 Representation of Sinusoidal Quantities

Why Representation is Needed

In AC circuit analysis, voltage and current are not simple fixed numbers. They have both magnitude and phase angle. To make calculations easy, we represent sinusoidal quantities in different mathematical forms.

The three forms in your syllabus are:

  1. Exponential form
  2. Complex form
  3. Polar form

Phasor Concept

Phasor

A phasor is a rotating vector used to represent a sinusoidal quantity by its magnitude and phase angle.

For example, a sinusoidal voltage

v = Vm sin(ωt + φ)

can be represented by a phasor of magnitude V and angle φ.

Fig. 3.4: Phasor representation of a sinusoidal quantity

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

1. Polar Form

Polar Form

In polar form, a sinusoidal quantity is written in terms of its magnitude and angle.

Polar Form

V = |V| ∠ θ

Example:

20 ∠ 30°

This means magnitude is 20 and phase angle is 30°.

2. Complex / Rectangular Form

Complex Form

In complex form, a phasor is written as the sum of a real part and an imaginary part.

Complex Form

V = a + jb

  • a = real part
  • jb = imaginary part
  • j = √(-1)

Example:

10 + j5

Important: In electrical engineering we use j instead of i because i is already used for current.

3. Exponential Form

Exponential Form

In exponential form, a phasor is represented using Euler’s formula.

Exponential Form

V = |V| ejθ

Example:

20ej30°

Relation Between Forms

Euler’s Formula

ejθ = cos θ + j sin θ

Therefore,

V ∠ θ = V(cos θ + j sin θ)

So rectangular form becomes:

a + jb = V cos θ + j V sin θ

Conversion from Polar to Rectangular

Polar → Rectangular

a = V cos θ

b = V sin θ

V∠θ = a + jb

Conversion from Rectangular to Polar

Rectangular → Polar

V = √(a² + b²)

θ = tan-1(b/a)

Fig. 3.5: Conversion between polar and rectangular form using right triangle

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Examples of Representation

Example 4

Convert 10 ∠ 30° into rectangular form.

Solution
Given: V = 10 ∠ 30°
Real part = 10 cos 30° = 10 × 0.866 = 8.66
Imaginary part = 10 sin 30° = 10 × 0.5 = 5
Rectangular form = 8.66 + j5

✅ 10 ∠ 30° = 8.66 + j5

Example 5

Convert 6 + j8 into polar form.

Solution
Magnitude = √(6² + 8²) = √(36 + 64) = √100 = 10
Angle = tan-1(8/6) = tan-1(1.333)
Angle ≈ 53.13°

✅ 6 + j8 = 10 ∠ 53.13°

Exam Tip

In diploma level problems, usually the most useful forms are:

  • Polar form for multiplication and division
  • Rectangular form for addition and subtraction

3.3 Pure Resistance, Inductance and Capacitance with Sinusoidal Excitation

Basic Idea

When AC supply is applied to a pure resistor, pure inductor or pure capacitor, the current and voltage do not always behave in the same way.

  • In a pure resistor, voltage and current are in phase.
  • In a pure inductor, current lags voltage by 90°.
  • In a pure capacitor, current leads voltage by 90°.

This topic is extremely important because all practical AC circuits are built from these three basic elements.

A. Pure Resistive Circuit

Pure Resistive Circuit

A circuit containing only resistance and no inductance or capacitance is called a pure resistive circuit.

If AC voltage is applied:

v = Vm sin ωt

Then by Ohm’s law,

i = vR = VmR sin ωt = Im sin ωt

Key Result: In a pure resistor, voltage and current are in phase. Phase difference = 0°.
Resistive Circuit Relations

I = V / R

Z = R

φ = 0°

Fig. 3.6: Pure resistive AC circuit with waveform and phasor diagram showing V and I in phase

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

B. Pure Inductive Circuit

Pure Inductive Circuit

A circuit containing only inductance and negligible resistance is called a pure inductive circuit.

For an inductor, voltage-current relation is:

v = L didt

If applied voltage is:

v = Vm sin ωt

Then on solving, the current becomes:

i = Im sin (ωt - 90°)

Key Result: In a pure inductor, current lags voltage by 90°.
Or, voltage leads current by 90°.

Inductive Reactance

Inductive Reactance

The opposition offered by an inductor to AC is called inductive reactance.

Formula: Inductive Reactance

XL = ωL = 2πfL

I = V / XL

Z = XL

Thus, higher the frequency, greater is the inductive reactance.

Fig. 3.7: Pure inductive AC circuit with waveform and phasor diagram showing current lagging by 90°

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

C. Pure Capacitive Circuit

Pure Capacitive Circuit

A circuit containing only capacitance and negligible resistance is called a pure capacitive circuit.

For a capacitor, current-voltage relation is:

i = C dvdt

If voltage is:

v = Vm sin ωt

Then current becomes:

i = Im sin (ωt + 90°)

Key Result: In a pure capacitor, current leads voltage by 90°.
Or, voltage lags current by 90°.

Capacitive Reactance

Capacitive Reactance

The opposition offered by a capacitor to AC is called capacitive reactance.

Formula: Capacitive Reactance

XC = 1ωC = 12πfC

I = V / XC

Z = XC

Thus, higher the frequency, smaller is the capacitive reactance.

Fig. 3.8: Pure capacitive AC circuit with waveform and phasor diagram showing current leading by 90°

Solved Examples

Example 6

A pure inductive circuit has L = 0.2 H and frequency 50 Hz. Find inductive reactance.

Solution
Given: L = 0.2 H, f = 50 Hz
XL = 2πfL
XL = 2 × 3.14 × 50 × 0.2 = 62.8 Ω

✅ Inductive reactance = 62.8 Ω

Example 7

A pure capacitive circuit has C = 50 μF and frequency 50 Hz. Find capacitive reactance.

Solution
Given: C = 50 μF = 50 × 10-6 F, f = 50 Hz
XC = 1 / (2πfC)
XC = 1 / (2 × 3.14 × 50 × 50 × 10-6)
XC ≈ 63.7 Ω

✅ Capacitive reactance = 63.7 Ω

3.4 R-L, R-C, R-L-C Series and Parallel Circuits

Practical AC Circuits

In real life, AC circuits rarely contain only a pure resistor, only a pure inductor, or only a pure capacitor. Most circuits are combinations like:

  • R-L series
  • R-C series
  • R-L-C series
  • Parallel AC circuits

A. Series R-L Circuit

In a series RL circuit, resistance and inductance are connected in series across AC supply.

RL Series Formulas

XL = 2πfL

Z = √(R² + XL²)

I = V / Z

tan φ = XL / R

cos φ = R / Z

Key Result: In RL series circuit, current lags voltage by angle φ.

Fig. 3.9: Series RL circuit and phasor diagram showing current lagging voltage

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Voltage across components:

  • VR = IR (in phase with current)
  • VL = IXL (leads current by 90°)

Supply voltage is vector sum:

V = √(VR² + VL²)

B. Series R-C Circuit

In a series RC circuit, resistance and capacitance are connected in series.

RC Series Formulas

XC = 12πfC

Z = √(R² + XC²)

I = V / Z

tan φ = XC / R

cos φ = R / Z

Key Result: In RC series circuit, current leads voltage by angle φ.

Fig. 3.10: Series RC circuit and phasor diagram showing current leading voltage

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Voltage across components:

  • VR = IR (in phase with current)
  • VC = IXC (lags current by 90°)

Supply voltage:

V = √(VR² + VC²)

C. Series R-L-C Circuit

In a series RLC circuit, resistance, inductance and capacitance are connected in series.

RLC Series Formulas

XL = 2πfL

XC = 12πfC

Net Reactance, X = XL - XC

Z = √(R² + (XL - XC)²)

I = V / Z

tan φ = (XL - XC) / R

Cases in RLC Circuit
  • If XL > XC → circuit is inductive, current lags
  • If XC > XL → circuit is capacitive, current leads
  • If XL = XC → circuit behaves as pure resistive, current in phase

Fig. 3.11: Series RLC circuit with phasor diagram and impedance triangle

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

D. Parallel AC Circuits (Basic Idea)

Parallel AC Circuit

In a parallel AC circuit, all branches are connected across the same supply voltage, so voltage is same across each branch, while branch currents differ.

At diploma level, you mainly need the concept and simple cases.

Parallel RL Circuit

  • Voltage same across R and L
  • Current through resistance is in phase with voltage
  • Current through inductance lags voltage by 90°
  • Total current is vector sum of branch currents

Parallel RC Circuit

  • Voltage same across R and C
  • Current through R is in phase with voltage
  • Current through capacitor leads voltage by 90°
  • Total current is phasor sum of branch currents

Parallel RLC Circuit

  • All branches get same voltage
  • Branch currents depend on R, L and C values
  • Total current is found by phasor addition

Fig. 3.12: Parallel AC circuits and branch current phasor diagrams

Common Mistake

Students often forget:

  • In series circuits, current is same
  • In parallel circuits, voltage is same

Solved Examples on Series AC Circuits

Example 8: RL Series Circuit

An RL series circuit has R = 8 Ω, L = 0.05 H and f = 50 Hz. Find XL, Z and current when connected to 100 V supply.

Solution
Given: R = 8 Ω, L = 0.05 H, f = 50 Hz, V = 100 V
XL = 2πfL = 2 × 3.14 × 50 × 0.05 = 15.7 Ω
Z = √(R² + XL²) = √(8² + 15.7²)
Z = √(64 + 246.49) = √310.49 ≈ 17.62 Ω
I = V/Z = 100/17.62 ≈ 5.68 A

✅ XL = 15.7 Ω, Z = 17.62 Ω, I = 5.68 A

Example 9: RC Series Circuit

An RC series circuit has R = 10 Ω, C = 100 μF and f = 50 Hz. Find XC and impedance.

Solution
Given: R = 10 Ω, C = 100 μF = 100 × 10-6 F, f = 50 Hz
XC = 1/(2πfC)
XC = 1 / (2 × 3.14 × 50 × 100 × 10-6)
XC ≈ 31.85 Ω
Z = √(R² + XC²) = √(10² + 31.85²)
Z ≈ √(100 + 1014.42) = √1114.42 ≈ 33.38 Ω

✅ XC = 31.85 Ω, Z = 33.38 Ω

Example 10: RLC Series Circuit

A series RLC circuit has R = 6 Ω, XL = 10 Ω and XC = 4 Ω. Find impedance and phase angle.

Solution
Net reactance X = XL − XC = 10 − 4 = 6 Ω
Z = √(R² + X²) = √(6² + 6²) = √72 = 8.49 Ω
tan φ = X/R = 6/6 = 1
φ = 45°
Since XL > XC, circuit is inductive, so current lags.

✅ Z = 8.49 Ω, φ = 45° lagging

3.5 Impedance, Power Factor, Active, Reactive and Apparent Power

Power in AC Circuit

In DC circuits, power is simply P = VI. But in AC circuits, voltage and current may not be in phase. So all supplied power is not actually used as useful power.

That is why we study:

  • Active power
  • Reactive power
  • Apparent power
  • Power factor

Impedance Triangle

For a series AC circuit, impedance can be shown by a right-angled triangle:

  • Base = Resistance R
  • Perpendicular = Reactance X
  • Hypotenuse = Impedance Z
Impedance Triangle Relations

Z = √(R² + X²)

cos φ = R/Z

sin φ = X/Z

tan φ = X/R

Fig. 3.13: Impedance triangle showing R, X and Z with phase angle φ

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Power Factor

Power Factor

Power factor is the cosine of the phase angle between voltage and current in an AC circuit.

Formula: Power Factor

Power Factor = cos φ = R/Z

Unity Power Factor

When φ = 0°, cos φ = 1. This happens in pure resistive circuit.

Lagging Power Factor

When current lags voltage, power factor is called lagging. Example: RL circuit.

Leading Power Factor

When current leads voltage, power factor is called leading. Example: RC circuit.

🔥 Exam Important: Power factor tells how effectively electrical power is being converted into useful work. Higher power factor is better.

Active / True / Real Power

Active Power

Active power is the actual power consumed in the circuit and converted into useful work such as heat, light or mechanical energy.

Formula: Active Power

P = VI cos φ

Unit = Watt (W)

Reactive Power

Reactive Power

Reactive power is the power that oscillates between source and reactive components (L and C). It does not perform useful work.

Formula: Reactive Power

Q = VI sin φ

Unit = VAR (Volt-Ampere Reactive)

Apparent Power

Apparent Power

Apparent power is the product of RMS voltage and RMS current, without considering phase angle.

Formula: Apparent Power

S = VI

Unit = VA (Volt-Ampere)

Power Triangle

The relation between active, reactive and apparent power is shown by power triangle.

Power Triangle Relations

S = √(P² + Q²)

cos φ = P/S

tan φ = Q/P

Fig. 3.14: Power triangle showing active power P, reactive power Q and apparent power S

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Solved Examples on AC Power

Example 11

An AC circuit takes 10 A current from 200 V supply at power factor 0.8 lagging. Find active, reactive and apparent power.

Solution
Given: V = 200 V, I = 10 A, cosφ = 0.8
Apparent power, S = VI = 200 × 10 = 2000 VA
Active power, P = VI cosφ = 200 × 10 × 0.8 = 1600 W
sinφ = √(1 - cos²φ) = √(1 - 0.64) = 0.6
Reactive power, Q = VI sinφ = 200 × 10 × 0.6 = 1200 VAR

✅ S = 2000 VA, P = 1600 W, Q = 1200 VAR

Example 12

An AC circuit has R = 12 Ω and X = 5 Ω. Find impedance and power factor.

Solution
Z = √(R² + X²) = √(12² + 5²) = √(144 + 25) = √169 = 13 Ω
Power factor = cosφ = R/Z = 12/13 = 0.923

✅ Z = 13 Ω, Power factor = 0.923

3.6 Voltage and Current Relationship in Star and Delta Connections

Introduction

In three-phase circuits, loads can be connected in two common ways:

  • Star connection (Y)
  • Delta connection (Δ)

Even though detailed three-phase analysis is advanced, the basic voltage-current relations are very important and often asked in diploma exams.

A. Star Connection (Y)

Star Connection

In star connection, one end of each of the three phases is connected together to form a common neutral point.

Fig. 3.15: Three-phase star connection showing line voltage and phase voltage

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Star Connection Relations

VL = √3 Vph

IL = Iph

  • VL = line voltage
  • Vph = phase voltage
  • IL = line current
  • Iph = phase current

B. Delta Connection (Δ)

Delta Connection

In delta connection, the three phases are connected end-to-end so that they form a closed loop like a triangle.

Fig. 3.16: Three-phase delta connection showing line current and phase current

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Delta Connection Relations

VL = Vph

IL = √3 Iph

Example 13

In a star connected system, phase voltage is 230 V. Find line voltage.

Solution
Given: Vph = 230 V
In star, VL = √3Vph
VL = 1.732 × 230 = 398.36 V

✅ Line voltage = 398.36 V

Example 14

In a delta connected system, phase current is 5 A. Find line current.

Solution
Given: Iph = 5 A
In delta, IL = √3 Iph
IL = 1.732 × 5 = 8.66 A

✅ Line current = 8.66 A

Important Definitions

Alternating Current

Alternating current is an electric current that changes both magnitude and direction periodically with time.

Cycle

One complete positive and negative alternation of an AC waveform is called one cycle.

Time Period

Time period is the time taken by an AC waveform to complete one cycle.

Frequency

Frequency is the number of cycles completed per second by an AC waveform.

Angular Frequency

Angular frequency is the angular speed of a rotating phasor and is given by ω = 2πf.

Amplitude

Amplitude is the maximum value of an alternating quantity measured from the zero axis.

RMS Value

RMS value is the equivalent DC value that produces the same heating effect in a resistor as the AC quantity.

Average Value

Average value is the arithmetic mean of the instantaneous values of an alternating quantity taken over one half cycle.

Form Factor

Form factor is the ratio of RMS value to average value of an AC waveform.

Peak Factor

Peak factor is the ratio of maximum value to RMS value of an AC waveform.

Phase

Phase is the angular position of a sinusoidal quantity with respect to a reference.

Phase Difference

Phase difference is the angular difference between two sinusoidal quantities of the same frequency.

Phasor

A phasor is a rotating vector used to represent a sinusoidal quantity by magnitude and phase angle.

Impedance

Impedance is the total opposition offered by an AC circuit to the flow of current.

Inductive Reactance

Inductive reactance is the opposition offered by an inductor to AC and is given by XL = 2πfL.

Capacitive Reactance

Capacitive reactance is the opposition offered by a capacitor to AC and is given by XC = 1/(2πfC).

Power Factor

Power factor is the cosine of the phase angle between voltage and current in an AC circuit.

Active Power

Active power is the actual useful power consumed in an AC circuit and converted into work.

Reactive Power

Reactive power is the power exchanged between source and reactive elements and does not do useful work.

Apparent Power

Apparent power is the product of RMS voltage and RMS current in an AC circuit.

Star Connection

In star connection, one end of each of the three phases is connected to a common neutral point.

Delta Connection

In delta connection, the three phases are connected end to end to form a closed triangular loop.

Long Answer Questions (5–7 Marks Style)

Q 1. Explain the important AC terms: cycle, frequency, time period, RMS value, average value, form factor and peak factor.

Cycle: One complete positive and negative alternation of an AC waveform.

Frequency: Number of cycles per second, measured in hertz. f = 1/T.

Time period: Time taken to complete one cycle. T = 1/f.

RMS value: Equivalent DC value producing same heating effect. For sine wave, RMS = 0.707 of maximum value.

Average value: Mean value over one half cycle. For sine wave, average = 0.637 of maximum value.

Form factor: Ratio of RMS value to average value. For sine wave, it is 1.11.

Peak factor: Ratio of maximum value to RMS value. For sine wave, it is 1.414.

Q 2. Explain pure resistive, pure inductive and pure capacitive AC circuits with phase relation.

Pure Resistive Circuit: Contains only resistance. Voltage and current are in phase. Phase angle = 0°.

Pure Inductive Circuit: Contains only inductance. Current lags voltage by 90°. Inductive reactance XL = 2πfL.

Pure Capacitive Circuit: Contains only capacitance. Current leads voltage by 90°. Capacitive reactance XC = 1/(2πfC).

These are the three basic AC circuits from which all practical circuits are formed.

Q 3. Explain series RL, RC and RLC circuits with impedance relations.

Series RL Circuit: Contains resistance and inductance. Impedance is Z = √(R² + XL²). Current lags voltage.

Series RC Circuit: Contains resistance and capacitance. Impedance is Z = √(R² + XC²). Current leads voltage.

Series RLC Circuit: Contains resistance, inductance and capacitance. Net reactance = XL − XC. Impedance is Z = √(R² + (XL-XC)²).

If XL > XC, circuit is inductive. If XC > XL, circuit is capacitive. If XL = XC, circuit behaves like pure resistance.

Q 4. What is power factor? Explain active, reactive and apparent power with power triangle.

Power factor is the cosine of the phase angle between voltage and current in an AC circuit. Power factor = cosφ.

Active power is actual useful power consumed in the circuit, P = VIcosφ, unit watt.

Reactive power is the power exchanged with reactive elements, Q = VIsinφ, unit VAR.

Apparent power is total supplied power, S = VI, unit VA.

These form a power triangle where:

S = √(P² + Q²)

cosφ = P/S

Higher power factor means better utilization of electrical power.

Q 5. Explain star and delta connections with voltage-current relations.

Star connection: One end of each phase is connected to a common neutral point. Relations are:

VL = √3Vph, IL = Iph

Delta connection: The three phases are connected end-to-end to form a closed triangle. Relations are:

VL = Vph, IL = √3Iph

Star connection is commonly used in distribution systems, while delta connection is common in motor load circuits.

Q 6. Explain phasor representation and the polar, rectangular and exponential forms of sinusoidal quantities.

A phasor is a rotating vector used to represent a sinusoidal quantity by its magnitude and phase angle.

Polar form: V = |V|∠θ. It shows magnitude and angle directly.

Rectangular/complex form: V = a + jb, where a is real part and jb is imaginary part.

Exponential form: V = |V|ejθ.

Using Euler’s formula ejθ = cosθ + jsinθ, conversion between forms is possible. Rectangular form is useful for addition/subtraction and polar form is useful for multiplication/division.
