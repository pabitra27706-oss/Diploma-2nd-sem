6.1.2 Parts of a Transformer

Transformer

A static device (no rotating parts) which transfers electrical energy from one AC circuit to another with the desired change in voltage or current level and without change in frequency.

Fig. 6.1: Front view of transformer — HV/LV bushings, conservator, Buchholz relay, breather, cooling vent, tank body, pressure vent, winding

🔍 Click to view

a. Core & Windings

Core: high-grade silicon steel laminations (0.35–0.5mm), varnished/enameled. Provides low reluctance path for flux. Windings: primary & secondary, former wound using insulated copper conductor (round wire or strip), placed on limbs.

b. Transformer Tank

For ratings >50 kVA. Fabricated sheet metal tank, assembly immersed in oil for insulation and cooling. Cooling tubes increase surface area.

c. Terminal Bushings

Porcelain bushings insulate winding terminals from tank body.

d. Conservator

Airtight cylindrical drum connected by pipe to tank. Takes up oil expansion/contraction with temperature. Main tank stays full — oil surface not directly exposed to air.

e. Breather

Contains drying agent (silica gel/calcium chloride). Extracts moisture and dust from air during transformer "breathing".

f. Buchholz Relay

Protective device in pipeline between tank and conservator. Gases from oil decomposition during faults operate it — gives alarm or trips breaker.

g. Explosion Vent

Bent pipe on tank top with glass/aluminium diaphragm. Bursts to release excessive pressure during faults.

6.1.3 Types & Working Principle

Core Type

  • Rectangular laminated structure, single magnetic circuit
  • Cylindrical, concentric coils (LV near core)
  • Windings distributed on two limbs → better cooling
  • Coils withdrawn by dismantling top yoke

Shell Type

  • Double magnetic circuit
  • Sandwich-type windings on central limb only
  • H.V./L.V. pancake coils interleaved
  • Core surrounds windings → mechanical protection
  • Poorer natural cooling; repair more difficult

6.1.3.1 Principle of Working

Mutual Induction

Based on mutual induction between two circuits linked by a common magnetic field. Primary winding receives energy from AC supply → alternating current → alternating flux → links secondary winding through iron core → induces emf by Faraday's law. Frequency of induced emf = supply frequency.

6.1.3.2–6.1.3.3 EMF Equation & Turns Ratio

EMF Equation Derivation
\[\varphi = \varphi_m \cos\omega t\] ...(6.1)
\[e_1 = -N_1\frac{d\varphi}{dt} = N_1\omega\varphi_m\sin\omega t\] ...(6.2–6.3)

Maximum induced EMF: \(E_m = 2\pi f N_1 \varphi_m\)

\[E_1 = \frac{2\pi f N_1\varphi_m}{\sqrt{2}} = 4.44\,f\,N_1\,\varphi_m\] ...(6.4)
\[E_2 = 4.44\,f\,N_2\,\varphi_m\] ...(6.5)
Voltage Transformation Ratio (K)
\[\frac{E_1}{E_2} = \frac{V_1}{V_2} = \frac{N_1}{N_2} = \frac{I_2}{I_1} = \frac{1}{K}\] ...(6.6)

K < 1: Step-down transformer. K > 1: Step-up transformer.

Ideal Transformer Assumptions
  • Winding resistances are negligible
  • All flux confined to core, links both windings fully
  • Core permeability is high → negligible magnetizing current
  • Hysteresis and eddy current losses are negligible
  • V₁I₁ = V₂I₂ (input VA = output VA)

6.1.3.4 No Load Condition

At no load: I₂ = 0, primary carries small no-load current I₀ with two components:

Magnetizing (Iμ)

Produces magnetizing flux, in phase with flux. Iμ = I₀ sin Ø₀

Active/Power (Iω)

Supplies hysteresis & eddy current losses, in phase with V₁. Iω = I₀ cos Ø₀

No Load Formulas
\[P_0 = V_1 I_0 \cos\phi_0\] ...(6.7)
\[I_0 = \sqrt{I_\mu^2 + I_\omega^2}\] ...(6.8)

6.1.4 Autotransformer

Autotransformer

A transformer where part of the winding is common to both primary and secondary. Electrically connected AND magnetically coupled (not electrically isolated).

Fig. 6.6: Autotransformer — PR (primary N₁), QR (secondary N₂), common winding

🔍 Click to view
Autotransformer Relations
\[\frac{V_1}{V_2} = \frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{N_1}{N_2} = \frac{1}{K}\] ...(6.9)

Transformed (inductive) power = V₂I₂(1−K) ...(6.10)

Conducted power = KV₂I₂ ...(6.11)

Advantages & Applications

Advantages: Less copper, smaller size for same rating.

Applications: Compensate voltage drops on feeders, provide variable voltage control, adjust output voltage with varying load.

6.2.2 DC Motor

DC Motor

A machine that converts electrical energy into mechanical energy using DC supply.

6.2.2.1 Construction

Stator: cylindrical yoke, field poles with windings, pole shoes. Rotor (armature): laminated silicon steel core with slots for armature coils, connected to commutator. Commutator: copper segments separated by mica. Carbon brushes with spring-loaded holders conduct current to armature.

Fig. 6.7: Sectional view of DC machine — pole, field winding, pole shoe, yoke, armature winding, rotor, commutator, brush

🔍 Click to view

6.2.2.2 Working Principle

Fleming's Left Hand Rule

Current-carrying conductor in magnetic field → mechanical force. Direction by Fleming's Left Hand Rule.

\[F = BIL\] ...(6.12)

B = field strength (Wb/m²), I = current (A), L = conductor length (m).

Back EMF (Eb)

When armature rotates, conductors cut magnetic lines → induced emf opposing applied voltage.

\[E_b \propto \phi N\] ...(6.13)

6.2.2.4 Types of DC Motors

DC Series Motor

Field winding in series with armature. Same current flows through both. Few turns of thick wire. Flux varies with current till saturation.

DC Shunt Motor

Field winding in parallel with armature. Many turns of thin wire. Field current constant (DC supply constant) → flux practically constant.

6.2.2.5–6 DC Motor Characteristics & Applications

Torque Characteristics (T vs Ia)

\[T \propto \phi I_a\] ...(6.14)

Series: T ∝ Ia²

φ ∝ Ia (until saturation) → T ∝ Ia² (parabolic). After saturation → linear. Extremely high starting torque.

Shunt: T ∝ Ia

φ = constant → T directly proportional to Ia (linear).

Speed Characteristics (N vs Ia)

\[N \propto \frac{E_b}{\phi}\] ...(6.16)

Series: N ∝ 1/Ia

Speed decreases with load. Never run without load — dangerously high speed!

Shunt: N ≈ constant

Slight decrease with load. Considered constant speed motor.

Applications

DC Series Motor Applications

High starting torque needed: Cranes, Lifts, Electric traction, Vacuum cleaner, Hair drier, Sewing machine, Power tools, Air compressor.

DC Shunt Motor Applications

Constant speed needed: Lathe, Fans, Spinning machines, Centrifugal pumps, Boring machines, Blowers, Conveyors, Weighing machines.

6.2.3 Three-Phase AC Motors

Rotating Magnetic Field

Three-phase balanced supply to stator coils displaced 120° → rotating magnetic field. Speed:

\[N_s = \frac{120f}{P}\] ...(6.18)

Torque: \(T = F_s F_r \sin\lambda\) ...(6.19)

3-Phase Induction Motor

Slip

Difference between synchronous speed Ns and rotor speed Nr: s = (Ns − Nr)/Ns. Rotor frequency fr = s × f.

3-Phase Synchronous Motor

Stator carries AC, rotor is DC excited. Runs at exactly synchronous speed. Used for power factor improvement and high-power constant-speed applications.

6.2.4 Single-Phase AC Motors

Single-phase induction motors are not self-starting (pulsating field). Solution: 2-winding motors with main + auxiliary winding at 90° electrical, with impedance for phase splitting.

6.2.4.1 Capacitor Split Phase Motors

Most widely used for household. Types: Capacitor start, Permanent split capacitor, Capacitor start-capacitor run.

Working

Capacitor in series with auxiliary winding → Ia leads Im → creates 2-phase motor effect → starting torque produced → rotor starts. Typical capacitor: 40–100 μF.

Ceiling fans, compressor motors, household pumps, refrigerators, air conditioners, kitchen equipment.

Solved Problems

Ex 6.1.1

Transformer: N₁=400, N₂=1000, A=60cm², V₁=500V, f=50Hz. Find Bm.

Solution

500 = 4.44 × 50 × 400 × Bm × 60×10⁻⁴ → Bm = 0.938 Wb/m²

Ex 6.1.2

200/100V, 50Hz transformer excited at 40Hz from 100V side, same Iμ. Find voltage.

Solution

Same φm: E₂(40Hz)/E₂(50Hz) = 40/50 → E₂ = 100 × 40/50 = 80V

Ex 6.2.1

DC shunt motor, unsaturated, 1000 rpm at rated V. Voltage halved. Find speed.

Solution

V = k×If×1000 ...(1). V/2 = k×If/2×N ...(2). Equating: N = 1000 rpm

Ex 6.2.2

3-phase IM, 4 poles, 50Hz, slip 4%. Find Ns, Nr, fr.

Solution

Ns = 120×50/4 = 1500 rpm. Nr = 1500(1−0.04) = 1440 rpm. fr = 0.04×50 = 2 Hz

B. Subjective Questions

Q1. Explain working of a two winding transformer.

AC supply to primary → alternating current → alternating flux in core → links secondary winding → induces EMF by Faraday's law (mutual induction) → connects to load → current flows. E₁=4.44fN₁φₘ, E₂=4.44fN₂φₘ. V₁/V₂=N₁/N₂.

Q4. 3000/220V, 50Hz, A=400cm², B=1Wb/m². Find N₁, N₂.

φₘ=B×A=1×400×10⁻⁴=0.04Wb. N₁=3000/(4.44×50×0.04)=338 turns. N₂=220/(4.44×50×0.04)=25 turns.

Q7. What happens when load is removed from DC series motor?

N∝1/Ia. Without load, Ia becomes very small → speed increases to dangerously high values. Motor may get damaged due to excessive centrifugal force. Never run DC series motor without load.

Q9. Describe construction of 3-phase induction motor.

Stator: Cylindrical laminated core with slots for 3-phase winding displaced 120°. Rotor: Either squirrel cage (Cu/Al bars shorted by end rings) or wound rotor (winding connected to slip rings for external resistance). Air gap separates stator and rotor.

Q10. Types of single-phase induction motors and applications.

Types: Resistance split-phase, Capacitor start, Permanent split capacitor, Capacitor start-capacitor run. Applications: Ceiling fans, refrigerator compressors, household pumps, air conditioners, washing machines, kitchen equipment.

Unit Summary

Key Takeaways
  • Transformers: Core type (single magnetic circuit) and Shell type (double magnetic circuit).
  • Two-winding transformer: step-down (K<1) or step-up (K>1). E = 4.44fNφm.
  • Transformer is a static device — changes V or I level, frequency unchanged.
  • Autotransformer: power transfer via conductive + inductive coupling.
  • DC Series motor: few turns thick wire, series connection, T∝Ia², high starting torque.
  • DC Shunt motor: many turns thin wire, parallel, constant speed.
  • Series motor: traction, cranes, hoists. Shunt motor: lathe, pumps, fans.
  • AC motors: 3-phase (induction, synchronous) and 1-phase.
  • Synchronous speed: Ns = 120f/P.
  • Induction motor T-N characteristic ≈ DC shunt motor.
  • Single-phase motors need phase splitting (capacitor) for self-starting.
