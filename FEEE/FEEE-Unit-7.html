1.1 Energy Band Theory

Energy Bands

In a single isolated atom, electrons occupy discrete energy levels. When atoms come together to form a solid, these discrete levels broaden into energy bands due to interaction between atoms. The two most important bands are:

  • Valence Band (VB): Highest energy band that is fully or partially occupied by electrons at absolute zero. Electrons here are bound to atoms.
  • Conduction Band (CB): The next higher energy band above the valence band. Electrons here are free to move and conduct electricity.
  • Forbidden Energy Gap (Eg): The energy difference between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band. No electron can exist in this gap.

Key Concepts
  • Conductor: Valence and conduction bands overlap — electrons freely available for conduction even at 0 K.
  • Semiconductor: At 0 K, VB is full and CB is empty — acts like insulator. At room temperature, thermal energy excites some electrons across the small gap into CB → partial conductivity.
  • Insulator: Gap is so large (~6 eV for diamond) that practically no electrons can gain enough energy to cross it.
Why Semiconductors Are Special

Semiconductors can have their conductivity precisely controlled by: (1) Temperature (2) Light (3) Impurity addition (doping) (4) Electric field. This controllability makes them the foundation of all electronic devices.

1.2 Intrinsic Semiconductor

Intrinsic (Pure) Semiconductor

A semiconductor in its purest form without any impurity atoms. Silicon (Si) and Germanium (Ge) are the most common. Both have 4 valence electrons forming covalent bonds with neighbouring atoms in a crystal lattice.

Properties of Intrinsic Semiconductor
  • At absolute zero (0 K): All covalent bonds are intact, no free electrons → behaves as insulator.
  • At room temperature: Thermal energy breaks some covalent bonds → electrons freed into CB, leaving behind holes in VB.
  • Number of electrons (n) = Number of holes (p) = ni (intrinsic carrier concentration).
  • ni for Silicon at 300K ≈ 1.5 × 10¹⁰ /cm³; for Germanium ≈ 2.5 × 10¹³ /cm³.
  • Both electrons and holes contribute to current flow.
Mass Action Law
\[ n \cdot p = n_i^2 \] ...(7.1)

This law holds for both intrinsic and extrinsic semiconductors at thermal equilibrium.

1.2 Extrinsic Semiconductor & Doping

Doping

The process of adding controlled amounts of impurity atoms to an intrinsic semiconductor to increase its conductivity. The impurity concentration is typically 1 impurity atom per 10⁶ to 10⁸ semiconductor atoms.

Extrinsic Semiconductor

A doped semiconductor with significantly altered electrical properties. Two types based on the type of impurity added:

N-Type Semiconductor

Dopant: Pentavalent (Group V) — Phosphorus (P), Arsenic (As), Antimony (Sb).

5 valence electrons → 4 form covalent bonds + 1 free electron (donor).

Majority carriers: Electrons. Minority carriers: Holes.

n >> p; n ≈ ND (donor concentration).

Electrically neutral (not negatively charged!).

Donor energy level is just below conduction band.

P-Type Semiconductor

Dopant: Trivalent (Group III) — Boron (B), Aluminium (Al), Gallium (Ga), Indium (In).

3 valence electrons → form only 3 bonds + 1 hole (acceptor).

Majority carriers: Holes. Minority carriers: Electrons.

p >> n; p ≈ NA (acceptor concentration).

Electrically neutral (not positively charged!).

Acceptor energy level is just above valence band.

1.3 P-N Junction Diode

P-N Junction Formation

When a P-type and N-type semiconductor are brought together (formed in a single crystal), a P-N junction is created. At the junction:

  1. Electrons from N-side diffuse to P-side; holes from P-side diffuse to N-side.
  2. This diffusion leaves behind immobile ionized atoms — positive donor ions on N-side and negative acceptor ions on P-side.
  3. These ions create an electric field from N to P (opposing further diffusion).
  4. A region devoid of free carriers forms — called the Depletion Region (or space charge region).
  5. A barrier potential (V₀) develops: ~0.7V for Si, ~0.3V for Ge.

Biasing of P-N Junction

Forward Bias

P connected to +ve, N to −ve of battery. External voltage opposes barrier potential → depletion region narrows → majority carriers cross junction → current flows.

Diode conducts when V > V₀ (0.7V Si, 0.3V Ge). Acts as closed switch.

Reverse Bias

P connected to −ve, N to +ve. External voltage aids barrier → depletion region widens → only tiny reverse saturation current (I₀) flows due to minority carriers.

Acts as open switch. At breakdown voltage → avalanche/Zener breakdown.

Diode Current Equation (Shockley)
\[ I = I_0\left(e^{V/\eta V_T} - 1\right) \] ...(7.3)

Where I₀ = reverse saturation current, V = applied voltage, η = ideality factor (1 for Ge, 2 for Si), VT = thermal voltage = kT/q ≈ 26 mV at 300K.

Properties of PN Junction Diode
  • Unidirectional device — conducts in one direction only
  • Used as rectifier (AC to DC conversion)
  • Static resistance: R = V/I; Dynamic resistance: rd = ΔV/ΔI
  • Applications: Rectifiers, voltage regulators (Zener), clippers, clampers, switches, demodulators

1.4 Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT)

Transistor

A three-terminal semiconductor device formed by sandwiching one type of semiconductor between two layers of the other type. Two types: NPN and PNP. Three regions: Emitter (E), Base (B), Collector (C).

NPN Transistor

P-type base sandwiched between two N-type regions. Majority current carriers: electrons.

Emitter-Base: Forward biased. Collector-Base: Reverse biased.

Current flow: Electrons from emitter → base → collector. IE = IB + IC.

More widely used than PNP (electron mobility > hole mobility → faster).

PNP Transistor

N-type base sandwiched between two P-type regions. Majority current carriers: holes.

Emitter-Base: Forward biased. Collector-Base: Reverse biased.

Current flow: Holes from emitter → base → collector. IE = IB + IC.

Biasing polarities are opposite to NPN.

Key Features
  • Emitter: Heavily doped, medium size. Emits majority carriers.
  • Base: Very thin (<1μm), lightly doped. Controls carrier flow.
  • Collector: Moderately doped, largest region. Collects carriers.
  • Called "Bipolar" because both electrons AND holes participate in current flow.
Current Gain
\[ \alpha = \frac{I_C}{I_E} \quad (0.95\text{–}0.99) \] ...(7.4)
\[ \beta = \frac{I_C}{I_B} \quad (20\text{–}500) \] ...(7.5)
\[ \beta = \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}; \quad \alpha = \frac{\beta}{1+\beta} \] ...(7.6)
BJT Applications
  • Amplification (audio, RF, signal)
  • Switching (digital circuits)
  • Oscillators
  • Voltage regulators
  • Current sources
  • Motor drivers
  • Darlington pair (high gain)

FET (Field Effect Transistor)

FET

A voltage-controlled, unipolar device — current flow involves only one type of charge carrier (electrons in N-channel, holes in P-channel). Three terminals: Gate (G), Drain (D), Source (S).

JFET (Junction FET)

A bar of N-type (or P-type) semiconductor with P-type (or N-type) regions diffused on both sides forming PN junctions. The gate voltage controls the depletion region width and hence the channel width available for current flow.

  • Gate is reverse biased → very high input impedance (10⁹–10¹² Ω)
  • VGS = 0: Maximum drain current (IDSS)
  • VGS = VP (pinch-off): Channel pinched off, ID ≈ 0

MOSFET

MOSFET (Metal Oxide Semiconductor FET)

Also called IGFET (Insulated Gate FET). Gate is insulated from the channel by a thin layer of silicon dioxide (SiO₂). Two types: Enhancement mode and Depletion mode. Most widely used transistor in digital ICs.

Enhancement MOSFET (E-MOSFET)

No physical channel exists at zero gate voltage. Channel is induced by applying gate voltage above threshold VTH. Normally OFF device.

N-channel: Needs +VGS > VTH to turn ON.

Most commonly used in digital CMOS circuits.

Depletion MOSFET (D-MOSFET)

Physical channel exists at zero gate voltage. Can operate in both depletion mode (negative VGS) and enhancement mode (positive VGS). Normally ON device.

CMOS Technology

CMOS (Complementary MOS)

Uses complementary pairs of N-channel and P-channel Enhancement MOSFETs. The most widely used technology for digital ICs. Key feature: extremely low power consumption — power is consumed only during switching transitions.

CMOS Inverter (NOT Gate)

Basic building block of all CMOS logic. P-MOSFET (top, connected to VDD) and N-MOSFET (bottom, connected to ground) with gates tied together as input.

  • Input = 0 (LOW): PMOS ON, NMOS OFF → Output = VDD (HIGH)
  • Input = 1 (HIGH): PMOS OFF, NMOS ON → Output = 0 (LOW)
  • At any time, only one transistor is ON → no DC path from VDD to ground → zero static power.

Advantages of CMOS
  1. Very low static power consumption (nanowatts)
  2. High noise immunity
  3. Wide supply voltage range (3V to 15V)
  4. High packing density (billions of transistors per chip)
  5. High fan-out capability
  6. Both logic levels are well-defined (rail-to-rail output)
CMOS Applications
  • All modern microprocessors and memory chips (RAM, ROM, Flash)
  • Digital watches and calculators
  • Image sensors (CMOS cameras)
  • Logic gates (4000 series)
  • Smartphones, tablets, IoT devices
  • FPGA and ASIC designs

Solved Problems

Example 7.1

In a silicon semiconductor, ND = 10¹⁷ /cm³. Find majority and minority carrier concentrations (ni = 1.5×10¹⁰ /cm³).

Solution

N-type: n ≈ ND = 10¹⁷ /cm³ (majority)

p = ni²/ND = (1.5×10¹⁰)²/10¹⁷ = 2.25×10²⁰/10¹⁷ = 2.25×10³ /cm³ (minority)

Example 7.2

A transistor has α = 0.98. Find β and IC if IB = 50 μA.

Solution

β = α/(1−α) = 0.98/0.02 = 49

IC = β × IB = 49 × 50 μA = 2.45 mA

IE = IC + IB = 2.45 + 0.05 = 2.5 mA

Example 7.3

If IC = 10 mA and β = 100, find IB and IE.

Solution

IB = IC/β = 10/100 = 0.1 mA = 100 μA

IE = IC + IB = 10 + 0.1 = 10.1 mA

α = β/(1+β) = 100/101 = 0.99

Example 7.4

The forbidden energy gap of Ge is 0.72 eV and Si is 1.1 eV. Which is more conductive at room temperature? Why?

Solution

Germanium is more conductive at room temperature because its energy gap (0.72 eV) is smaller than Silicon (1.1 eV). At room temperature, more electrons can gain sufficient thermal energy to cross the smaller gap of Ge into the conduction band, resulting in higher intrinsic carrier concentration (ni(Ge) ≈ 2.5×10¹³ >> ni(Si) ≈ 1.5×10¹⁰).

B. Subjective Questions

Q1. Draw and explain energy band diagrams of conductors, semiconductors, and insulators.

Conductor: VB and CB overlap, Eg=0, electrons freely available. Semiconductor: Small gap (Si=1.1eV, Ge=0.72eV), at room temp some electrons jump to CB. Insulator: Large gap (>3eV, diamond=6eV), electrons cannot cross. The forbidden gap determines electrical behavior of the material.

Q2. Differentiate between intrinsic and extrinsic semiconductors.

Intrinsic: Pure, n=p=ni, low conductivity, both carriers equal. Extrinsic: Doped with impurities, n≠p, higher conductivity, one carrier type dominates. N-type (pentavalent dopant, electrons majority) or P-type (trivalent dopant, holes majority).

Q3. Explain P-N junction formation and the concept of depletion region.

When P and N types join: diffusion of majority carriers across junction → recombination → immobile ions left behind → electric field develops opposing diffusion → equilibrium → depletion region (no free carriers) with barrier potential (0.7V Si, 0.3V Ge). Width depends on doping concentration.

Q4. Compare NPN and PNP transistors. Why is NPN more commonly used?

Both have 3 regions (E, B, C). NPN: electrons are majority carriers; PNP: holes are majority. NPN is more common because electron mobility > hole mobility → faster switching, better high-frequency performance, easier to fabricate in IC technology.

Q5. Compare BJT, FET, and MOSFET.

BJT: Bipolar, current-controlled, low Zin, higher noise. JFET: Unipolar, voltage-controlled, very high Zin, lower noise. MOSFET: Insulated gate (SiO₂), highest Zin, most widely used in ICs, basis of CMOS technology. Enhancement MOSFET is normally OFF.

Q6. Explain CMOS technology and its advantages.

CMOS uses complementary NMOS + PMOS pairs. In any logic state, one transistor is OFF → no DC current path → near-zero static power. Advantages: lowest power, high noise immunity, wide VDD range, high density. Used in all modern processors, memory, smartphones.

Unit Summary

Key Takeaways
  • Materials classified by energy gap: Conductors (0 eV), Semiconductors (~1 eV), Insulators (>3 eV).
  • Intrinsic: Pure semiconductor, n = p = ni.
  • Doping: Adding impurities to control conductivity.
  • N-type: Pentavalent dopant → electrons majority. P-type: Trivalent dopant → holes majority.
  • PN Junction: Depletion region + barrier potential (0.7V Si). Forward bias → conducts; Reverse bias → blocks.
  • BJT: Bipolar, current-controlled, NPN/PNP, IE = IB + IC, β = IC/IB.
  • FET: Unipolar, voltage-controlled, very high input impedance.
  • MOSFET: Insulated gate (SiO₂), Enhancement (normally OFF) and Depletion (normally ON) types.
  • CMOS: Complementary NMOS + PMOS, near-zero static power, basis of all modern digital ICs.
