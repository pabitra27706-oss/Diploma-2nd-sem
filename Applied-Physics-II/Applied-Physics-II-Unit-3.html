Coulomb's Law & Unit of Charge 🔥🔥🔥

Electric Charge — Basics

Definition — Electric Charge

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force when placed near other charged matter. Charge can be positive (+) or negative (−).

SI Unit: Coulomb (C). Symbol: q or Q.

Key facts about charge:

  • Like charges repel each other (+ repels +, − repels −).
  • Unlike charges attract each other (+ attracts −).
  • Charge is quantized — it always exists in integer multiples of the elementary charge: q = ne, where e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.
  • Charge is conserved — it cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred.
  • The electron has charge −e = −1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.
  • The proton has charge +e = +1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.
Analogy

Think of charges like tiny magnets. Two north poles repel, two south poles repel, but a north and south attract. Similarly, two positive charges repel, two negative repel, but positive and negative attract.

⭐ Coulomb's Law

Statement — Coulomb's Law

Coulomb's Law states that the electrostatic force of attraction or repulsion between two point charges is:

  1. Directly proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the two charges.
  2. Inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
  3. Acts along the line joining the two charges.

Formula 3.1 — Coulomb's Law:

\[ F = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} = k \cdot \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \]

where:

  • F = electrostatic force between the charges (in Newton, N)
  • q₁, q₂ = magnitudes of the two point charges (in Coulomb, C)
  • r = distance between the two charges (in metre, m)
  • k = Coulomb's constant = \(\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\) = 9 × 10⁹ N m² C⁻²
  • ε₀ = permittivity of free space = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² C² N⁻¹ m⁻² (or F/m)

Important Points about Coulomb's Law
  1. The force is attractive if charges are of opposite sign, and repulsive if same sign.
  2. The force follows Newton's Third Law — the force on q₁ due to q₂ is equal and opposite to the force on q₂ due to q₁.
  3. The law applies only to point charges (charges whose size is negligible compared to the distance between them).
  4. Coulomb's law is mathematically similar to Newton's law of gravitation: F = Gm₁m₂/r². But gravitational force is always attractive, while electrostatic force can be attractive or repulsive.
  5. In a medium other than vacuum, F = kq₁q₂/(κr²), where κ (kappa) = dielectric constant of the medium. Force is reduced in a medium.

Unit of Charge — The Coulomb

Definition — Coulomb

One Coulomb (1 C) is the amount of charge that, when placed at a distance of 1 metre from an equal charge in vacuum, experiences a repulsive force of 9 × 10⁹ Newton.

Alternatively: 1 Coulomb = charge carried by 6.25 × 10¹⁸ electrons.

Practically: If 1 ampere current flows for 1 second, the charge transferred = 1 Coulomb. (Q = It)

Common Mistakes
  • Don't forget to convert μC (microcoulomb) to C: 1 μC = 10⁻⁶ C
  • Don't forget to convert cm to m in distance: 1 cm = 10⁻² m
  • Always use k = 9 × 10⁹ (not 9 × 10⁻⁹)
  • The formula gives the magnitude of force. State direction (attraction/repulsion) separately.

Electric Field 🔥🔥

What is an Electric Field?

Definition — Electric Field

The electric field is the region or space around a charged body within which another charged body experiences an electrostatic force. It is a vector quantity — it has both magnitude and direction.

Analogy: Imagine a powerful magnet. Even without touching it, iron filings placed near it get pulled. There is an invisible "field" around the magnet that exerts force. Similarly, a charged object creates an invisible electric field around it that exerts force on other charges placed in that region.

⭐ Electric Field Intensity (E)

Definition — Electric Field Intensity

The electric field intensity (E) at a point is defined as the force experienced per unit positive test charge placed at that point (without disturbing the original field).

Formula 3.2 — Electric Field Intensity:

\[ E = \frac{F}{q_0} \]

where:

  • E = electric field intensity (N/C or V/m)
  • F = force on the test charge (N)
  • q₀ = magnitude of the positive test charge (C)

SI Unit: Newton per Coulomb (N/C) or Volt per metre (V/m). Both are equivalent.

Direction: Same as the direction of force on a positive test charge.

Electric Field Due to a Point Charge

The electric field at a distance r from a point charge Q is found by combining Coulomb's law with the definition of E:

F = kQq₀/r², so E = F/q₀ = kQ/r²

Formula 3.3 — Electric Field due to Point Charge:

\[ E = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{Q}{r^2} = k \cdot \frac{Q}{r^2} \]

where:

  • E = electric field intensity at distance r (N/C)
  • Q = source charge (C)
  • r = distance from the charge (m)
  • k = 9 × 10⁹ Nm²C⁻²
Direction of Electric Field
  • For a positive charge (+Q): E points radially outward (away from the charge).
  • For a negative charge (−Q): E points radially inward (towards the charge).

Relationship between Force and Field

If you know the electric field E at a point, the force on any charge q placed at that point is:

F = qE

This is very useful! Once E is known, you can find force on any charge without using Coulomb's law again.

Electric Lines of Force & Electric Flux 🔥

⭐ Electric Lines of Force (Field Lines)

Definition — Electric Lines of Force

Electric lines of force (or electric field lines) are imaginary curves drawn in an electric field such that the tangent at any point on the curve gives the direction of the electric field at that point. They were introduced by Michael Faraday to visualize electric fields.

⭐ Properties of Electric Lines of Force

Properties (Very Important for Exam!)
  1. Electric lines of force originate from positive charge and terminate on negative charge.
  2. The tangent drawn at any point on a field line gives the direction of the electric field at that point.
  3. Two electric lines of force never cross or intersect each other. (If they did, there would be two directions of E at the intersection point, which is impossible.)
  4. The lines are closer together where the field is strong and farther apart where the field is weak.
  5. Electric field lines are continuous curves. They don't break in the middle.
  6. The lines contract longitudinally (along their length) — this shows the attraction between unlike charges.
  7. The lines exert lateral (sideways) pressure — this shows the repulsion between like charges.
  8. In a uniform electric field, the lines are parallel, equidistant, and straight.
  9. Electric lines of force are perpendicular to the surface of a conductor at every point.
  10. No electric lines of force exist inside a conductor (E = 0 inside a conductor).
Exam Favourite!

"State the properties of electric lines of force" is a very common 5-mark question. Write at least 6-7 properties with diagrams (draw field lines for + charge, − charge, and two charges).

⭐ Electric Flux (φ or ΦE)

Definition — Electric Flux

Electric flux is the total number of electric field lines passing through a given surface. It is a scalar quantity and is a measure of the "flow" of the electric field through a surface.

Formula 3.4 — Electric Flux:

\[ \Phi_E = E \cdot A \cdot \cos\theta = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} \]

where:

  • ΦE = electric flux (Nm²/C or Vm)
  • E = electric field intensity (N/C)
  • A = area of the surface (m²)
  • θ = angle between E and the normal to the surface
Special Cases
  • When E is perpendicular to the surface (θ = 0°): Φ = EA (maximum flux)
  • When E is parallel to the surface (θ = 90°): Φ = 0 (no flux — field lines don't pass through)

Analogy: Think of electric flux like water flowing through a net. If you hold the net perpendicular to the water flow, maximum water passes through. If you tilt the net to be parallel to the flow, no water passes through. Electric flux works the same way with field lines and surfaces.

Electric Potential & Potential Difference 🔥🔥🔥

⭐ Electric Potential (V)

Definition — Electric Potential

The electric potential at a point in an electric field is defined as the work done in bringing a unit positive charge from infinity to that point against the electric field.

Or equivalently: Potential = Work done per unit charge.

Formula 3.5 — Electric Potential:

\[ V = \frac{W}{q} \]

where:

  • V = electric potential (Volt, V)
  • W = work done (Joule, J)
  • q = charge (Coulomb, C)

SI Unit: Volt (V). 1 Volt = 1 Joule / 1 Coulomb.

Electric potential is a scalar quantity (has magnitude but no direction).

Potential Due to a Point Charge

Formula 3.6 — Potential due to Point Charge:

\[ V = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{Q}{r} = k \cdot \frac{Q}{r} \]

where:

  • V = electric potential at distance r (Volt)
  • Q = charge (C)
  • r = distance from the charge (m)
  • k = 9 × 10⁹ Nm²/C²

Note: V is positive for +Q and negative for −Q.

Easy way to remember

Electric field E goes as 1/r² (inverse square) and is a vector.

Electric potential V goes as 1/r (inverse) and is a scalar.

⭐ Potential Difference

Definition — Potential Difference

The potential difference between two points A and B in an electric field is the work done in moving a unit positive charge from point B to point A.

\[ V_A - V_B = \frac{W_{B \to A}}{q} \]

Potential difference is what drives current in circuits. It is commonly called voltage.

Key Points
  • A positive charge moves from higher potential to lower potential naturally (like a ball rolling downhill).
  • A negative charge moves from lower potential to higher potential naturally.
  • Work is done by the field when charge moves in the direction of the field.
  • Work is done against the field when charge moves opposite to the field direction.
  • Equipotential surface: A surface where all points have the same potential. No work is done in moving a charge along an equipotential surface.

Relationship between Electric Field and Potential

Formula 3.7:

\[ E = -\frac{dV}{dr} = -\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta r} \]

The electric field is the negative gradient (rate of change) of potential with distance.

For a uniform field: \( E = \frac{V}{d} \) (V = potential difference, d = distance between plates).

Exam Must-Know

1 Volt = 1 J/C. 1 electron-volt (eV) = energy gained by 1 electron through 1 V potential difference = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J. eV is a unit of energy, not voltage!

Gauss's Law & Application to Charged Sphere 🔥🔥🔥

⭐ Gauss's Law (Statement Only)

Statement — Gauss's Law

Gauss's Law states that the total electric flux through any closed surface (called Gaussian surface) is equal to 1/ε₀ times the total charge enclosed within that surface.

Formula 3.8 — Gauss's Law:

\[ \Phi_E = \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{q_{enclosed}}{\varepsilon_0} \]

In simple terms:

\[ \text{Total electric flux through closed surface} = \frac{\text{Total charge inside}}{\varepsilon_0} \]

where:

  • ΦE = total electric flux through the closed surface (Nm²/C)
  • qenclosed = total charge enclosed within the surface (C)
  • ε₀ = permittivity of free space = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² C²N⁻¹m⁻²
  • ∮ represents integration over the entire closed surface
Key Points about Gauss's Law
  • The Gaussian surface is an imaginary closed surface chosen for mathematical convenience. It doesn't have to be a physical surface.
  • The flux depends only on the charge enclosed inside the surface, not on charges outside.
  • The shape of the Gaussian surface can be anything (sphere, cylinder, cube, etc.), but we choose the shape that makes calculation easiest (usually matching the symmetry of the charge distribution).
  • If no charge is enclosed, the net flux through the surface is zero (even if there are charges outside).
  • Gauss's law is extremely useful for finding electric fields of symmetric charge distributions (sphere, infinite plane, infinite wire, cylinder).

⭐ Application: Electric Field due to a Uniformly Charged Sphere

Consider a sphere of radius R carrying a total charge Q uniformly distributed on its surface (conducting sphere) or throughout its volume (non-conducting sphere). We want to find E at a distance r from the centre.

Case 1: At a point OUTSIDE the sphere (r > R)

Finding E Outside a Charged Sphere

Step 1: Choose a spherical Gaussian surface of radius r (r > R) concentric with the charged sphere.

Step 2: By symmetry, E is uniform over this Gaussian surface and directed radially outward (for positive charge).

Step 3: Apply Gauss's Law:

\[ \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{q_{enclosed}}{\varepsilon_0} \]

Since E is constant over the Gaussian surface and parallel to dA everywhere:

\[ E \times 4\pi r^2 = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0} \]

Formula 3.9 — E outside charged sphere (r > R):

\[ E = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{Q}{r^2} = \frac{kQ}{r^2} \]

This is exactly the same as the field due to a point charge Q at the centre! Outside the sphere, the charged sphere behaves like a point charge.

Case 2: At a point ON the surface (r = R)

Formula 3.10 — E on the surface (r = R):

\[ E_{surface} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{Q}{R^2} = \frac{kQ}{R^2} \]

This is the maximum value of E for a charged conducting sphere.

Case 3: At a point INSIDE a conducting sphere (r < R)

Formula 3.11 — E inside conducting sphere (r < R):

\[ E = 0 \]

Inside a conductor, all charge resides on the surface. A Gaussian surface inside encloses no charge. By Gauss's law, the net flux is zero, so E = 0.

Fig. 3.7: Electric field due to a charged conducting sphere — graph of E vs r showing E=0 inside, max at surface, inverse square outside

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Summary: E for Charged Conducting Sphere
  • Inside (r < R): E = 0 (no charge enclosed)
  • On surface (r = R): E = kQ/R² (maximum)
  • Outside (r > R): E = kQ/r² (decreases as inverse square of distance)
  • The sphere behaves as a point charge for external points.
Very Important for Exam!

"Apply Gauss's law to find the electric field due to a charged sphere" is a very common 5-7 mark question. You need to: (1) State Gauss's law, (2) Choose Gaussian surface, (3) Apply the formula for r > R, r = R, and r < R cases, (4) Draw the E vs r graph.

Capacitor — Working, Types & Capacitance 🔥🔥🔥

What is a Capacitor?

Definition — Capacitor

A capacitor is an electrical device that stores electric charge and electrical energy in the form of an electric field. It consists of two conducting plates (electrodes) separated by an insulating material (dielectric).

Real-world analogy: Think of a capacitor like a water tank. Just as a tank stores water, a capacitor stores electric charge. The bigger the tank (more capacitance), the more water (charge) it can store. The water pressure (voltage) determines how fast the tank fills up.

Where are capacitors used?

  • Every electronic circuit (phones, TVs, computers, radios)
  • Camera flash (stores energy, releases quickly)
  • Power supply smoothing (removes fluctuations)
  • Tuning circuits in radios
  • Defibrillators in hospitals (stores charge to restart heart)
  • Electric fans (starting capacitor)
  • Air conditioning units

⭐ Working Principle of a Capacitor

How a Capacitor Works
  1. A capacitor has two metal plates separated by an insulator (dielectric).
  2. When connected to a battery (voltage source), electrons flow from the battery to one plate, making it negatively charged (−Q).
  3. Equal number of electrons are pulled away from the other plate by the battery, making it positively charged (+Q).
  4. This creates an electric field between the plates (from + to −).
  5. Energy is stored in this electric field.
  6. Charge continues to flow until the potential difference across the plates equals the battery voltage. Then current stops.
  7. The capacitor is now "charged" — it holds the charge even after the battery is removed.
  8. When the capacitor is connected to a circuit (load), it releases (discharges) the stored charge and energy.

⭐ Capacitance

Definition — Capacitance

Capacitance (C) of a capacitor is the ratio of the charge (Q) stored on either plate to the potential difference (V) applied across the plates.

It is a measure of the capacitor's ability to store charge.

Formula 3.12 — Capacitance:

\[ C = \frac{Q}{V} \]

where:

  • C = capacitance (Farad, F)
  • Q = charge stored (Coulomb, C)
  • V = potential difference across the plates (Volt, V)

SI Unit: Farad (F). 1 Farad = 1 Coulomb / 1 Volt.

Practical Units of Capacitance

1 Farad is an extremely large unit. In practice, we use smaller units:

  • 1 millifarad (mF) = 10⁻³ F
  • 1 microfarad (μF) = 10⁻⁶ F
  • 1 nanofarad (nF) = 10⁻⁹ F
  • 1 picofarad (pF) = 10⁻¹² F

Most practical capacitors range from pF to mF.

Common Mistake

Don't confuse C (capacitance, unit: Farad) with C (Coulomb, unit of charge). In formulas, Q represents charge in Coulombs, and C represents capacitance in Farads. Context makes it clear!

⭐ Types of Capacitors

Classification of Capacitors

A. Based on Construction:

  1. Parallel Plate Capacitor: Two flat metal plates separated by a dielectric. Most basic type. Used in theory and simple circuits.
  2. Cylindrical Capacitor: Two coaxial cylinders (one inside the other) with dielectric between them. Used in cables.
  3. Spherical Capacitor: Two concentric spheres with dielectric between them.

B. Based on Dielectric Material:

  1. Air Capacitor: Air as dielectric. Used in radio tuning circuits. Variable type.
  2. Mica Capacitor: Mica sheets as dielectric. High stability, low loss. Used in high-frequency circuits.
  3. Paper Capacitor: Waxed paper as dielectric between aluminium foils. Inexpensive.
  4. Ceramic Capacitor: Ceramic material as dielectric. Very small, used in PCBs.
  5. Electrolytic Capacitor: Oxide layer as dielectric. Very high capacitance. Has polarity (+ and − terminals). Used in power supplies.

C. Based on Capacitance Value:

  1. Fixed Capacitor: Capacitance value is fixed during manufacturing.
  2. Variable Capacitor: Capacitance can be changed (tuning capacitor in radio, trimmer).

⭐ Capacitance of a Parallel Plate Capacitor (Formula Only)

Formula 3.13 — Parallel Plate Capacitor:

\[ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d} \]

With dielectric between the plates:

\[ C = \frac{\kappa \varepsilon_0 A}{d} = \frac{\varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r A}{d} \]

where:

  • C = capacitance (F)
  • ε₀ = permittivity of free space = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m
  • A = area of each plate (m²)
  • d = distance between the plates (m)
  • κ or εr = dielectric constant (relative permittivity) of the material between plates
Factors Affecting Capacitance

From the formula C = ε₀εᵣA/d, capacitance increases when:

  1. Area (A) increases — larger plates store more charge.
  2. Distance (d) decreases — plates closer together → stronger field → more capacitance.
  3. Dielectric constant (εᵣ) increases — better insulator between plates → more capacitance.

Energy Stored in a Capacitor

Formula 3.14 — Energy Stored:

\[ U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}QV = \frac{Q^2}{2C} \]

where:

  • U = energy stored (Joule, J)
  • C = capacitance (F)
  • V = potential difference (V)
  • Q = charge stored (C)
Remember These Three Forms

Energy = ½CV² = ½QV = Q²/2C. All three give the same answer — use whichever is convenient based on what data is given in the problem.

Series & Parallel Combination of Capacitors 🔥🔥🔥

Why Combine Capacitors?

In practice, we often need a specific capacitance value that may not be available as a single capacitor. By combining capacitors in series or parallel, we can achieve the desired value. This is similar to combining resistors, but the formulas are reversed!

⭐ Capacitors in Series

Series Combination

Capacitors are said to be in series when they are connected end to end so that the same charge (Q) flows through each capacitor, but the voltage divides among them.

Fig. 3.12: Capacitors C₁, C₂, C₃ connected in series

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Properties of Series Combination
  • Charge is SAME on each capacitor: Q = Q₁ = Q₂ = Q₃
  • Voltage DIVIDES: V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃
  • The equivalent capacitance is LESS than the smallest individual capacitance.

Derivation:

Total voltage: V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃

Since V = Q/C: Q/Ceq = Q/C₁ + Q/C₂ + Q/C₃

Dividing by Q:

Formula 3.15 — Capacitors in Series:

\[ \frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \frac{1}{C_3} + \ldots \]

For two capacitors in series:

\[ C_{eq} = \frac{C_1 \times C_2}{C_1 + C_2} \]

(Product divided by sum — easy to remember!)

⭐ Capacitors in Parallel

Parallel Combination

Capacitors are said to be in parallel when they are connected across the same two points so that the same voltage (V) is applied across each capacitor, but the charge divides among them.

Fig. 3.13: Capacitors C₁, C₂, C₃ connected in parallel

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Properties of Parallel Combination
  • Voltage is SAME across each capacitor: V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃
  • Charge DIVIDES: Q = Q₁ + Q₂ + Q₃
  • The equivalent capacitance is MORE than the largest individual capacitance.

Derivation:

Total charge: Q = Q₁ + Q₂ + Q₃

Since Q = CV: CeqV = C₁V + C₂V + C₃V

Dividing by V:

Formula 3.16 — Capacitors in Parallel:

\[ C_{eq} = C_1 + C_2 + C_3 + \ldots \]

Simply ADD the capacitances!

Dielectric & Its Effect on Capacitance 🔥🔥

What is a Dielectric?

Definition — Dielectric

A dielectric is an insulating material (non-conducting material) that can be placed between the plates of a capacitor. When placed in an electric field, a dielectric gets polarized (positive and negative charges shift slightly within its molecules), which reduces the net electric field between the plates and increases the capacitance.

Examples of dielectric materials: Air, vacuum, paper, glass, mica, ceramic, plastic, rubber, oil, wax, porcelain, distilled water.

⭐ Dielectric Constant (κ or εr)

Definition — Dielectric Constant

The dielectric constant (κ) or relative permittivity (εr) of a material is the ratio of the capacitance of a capacitor with the dielectric to the capacitance with vacuum (air) between the plates.

\[ \kappa = \varepsilon_r = \frac{C_{with\,dielectric}}{C_{with\,vacuum}} = \frac{C'}{C_0} \]

It is a dimensionless quantity (no unit) and always ≥ 1.

⭐ Effect of Dielectric on Capacitance

Key Effect: Dielectric INCREASES Capacitance

When a dielectric material (κ > 1) is inserted between the plates of a capacitor:

\[ C' = \kappa \cdot C_0 = \kappa \cdot \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d} \]

Since κ > 1, C' > C₀. The capacitance increases by a factor of κ.

Why? The dielectric gets polarized, creating an internal field that opposes the original field. This reduces the net field, reduces the voltage (for same charge), and by C = Q/V, capacitance increases.

What Happens When Dielectric is Inserted?

If the capacitor is disconnected from battery (constant Q):

  • Charge Q stays the same
  • Capacitance C increases by factor κ
  • Voltage V = Q/C decreases by factor κ
  • Electric field E decreases by factor κ
  • Energy U = Q²/2C decreases by factor κ

  • Voltage V stays the same
  • Capacitance C increases by factor κ

  • Energy U = ½CV² increases by factor κ

⭐ Dielectric Breakdown

Definition — Dielectric Breakdown

Dielectric breakdown occurs when the electric field applied across a dielectric material exceeds a critical value (called the dielectric strength). At this point, the dielectric loses its insulating property and starts conducting electricity. This can cause sparking, damage to the capacitor, and even fire.

Key Points about Dielectric Breakdown
  • Dielectric strength = maximum electric field a dielectric can withstand without breakdown. Unit: V/m or kV/mm.
  • Example: Air breaks down at about 3 × 10⁶ V/m (3 kV/mm). This is why we see sparks and lightning!
  • Glass: ~10 kV/mm, Mica: ~200 kV/mm, Rubber: ~20 kV/mm
  • Every capacitor has a maximum voltage rating. Exceeding it causes dielectric breakdown and destroys the capacitor.
  • Lightning is a natural example of dielectric breakdown of air.
Safety Note

Never exceed the voltage rating printed on a capacitor! Dielectric breakdown can cause the capacitor to explode, especially electrolytic capacitors. Always check polarity for electrolytic capacitors — reversing polarity can also cause breakdown.

Exam Points

Common exam questions: "What is a dielectric? How does it affect capacitance?" and "What is dielectric breakdown?" Write: definition → dielectric constant → C' = κC₀ → capacitance increases → breakdown occurs when field exceeds dielectric strength.

Numerical Solving Tips for Electrostatics

  3. Convert all capacitances to Farads (1 μF = 10⁻⁶ F, 1 pF = 10⁻¹² F)

Numerical 1: Coulomb's Law ⭐

Problem

Two charges of 3 μC and 5 μC are placed 20 cm apart in air. Calculate the force between them. Is it attractive or repulsive?

Solution

q₁ = 3 μC = 3 × 10⁻⁶ C

q₂ = 5 μC = 5 × 10⁻⁶ C

r = 20 cm = 0.2 m

k = 9 × 10⁹ Nm²/C²

F = kq₁q₂/r²

F = (9 × 10⁹ × 3 × 10⁻⁶ × 5 × 10⁻⁶) / (0.2)²

F = (9 × 10⁹ × 15 × 10⁻¹²) / 0.04

F = (135 × 10⁻³) / 0.04

F = 0.135 / 0.04

F = 3.375 N

Both charges are positive (+3 μC and +5 μC). Like charges → Repulsive force.

R = 10 cm = 0.1 m, Q = 2 × 10⁻⁸ C

Numerical 14: Two Capacitors in Series — Finding Individual Capacitances

Problem

Two capacitors when connected in parallel give an equivalent capacitance of 9 μF and when connected in series give 2 μF. Find the individual capacitances.

Solution

Parallel: C₁ + C₂ = 9 ... (i)

Series: C₁C₂/(C₁+C₂) = 2 ... (ii)

C₁C₂ / 9 = 2 → C₁C₂ = 18 ... (iii)

C₁ and C₂ are roots of: x² − 9x + 18 = 0

x² − 9x + 18 = 0

(x − 3)(x − 6) = 0

x = 3 or x = 6

C₁ = 3 μF and C₂ = 6 μF

Parallel: 3 + 6 = 9 μF ✓

Series: (3 × 6)/(3 + 6) = 18/9 = 2 μF ✓

Important Definitions

Exam Strategy

Definition questions = guaranteed marks. Write: definition → formula → SI unit → one key point.

1. Electric Charge

A fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force in an electric field. Two types: positive and negative. Like charges repel, unlike attract. SI Unit: Coulomb (C). Quantized: q = ne where e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.

2. Coulomb's Law

The electrostatic force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. F = kq₁q₂/r², where k = 9 × 10⁹ Nm²/C². The force acts along the line joining the charges.

3. Electric Field

The region around a charged body where another charge experiences an electrostatic force. It is a vector quantity. SI Unit: N/C or V/m.

4. Electric Field Intensity (E)

The force experienced per unit positive test charge placed at a point in the electric field. E = F/q₀. For a point charge: E = kQ/r². SI Unit: N/C or V/m.

5. Electric Lines of Force

Imaginary curves drawn in an electric field such that the tangent at any point gives the direction of electric field at that point. They originate from positive charge and terminate on negative charge. They never cross each other.

6. Electric Flux

The total number of electric field lines passing through a given surface. Φ = EA cos θ. Maximum when surface is perpendicular to E (θ = 0°). SI Unit: Nm²/C or Vm.

7. Electric Potential (V)

The work done in bringing a unit positive charge from infinity to a given point against the electric field. V = W/q = kQ/r. It is a scalar quantity. SI Unit: Volt (V). 1 V = 1 J/C.

8. Potential Difference

The work done in moving a unit positive charge from one point to another in an electric field. VA − VB = WB→A/q. SI Unit: Volt (V). Also called voltage.

9. Gauss's Law

The total electric flux through any closed surface is equal to 1/ε₀ times the total charge enclosed within that surface. Φ = qenclosed/ε₀. Used to find electric field for symmetric charge distributions.

10. Capacitor

An electrical device consisting of two conducting plates separated by an insulating material (dielectric), used to store electric charge and energy. The stored energy is in the form of an electric field between the plates.

11. Capacitance (C)

The ratio of charge stored on a capacitor to the potential difference across its plates. C = Q/V. It measures the ability of a capacitor to store charge. SI Unit: Farad (F). 1 F = 1 C/V. Practical units: μF, nF, pF.

12. Dielectric

An insulating material placed between the plates of a capacitor. When polarized by the electric field, it increases the capacitance by a factor of κ (dielectric constant). Examples: air, mica, glass, paper, ceramic.

13. Dielectric Constant (κ or εr)

The ratio of capacitance with dielectric to capacitance without dielectric (vacuum/air). κ = C'/C₀. It is dimensionless and always ≥ 1. Higher κ means the dielectric increases capacitance more.

14. Dielectric Breakdown

The phenomenon in which a dielectric material loses its insulating property and starts conducting electricity when the applied electric field exceeds a critical value called the dielectric strength. Example: Lightning is dielectric breakdown of air.

15. Equipotential Surface

A surface on which every point has the same electric potential. No work is done in moving a charge along an equipotential surface. Electric field lines are always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces.

16. Electron-Volt (eV)

A unit of energy. It is the energy gained by one electron when it moves through a potential difference of 1 volt. 1 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J. It is a unit of energy, NOT voltage.

Short Answer Questions (2 Marks)

Q1. State Coulomb's law.

Coulomb's law states that the electrostatic force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. F = kq₁q₂/r², where k = 9 × 10⁹ Nm²/C². The force acts along the line joining the charges and is attractive for unlike charges and repulsive for like charges.

Q2. Define electric field intensity and give its SI unit.

Electric field intensity (E) at a point is the force experienced per unit positive test charge placed at that point. E = F/q₀. For a point charge Q: E = kQ/r². SI Unit: Newton per Coulomb (N/C) or Volt per metre (V/m). It is a vector quantity directed outward from positive charge and inward towards negative charge.

Q3. Define electric potential and potential difference.

Electric potential (V): Work done in bringing a unit positive charge from infinity to a point against the electric field. V = W/q. Unit: Volt (V).

Potential difference: Work done in moving a unit positive charge from one point to another. VA − VB = WB→A/q. It drives current in circuits.

Q4. What is a capacitor? Give its SI unit of capacitance.

A capacitor is an electrical device consisting of two conducting plates separated by an insulating material (dielectric), used to store electric charge and energy. C = Q/V. SI Unit of capacitance: Farad (F). 1 F = 1 C/V. Practical units: μF (10⁻⁶ F), pF (10⁻¹² F).

Q5. State Gauss's law.

Gauss's law states that the total electric flux through any closed surface (Gaussian surface) is equal to 1/ε₀ times the total charge enclosed within that surface. Mathematically: Φ = qenclosed/ε₀, where ε₀ = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m is the permittivity of free space. The flux is independent of charges outside the surface.

Q6. What is dielectric breakdown? Give one example.

Dielectric breakdown is the phenomenon where a dielectric (insulating) material loses its insulating property and starts conducting electricity when the applied electric field exceeds the dielectric strength of the material. The material may get damaged permanently. Example: Lightning — the electric field between clouds and ground exceeds the dielectric strength of air (~3 × 10⁶ V/m), causing air to break down and conduct.

Q7. Why do two electric lines of force never cross each other?

If two electric lines of force crossed at a point, there would be two tangents at that point, meaning two different directions of the electric field at the same point. This is physically impossible because the electric field at any point has only one unique direction. Therefore, electric lines of force never cross each other.

Q8. How does dielectric affect the capacitance of a capacitor?

When a dielectric material (dielectric constant κ > 1) is inserted between the plates, the capacitance increases by a factor of κ. C' = κC₀. This happens because the dielectric gets polarized, creating an opposing field that reduces the net field, reducing the voltage for the same charge, and hence increasing C = Q/V. For example, if κ = 5, capacitance becomes 5 times the original.

Q9. Write the formula for capacitors in series and in parallel.

Series: 1/Ceq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ + ... (Equivalent capacitance is less than the smallest.)

Parallel: Ceq = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ + ... (Equivalent capacitance is the sum — more than the largest.)

Note: These are opposite to resistor formulas!

Q10. What is an equipotential surface?

An equipotential surface is a surface on which every point has the same electric potential. No work is done in moving a charge along an equipotential surface (since ΔV = 0). Electric field lines are always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces. For a point charge, equipotential surfaces are concentric spheres.

Both follow inverse square law.

Q2. State the properties of electric lines of force with diagrams. [5 marks] 🔥🔥

Properties:

  1. Originate from +ve charge and terminate on −ve charge.
  2. Tangent at any point gives the direction of E at that point.
  3. Never cross or intersect each other.
  4. Closer together = stronger field; farther = weaker field.
  5. Continuous curves; do not break.
  6. Contract longitudinally (attraction between unlike charges).
  7. Exert lateral pressure (repulsion between like charges).
  8. Perpendicular to conductor surface.
  9. No field lines inside a conductor (E = 0).
  10. Parallel and equidistant in uniform field.

Q3. State Gauss's law. Apply it to find the electric field due to a uniformly charged conducting sphere for points (a) outside, (b) on the surface, and (c) inside. Draw E vs r graph. [7 marks] 🔥🔥🔥

Gauss's Law: Total electric flux through any closed surface = qenclosed/ε₀.

(a) Outside sphere (r > R): Choose spherical Gaussian surface of radius r. All charge Q is enclosed.

E × 4πr² = Q/ε₀ → E = kQ/r² (same as point charge)

(b) On surface (r = R):

E = kQ/R² (maximum value)

(c) Inside conductor (r < R): Gaussian surface inside encloses no charge (all charge on surface).

E × 4πr² = 0 → E = 0

[Draw E vs r graph: E = 0 for r < R, sharp rise to max at r = R, then decreases as 1/r² for r > R]

Q4. What is a capacitor? Explain the working of a parallel plate capacitor. Write the formula for capacitance and explain each term. [5 marks] 🔥

Capacitor: A device with two conducting plates separated by a dielectric, used to store charge and energy.

Working: When connected to a battery, one plate gets +Q and the other −Q. An electric field is created between plates. Energy is stored in this field. Charging stops when voltage across plates = battery voltage.

Formula: C = ε₀εᵣA/d

  • C = capacitance (F)
  • ε₀ = permittivity of free space = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m
  • εᵣ = dielectric constant of material between plates
  • A = area of each plate (m²)
  • d = distance between plates (m)

C increases with: larger A, smaller d, higher εᵣ.

Q5. Derive the formula for equivalent capacitance of capacitors in series and in parallel. [7 marks] 🔥🔥

Series Combination:

Same charge Q on each. Voltage divides: V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃

V₁ = Q/C₁, V₂ = Q/C₂, V₃ = Q/C₃

V = Q/C₁ + Q/C₂ + Q/C₃

Q/Ceq = Q/C₁ + Q/C₂ + Q/C₃

1/Ceq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃

Ceq < smallest individual capacitance.

Parallel Combination:

Same voltage V across each. Charge divides: Q = Q₁ + Q₂ + Q₃

Q₁ = C₁V, Q₂ = C₂V, Q₃ = C₃V

CeqV = C₁V + C₂V + C₃V

Ceq = C₁ + C₂ + C₃

Ceq > largest individual capacitance.

Q6. What is a dielectric? Explain dielectric constant and its effect on capacitance. What is dielectric breakdown? [7 marks] 🔥

Dielectric: An insulating material placed between the plates of a capacitor. Examples: air, glass, mica, paper, ceramic.

Dielectric constant (κ): Ratio of capacitance with dielectric to capacitance without: κ = C'/C₀. Dimensionless, always ≥ 1.

Effect on capacitance: C' = κC₀. The capacitance increases by factor κ when dielectric is inserted. This happens because the dielectric gets polarized, reducing the net electric field, reducing voltage for the same charge, and thus increasing capacitance.

Factors: C = ε₀εᵣA/d — higher εᵣ (= κ) gives higher C.

Dielectric Breakdown: When the applied electric field exceeds the maximum that a dielectric can withstand (called dielectric strength), the dielectric loses its insulating property and starts conducting. This damages the capacitor. Example: Lightning (breakdown of air at ~3 MV/m). Every capacitor has a maximum voltage rating to prevent breakdown.

Q7. Define electric potential. Derive the expression for potential due to a point charge. [5 marks]

Electric Potential (V): Work done in bringing a unit positive charge from infinity to a given point against the electric field. V = W/q. SI Unit: Volt (V).

Derivation for point charge Q:

Consider bringing a test charge q₀ from infinity to a point at distance r from charge Q.

At distance x, force on q₀: F = kQq₀/x²

Small work done: dW = F dx = kQq₀/x² dx

Total work from ∞ to r: W = ∫(∞ to r) kQq₀/x² dx = kQq₀[-1/x] from ∞ to r

W = kQq₀(−1/r + 1/∞) = kQq₀/r (since 1/∞ = 0)

Potential V = W/q₀ = kQ/r

V is positive for +Q and negative for −Q. V decreases with increasing distance.

Practice Numerical Problems

P1. Two charges of 2 μC and 3 μC are placed 10 cm apart in vacuum. Find the force between them. [3 marks]

q₁ = 2 × 10⁻⁶ C, q₂ = 3 × 10⁻⁶ C, r = 0.1 m

F = kq₁q₂/r² = (9 × 10⁹ × 2 × 10⁻⁶ × 3 × 10⁻⁶) / (0.1)²

F = (54 × 10⁻³) / 0.01 = 5.4 N (Repulsive)

P2. Find the electric potential at a distance of 50 cm from a charge of 2 × 10⁻⁶ C. [2 marks]

V = kQ/r = (9 × 10⁹ × 2 × 10⁻⁶) / 0.5

V = (18 × 10³) / 0.5 = 36000 V = 36 kV

P3. Three capacitors of 2 μF, 4 μF, and 4 μF are connected in series across a 120 V supply. Find (a) equivalent capacitance, (b) charge on each, (c) voltage across each. [5 marks]

(a) 1/C = 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/4 = 2/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 = 4/4 = 1

Ceq = 1 μF

(b) Q = CeqV = 1 × 120 = 120 μC (same on all)

(c) V₁ = Q/C₁ = 120/2 = 60 V

V₂ = Q/C₂ = 120/4 = 30 V

V₃ = Q/C₃ = 120/4 = 30 V

Check: 60 + 30 + 30 = 120 V ✓

P4. A 5 μF capacitor is charged to 200 V. Find the charge stored and energy stored. [3 marks]

Q = CV = 5 × 10⁻⁶ × 200 = 1 × 10⁻³ C = 1 mC

U = ½CV² = ½ × 5 × 10⁻⁶ × (200)² = ½ × 5 × 10⁻⁶ × 40000

U = 0.1 J = 100 mJ

P5. Find the equivalent capacitance: 3 μF and 6 μF in parallel, then this combination in series with 4 μF. [3 marks]

Step 1: 3 μF ∥ 6 μF: Cp = 3 + 6 = 9 μF

Step 2: 9 μF in series with 4 μF:

Ceq = (9 × 4)/(9 + 4) = 36/13 = 2.77 μF

P6. What is the electric field at a point 20 cm from the surface of a charged sphere of radius 10 cm carrying 5 × 10⁻⁹ C? [3 marks]

R = 10 cm, distance from surface = 20 cm

r = R + 20 = 10 + 20 = 30 cm = 0.3 m (distance from centre)

E = kQ/r² = (9 × 10⁹ × 5 × 10⁻⁹) / (0.3)²

E = 45/0.09 = 500 N/C

P7. How much work is done in moving 3 μC charge from a point at 20 V to a point at 120 V? [2 marks]

q = 3 μC = 3 × 10⁻⁶ C

ΔV = 120 − 20 = 100 V

W = qΔV = 3 × 10⁻⁶ × 100 = 3 × 10⁻⁴ J = 0.3 mJ

P8. A parallel plate capacitor with air has capacitance 10 pF. If a dielectric of constant 4 is inserted, find the new capacitance. [2 marks]

C₀ = 10 pF, κ = 4

C' = κC₀ = 4 × 10 = 40 pF

Capacitance increases 4 times!

Quick Revision — Unit Summary 🎯

Last-Minute Revision Points

🔵 Charge & Coulomb's Law

  • Charge: +ve and −ve. Like repel, unlike attract.
  • q = ne, e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
  • F = kq₁q₂/r², k = 9 × 10⁹ Nm²/C² 🔥
  • 1 Coulomb = very large charge. Practical: μC, nC

🔵 Electric Field

  • E = F/q₀ = kQ/r² (vector, unit: N/C or V/m)
  • F = qE (force on charge in field)
  • +Q: field outward. −Q: field inward

🔵 Electric Lines of Force

  • Originate from + charge, end on − charge
  • Never cross each other
  • Closer = stronger field
  • Perpendicular to conductor surface. E = 0 inside conductor.

🔵 Electric Flux

  • Φ = EA cos θ. Max when θ = 0° (surface ⊥ E)
  • Unit: Nm²/C

🔵 Electric Potential

  • V = W/q = kQ/r (scalar, unit: Volt)
  • 1 Volt = 1 J/C
  • 1 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J (unit of energy!)
  • E = −dV/dr (field = negative gradient of potential)

🔵 Gauss's Law

  • Φ = qenclosed/ε₀ 🔥
  • Charged sphere: E = 0 (inside), kQ/R² (surface), kQ/r² (outside)
  • Outside → point charge behavior

🔵 Capacitors

  • C = Q/V (unit: Farad). 1 μF = 10⁻⁶ F
  • Parallel plate: C = ε₀εᵣA/d
  • Series: 1/C = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ (Ceq < smallest) 🔥🔥
  • Parallel: C = C₁ + C₂ (Ceq > largest) 🔥🔥
  • Opposite to resistors! Don't confuse!
  • Energy: U = ½CV² = ½QV = Q²/2C

🔵 Dielectric

  • κ = C'/C₀ (dimensionless, ≥1)
  • Inserting dielectric → C increases by factor κ
  • Breakdown: insulator → conductor when E exceeds dielectric strength
  • Lightning = breakdown of air
Don't Forget on Exam Day!
  • ✅ k = 9 × 10⁹ (not 10⁻⁹!)
  • ✅ Convert μC → C (× 10⁻⁶) and cm → m (× 10⁻²)
  • ✅ Capacitors: Series → reciprocal formula, Parallel → add
  • ✅ Opposite to resistors! (This is the #1 mistake)
  • ✅ E = 0 inside a conductor (Gauss's law application)
  • ✅ V is scalar (no direction), E is vector (has direction)
  • ✅ Energy = ½CV² (don't forget the ½)
  • ✅ eV is a unit of ENERGY, not voltage
  • ✅ Draw diagrams — field lines, capacitor circuits. They get marks!
  • ✅ Always write UNITS with final answers!
