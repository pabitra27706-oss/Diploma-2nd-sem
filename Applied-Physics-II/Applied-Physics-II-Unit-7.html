Bohr’s Atom Model ⭐🔥

Why Bohr's Model Was Needed

Before Bohr, Rutherford gave the nuclear model of atom. According to Rutherford, electrons revolve around the nucleus just like planets revolve around the Sun. But this model had a serious problem.

According to classical electromagnetic theory, an accelerating charged particle should continuously radiate energy. Since an electron moving in a circular orbit is accelerating, it should lose energy continuously and finally fall into the nucleus. If that happened, atoms would collapse. But atoms are stable in reality. So Rutherford’s model could not explain atomic stability.

Why Bohr Model Became Important

Bohr solved the stability problem by introducing the idea of stationary orbits or quantized energy levels. This was a revolutionary step in modern physics.

Definition — Bohr’s Atom Model

Bohr’s atomic model states that electrons revolve around the nucleus only in certain permitted circular orbits of fixed energy called stationary orbits or energy levels, and they do not radiate energy while moving in those allowed orbits.

Postulates of Bohr’s Atomic Model 🔥

1. Central Nucleus

The atom has a small, dense, positively charged nucleus at the center. Almost all the mass of the atom is concentrated in the nucleus.

2. Electrons Revolve Around Nucleus

Electrons revolve around the nucleus in circular orbits under the electrostatic force of attraction.

3. Stationary Orbits

Electrons can revolve only in certain fixed permitted orbits called stationary orbits. In these orbits, they do not emit or absorb energy.

4. Quantization of Angular Momentum

The angular momentum of an electron in a permitted orbit is quantized and is given by: mvr = nh / 2π, where n = 1, 2, 3...

5. Radiation During Transition

An electron emits or absorbs radiation only when it jumps from one orbit to another orbit.

6. Frequency of Radiation

The frequency of emitted or absorbed radiation is given by: hν = E2 − E1

mvr = nh2π

Where:

  • m = mass of electron
  • v = velocity of electron
  • r = radius of orbit
  • n = principal quantum number = 1, 2, 3, ...
  • h = Planck’s constant = 6.63 × 10−34 J·s

Radius of Bohr Orbit

For hydrogen atom, the radius of the nth orbit is given by:

Radius of nth Orbit
rn = n2 a0

Where:

  • rn = radius of nth orbit
  • n = orbit number
  • a0 = radius of first Bohr orbit = 0.529 Å = 5.29 × 10−11 m

So:

  • r1 = 1² × 0.529 Å = 0.529 Å
  • r2 = 4 × 0.529 Å = 2.116 Å
  • r3 = 9 × 0.529 Å = 4.761 Å

Energy of Electron in nth Orbit

Definition — Energy Level

The fixed amount of energy possessed by an electron in a permitted orbit is called an energy level.

Energy of nth Orbit
En = −13.6n2 eV

Where:

  • En = energy of electron in nth orbit
  • n = principal quantum number

Examples:

  • E1 = −13.6 eV
  • E2 = −3.4 eV
  • E3 = −1.51 eV
Why is Energy Negative?

The energy is negative because the electron is bound to the nucleus. To remove the electron completely from the atom, energy must be supplied from outside. At infinite distance from the nucleus, the energy is taken as zero.

Emission and Absorption of Radiation

An electron can move from one orbit to another by absorbing or emitting energy.

  • If electron goes from lower orbit to higher orbit → it absorbs energy
  • If electron goes from higher orbit to lower orbit → it emits energy
Energy Difference and Frequency
hν = E2 − E1

Where:

  • h = Planck’s constant
  • ν = frequency of emitted or absorbed radiation
  • E2, E1 = energies of two levels

If E2 > E1, then absorption occurs. If electron falls from E2 to E1, emission occurs.

Ionization & Excitation Potentials ⭐🔥

Ground State and Excited State

Ground State

The lowest energy state of an atom is called the ground state. In hydrogen atom, the ground state corresponds to n = 1 and energy = −13.6 eV.

Excited State

When an atom absorbs energy and an electron jumps from the ground state to a higher energy level, the atom is said to be in an excited state.

Excitation Potential

Definition — Excitation Potential

Excitation Potential is the minimum potential difference required to excite an atom from its ground state to a higher energy state without removing the electron completely from the atom.

Simple Meaning

If we give a small amount of energy to an atom, its electron can jump to a higher orbit. The minimum voltage needed for this jump is called the excitation potential.

Excitation Energy and Excitation Potential
eVexc = E2 − E1
Vexc = E2 − E1e

In electron volt system, numerically:

Vexc (in volt) = Excitation energy (in eV)

Example: First Excitation Potential of Hydrogen

For hydrogen atom:

  • Energy of first orbit, E1 = −13.6 eV
  • Energy of second orbit, E2 = −3.4 eV
Calculation
Excitation energy = E2 − E1 = (−3.4) − (−13.6)
= 10.2 eV
First excitation potential of hydrogen = 10.2 V

Ionization Potential

Definition — Ionization Potential

Ionization Potential is the minimum potential difference required to remove the most loosely bound electron completely from an isolated gaseous atom in its ground state to infinity.

Simple Meaning

Excitation means the electron is still inside the atom but goes to a higher orbit. Ionization means the electron is completely removed from the atom. Then the atom becomes an ion.

Ionization Energy and Ionization Potential
eVion = 0 − E1

Because energy at infinity is taken as zero.

Vion (in volt) = Ionization energy (in eV)

Example: Ionization Potential of Hydrogen

For hydrogen atom, ground state energy:

E1 = −13.6 eV
Ionization energy = 0 − (−13.6) = 13.6 eV
Ionization potential of hydrogen = 13.6 V

Common Mistake

Do not write that excitation potential and ionization potential are the same. They are different. Excitation only raises the electron to a higher orbit. Ionization removes it completely.

Must Remember
  • First excitation potential of hydrogen = 10.2 V
  • Ionization potential of hydrogen = 13.6 V
  • Ionization potential is always greater than excitation potential

X-Rays — Production, Types and Uses ⭐🔥

Introduction to X-Rays

X-rays were discovered in 1895 by the German scientist Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen. They are invisible electromagnetic waves of very short wavelength and very high frequency.

Definition — X-Rays

X-rays are electromagnetic radiations of very short wavelength (about 10−8 m to 10−12 m) produced when high-speed electrons strike a heavy metal target or when inner shell electrons of atoms undergo transitions.

Properties of X-Rays

  • They are electromagnetic waves
  • They travel with the speed of light
  • They have very short wavelength and high penetrating power
  • They are electrically neutral, so they are not deflected by electric or magnetic fields
  • They affect photographic plates
  • They can ionize gases
  • They produce fluorescence in certain substances
  • They can damage living tissues in large doses

Production of X-Rays — Coolidge Tube ⭐🔥

Coolidge Tube

Coolidge tube is a modern X-ray tube used for producing X-rays. It consists of a heated cathode that emits electrons and a heavy metal target (anode) on which these electrons strike to produce X-rays.

Construction of Coolidge Tube

  • A highly evacuated glass tube
  • Cathode: Tungsten filament in a focusing cup
  • Anode / Target: Heavy metal like tungsten, molybdenum or platinum
  • High voltage supply between cathode and anode
  • Cooling arrangement to remove heat from target

Fig. 7.5: Coolidge tube showing cathode filament, focusing cup, anode target, high voltage supply, and X-ray output

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Working of Coolidge Tube

Step-by-Step Working
  1. When current passes through the tungsten filament, it becomes hot and emits electrons by thermionic emission.
  2. The focusing cup directs these electrons towards the target.
  3. A high potential difference (in kV) is applied between cathode and anode.
  4. The electrons are accelerated to very high speed towards the heavy metal target.
  5. When these high-speed electrons strike the target:
    • Most of their energy converts into heat
    • A small part converts into X-rays
  6. The emitted X-rays come out through a suitable window in the tube.
Important Fact

About 99% energy becomes heat and only about 1% energy becomes X-rays. That is why heavy cooling of the target is necessary.

Continuous X-Rays (Bremsstrahlung)

Continuous X-Rays

Continuous X-rays are produced when high-speed electrons are suddenly decelerated on striking the target. Because the electrons can lose different amounts of energy, a continuous spectrum of X-rays is obtained.

The German word Bremsstrahlung means “braking radiation”.

Why Continuous?

Different electrons lose different amounts of energy during collision. So the emitted X-rays have different wavelengths, forming a continuous range.

Characteristic X-Rays

Characteristic X-Rays

Characteristic X-rays are produced when an incoming high-speed electron knocks out an inner shell electron of the target atom. Then an electron from a higher shell falls into the vacant lower shell and emits X-ray photons of definite energies.

Since the energy levels depend on the target material, the wavelengths are characteristic of that material. Hence they are called characteristic X-rays.

Important X-Ray Formula

Duane-Hunt Law

The minimum wavelength of X-rays produced in an X-ray tube is given by:

λmin = hceV

Where:

  • λmin = minimum wavelength
  • h = Planck’s constant
  • c = speed of light
  • e = electronic charge
  • V = accelerating voltage

This formula shows that higher accelerating voltage gives smaller wavelength X-rays.

Laser Basics — Spontaneous & Stimulated Emission ⭐🔥

What is LASER?

Full Form of LASER

LASER stands for Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation.

Definition — Laser

A laser is a device that produces a highly intense, highly directional, monochromatic and coherent beam of light by the process of stimulated emission.

Why Laser is Different from Ordinary Light

Ordinary light from a bulb spreads in all directions, contains many colours (wavelengths), and the light waves are not in step with each other. Laser light is very special:

  • It goes in one direction
  • It has one colour / one wavelength nearly
  • Its waves are in phase
  • It is very intense

Energy Levels and Radiation Processes

To understand laser action, we must first understand how atoms emit radiation. Consider an atom having two energy levels:

  • Lower energy level: E1
  • Higher energy level: E2

If an electron is in the higher level, it can come down to the lower level by releasing energy in the form of a photon.

Energy of Emitted Photon
hν = E2 − E1

Where:

  • h = Planck's constant
  • ν = frequency of emitted radiation
  • E2 = upper energy level
  • E1 = lower energy level

Spontaneous Emission

Definition — Spontaneous Emission

Spontaneous emission is the process in which an excited atom returns to a lower energy state on its own and emits a photon without any external influence.

When an atom is in the excited state, it cannot remain there forever. After a short time (called lifetime), it comes back to a lower state and emits radiation automatically.

Characteristics of Spontaneous Emission:

  • Occurs naturally on its own
  • Emitted photons go in random directions
  • Photons have random phase
  • Not suitable for producing laser light
  • Ordinary light sources like bulb and candle mainly work due to spontaneous emission

Stimulated Emission ⭐🔥

Definition — Stimulated Emission

Stimulated emission is the process in which an excited atom, when struck by a photon of proper energy, is forced to return to a lower energy state and emits a second photon identical to the first photon.

This is the most important process in laser action.

What happens in stimulated emission?

  1. An atom is already in the excited state
  2. A photon of energy hν = E2 − E1 approaches the atom
  3. This photon forces the excited atom to return to the lower state
  4. The atom emits another photon
  5. The emitted photon is exactly identical to the incident photon

The two photons are identical in:

  • Frequency
  • Wavelength
  • Phase
  • Direction
  • Speed
  • Polarization
Why Stimulated Emission is Important

Because the emitted photon is exactly identical to the incident photon, the number of identical photons increases. This causes amplification of light. This is the basic principle of LASER.

Absorption

Definition — Absorption

Absorption is the process in which an atom in the lower energy state absorbs a photon of proper energy and moves to a higher energy state.

Condition for Absorption
hν = E2 − E1

Population Inversion

Definition — Population Inversion

Population inversion is the condition in which the number of atoms in the excited state becomes greater than the number of atoms in the lower energy state.

Normally, most atoms stay in the lower energy state. For laser action, we need more atoms in the excited state than in the lower state. This special condition is called population inversion.

Why Population Inversion is Necessary

If most atoms are in the lower level, incoming photons will be mostly absorbed. But if more atoms are in the excited state, then stimulated emission will dominate and light amplification will take place.

Pumping

Definition — Pumping

Pumping is the process of supplying energy to the active medium to achieve population inversion.

Methods of pumping include:

  • Optical pumping
  • Electrical discharge pumping
  • Chemical pumping
  • Current injection pumping

Active Medium and Resonator

Active Medium

The material in which population inversion is created and laser action takes place is called the active medium.

Resonator

The arrangement of two mirrors placed at the ends of the active medium to reflect photons back and forth for amplification is called the optical resonator.

Properties of Laser Light ⭐🔥

1. Monochromatic

Laser light has almost a single wavelength or single colour. Ordinary light has many wavelengths.

2. Coherent

The waves of laser light are in the same phase. This means the crests and troughs move together.

3. Highly Directional

Laser beam travels in a very narrow path and spreads very little.

4. Highly Intense

A large amount of energy is concentrated in a very small area, so the intensity is very high.

5. Polarized

Laser light is often polarized, which means vibrations occur in a particular plane.

6. Can Travel Long Distance

Because of low divergence, laser light can travel long distances without spreading much.

Applications of Laser in General
  1. Medical surgery (eye surgery, dental treatment, cancer therapy)
  2. Industrial cutting, welding and drilling
  3. Optical fiber communication
  4. Barcode readers
  5. CD/DVD players
  6. Range finding and target designation
  7. Holography
  8. Printing and scanning devices

He-Ne Laser — Construction, Working & Applications ⭐🔥

Definition — He-Ne Laser

He-Ne laser is a gas laser in which the active medium is a mixture of helium and neon gases. It generally produces red laser light of wavelength 632.8 nm.

Construction of He-Ne Laser

The He-Ne laser consists of the following parts:

  • A narrow glass discharge tube containing a mixture of helium and neon gases
  • Gas pressure is kept low
  • Electrodes connected to a high voltage power supply
  • Two mirrors at the ends forming an optical resonator:
    • One mirror is fully reflecting
    • The other is partially reflecting

Fig. 7.11: Construction of He-Ne laser showing discharge tube, electrodes, helium-neon gas mixture, and mirrors

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Working of He-Ne Laser

Step-by-Step Working
  1. An electric discharge is passed through the gas mixture.
  2. Helium atoms get excited to higher metastable energy states.
  3. These excited helium atoms collide with neon atoms.
  4. During collision, helium transfers its energy to neon atoms because their energy levels are nearly equal.
  5. Thus neon atoms get excited to higher energy states and population inversion is achieved in neon.
  6. Now stimulated emission starts in neon atoms.
  7. Photons move back and forth between the two mirrors, causing amplification.
  8. Finally, a part of the light escapes through the partially reflecting mirror as the laser beam.

Fig. 7.12: Energy level diagram of He-Ne laser showing energy transfer from helium to neon and laser transition

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Important Working Idea

In He-Ne laser, helium atoms help in exciting neon atoms. Neon is the actual laser-active atom producing laser radiation, while helium helps in pumping.

Elementary Characteristics of He-Ne Laser

  • Produces continuous laser beam
  • Common output wavelength = 632.8 nm (red colour)
  • Highly monochromatic
  • Highly coherent
  • Highly directional
  • Output power is usually low
  • Very stable beam
Wavelength of Common He-Ne Laser
λ = 632.8 nm = 632.8 × 10−9 m

Applications of He-Ne Laser

Practical Applications
  1. Holography

Fiber Optics — Introduction, Propagation & Applications ⭐🔥

Introduction to Optical Fiber

Definition — Optical Fiber

An optical fiber is a thin, flexible, transparent fiber made of glass or plastic through which light can travel from one end to the other by the phenomenon of total internal reflection.

Optical fibers are used to transmit information in the form of light pulses. They form the backbone of modern telecommunication systems.

Simple Analogy

Think of an optical fiber like a smooth pipe for light. Just as water flows through a pipe, light pulses travel through the fiber. But instead of physical walls forcing the light to move, the light is trapped inside by total internal reflection.

Basic Structure of Optical Fiber

  • Core: Central inner part through which light travels
  • Cladding: Surrounding layer having slightly lower refractive index than the core
  • Protective coating / jacket: Outer layer to protect the fiber from damage
Key Condition

For optical fiber to work properly:

ncore > ncladding

The refractive index of the core must be greater than that of the cladding.

Mechanism of Light Propagation — Total Internal Reflection ⭐🔥

The working of optical fiber is based on total internal reflection (TIR).

Total Internal Reflection

When a ray of light travels from a denser medium to a rarer medium and the angle of incidence is greater than the critical angle, the light is reflected completely back into the denser medium. This is called total internal reflection.

Conditions for Total Internal Reflection:

  1. Light must travel from denser medium to rarer medium
  2. Angle of incidence must be greater than critical angle

In an optical fiber:

  • Core acts as denser medium
  • Cladding acts as rarer medium
  • Light ray strikes the core-cladding boundary at angle greater than critical angle
  • So it is totally internally reflected and continues to move through the fiber

Acceptance Angle

Acceptance Angle

The acceptance angle is the maximum angle with the fiber axis at which light can enter the fiber and still travel through it by total internal reflection.

If the incident angle is greater than the acceptance angle, the light will not remain trapped inside the fiber.

Numerical Aperture (NA) ⭐🔥

Definition — Numerical Aperture

Numerical aperture (NA) is the light gathering ability of an optical fiber. It determines how much light can enter the fiber and be guided through it.

Numerical Aperture Formula
NA = sin θa = √(n12 − n22)

Where:

  • θa = acceptance angle
  • n1 = refractive index of core
  • n2 = refractive index of cladding
Acceptance Angle from NA
θa = sin−1(NA)
Meaning of NA

Higher numerical aperture means the fiber can accept light over a wider angle. So it can collect more light easily.

Nanoscience & Nanotechnology — Introduction ⭐

What Does “Nano” Mean?

The word nano means one-billionth.

Nano Scale
1 nanometer (1 nm) = 10−9 m

To understand how small this is:

  • Human hair diameter ≈ 80,000 to 100,000 nm
  • Red blood cell ≈ 7000 nm
  • DNA width ≈ 2 nm
Definition — Nanoscience

Nanoscience is the study of materials, structures, and devices at the nanoscale, usually between 1 nm and 100 nm.

Definition — Nanotechnology

Nanotechnology is the science and technology of designing, producing, and using materials and devices by controlling matter at the nanoscale.

Why Nano Materials Behave Differently

When the size of a material becomes extremely small, its properties can change. At nanoscale:

  • Surface area becomes very large
  • Quantum effects become important
  • Mechanical, electrical, optical and chemical properties may change

For example, a bulk material may be dull, but its nanoparticles may show bright colours or higher reactivity.

Important Definitions 📝

1. Bohr’s Atomic Model

The model which states that electrons revolve around the nucleus only in certain fixed circular orbits of definite energy called stationary orbits.

2. Ground State

The lowest energy state of an atom is called the ground state.

3. Excited State

The higher energy state of an atom obtained when an electron absorbs energy and jumps to a higher orbit.

4. Excitation Potential

The minimum potential difference required to raise an electron from ground state to a higher energy state without removing it from the atom.

5. Ionization Potential

The minimum potential difference required to remove an electron completely from an isolated gaseous atom in its ground state.

6. X-Rays

Electromagnetic radiations of very short wavelength and high frequency produced when high-speed electrons strike a heavy metal target.

7. Continuous X-Rays

X-rays produced due to sudden deceleration of high-speed electrons on striking the target. They form a continuous spectrum.

8. Characteristic X-Rays

X-rays of definite wavelength emitted when electrons from higher shells fill vacancies in lower shells of target atoms.

9. Spontaneous Emission

The emission of radiation by an excited atom on its own without any external cause.

10. Stimulated Emission

The emission of radiation by an excited atom under the influence of an incident photon of proper energy.

11. Population Inversion

The condition in which the number of atoms in the excited state is greater than the number in the lower state.

12. Laser

A device that produces a highly intense, monochromatic, coherent and directional beam of light by stimulated emission.

13. Optical Fiber

A thin transparent fiber made of glass or plastic through which light travels by total internal reflection.

14. Acceptance Angle

The maximum angle at which light can enter the fiber and still propagate through it by total internal reflection.

15. Numerical Aperture

The light gathering ability of an optical fiber.

16. Nanoscience

The study of structures and materials in the size range of about 1 to 100 nm.

17. Nanotechnology

The science and technology of designing and using materials and devices by controlling matter at nanoscale.

Long Answer Questions (5–7 Marks Style) 📄

Q 7.26

State and explain the postulates of Bohr’s atomic model.

Bohr’s atomic model was proposed to explain the stability of atom and the line spectrum of hydrogen.

Postulates:

  1. The atom has a small, dense, positively charged nucleus at the center.
  2. Electrons revolve around the nucleus in certain permitted circular orbits.
  3. These orbits are called stationary orbits. While moving in these orbits, electrons do not radiate energy.
  4. The angular momentum of electron is quantized:
    mvr = nh / 2π
  5. Electrons emit or absorb radiation only when they jump from one orbit to another.
  6. The frequency of emitted or absorbed radiation is given by:
    hν = E2 − E1

This model explained atomic stability and the hydrogen spectrum successfully.

Q 7.27

Explain excitation potential and ionization potential. Distinguish between them.

Excitation potential is the minimum potential difference required to move an electron from ground state to a higher orbit without removing it from the atom.

Ionization potential is the minimum potential difference required to remove the electron completely from the atom.

Difference:

  • Excitation → electron remains inside atom; Ionization → electron leaves atom
  • Excitation requires less energy; Ionization requires more energy
  • Excitation leads to excited state; Ionization leads to ion formation

For hydrogen:

  • First excitation potential = 10.2 V
  • Ionization potential = 13.6 V

Q 7.28

Describe the construction and working of Coolidge tube.

Coolidge tube is a modern X-ray tube used to produce X-rays.

Construction:

  • A highly evacuated glass tube
  • Tungsten filament as cathode inside focusing cup
  • Heavy metal target like tungsten as anode
  • High voltage supply between cathode and anode
  • Cooling arrangement

Working:

  1. The filament is heated and emits electrons by thermionic emission.
  2. High voltage accelerates these electrons towards the target.
  3. When electrons strike the target, they are suddenly decelerated.
  4. A small part of their kinetic energy is converted into X-rays and most into heat.
  5. X-rays come out through the window of the tube.

Coolidge tube produces both continuous and characteristic X-rays.

Q 7.29

What are X-rays? Explain continuous and characteristic X-rays. Also distinguish between soft and hard X-rays.

X-rays are electromagnetic radiations of very short wavelength and high frequency.

Continuous X-rays: Produced due to sudden deceleration of high-speed electrons striking the target. They form a continuous spectrum and are also called bremsstrahlung.

Characteristic X-rays: Produced when inner shell electrons of target atoms are knocked out and outer electrons fall into the vacancy, emitting photons of definite energies.

Soft X-rays: Longer wavelength, lower energy, lower penetrating power.

Hard X-rays: Shorter wavelength, higher energy, greater penetrating power.

Q 7.30

Explain spontaneous emission and stimulated emission. Why is stimulated emission important for laser action?

Spontaneous emission occurs when an excited atom returns to lower state on its own and emits a photon. The emitted photons have random phase and direction.

Stimulated emission occurs when an excited atom is struck by an incident photon of proper energy and emits a second identical photon.

Importance:

  • The emitted photon is identical to the incident photon
  • Both photons move in same direction and phase
  • This causes amplification of light
  • Hence stimulated emission is the basic principle of laser action

Q 7.31

What is laser? Write the properties and applications of laser light.

LASER means Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. It is a device that produces a highly intense, coherent, monochromatic, and directional beam of light.

Properties:

  • Monochromatic
  • Coherent
  • Highly directional
  • Highly intense
  • Often polarized

Applications:

  • Medical surgery
  • Cutting and welding
  • Optical communication
  • Barcode reading
  • Holography
  • Scientific measurement

Q 7.32

Describe the construction, working and applications of He-Ne laser.

He-Ne laser is a gas laser containing a mixture of helium and neon gases.

Construction:

  • Glass discharge tube containing helium-neon gas mixture
  • Electrodes with high voltage supply
  • Two mirrors at ends, one fully reflecting and one partially reflecting

Working:

  1. Electric discharge excites helium atoms.
  2. Excited helium transfers energy to neon atoms by collision.
  3. Neon atoms get excited and population inversion is achieved.
  4. Stimulated emission occurs in neon.
  5. Amplified light emerges through partially reflecting mirror.

Applications: Holography, alignment, interferometry, laboratory use, barcode scanning.

Q 7.33

Explain the construction and working principle of optical fiber. Mention its applications.

An optical fiber is a thin transparent glass or plastic fiber through which light travels by total internal reflection.

Construction:

  • Core of refractive index n1
  • Cladding of refractive index n2
  • Protective outer coating

For proper working: n1 > n2.

Working Principle:

  • Light enters the core within the acceptance angle
  • It strikes the core-cladding interface at angle greater than critical angle
  • Thus it suffers total internal reflection repeatedly
  • So the light travels through the fiber

Applications: Telecommunication, medical endoscopy, industrial inspection, sensors, internet communication, undersea cables.
