Energy Bands in Solids ⭐🔥

Starting from the Atom

To understand energy bands, let's first recall what happens inside a single atom.

In a single isolated atom, electrons revolve around the nucleus in definite orbits (shells). Each orbit has a fixed energy. These fixed energies are called energy levels.

Key Concept — Energy Levels in an Atom

Think of energy levels like stairs in a building. An electron can stand on stair 1, stair 2, stair 3, etc., but it cannot stand between two stairs. Similarly, electrons can exist at specific energy levels but NOT between them.

The gaps between energy levels where NO electron can exist are called forbidden gaps.

From Energy Levels to Energy Bands

Now, in a solid, billions and billions of atoms are packed very closely together. When atoms come very close to each other, their electron orbits start to overlap and interact with each other.

Due to this interaction, a single energy level of an isolated atom splits into a large number of closely spaced energy levels. These closely spaced energy levels form a continuous band of energy — called an Energy Band.

Definition — Energy Band

Energy Band: When a large number of atoms are brought close together in a solid, the discrete energy levels of individual atoms merge and form a continuous range of energies called an energy band.

Simple Analogy

Imagine one person singing a note — you hear one clear sound (one energy level). Now imagine 1 million people singing the same note — their voices slightly differ, and you hear a band of sound instead of one note. This is how energy levels become energy bands!

Three Important Energy Bands

In any solid material, three energy bands are of great importance:

⬆️ CONDUCTION BAND (CB) — Free electrons here
🚫 FORBIDDEN GAP (Eg) — No electrons allowed
⬇️ VALENCE BAND (VB) — Bonded electrons here

1. Valence Band (VB)

Definition — Valence Band

Valence Band: The energy band formed by the energy levels of the outermost shell (valence shell) electrons is called the valence band. These electrons are involved in chemical bonding and are tightly bound to the atoms.

  • Contains the valence electrons (outermost electrons)
  • At absolute zero temperature (0 K), this band is completely filled
  • Electrons in this band are not free to move — they are bound to atoms
  • This band is the highest filled energy band

2. Conduction Band (CB)

Definition — Conduction Band

Conduction Band: The energy band just above the valence band is called the conduction band. Electrons in this band are free to move throughout the solid and can conduct electric current.

  • Contains free electrons that can carry current
  • At absolute zero, this band is empty in semiconductors and insulators
  • When electrons gain enough energy, they jump from VB to CB
  • More electrons in CB = better conductor

3. Forbidden Energy Gap (Eg)

Definition — Forbidden Energy Gap

Forbidden Energy Gap (Eg): The energy difference between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band is called the forbidden energy gap or band gap. No electron can exist with energy in this range.

  • It is measured in electron volts (eV)
  • 1 eV = 1.6 × 10−19 Joules
  • The size of Eg determines whether a material is a conductor, semiconductor, or insulator
  • Larger Eg → harder for electrons to jump → poorer conductor
⭐ Exam Important

The forbidden energy gap (Eg) is the key factor that decides the electrical behaviour of a solid. This concept is the foundation of the entire unit. Remember:

  • Large Eg → Insulator (e.g., Diamond: Eg ≈ 6 eV)
  • Small Eg → Semiconductor (e.g., Silicon: Eg ≈ 1.1 eV, Germanium: Eg ≈ 0.7 eV)
  • Zero or overlapping bands → Conductor (e.g., Copper, Silver)

Students often confuse: "electrons cannot exist IN the forbidden gap" does NOT mean there is a physical gap/space. It means electrons cannot have energy VALUES that fall in this range. The forbidden gap is an energy gap, not a physical gap.

Types of Materials — Conductor, Semiconductor, Insulator ⭐🔥

Based on the size of the forbidden energy gap (Eg) and the electrical conductivity, solid materials are classified into three types:

Conductor

Eg ≈ 0 eV (bands overlap)

VB and CB overlap — no forbidden gap. Plenty of free electrons available at room temperature.

Examples: Copper, Silver, Gold, Aluminium, Iron

Conductivity: Very high (104 to 107 S/m)

Semiconductor

Eg ≈ 0.1 to 3 eV (small gap)

Small forbidden gap. At room temperature, some electrons can jump from VB to CB.

Examples: Silicon (1.1 eV), Germanium (0.7 eV), GaAs

Conductivity: Moderate (10−6 to 104 S/m)

Insulator

Eg > 3 eV (large gap)

Very large forbidden gap. Electrons cannot cross to CB at normal temperatures.

Examples: Diamond (6 eV), Glass, Rubber, Mica, Wood

Conductivity: Very low (10−20 to 10−10 S/m)

Key Point for Exam

Why does conductivity of semiconductors INCREASE with temperature?

When temperature increases, more thermal energy is available. This energy helps electrons in the valence band to jump across the forbidden gap into the conduction band. More electrons in CB = more current carriers = higher conductivity.

This is the opposite of conductors, where increased temperature causes more atomic vibrations, which obstruct electron flow, reducing conductivity.

Remember This!

🔥 "Semiconductor's conductivity increases with temperature" — This is asked in almost every WBSCTE exam in some form!

💡 Mnemonic: "Semi gets better when hot, Metal gets worse when hot"

Special Properties of Semiconductors

Semiconductors have some unique properties that make them incredibly useful in electronics:

  1. Temperature sensitivity: Conductivity increases with temperature
  2. Doping ability: Adding tiny amounts of impurities can dramatically change conductivity
  3. Light sensitivity: Light can create free electrons (used in solar cells, photocells)
  4. Controlled conduction: Current flow can be precisely controlled (used in transistors)
  5. One-way conduction: When made into p-n junctions, they allow current in only one direction (diodes)

Intrinsic & Extrinsic Semiconductors ⭐🔥

Intrinsic Semiconductor (Pure Semiconductor)

Definition — Intrinsic Semiconductor

Intrinsic Semiconductor: A semiconductor in its pure form, without any added impurity, is called an intrinsic semiconductor. Examples: Pure Silicon (Si), Pure Germanium (Ge).

Properties of Intrinsic Semiconductors:

  • Made of only one type of atom (e.g., only Si atoms or only Ge atoms)
  • Each atom has 4 valence electrons and forms 4 covalent bonds with neighboring atoms
  • At absolute zero (0 K), all covalent bonds are intact — no free electrons — acts like an insulator
  • At room temperature, some covalent bonds break due to thermal energy
  • When a bond breaks, it creates a free electron and a hole (vacancy left by the electron)
  • Number of free electrons (ne) = Number of holes (nh)
What is a Hole?

When an electron breaks free from a covalent bond, it leaves behind an empty space. This empty space behaves like a positive charge and is called a hole.

Analogy: Imagine a row of chairs in a cinema hall, all occupied. If one person (electron) gets up and moves, an empty chair (hole) is created. The next person can move into that empty chair, creating a new empty chair at their old position. The empty chair appears to "move" in the opposite direction!

Similarly, holes appear to move in the opposite direction to electrons and carry positive charge.

In an intrinsic semiconductor:

ne = nh = ni

Where:

  • ne = number of free electrons per unit volume
  • nh = number of holes per unit volume
  • ni = intrinsic carrier concentration
Limitation of Intrinsic Semiconductors

Pure semiconductors have very few charge carriers at room temperature, so their conductivity is too low for practical use in electronic circuits. That's why we add impurities — this process is called DOPING.

Extrinsic Semiconductor (Impure/Doped Semiconductor) 🔥

Definition — Extrinsic Semiconductor

Extrinsic Semiconductor: When a small, controlled amount of impurity is added to a pure (intrinsic) semiconductor to increase its conductivity, it is called an extrinsic semiconductor. The process of adding impurity is called doping.

Definition — Doping

Doping: The process of deliberately adding a small amount of suitable impurity to a pure semiconductor to increase its electrical conductivity is called doping. The added impurity is called a dopant.

There are two types of extrinsic semiconductors based on the type of impurity added:

N-Type Semiconductor

Impurity with 5 valence electrons (pentavalent)

Dopants: Phosphorus (P), Arsenic (As), Antimony (Sb), Bismuth (Bi)

Majority carriers: Electrons

Minority carriers: Holes

P-Type Semiconductor

Impurity with 3 valence electrons (trivalent)

Dopants: Boron (B), Aluminium (Al), Gallium (Ga), Indium (In)

Majority carriers: Holes

Minority carriers: Electrons

🔵 N-Type Semiconductor — Detailed Explanation

When a pentavalent impurity (5 valence electrons) like Phosphorus is added to pure Silicon:

  1. The impurity atom replaces a Silicon atom in the crystal lattice
  2. It forms 4 covalent bonds with 4 neighboring Silicon atoms using 4 of its 5 valence electrons
  3. The 5th electron is extra — it is loosely bound and becomes a free electron easily
  4. Each pentavalent impurity atom donates one free electron, so these impurities are called DONOR impurities
  5. The free electrons donated by impurities are much more than the thermally generated electron-hole pairs
Why "N-Type"?

"N" stands for Negative because the majority charge carriers are electrons (which carry negative charge). However, the material itself is electrically neutral — it is NOT negatively charged!

🔴 P-Type Semiconductor — Detailed Explanation

When a trivalent impurity (3 valence electrons) like Boron is added to pure Silicon:

  1. The impurity atom replaces a Silicon atom in the crystal lattice
  2. It can form only 3 covalent bonds with neighboring Silicon atoms (since it has only 3 valence electrons)
  3. The 4th bond is incomplete — there is a vacancy or hole
  4. This hole can easily accept an electron from a nearby bond, so these impurities are called ACCEPTOR impurities
  5. Each trivalent impurity atom creates one hole → many extra holes are available
Why "P-Type"?

"P" stands for Positive because the majority charge carriers are holes (which behave like positive charges). However, the material itself is electrically neutral — it is NOT positively charged!

P-N Junction ⭐🔥

Definition — P-N Junction

P-N Junction: When a p-type semiconductor and an n-type semiconductor are joined together (in a single crystal), the contact surface between them is called a p-n junction.

How is a P-N Junction Formed?

A p-n junction is NOT formed by simply pressing two pieces together. It is formed by a special process where one half of a single semiconductor crystal is doped with p-type impurity and the other half is doped with n-type impurity.

Common methods: Diffusion process or Ion implantation

Formation of Depletion Layer

When a p-n junction is formed, the following happens at the junction:

  1. Diffusion: At the junction, electrons from the n-side (where they are majority) diffuse (move) to the p-side. Similarly, holes from the p-side diffuse to the n-side.
  2. Recombination: When electrons cross to the p-side, they recombine with holes (fill up the vacancies). When holes cross to n-side, they recombine with electrons.
  3. Uncovered ions: This recombination leaves behind:
    • Positive immobile ions on the n-side (donor atoms that lost their extra electrons)
    • Negative immobile ions on the p-side (acceptor atoms that gained electrons)
  4. Electric field: These immobile ions create an internal electric field pointing from n-side to p-side (from + ions to − ions)
  5. Barrier: This electric field opposes further diffusion of charge carriers. Eventually, equilibrium is reached.
Definition — Depletion Region

Depletion Region (Depletion Layer): The narrow region around the p-n junction that is depleted (emptied) of free charge carriers (both electrons and holes) and contains only immobile ions is called the depletion region or depletion layer.

Barrier Potential (Junction Potential)

Definition — Barrier Potential

Barrier Potential (VB): The potential difference that develops across the depletion region due to the immobile ions is called the barrier potential or junction potential or contact potential.

Typical Values of Barrier Potential
  • For Silicon (Si): VB ≈ 0.7 V
  • For Germanium (Ge): VB ≈ 0.3 V
Key Points about Depletion Layer
  • Width of depletion region is typically about 0.1 to 1 micrometer (very thin)
  • There are no free charge carriers in the depletion region
  • The depletion region acts as an insulator
  • The barrier potential opposes the flow of majority carriers across the junction
  • Barrier potential supports the flow of minority carriers
  • The depletion region is wider on the lightly doped side
Analogy for Understanding

Think of the depletion region like a hill between two valleys. Majority carriers (balls) need to be pushed (by external voltage) to go over the hill. The barrier potential is the height of the hill. If you push hard enough (apply voltage > VB), the balls roll over!

Junction Diode & V-I Characteristics ⭐🔥

Definition — Junction Diode

Junction Diode: A p-n junction with connecting leads and a protective casing is called a junction diode or simply a diode. It is a two-terminal electronic device that allows current to flow in only one direction.

Circuit Symbol of a Diode:

The diode symbol looks like an arrow pointing towards a vertical line (wall).

  • Anode (A): p-side — the arrow side — positive terminal
  • Cathode (K): n-side — the vertical line side — negative terminal
  • Current flows in the direction of the arrow (from Anode to Cathode)

Forward Bias

Definition — Forward Bias

Forward Bias: When the positive terminal of the battery is connected to the p-side and the negative terminal to the n-side of a p-n junction diode, the diode is said to be in forward bias.

What happens in Forward Bias?

  1. The external voltage pushes holes in p-side and electrons in n-side towards the junction
  2. This reduces the width of the depletion layer
  3. The barrier potential decreases
  4. When the applied voltage exceeds the barrier potential (> 0.7V for Si, > 0.3V for Ge), current starts flowing
  5. The resistance of the diode in forward bias is very low
  6. This voltage at which significant current starts flowing is called the knee voltage or threshold voltage or cut-in voltage
Knee Voltage / Cut-in Voltage
  • Silicon diode: Vknee ≈ 0.7 V
  • Germanium diode: Vknee ≈ 0.3 V

Reverse Bias

Definition — Reverse Bias

Reverse Bias: When the positive terminal of the battery is connected to the n-side and the negative terminal to the p-side of a p-n junction diode, the diode is said to be in reverse bias.

What happens in Reverse Bias?

  1. The external voltage pulls majority carriers away from the junction
  2. This increases the width of the depletion layer
  3. The barrier potential increases
  4. Practically no current flows due to majority carriers
  5. Only a very small current flows due to minority carriers — called reverse saturation current (Is) — typically in microamperes (μA)
  6. The resistance of the diode in reverse bias is very high
Reverse Breakdown

If the reverse voltage is increased beyond a certain limit, the depletion layer breaks down and a large reverse current flows suddenly. This voltage is called the breakdown voltage (VBR).

This can permanently damage a normal diode, but it is the working principle of Zener diodes (used for voltage regulation).

V-I Characteristics of a Junction Diode 🔥

Definition — V-I Characteristics

V-I Characteristics: The graph showing the relationship between the voltage applied across the diode (V) and the current flowing through it (I) is called the V-I characteristics (or current-voltage characteristics) of the diode.

Key Features of the V-I Curve:

Understanding the V-I Curve

Forward Bias Region (Right side of graph, 1st quadrant):

  • Initially, as voltage increases from 0, current is almost zero
  • At the knee voltage (0.7V for Si, 0.3V for Ge), current starts to increase
  • Beyond knee voltage, current increases rapidly (almost exponentially) with small increase in voltage
  • The forward current is measured in milliamperes (mA)
  • Forward resistance = very low (few ohms)

Reverse Bias Region (Left side of graph, 3rd quadrant):

  • As reverse voltage increases, only a very small reverse saturation current flows
  • This current remains nearly constant regardless of reverse voltage (until breakdown)
  • Reverse current is measured in microamperes (μA)
  • At breakdown voltage, current increases sharply
  • Reverse resistance = very high (mega-ohms)
⭐ Why Diode is Called a "Unidirectional Device"

A diode allows current to flow easily in one direction (forward bias — low resistance) and blocks current in the other direction (reverse bias — very high resistance). This is why it is called a unidirectional device.

This property makes diodes perfect for rectification — converting AC to DC.

Diode as Rectifier — Half Wave & Full Wave ⭐🔥

Definition — Rectifier

Rectifier: A rectifier is an electronic circuit that converts Alternating Current (AC) into Direct Current (DC). It uses the unidirectional property of a diode to achieve this conversion.

Why Do We Need Rectifiers?

The electricity supply at our homes is AC (Alternating Current) — it changes direction 50 times per second (50 Hz). But most electronic devices (mobile phones, laptops, LED bulbs, etc.) need DC (Direct Current) — which flows in one direction only.

Rectifiers bridge this gap by converting AC to DC. That's why every mobile charger has a rectifier inside!

Half Wave Rectifier 🔥

Definition — Half Wave Rectifier

Half Wave Rectifier: A rectifier circuit that converts only one half (positive half or negative half) of the AC input into DC output is called a half wave rectifier. It uses one diode.

Components:

  • Transformer: Steps down AC voltage to desired level
  • One Diode (D): Allows current in one direction only
  • Load Resistance (RL): The device being powered

Working:

Step-by-Step Working of Half Wave Rectifier

During Positive Half Cycle of AC Input:

  • The anode of the diode becomes positive with respect to cathode
  • The diode is forward biased
  • Current flows through the diode and through RL
  • Output voltage appears across RL

During Negative Half Cycle of AC Input:

  • The anode of the diode becomes negative with respect to cathode
  • The diode is reverse biased
  • No current flows through the diode
  • No output appears across RL (output = 0)

Result: Only the positive half cycles appear at the output. The output is a pulsating DC — it's DC (flows in one direction) but it's not smooth (it pulses on and off).

Key Formulas for Half Wave Rectifier:

Half Wave Rectifier Formulas

1. DC Output Voltage (VDC):

VDC = Vmπ = 0.318 × Vm

2. DC Output Current (IDC):

IDC = Imπ = 0.318 × Im

3. RMS Output Voltage (Vrms):

Vrms = Vm2 = 0.5 × Vm

4. Ripple Factor (γ):

γ = 1.21

5. Efficiency (η):

η = 40.6%

6. Output Frequency:

fout = fin

(Same as input frequency)

Where:

  • Vm = peak (maximum) value of AC input voltage
  • Im = peak value of current = Vm / (rf + RL)
  • rf = forward resistance of diode
  • RL = load resistance

Full Wave Rectifier (Centre-Tapped) 🔥

Definition — Full Wave Rectifier

Full Wave Rectifier: A rectifier circuit that converts both halves (positive and negative) of the AC input into DC output is called a full wave rectifier. The centre-tapped type uses two diodes and a centre-tapped transformer.

Components:

  • Centre-tapped transformer: Has a centre tap (midpoint) on the secondary winding, dividing it into two equal halves
  • Two Diodes (D1 and D2): Connected to the two ends of the secondary winding
  • Load Resistance (RL): Connected between the diode outputs and the centre tap

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Working:

Step-by-Step Working of Full Wave Rectifier

During Positive Half Cycle:

  • End A of transformer secondary is positive, end B is negative
  • Diode D1 is forward biased → conducts current
  • Diode D2 is reverse biased → does not conduct
  • Current flows: A → D1 → RL → Centre tap
  • Output appears across RL

During Negative Half Cycle:

  • End B of transformer secondary is positive, end A is negative
  • Diode D2 is forward biased → conducts current
  • Diode D1 is reverse biased → does not conduct
  • Current flows: B → D2 → RL → Centre tap
  • Output appears across RL (in the same direction as before!)

Result: During BOTH half cycles, current flows through RL in the same direction. The output is pulsating DC, but with no gaps — it utilizes both halves of the AC input.

Key Formulas for Full Wave Rectifier:

Full Wave Rectifier Formulas

1. DC Output Voltage (VDC):

VDC = 2Vmπ = 0.636 × Vm

2. DC Output Current (IDC):

IDC = 2Imπ = 0.636 × Im

3. RMS Output Voltage (Vrms):

Vrms = Vm√2 = 0.707 × Vm

4. Ripple Factor (γ):

γ = 0.48

5. Efficiency (η):

η = 81.2%

6. Output Frequency:

fout = 2 × fin

(Double the input frequency — because we get two pulses per cycle)

Definition — Ripple Factor

Ripple Factor (γ): The ripple factor is a measure of the AC component (ripple) present in the DC output of a rectifier. Lower ripple factor means smoother (purer) DC output.

γ = AC component (Vrms(ac))DC component (VDC)
Definition — PIV (Peak Inverse Voltage)

PIV: The maximum reverse voltage that appears across the diode during the non-conducting half cycle is called the Peak Inverse Voltage. The diode must be rated to withstand this voltage without breakdown.

Transistor — PNP & NPN ⭐🔥

Definition — Transistor

Transistor: A transistor is a three-terminal semiconductor device formed by sandwiching one type of semiconductor between two layers of the other type. The word "transistor" comes from Transfer + Resistor because it transfers a signal from a low-resistance circuit to a high-resistance circuit.

Why is the Transistor So Important?

The transistor is the most important invention in electronics history. It was invented in 1947 by William Shockley, John Bardeen, and Walter Brattain at Bell Labs (they won the Nobel Prize for this).

  • Every computer processor (CPU) contains billions of transistors
  • Your smartphone has more transistors than there are grains of sand on many beaches
  • Transistors can work as amplifiers (increase signal strength) or as switches (ON/OFF)

Construction & Types of Transistor

A transistor is made by sandwiching a thin layer of one type of semiconductor between two thicker layers of the other type. This gives us two types:

NPN Transistor

A thin layer of P-type semiconductor is sandwiched between two layers of N-type semiconductor.

Structure: N — P — N

Arrow on emitter points outward (away from base)

More commonly used in practice

PNP Transistor

A thin layer of N-type semiconductor is sandwiched between two layers of P-type semiconductor.

Structure: P — N — P

Arrow on emitter points inward (towards base)

Less commonly used

Three Terminals of a Transistor

Every transistor has three regions and therefore three terminals:

Terminal Symbol Size / Doping Function
Emitter (E) E Moderate size, heavily doped Emits (sends out) charge carriers into the base. It supplies the majority charge carriers.
Base (B) B Very thin, lightly doped Controls the flow of charge carriers from emitter to collector. It is the control terminal.
Collector (C) C Largest in size, moderately doped Collects the charge carriers that pass through the base. It receives most of the emitter current.
⭐ Key Points to Remember
  • The base region is very thin (about 10–25 micrometers) — this is crucial for transistor action
  • The emitter is heavily doped to supply a large number of charge carriers
  • The base is lightly doped so that most carriers pass through it without recombining
  • The collector is largest in area to collect the maximum number of carriers
  • A transistor has two p-n junctions:
    • Emitter-Base junction (E-B junction) — always forward biased
    • Collector-Base junction (C-B junction) — always reverse biased

Current Relationships in a Transistor 🔥

Fundamental Current Equation
IE = IB + IC

Where:

  • IE = Emitter current (largest)
  • IB = Base current (smallest — typically 1–5% of IE)
  • IC = Collector current (almost equal to IE — about 95–99% of IE)

Typical values: If IE = 10 mA, then IC ≈ 9.8 mA and IB ≈ 0.2 mA

Analogy — Water Pipe

Think of a transistor like a water system:

  • Emitter = Main water supply pipe (large flow)
  • Base = A small tap/valve (controls flow — very small amount of water goes through it)
  • Collector = Output pipe (receives almost all the water from the main supply)

By controlling the small base current (turning the small tap), you control the large collector current. This is the amplification principle!

Transistor Configurations

A transistor has 3 terminals but a circuit needs 4 terminals (2 for input, 2 for output). So one terminal is made common to both input and output. This gives three configurations:

Configuration Common Terminal Input Output Used For
Common Base (CB) Base E-B C-B High frequency circuits
Common Emitter (CE) ⭐ Emitter B-E C-E Amplifiers (most common)

For Exam

The Common Emitter (CE) configuration is the most important for your syllabus. The next section covers the CE amplifier in detail. The CE mode gives both current gain and voltage gain, making it ideal for amplification.

Transistor as Amplifier — CE Mode ⭐🔥

Definition — Amplifier

Amplifier: An amplifier is an electronic circuit that takes a weak input signal and produces a stronger output signal of the same waveform. The process of increasing the strength of a signal is called amplification.

Real-World Example

When you speak into a microphone at a concert, your voice (weak signal) is amplified by the amplifier circuit and comes out of the loudspeakers as a much louder version of the same sound. The transistor is the heart of this amplifier circuit.

The amplifier does NOT create energy from nothing — it uses the DC power supply (battery) to boost the weak signal. The small input signal controls how much of the battery's power reaches the output.

Common Emitter (CE) Amplifier — Concept

In the Common Emitter (CE) configuration:

  • The Emitter terminal is common to both input and output circuits
  • Input signal is applied between Base and Emitter (B-E)
  • Output signal is taken between Collector and Emitter (C-E)
  • E-B junction is forward biased (by VBB)
  • C-B junction is reverse biased (by VCC)

Working Principle of CE Amplifier

Step-by-Step Working
  1. DC Biasing: Battery VBB forward biases the E-B junction. Battery VCC reverse biases the C-B junction. A small base current (IB) flows due to forward bias.
  2. Without input signal: A steady (DC) collector current IC flows. The output voltage across RL is Vout = VCC − ICRL. This is the operating point (Q-point).
  3. When AC input signal is applied:
    • During positive half cycle of input → base current IB increases slightly → collector current IC increases by a large amount (β times) → voltage drop across RL increases → output voltage decreases
    • During negative half cycle of input → base current IB decreases slightly → collector current IC decreases by a large amount → voltage drop across RL decreases → output voltage increases
  4. Result: A small change in base current produces a large change in collector current, and hence a large change in output voltage. The signal is amplified!
⭐ Key Point — Phase Reversal

In the CE amplifier, the output signal is 180° out of phase with the input signal. This means:

  • When input goes positive → output goes negative
  • When input goes negative → output goes positive

This is called phase inversion or phase reversal. It is a unique characteristic of the CE configuration.

Important Parameters of CE Amplifier

Current Gain (β or hfe) 🔥

Current Gain (β) is the ratio of change in collector current to change in base current at constant VCE.

β = ΔICΔIB   (at constant VCE)

Or in DC terms:

βDC = ICIB

Where:

  • ΔIC = change in collector current
  • ΔIB = change in base current
  • Typical value of β: 20 to 500 (commonly around 50–200)

This means a small change in IB produces a β-times larger change in IC.

Voltage Gain (Av)

Voltage Gain is the ratio of output voltage to input voltage.

Av = VoutVin = ΔVCEΔVBE

Or approximately:

Av = β × RLRin

Where:

  • RL = load resistance (in collector circuit)
  • Rin = input resistance (of the transistor)

Voltage gain in CE mode is typically greater than 1 (can be several hundred).

Power Gain (Ap)
Ap = Av × β = β2 × RLRin

Power gain is always much greater than 1 in CE mode.

Relationship between α and β

Where α = current gain in CB mode = IC/IE (always less than 1, typically 0.95–0.99)

β = α1 − α
α = β1 + β

Advantages of CE Configuration

  • ✅ High current gain (β >> 1)
  • ✅ High voltage gain
  • ✅ High power gain (highest among all three configurations)
  • ✅ Moderate input impedance (suitable for most applications)
  • ✅ Moderate output impedance
  • ✅ Most widely used configuration for amplification

Common Exam Mistake

Students often forget that in CE mode, there is 180° phase reversal. If the question asks "What is the phase difference between input and output in CE amplifier?" — the answer is 180° (not 0° or 90°).

Photocells, Solar Cells & LED ⭐🔥

Photocells (Photoelectric Cells)

Definition — Photocell

Photocell: A photocell is a device that converts light energy into electrical energy. It works on the principle of the photoelectric effect — when light falls on certain materials, electrons are emitted or freed.

Types of Photocells:

1. Photo-emissive Cell

Light causes emission of electrons from a metal surface (true photoelectric effect).

Materials: Cesium, Potassium

Use: Light meters, burglar alarms

2. Photo-voltaic Cell

Light falling on a p-n junction generates a voltage (EMF) directly.

Materials: Silicon, Selenium

Use: Solar cells, exposure meters

3. Photo-conductive Cell

Light causes decrease in resistance of the material (increased conductivity).

Materials: CdS (Cadmium Sulphide), LDR

Use: Street light automation, camera exposure

Working Principle of Photocell (General):

  1. When light (photons) falls on the sensitive material of the photocell
  2. The photon energy is absorbed by the electrons in the material
  3. If the photon energy is sufficient, electrons gain enough energy to:
    • Photo-emissive: Escape from the surface entirely
    • Photo-voltaic: Cross the p-n junction, creating a voltage
    • Photo-conductive: Jump to conduction band, reducing resistance
  4. This produces an electric current or voltage proportional to the light intensity

Solar Cells (Photovoltaic Cells) 🔥

Definition — Solar Cell

Solar Cell: A solar cell is a semiconductor device (p-n junction) that directly converts solar light energy into electrical energy. It works on the photovoltaic effect. It generates a voltage when sunlight falls on it.

Construction:

  • Made from a p-n junction of silicon (most common) or gallium arsenide
  • A thin layer of n-type silicon is placed on top of a thicker p-type silicon base
  • The top n-layer is very thin (about 0.3 μm) so that light can easily reach the junction
  • Metal fingers (grid pattern) on top serve as the front contact (allows light to pass through gaps)
  • A continuous metal sheet on the bottom acts as the back contact
  • An anti-reflection coating is applied on top to minimize light reflection

Working Principle:

How a Solar Cell Works
  1. When sunlight (photons) falls on the solar cell, it passes through the thin n-type layer and reaches the p-n junction
  2. Photons with energy greater than the band gap (Eg) are absorbed
  3. This energy breaks covalent bonds and generates electron-hole pairs near the junction
  4. The built-in electric field at the depletion region pushes:
    • Electrons → towards the n-side
    • Holes → towards the p-side
  5. This creates a potential difference (voltage) across the junction — called photovoltage
  6. If an external load (like a bulb) is connected, current flows through it
  7. A single silicon solar cell produces about 0.5 to 0.6 V

Solar Cell Parameters
  • Open circuit voltage (VOC): ≈ 0.5 – 0.6 V (for Si solar cell)
  • Short circuit current (ISC): Depends on light intensity and cell area
  • Efficiency: 15–25% for commercial silicon solar cells
  • Fill Factor (FF): Ratio of maximum power to product of VOC and ISC
η = PoutPin × 100% = VOC × ISC × FFPin × 100%

Applications of Solar Cells:

Engineering & Practical Applications
  1. Solar power plants: Large-scale electricity generation
  2. Satellites & spacecraft: Primary power source in space
  3. Solar water heaters: For domestic and industrial use
  4. Street lighting: Solar-powered LED street lights
  5. Calculators & watches: Small solar cells power these devices
  6. Remote area electrification: Villages without grid connection
  7. Traffic signals: Solar powered traffic lights
  8. Mobile phone chargers: Portable solar chargers
  9. Water pumping: Solar-powered agricultural pumps
  10. Electric vehicles: Solar panels on roof of cars
Solar Panel vs Solar Cell

A single solar cell produces only about 0.5 V. To get higher voltage, many cells are connected in series. To get higher current, cells are connected in parallel. A combination of many solar cells is called a Solar Panel or Solar Module.

LED (Light Emitting Diode) 🔥

Definition — LED

LED (Light Emitting Diode): An LED is a specially designed p-n junction diode that emits light when forward biased. It converts electrical energy directly into light energy. It works on the principle of electroluminescence.

Solar Cell vs LED — Reverse Processes!

A solar cell and an LED are essentially opposite devices:

  • Solar Cell: Light energy → Electrical energy (light in, electricity out)
  • LED: Electrical energy → Light energy (electricity in, light out)

Both are p-n junction devices!

Working Principle:

How an LED Works
  1. When the LED is forward biased, current flows through it
  2. Electrons from the n-side cross the junction and enter the p-side
  3. In the p-side, these electrons recombine with holes
  4. During recombination, the electron drops from the conduction band to the valence band
  5. The energy difference (equal to the band gap Eg) is released as a photon (light)
  6. The colour of light depends on the band gap of the semiconductor material used
Energy of Emitted Photon
E = hf = hcλ

Where:

  • E = energy of photon (equal to band gap Eg)
  • h = Planck's constant = 6.63 × 10−34 J·s
  • f = frequency of light
  • c = speed of light = 3 × 108 m/s
  • λ = wavelength of light

Numerical 1 — Half Wave Rectifier

A half wave rectifier uses a diode with a peak input voltage of 25 V. Calculate: (a) DC output voltage, (b) RMS value of output voltage, (c) DC output current if load resistance RL = 1 kΩ (neglect diode resistance).

Solution

Vm = 25 V, RL = 1 kΩ = 1000 Ω

DC output voltage for HWR:

VDC = Vmπ = 253.14
VDC = 7.96 V ≈ 7.96 V

RMS value of output voltage for HWR:

Vrms = Vm2 = 252
Vrms = 12.5 V

DC output current:

IDC = VDCRL = 7.961000
IDC = 7.96 × 10−3 A = 7.96 mA

A full wave centre-tapped rectifier has a peak input voltage of 30 V. Calculate: (a) DC output voltage, (b) DC output current if RL = 500 Ω, (c) Output frequency if input frequency is 50 Hz.

Solution

Vm = 30 V, RL = 500 Ω, fin = 50 Hz

VDC = 2Vmπ = 2 × 303.14 = 603.14
VDC = 19.11 V

DC output current:

IDC = VDCRL = 19.11500

fout = 2 × fin = 2 × 50
fout = 100 Hz
Numerical 3 — Transistor Current (CE)

In a transistor, the collector current is 4.9 mA and the base current is 0.1 mA. Find: (a) Emitter current, (b) Current gain β, (c) Current gain α.

Solution

IC = 4.9 mA, IB = 0.1 mA

Emitter current:

IE = IB + IC = 0.1 + 4.9
IE = 5.0 mA

Current gain β (CE mode):

β = ICIB = 4.90.1
β = 49

Current gain α (CB mode):

α = ICIE = 4.95.0
α = 0.98

Verification: α = β / (1+β) = 49/50 = 0.98 ✅

Numerical 4 — α to β Conversion

The current gain α of a transistor in CB mode is 0.97. Find the current gain β in CE mode. If the emitter current is 10 mA, find the base current and collector current.

Solution

α = 0.97, IE = 10 mA

β = α1 − α = 0.971 − 0.97 = 0.970.03
β = 32.33 ≈ 32

We know α = IC / IE

IC = α × IE = 0.97 × 10
IC = 9.7 mA
IB = IE − IC = 10 − 9.7
IB = 0.3 mA

Check: β = IC/IB = 9.7/0.3 = 32.33 ✅

Numerical 5 — CE Amplifier Voltage Gain

A CE amplifier has a current gain β = 100, load resistance RL = 5 kΩ, and input resistance Rin = 1 kΩ. Find: (a) Voltage gain, (b) Power gain.

Solution

β = 100, RL = 5 kΩ, Rin = 1 kΩ

Voltage gain:

Av = β × RLRin = 100 × 51
Av = 500

Power gain:

Ap = Av × β = 500 × 100
Ap = 50,000

Or equivalently: Ap = β² × RL/Rin = 10000 × 5 = 50,000 ✅

Numerical 6 — Solar Cell Efficiency

A solar cell receives solar radiation of power 100 mW/cm². The cell area is 4 cm². If the open-circuit voltage is 0.55 V, short-circuit current is 120 mA, and fill factor is 0.80, find the efficiency of the solar cell.

Solution

Solar power density = 100 mW/cm², Area = 4 cm²

VOC = 0.55 V, ISC = 120 mA = 0.12 A, FF = 0.80

Pin = Power density × Area = 100 × 4 = 400 mW = 0.4 W
η = VOC × ISC × FFPin × 100%
η = 0.55 × 0.12 × 0.800.4 × 100%
η = 0.05280.4 × 100%
η = 13.2%
Numerical 7 — Comparison of HWR and FWR

An AC supply of peak voltage 50 V is applied to (a) a half wave rectifier, and (b) a full wave rectifier. Compare the DC output voltages and ripple factors of both.

Solution

Vm = 50 V

VDC(HWR) = Vmπ = 503.14
VDC(HWR) = 15.92 V, γHWR = 1.21
VDC(FWR) = 2Vmπ = 2 × 503.14
VDC(FWR) = 31.85 V, γFWR = 0.48

VDC(FWR) / VDC(HWR) = 31.85 / 15.92 = 2

The full wave rectifier gives double the DC voltage of the half wave rectifier.

The ripple factor of FWR (0.48) is much less than HWR (1.21), meaning FWR gives smoother DC.

Important Definitions 📝

1. Energy Band

When a large number of atoms are brought close together in a solid, the discrete energy levels of individual atoms merge and form a continuous range of energies called an energy band.

2. Valence Band

The energy band formed by the energy levels of the valence (outermost) electrons. At 0 K, this band is completely filled. Electrons here are bound to atoms.

3. Conduction Band

The energy band just above the valence band. Electrons in this band are free to move and can conduct electric current.

4. Forbidden Energy Gap (Eg)

The energy difference between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band. No electron can have energy within this gap. Measured in electron volts (eV).

5. Intrinsic Semiconductor

A semiconductor in its pure form, without any added impurity. In intrinsic semiconductors, the number of free electrons equals the number of holes (ne = nh). Example: Pure Si, Pure Ge.

6. Extrinsic Semiconductor

A semiconductor whose conductivity has been increased by adding a small amount of suitable impurity (dopant). Two types: N-type and P-type.

7. Doping

The process of deliberately adding a controlled amount of impurity to a pure semiconductor to increase its electrical conductivity.

8. Hole

A vacancy or empty space left behind when an electron breaks free from a covalent bond. It behaves like a positive charge carrier.

9. P-N Junction

The boundary or contact surface formed when a p-type and an n-type semiconductor are joined together in a single crystal.

10. Depletion Region

The narrow region around the p-n junction that is depleted of free charge carriers and contains only immobile positive and negative ions. It acts as a barrier for majority carriers.

11. Barrier Potential

The potential difference developed across the depletion region due to immobile ions. For Si ≈ 0.7 V, for Ge ≈ 0.3 V.

12. Forward Bias

The condition when the positive terminal of a battery is connected to the p-side and the negative terminal to the n-side of a p-n junction diode. The depletion region narrows and current flows.

13. Reverse Bias

The condition when the positive terminal of a battery is connected to the n-side and the negative terminal to the p-side of a p-n junction diode. The depletion region widens and practically no current flows.

14. Rectifier

An electronic circuit that converts alternating current (AC) into direct current (DC) using the unidirectional property of a diode.

15. Ripple Factor

The ratio of the AC component (ripple) to the DC component in the output of a rectifier. Lower value means smoother DC output.

16. Transistor

A three-terminal (Emitter, Base, Collector) semiconductor device formed by sandwiching one type of semiconductor between two layers of the other type. Used for amplification and switching.

17. Amplifier

An electronic circuit that increases the strength (amplitude) of a weak input signal without changing its waveform shape. The transistor is the active element in most amplifiers.

18. Current Gain (β)

In CE configuration, the ratio of collector current to base current: β = IC/IB. Typical value: 20 to 500.

19. LED (Light Emitting Diode)

A p-n junction diode that emits light when forward biased due to recombination of electrons and holes (electroluminescence). The colour depends on the semiconductor material used.

20. Solar Cell

A semiconductor p-n junction device that directly converts solar light energy into electrical energy by the photovoltaic effect. A single silicon cell produces about 0.5–0.6 V.

The forbidden energy gap in a conductor is approximately:

Q 6.16

What is forbidden energy gap?

The energy difference between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band is called the forbidden energy gap or band gap. No electron can exist in this energy range. It is measured in electron volts (eV).

Q 6.17

Define intrinsic semiconductor with example.

An intrinsic semiconductor is a pure semiconductor without any added impurity. In it, the number of free electrons equals the number of holes. Examples: Pure Silicon (Si) and Pure Germanium (Ge).

Q 6.18

What is doping?

Doping is the process of adding a small, controlled amount of suitable impurity to a pure semiconductor to increase its electrical conductivity.

Q 6.19

What is depletion region?

The thin region around a p-n junction that is free from mobile charge carriers and contains only immobile ions is called the depletion region. It acts like an insulating barrier.

Q 6.20

Why is a diode called a unidirectional device?

A diode is called a unidirectional device because it allows current to flow easily in one direction (forward bias) and blocks current in the opposite direction (reverse bias).

Q 6.21

A full wave rectifier uses both half cycles of the AC input, so it gives higher efficiency, higher DC output, and lower ripple than a half wave rectifier.

Long Answer Questions (5–7 Marks Style) 📄

Q 6.26

Explain energy bands in solids and classify materials into conductor, semiconductor, and insulator on the basis of band theory.

In an isolated atom, electrons have discrete energy levels. But in a solid, a very large number of atoms come close together, and their energy levels split into closely spaced levels which form energy bands.

The important bands are:

  • Valence band: Highest filled band containing valence electrons
  • Conduction band: Band above valence band containing free electrons
  • Forbidden energy gap: Energy gap between valence band and conduction band

Classification:

  1. Conductors: In conductors, valence band and conduction band overlap, so the forbidden gap is zero. Therefore many free electrons are available. Example: Copper, Silver.
  2. Semiconductors: In semiconductors, the forbidden gap is small (about 0.7 eV for Ge, 1.1 eV for Si). At room temperature some electrons can jump to the conduction band. Example: Silicon, Germanium.
  3. Insulators: In insulators, the forbidden gap is very large (>3 eV), so electrons cannot jump to conduction band at room temperature. Example: Glass, Rubber, Mica.

Thus the size of the forbidden energy gap determines whether a material is a conductor, semiconductor, or insulator.

Q 6.27

What are intrinsic and extrinsic semiconductors? Explain N-type and P-type semiconductors.

An intrinsic semiconductor is a pure semiconductor without any impurity. Examples are pure silicon and pure germanium. In it, the number of electrons equals the number of holes.

An extrinsic semiconductor is obtained by adding a small amount of impurity to a pure semiconductor. This process is called doping.

N-type semiconductor:

  • Formed by adding a pentavalent impurity such as Phosphorus, Arsenic, or Antimony
  • These impurities have 5 valence electrons
  • Four electrons form covalent bonds; the fifth becomes a free electron
  • Thus electrons are majority carriers and holes are minority carriers
  • Pentavalent impurities are called donor impurities

P-type semiconductor:

  • Formed by adding a trivalent impurity such as Boron, Aluminium, or Gallium
  • These impurities have 3 valence electrons
  • One bond remains incomplete, creating a hole
  • Thus holes are majority carriers and electrons are minority carriers
  • Trivalent impurities are called acceptor impurities

Both N-type and P-type semiconductors are electrically neutral.

Q 6.28

Explain the formation of p-n junction and depletion layer. What is barrier potential?

When a p-type and an n-type semiconductor are joined together, a p-n junction is formed.

At the junction:

  • Electrons diffuse from n-side to p-side
  • Holes diffuse from p-side to n-side
  • These electrons and holes recombine near the junction
  • This leaves behind immobile positive ions on n-side and immobile negative ions on p-side

The region around the junction, which is free from mobile charge carriers and contains only immobile ions, is called the depletion region.

These ions create an electric field across the junction. Due to this electric field, a potential difference develops across the depletion layer. This is called the barrier potential or junction potential.

Typical values:

  • Silicon: 0.7 V
  • Germanium: 0.3 V

The barrier potential opposes the further movement of majority carriers across the junction.

Q 6.29

Describe the working of a p-n junction diode in forward bias and reverse bias. Also explain its V-I characteristics.

A p-n junction diode is a two-terminal device that allows current mainly in one direction.

Forward Bias:

  • Positive terminal of battery is connected to p-side and negative to n-side
  • Depletion layer width decreases
  • Barrier potential decreases
  • Majority carriers cross the junction easily
  • Large current flows after cut-in voltage is reached (0.7 V for Si, 0.3 V for Ge)

Reverse Bias:

  • Positive terminal is connected to n-side and negative to p-side
  • Depletion layer width increases
  • Barrier potential increases
  • Majority carriers are pulled away from junction
  • Only a very small reverse saturation current flows due to minority carriers

V-I Characteristics:

  • In forward bias, current remains small until knee voltage, then rises rapidly
  • In reverse bias, current remains very small and nearly constant until breakdown voltage

Thus a diode acts as a unidirectional device.

Q 6.30

Explain the working of a half wave rectifier with waveform. Mention its main limitations.

A half wave rectifier uses one diode to convert only one half of the AC input into DC.

Circuit: It consists of a transformer, one diode, and a load resistance.

Working:

  • During the positive half cycle, the diode is forward biased and current flows through the load
  • During the negative half cycle, the diode is reverse biased and no current flows
  • Thus only one half cycle appears in the output

The output is pulsating DC.

Main formulas:

  • VDC = Vm/π
  • Efficiency = 40.6%
  • Ripple factor = 1.21

Limitations:

  1. Uses only one half cycle of AC input
  2. Low efficiency
  3. High ripple factor
  4. Output is not smooth
  5. Poor transformer utilization

Therefore, in practice, full wave rectifiers are preferred.

Q 6.31

Explain the working of a full wave centre-tapped rectifier. Why is it better than a half wave rectifier?

A full wave centre-tapped rectifier uses two diodes and a centre-tapped transformer to convert both half cycles of AC into DC.

Working:

  • During the positive half cycle, one end of the secondary becomes positive and diode D1 conducts, while D2 is reverse biased
  • During the negative half cycle, the other end becomes positive and diode D2 conducts, while D1 is reverse biased
  • In both cases, current flows through the load in the same direction

Thus both halves of the AC are used and a pulsating DC output is obtained.

Important formulas:

  • VDC = 2Vm/π
  • Efficiency = 81.2%
  • Ripple factor = 0.48
  • Output frequency = 2f

Advantages over half wave rectifier:

  1. Higher DC output voltage
  2. Higher efficiency
  3. Lower ripple factor
  4. Smoother DC output
  5. Better transformer utilization

Q 6.32

Describe the construction and working of a transistor. Distinguish between NPN and PNP transistor.

A transistor is a three-terminal semiconductor device having two p-n junctions. It consists of three regions: Emitter, Base, and Collector.

Construction:

  • Emitter is heavily doped
  • Base is very thin and lightly doped
  • Collector is moderately doped and larger in area

Working:

  • The emitter-base junction is forward biased
  • The collector-base junction is reverse biased
  • The emitter sends charge carriers into the base
  • Since the base is very thin, most carriers pass into the collector
  • Thus a small base current controls a large collector current

NPN transistor: P-layer is between two N-layers, electrons are majority carriers, emitter arrow points outward.

PNP transistor: N-layer is between two P-layers, holes are majority carriers, emitter arrow points inward.

Q 6.33

Explain transistor as an amplifier in common emitter mode.

In common emitter (CE) configuration, the emitter terminal is common to both input and output circuits.

Input: Applied between base and emitter

Output: Taken between collector and emitter

Working:

  1. The emitter-base junction is forward biased and collector-base junction is reverse biased
  2. A small input signal is applied to the base
  3. This causes a small change in base current
  4. Because the transistor has current gain β, this small change in base current produces a much larger change in collector current
  5. The large change in collector current causes a large voltage variation across the load resistance in the collector circuit
  6. Therefore the output signal is much larger than the input signal

Advantages:

  • High current gain
  • High voltage gain
  • High power gain

Special feature: The output is 180° out of phase with the input.
