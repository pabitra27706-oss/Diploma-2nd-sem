Intrinsic & Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic Semiconductor

Pure semiconductor (Si, Ge). Equal number of electrons and holes. Conductivity low.

Extrinsic Semiconductor

Doped with impurities to increase conductivity.

  • N-type: Pentavalent impurity (P, As) – electrons majority
  • P-type: Trivalent impurity (B, Al) – holes majority

P-N Junction · Diode · V-I Characteristics

Formation of P-N Junction

When P-type and N-type semiconductors are joined, diffusion creates depletion region and built-in potential.

Junction Diode Symbol
▶

Arrow points from P to N (direction of conventional current).

V-I Characteristics
  • Forward bias: Current increases exponentially after knee voltage (0.7V for Si, 0.3V for Ge).
  • Reverse bias: Very small leakage current until breakdown.

Diode as Rectifier: Half Wave & Full Wave

Half Wave Rectifier

Single diode. Converts only one half of AC to DC. Output pulsating DC.

Efficiency ~40.6%.

Full Wave Rectifier (Centre Tapped)

Two diodes and centre-tapped transformer. Both halves rectified. Efficiency ~81.2%.

Circuit Diagram (Conceptual)

Half wave: AC source → Diode → Load → return.

Full wave: Centre-tapped transformer with two diodes.

Transistor as Amplifier (CE Mode)

Common emitter configuration provides voltage and current gain.

Small base current controls larger collector current. β = IC / IB.

Optoelectronic Devices: Photocell · Solar Cell · LED

Photocell

Working: Light increases conductivity (photoconductive effect) or generates voltage (photovoltaic).

Applications: Light sensors, automatic lighting, burglar alarms.

Solar Cell

Working: P-N junction converts light directly into electricity (photovoltaic effect).

Applications: Solar panels, calculators, satellites.

LED (Light Emitting Diode)

Working: Forward bias causes electron-hole recombination, emitting light (electroluminescence).

Applications: Indicators, displays, lighting.
