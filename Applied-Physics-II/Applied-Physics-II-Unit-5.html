Unit Overview

What This Unit Covers

This unit deals with electromagnetism, which connects electricity and magnetism. You will learn how moving charges produce magnetic fields, how magnetic fields act on moving charges and current carrying conductors, and how changing magnetic field can produce electricity.

This unit is extremely important for understanding motors, generators, transformers, galvanometers, ammeters, voltmeters, relays, speakers, induction cookers, and many other electrical devices.

Why This Unit Matters

  • ⚡ Electric motors work because a current carrying conductor experiences force in a magnetic field
  • 🔄 Generators work due to electromagnetic induction
  • 📟 Galvanometer, ammeter and voltmeter are based on magnetic effect of current
  • 🧲 Electromagnets are used in bells, cranes, relays and machines
  • 🏭 This unit is the base of electrical engineering, electronics and instrumentation

Magnetic Field and Its Origin ⭐🔥

What is a Magnetic Field?

Definition — Magnetic Field

A magnetic field is the region around a magnet, current carrying conductor, or moving charge where a magnetic force can be experienced.

Just as an electric field exists around a charge, a magnetic field exists around a magnet or moving charge.

Simple Analogy

Imagine a magnet like a person spraying perfume. You cannot see the perfume directly, but you know it is present because you can smell it nearby. Similarly, magnetic field is invisible, but we know it exists because it exerts force on magnetic materials and moving charges.

Origin of Magnetic Field

Magnetic field can arise due to:

  1. Permanent magnets (like bar magnets)
  2. Moving electric charges
  3. Current carrying conductors
  4. Electrons revolving and spinning inside atoms
Very Important Concept

Electric charge at rest produces electric field only. Electric charge in motion produces both electric field and magnetic field.

Magnetic Lines of Force

Magnetic Lines of Force

Magnetic lines of force are imaginary lines drawn in a magnetic field such that the tangent at any point gives the direction of the magnetic field at that point.

Properties of Magnetic Lines of Force

  • They form closed continuous curves
  • Outside the magnet, they go from North pole to South pole
  • Inside the magnet, they go from South pole to North pole
  • Two magnetic field lines never intersect each other
  • Closer the lines, stronger the magnetic field

Magnetic Field Strength and Unit

Magnetic Flux Density

The intensity or strength of magnetic field is represented by B and is called magnetic flux density.

SI Unit of Magnetic Field
SI unit of B = tesla (T)

Other smaller unit sometimes used: gauss

1 tesla = 104 gauss
Symbol Reminder

Magnetic field is often represented by B. It is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

Lorentz Force — Force on Moving Charge ⭐🔥

Definition — Lorentz Force

The force experienced by a charged particle moving in electric and magnetic fields is called Lorentz force.

In this syllabus, the focus is on the magnetic part: force on a moving charge in a magnetic field.

Force on a Moving Charge in Magnetic Field

Lorentz Force Formula (Magnetic Part)
F = qvB sin θ

Where:

  • F = magnetic force on the charged particle (newton, N)
  • q = charge of particle (coulomb, C)
  • v = velocity of particle (m/s)
  • B = magnetic flux density (tesla, T)
  • θ = angle between velocity and magnetic field

Special Cases

Case 1: θ = 0°

F = qvB sin 0° = 0

If the charge moves parallel to the magnetic field, no magnetic force acts on it.

Case 2: θ = 90°

F = qvB

If the charge moves perpendicular to the magnetic field, force is maximum.

Case 3: 0° < θ < 90°

F = qvB sin θ

Force has intermediate value.

Direction of Lorentz Force

The direction of force on a positive charge is given by Fleming’s left hand rule or the vector rule F = q (v × B).

For a negative charge, the direction of force is opposite to that for a positive charge.

Fleming’s Left Hand Rule

Stretch the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of the left hand mutually perpendicular to each other:

  • Forefinger → direction of magnetic field (B)
  • Middle finger → direction of current or motion of positive charge
  • Thumb → direction of force (F)

Path of Charged Particle in Magnetic Field

  • If velocity is parallel to B → particle moves in straight line
  • If velocity is perpendicular to B → particle moves in circular path
  • If velocity makes some angle with B → particle moves in helical path
Exam Important Point

The magnetic force is always perpendicular to both the velocity and the magnetic field. Therefore, magnetic force changes only the direction of motion, not the speed, when only magnetic field is present.

Biot–Savart Law ⭐🔥

Statement of Biot–Savart Law

Biot–Savart Law

The magnetic field produced at a point by a small current element is directly proportional to the current, directly proportional to the length of the current element, directly proportional to the sine of angle between the element and the line joining the point, and inversely proportional to the square of the distance of the point from the current element.

Mathematical Form

Biot–Savart Law Formula
dB = μ04π × I dl sin θr2

Where:

  • dB = small magnetic field due to current element dl
  • μ0 = permeability of free space = 4π × 10−7 H/m
  • I = current through conductor
  • dl = small current element
  • r = distance of point from current element
  • θ = angle between dl and r
What This Law Means Physically
  • More current → stronger magnetic field
  • Longer current element → stronger magnetic contribution
  • Closer point → stronger field
  • Maximum field when θ = 90°
  • Zero field contribution when θ = 0°

Direction of dB

The direction of magnetic field due to current element is given by the right hand thumb rule.

Right Hand Thumb Rule

If we hold the current carrying conductor in the right hand with the thumb pointing in the direction of current, then the curled fingers give the direction of magnetic field around the conductor.

Why Biot-Savart Law is Important

Biot-Savart law is the magnetic equivalent of Coulomb’s law in electrostatics. It is used to derive the magnetic field due to:

  • Long straight conductor
  • Circular loop
  • Solenoid (advanced level)

Magnetic Field Due to a Straight Conductor ⭐🔥

Field Around a Long Straight Current Carrying Conductor

When current flows through a long straight conductor, concentric circular magnetic field lines are produced around it.

Magnetic Field at Distance r
B = μ0 I2πr

Where:

  • B = magnetic flux density at distance r
  • μ0 = permeability of free space
  • I = current in conductor
  • r = perpendicular distance from conductor

Important Observations

  • B is directly proportional to current I
  • B is inversely proportional to distance r
  • If current doubles, magnetic field doubles
  • If distance doubles, magnetic field becomes half

Magnetic Field Due to a Circular Loop ⭐🔥

Field at the Center of Circular Loop

When current flows through a circular loop, each small element of the loop produces a magnetic field at the center. The combined effect gives the total magnetic field at the center.

Magnetic Field at Center of Circular Loop
B = μ0 I2R

Where:

  • B = magnetic field at center
  • μ0 = permeability of free space
  • I = current in loop
  • R = radius of circular loop

For N turns circular coil

Magnetic Field at Center of N-turn Coil
B = μ0 N I2R

Where N = number of turns

Important Observations

  • B is directly proportional to current I
  • B is directly proportional to number of turns N
  • B is inversely proportional to radius R

Concept of Magnetic Dipole ⭐

What is a Magnetic Dipole?

Definition — Magnetic Dipole

A magnetic dipole is a system having two equal and opposite magnetic poles separated by a small distance. A bar magnet is a simple example of a magnetic dipole.

A current carrying circular loop also behaves like a magnetic dipole.

Why Current Loop is a Magnetic Dipole

When current flows in a circular loop, one face behaves like a North pole and the opposite face behaves like a South pole. Thus the loop acts like a tiny bar magnet or magnetic dipole.

Magnetic Dipole Moment

Magnetic Dipole Moment

The magnetic dipole moment is the product of pole strength and magnetic length in case of a bar magnet, or current and area in case of a current loop.

For Bar Magnet
M = m × 2l

Where:

  • M = magnetic dipole moment
  • m = pole strength
  • 2l = magnetic length
For Current Carrying Circular Loop
M = N I A

Where:

  • M = magnetic dipole moment
  • N = number of turns
  • I = current through loop
  • A = area of loop

For circular loop of radius R:

A = πR2
Therefore, M = N I πR2

Force on Current Carrying Conductor ⭐🔥

Basic Idea

When a conductor carrying current is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force. This is the basic principle behind electric motors, loudspeakers, moving coil galvanometers, and many electromechanical devices.

Definition

A current carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a mechanical force. The direction of force is perpendicular to both the direction of current and magnetic field.

Formula for Force

Force on Conductor
F = B I l sin θ

Where:

  • F = force on conductor (newton, N)
  • B = magnetic flux density (tesla, T)
  • I = current through conductor (ampere, A)
  • l = length of conductor in magnetic field (m)
  • θ = angle between current and magnetic field

Special Cases

  • When θ = 90°, F = BIl (maximum force)
  • When θ = 0°, F = 0 (no force)
Direction of Force

The direction of force is given by Fleming’s Left Hand Rule:

  • Forefinger → magnetic field
  • Middle finger → current
  • Thumb → force/motion

Torque on Rectangular Coil in Magnetic Field ⭐🔥

Concept

If a rectangular coil carrying current is placed in a magnetic field, opposite forces act on the two sides of the coil. These equal and opposite forces form a couple and produce a torque, causing the coil to rotate.

Torque on Coil

The turning effect experienced by a current carrying rectangular coil placed in a magnetic field is called torque.

Formula for Torque

Torque on Coil
τ = N B I A sin θ

Where:

  • τ = torque (N·m)
  • N = number of turns of coil
  • B = magnetic flux density
  • I = current through coil
  • A = area of coil
  • θ = angle between normal to the coil and magnetic field

Maximum Torque

When θ = 90°,

Maximum Torque
τmax = N B I A

Meaning of Area A

For a rectangular coil of length l and breadth b:

A = l × b

Electromagnetic Induction ⭐🔥

Introduction

Electromagnetic induction was discovered by Michael Faraday. He found that electricity can be produced from magnetism.

Definition — Electromagnetic Induction

The phenomenon of producing an electromotive force (emf) in a conductor or coil due to change in magnetic flux linked with it is called electromagnetic induction.

Induced EMF and Induced Current

Induced EMF

The emf produced in a conductor due to electromagnetic induction is called induced emf.

Induced Current

If the circuit is closed, the current produced due to induced emf is called induced current.

Magnetic Flux

Magnetic Flux

The total number of magnetic field lines passing normally through a surface is called magnetic flux.

Magnetic Flux Formula
Φ = B A cos θ

Where:

  • Φ = magnetic flux (weber, Wb)
  • B = magnetic flux density (tesla)
  • A = area of surface (m²)
  • θ = angle between magnetic field and normal to surface

How to Change Magnetic Flux?

Induced emf is produced only when flux changes. Flux can be changed by:

  1. Changing magnetic field strength B
  2. Changing area A of the coil
  3. Changing angle θ
  4. Moving magnet towards or away from coil
  5. Moving coil in magnetic field

Important Understanding

If a magnet and coil are both at rest relative to each other, there is no change in flux, so no induced emf is produced. Relative motion or changing field is necessary.

Faraday’s Laws of Electromagnetic Induction ⭐🔥

First Law of Faraday

Faraday’s First Law

Whenever the magnetic flux linked with a circuit changes, an emf is induced in the circuit. If the circuit is closed, an induced current flows.

Second Law of Faraday

Faraday’s Second Law

The magnitude of induced emf is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux linked with the circuit.

Faraday’s Law Formula
e = − N dΦdt

Where:

  • e = induced emf (volt)
  • N = number of turns in coil
  • dΦ/dt = rate of change of magnetic flux

Meaning of Negative Sign

Lenz’s Law

The direction of induced emf or induced current is such that it opposes the cause that produces it. This is called Lenz’s law.

The negative sign in Faraday’s law represents Lenz’s law.

Easy Explanation of Lenz’s Law

If you move a magnet towards a coil, the induced current in the coil will produce its own magnetic field to oppose the motion of the magnet. Nature always opposes the cause of change.

Moving Coil Galvanometer ⭐🔥

What is a Galvanometer?

Galvanometer

A moving coil galvanometer is a sensitive instrument used to detect and measure small electric currents.

Principle

It works on the principle that a current carrying coil placed in a magnetic field experiences a torque.

Deflecting Torque
τ = N B I A

For a radial magnetic field, sin θ = 1 always.

Construction

  • A rectangular coil of many turns of fine insulated copper wire
  • The coil is wound on a light metallic frame
  • Placed between the poles of a strong horseshoe permanent magnet
  • Soft iron cylindrical core used to make magnetic field radial and strong
  • Suspended by phosphor bronze strip or spring
  • Pointer attached to coil moving over a scale

Fig. 5.21: Construction of moving coil galvanometer showing coil, magnet, soft iron core, spring and pointer

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Working

  1. When current flows through the coil, a torque acts on it.
  2. The coil rotates and the pointer deflects over the scale.
  3. The spring provides restoring torque.
  4. At equilibrium:
    Deflecting torque = Restoring torque
  5. The deflection is proportional to the current.
Current-Deflection Relation
I ∝ θ

This means galvanometer has a uniform linear scale.

Fig. 5.22: Working of moving coil galvanometer showing coil rotation and pointer deflection

Limitation

A moving coil galvanometer can measure only small currents. If large current passes through it, the coil may be damaged. Therefore it is converted into an ammeter or voltmeter for practical use.

Conversion of Galvanometer into Ammeter & Voltmeter ⭐🔥

Conversion of Galvanometer into Ammeter

Ammeter

An ammeter is an instrument used to measure electric current in a circuit. It is always connected in series.

To convert a galvanometer into an ammeter, a small resistance called shunt is connected in parallel with the galvanometer.

Shunt

A shunt is a very low resistance connected in parallel with a galvanometer so that most of the current bypasses the galvanometer.

Why Shunt is Needed?

The galvanometer can carry only a small current Ig. To measure a larger current I, the excess current is sent through the shunt.

Shunt Resistance Formula
S = Ig GI − Ig

Where:

  • S = shunt resistance
  • G = resistance of galvanometer
  • Ig = maximum current that galvanometer can carry
  • I = current range of ammeter

Conversion of Galvanometer into Voltmeter

Voltmeter

A voltmeter is an instrument used to measure potential difference between two points. It is always connected in parallel.

To convert a galvanometer into a voltmeter, a high resistance is connected in series with it.

Multiplier Resistance

The high resistance connected in series with galvanometer to convert it into a voltmeter is called multiplier resistance.

Series Resistance Formula
R = VIg − G

Where:

  • R = series resistance
  • V = voltage range of voltmeter
  • Ig = galvanometer full scale current
  • G = galvanometer resistance

Types of Magnetic Materials — Dia, Para & Ferromagnetic ⭐🔥

Introduction

Different materials respond differently when placed in a magnetic field. Based on their magnetic behavior, they are classified into:

  1. Diamagnetic materials
  2. Paramagnetic materials
  3. Ferromagnetic materials

Diamagnetic Materials

Diamagnetic Material

Materials which are weakly repelled by a magnetic field are called diamagnetic materials.

Properties

  • Weakly repelled by magnetic field
  • Magnetic susceptibility is small and negative
  • Relative permeability is slightly less than 1
  • Do not retain magnetism after removal of field

Examples: Bismuth, Copper, Silver, Gold, Water, Mercury

Paramagnetic Materials

Paramagnetic Material

Materials which are weakly attracted by a magnetic field are called paramagnetic materials.

Properties

  • Weakly attracted by magnetic field
  • Magnetic susceptibility is small and positive
  • Relative permeability is slightly more than 1
  • Do not retain magnetism strongly

Examples: Aluminium, Platinum, Oxygen, Manganese, Chromium

Ferromagnetic Materials

Ferromagnetic Material

Materials which are strongly attracted by a magnetic field and can be permanently magnetized are called ferromagnetic materials.

Properties

  • Strongly attracted by magnetic field
  • Very large positive susceptibility
  • Relative permeability is very high
  • Can retain magnetism even after field is removed
  • Used to make permanent magnets and electromagnets

Examples: Iron, Cobalt, Nickel, Steel

Solved Numerical Examples 🔥

Numerical 1 — Lorentz Force

A charge of 2 × 10−6 C moves with velocity 3 × 105 m/s perpendicular to a magnetic field of 0.4 T. Find the magnetic force acting on it.

Solution
F = qvB sin θ

q = 2 × 10−6 C, v = 3 × 105 m/s, B = 0.4 T, θ = 90°

F = 2 × 10−6 × 3 × 105 × 0.4 × sin 90°
F = 2 × 3 × 0.4 × 10−1
F = 2.4 × 10−1
F = 0.24 N
Numerical 2 — Magnetic Field Due to Straight Conductor

Find the magnetic field at a point 5 cm away from a long straight conductor carrying current 10 A.

Solution
B = μ0 I2πr

I = 10 A, r = 5 cm = 0.05 m, μ0 = 4π × 10−7

B = 4π × 10−7 × 102π × 0.05
B = 4 × 10−60.1
B = 4 × 10−5 T
B = 4 × 10−5 T
Numerical 3 — Field at Center of Circular Loop

A circular loop of radius 10 cm carries current 5 A. Find the magnetic field at its center.

Solution
B = μ0 I2R

I = 5 A, R = 10 cm = 0.1 m

B = 4π × 10−7 × 52 × 0.1
B = 10π × 10−6 T
B ≈ 3.14 × 10−5 T
B ≈ 3.14 × 10−5 T
Numerical 4 — Force on Conductor

A conductor of length 0.2 m carries current 4 A and is placed perpendicular to a magnetic field of 0.5 T. Find the force on it.

Solution
F = B I l sin θ

B = 0.5 T, I = 4 A, l = 0.2 m, θ = 90°

F = 0.5 × 4 × 0.2 × sin 90°
F = 0.5 × 4 × 0.2
F = 0.4 N
F = 0.4 N
Numerical 5 — Torque on Coil

A rectangular coil of 50 turns and area 0.02 m² carries current 0.5 A in a magnetic field of 0.4 T. Calculate maximum torque.

Solution
τmax = N B I A

N = 50, B = 0.4 T, I = 0.5 A, A = 0.02 m²

τ = 50 × 0.4 × 0.5 × 0.02
τ = 0.2 N·m
Maximum torque = 0.2 N·m
Numerical 6 — Induced EMF by Faraday’s Law

A coil of 200 turns is linked with magnetic flux changing from 0.005 Wb to 0.001 Wb in 0.2 s. Find the induced emf.

Solution
e = N ΔΦΔt

N = 200, ΔΦ = 0.005 − 0.001 = 0.004 Wb, Δt = 0.2 s

e = 200 × 0.0040.2
e = 200 × 0.02
e = 4 V
Induced emf = 4 V
Numerical 7 — Ammeter Conversion

A galvanometer of resistance 50 Ω gives full scale deflection at 2 mA. Calculate the shunt required to convert it into an ammeter of range 2 A.

Solution
S = Ig GI − Ig

G = 50 Ω, Ig = 2 mA = 0.002 A, I = 2 A

S = 0.002 × 502 − 0.002
S = 0.11.998
S ≈ 0.050 Ω
Required shunt ≈ 0.05 Ω
Numerical 8 — Voltmeter Conversion

A galvanometer has resistance 100 Ω and full scale deflection current 1 mA. Find the resistance required to convert it into a voltmeter of range 10 V.

Solution
R = VIg − G

V = 10 V, Ig = 1 mA = 0.001 A, G = 100 Ω

R = 100.001 − 100
R = 10000 − 100
R = 9900 Ω
Required series resistance = 9900 Ω = 9.9 kΩ

Important Definitions 📝

1. Magnetic Field

The region around a magnet or current carrying conductor where magnetic force can be experienced is called magnetic field.

2. Magnetic Lines of Force

Imaginary lines drawn in a magnetic field such that tangent at any point gives the direction of the field at that point.

3. Lorentz Force

The force experienced by a charged particle moving in electric and magnetic fields is called Lorentz force.

4. Biot-Savart Law

The magnetic field due to a current element is directly proportional to current, length element, sin θ and inversely proportional to square of distance.

5. Magnetic Dipole

A system having two equal and opposite magnetic poles separated by a small distance is called a magnetic dipole.

6. Magnetic Dipole Moment

The product of pole strength and magnetic length, or for a current loop the product NIA, is called magnetic dipole moment.

7. Electromagnetic Induction

The phenomenon of production of induced emf due to change in magnetic flux linked with a circuit.

8. Magnetic Flux

The total number of magnetic field lines passing normally through a surface is called magnetic flux.

9. Induced EMF

The emf produced in a conductor or coil due to electromagnetic induction is called induced emf.

10. Faraday’s Laws

The laws stating that induced emf is produced when magnetic flux changes and its magnitude is proportional to the rate of change of flux.

11. Lenz’s Law

The direction of induced current is such that it opposes the cause producing it.

12. Moving Coil Galvanometer

A sensitive instrument used for detecting and measuring small electric currents.

13. Ammeter

An instrument used to measure electric current, connected in series and having low resistance.

14. Voltmeter

An instrument used to measure potential difference, connected in parallel and having high resistance.

15. Diamagnetic Material

A material weakly repelled by a magnetic field.

16. Paramagnetic Material

A material weakly attracted by a magnetic field.

17. Ferromagnetic Material

A material strongly attracted by magnetic field and capable of permanent magnetization.

Q 5.1

The SI unit of magnetic flux density is:

  • a. weber
  • b. tesla
  • c. henry
  • d. newton
Answer: b. tesla

The SI unit of magnetic field or magnetic flux density B is tesla (T).

Q 5.2

Lorentz force on a charge moving parallel to magnetic field is:

  • a. maximum
  • b. zero
  • c. half
  • d. infinite
Answer: b. zero

F = qvB sin θ. When the charge moves parallel to B, θ = 0°, so sin 0° = 0 and force is zero.

Q 5.3

The magnetic field at distance r from a long straight conductor carrying current I is:

  • a. μ₀I/2R
  • b. μ₀I/2πr
  • c. BIl
  • d. qvB
Answer: b. μ₀I/2πr

This is the standard formula for magnetic field due to a long straight current carrying wire.

Q 5.4

Biot-Savart law gives magnetic field due to:

  • a. stationary charge
  • b. magnetic pole
  • c. current element

Answer: c. current element

Biot-Savart law gives the magnetic field produced by a small current element dl.

Q 5.6

The force on a current carrying conductor is maximum when angle between current and magnetic field is:

F = BIl sin θ is maximum when sin θ = 1, i.e., θ = 90°.

Long Answer Questions (5–7 Marks Style) 📄

Q 5.26

Define magnetic field. What are magnetic lines of force? Write their properties.

A magnetic field is the region around a magnet or current carrying conductor where magnetic force can be experienced.

Magnetic lines of force are imaginary lines drawn in a magnetic field such that the tangent at any point gives the direction of the magnetic field.

Properties:

  • They form closed curves
  • Outside magnet they go from North to South
  • Inside magnet they go from South to North
  • They never intersect each other
  • Closer lines indicate stronger magnetic field

Q 5.27

State Biot-Savart law and derive the expression for magnetic field due to a long straight conductor.

Biot-Savart law states that the magnetic field due to a current element is directly proportional to current, current element and sin θ, and inversely proportional to square of distance.

dB = (μ₀/4π) × (I dl sin θ / r²)

On integrating for a long straight conductor, we get:

B = μ₀I / 2πr

This shows that the magnetic field is directly proportional to current and inversely proportional to distance from the wire.

Q 5.28

Derive the formula for magnetic field at the center of a circular loop.

Using Biot-Savart law, the field due to a small element dl of a circular loop at the center is:

dB = (μ₀/4π) × (I dl / R²)

Since every element contributes in the same direction, integrating over the whole circumference:

B = (μ₀I/4πR²) ∫dl

But ∫dl = 2πR

B = (μ₀I/4πR²) × 2πR = μ₀I/2R

Thus the magnetic field at the center of a circular loop is:

B = μ₀I / 2R

Q 5.29

Explain force on a current carrying conductor placed in magnetic field. Mention Fleming’s left hand rule.

When a current carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force because moving charges in the conductor interact with the magnetic field.

F = BIl sin θ

The force is maximum when the conductor is perpendicular to the magnetic field and zero when parallel.

Fleming’s Left Hand Rule:

  • Forefinger → magnetic field
  • Middle finger → current
  • Thumb → force or motion

This principle is used in electric motors and galvanometers.

Q 5.30

What is electromagnetic induction? State and explain Faraday’s laws.

Electromagnetic induction is the phenomenon of production of induced emf when magnetic flux linked with a circuit changes.

Faraday’s First Law: Whenever magnetic flux linked with a circuit changes, an emf is induced in it.

Faraday’s Second Law: The magnitude of induced emf is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux.

e = −N(dΦ/dt)

The negative sign indicates Lenz’s law, which gives the direction of induced current.

Q 5.31

Describe the principle, construction and working of moving coil galvanometer.

Principle: A current carrying coil in magnetic field experiences a torque.

Construction:

  • Rectangular coil of many turns
  • Strong permanent magnet
  • Soft iron core
  • Suspension spring
  • Pointer and scale

Working:

  • When current flows, the coil experiences torque and rotates
  • The pointer attached to coil deflects
  • The spring provides restoring torque
  • At equilibrium, deflection is proportional to current

It is used for measuring small currents.

Q 5.32

Explain how a galvanometer is converted into an ammeter and a voltmeter.

Galvanometer to Ammeter:

  • A low resistance shunt is connected in parallel
  • Most current passes through shunt
  • The galvanometer measures only small current
  • Shunt formula:
    S = IgG / (I − Ig)

Galvanometer to Voltmeter:

  • A high resistance is connected in series
  • This limits current through galvanometer
  • Series resistance formula:
    R = V/Ig − G

Ammeter is connected in series and has low resistance, whereas voltmeter is connected in parallel and has high resistance.

Q 5.33

Classify magnetic materials. Distinguish between diamagnetic, paramagnetic and ferromagnetic materials.

Magnetic materials are classified as:

  1. Diamagnetic materials
  2. Paramagnetic materials
  3. Ferromagnetic materials

Diamagnetic: Weakly repelled by magnetic field. Example: copper, bismuth.

Paramagnetic: Weakly attracted by magnetic field. Example: aluminium, platinum.

Ferromagnetic: Strongly attracted and can be permanently magnetized. Example: iron, cobalt, nickel.

Ferromagnetic materials have very high permeability and are used in magnets, motors, and transformers.

Q 5.34

A charge 5 × 10−6 C moves with speed 2 × 105 m/s at right angle to a magnetic field 0.3 T. Find the force.

F = qvB = 5 × 10−6 × 2 × 105 × 0.3
F = 3 × 10−1
Answer: 0.3 N

Q 5.35

Find the magnetic field at 2 cm from a wire carrying 8 A current.

B = μ₀I / 2πr
= (4π × 10−7 × 8) / (2π × 0.02)
= 8 × 10−5 T
Answer: 8 × 10−5 T

Q 5.36

A circular loop of radius 0.05 m carries current 2 A. Find magnetic field at center.

B = μ₀I / 2R
= (4π × 10−7 × 2) / (2 × 0.05)
= 8π × 10−6 T
Answer: 2.51 × 10−5 T (approx.)

Q 5.37

A conductor 0.5 m long carries 3 A current in a field of 0.2 T perpendicular to it. Find the force.

F = BIl = 0.2 × 3 × 0.5
Answer: 0.3 N

Q 5.38

A coil of 20 turns, area 0.01 m², carrying 1 A current is placed in 0.5 T field. Find maximum torque.

τ = NBIA = 20 × 0.5 × 1 × 0.01
Answer: 0.1 N·m

Q 5.39

A 100-turn coil experiences flux change of 0.02 Wb in 0.5 s. Find induced emf.

e = N(ΔΦ/Δt) = 100 × (0.02 / 0.5)
e = 100 × 0.04
Answer: 4 V

Q 5.40

A galvanometer of 40 Ω resistance gives full scale deflection at 1 mA. Find the series resistance to convert it into 5 V voltmeter.

R = V/Ig − G = 5/0.001 − 40
R = 5000 − 40
Answer: 4960 Ω

Q 5.41

A galvanometer of 20 Ω resistance gives full scale deflection at 2 mA. Find shunt required to make 1 A ammeter.

S = IgG / (I − Ig)
= (0.002 × 20) / (1 − 0.002)
= 0.04 / 0.998
Answer: 0.040 Ω (approx.)

Quick Revision / Unit Summary 🎯

Full Unit in Short
  • Magnetic field is the region where magnetic force is experienced.
  • Moving charges and current produce magnetic field.
  • Lorentz force on moving charge: F = qvB sin θ.
  • Biot-Savart law gives magnetic field due to current element.
  • For long straight conductor: B = μ₀I / 2πr.
  • For circular loop center: B = μ₀I / 2R.
  • Magnetic dipole moment of loop: M = NIA.
  • Force on current carrying conductor: F = BIl sin θ.
  • Torque on coil: τ = NBIA sin θ.
  • Electromagnetic induction is production of emf due to change in flux.
  • Magnetic flux: Φ = BA cos θ.
  • Faraday’s law: e = −N(dΦ/dt).
  • Lenz’s law gives direction of induced current.
  • Moving coil galvanometer works on torque on current carrying coil in magnetic field.
  • Galvanometer + shunt → ammeter.
  • Galvanometer + high series resistance → voltmeter.
  • Diamagnetic materials are weakly repelled.
  • Paramagnetic materials are weakly attracted.
  • Ferromagnetic materials are strongly attracted and can be magnetized permanently.
Must Remember for Exam
  • Fleming’s left hand rule
  • Right hand thumb rule
  • B due to wire and loop formulas
  • Faraday’s laws + Lenz’s law
  • Shunt formula for ammeter
  • Series resistance formula for voltmeter
  • Diamagnetic vs paramagnetic vs ferromagnetic
Common Mistakes
  • Do not confuse tesla (unit of B) with weber (unit of flux).
  • Do not use the straight wire formula for circular loop numericals.
  • Do not forget the negative sign in Faraday’s law indicates Lenz’s law.
  • Ammeter is connected in series, voltmeter in parallel.
  • Ammeter has low resistance, voltmeter has high resistance.
