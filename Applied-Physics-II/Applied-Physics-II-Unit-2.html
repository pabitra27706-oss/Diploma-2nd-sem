Basic Optical Laws — Reflection & Refraction 🔥

Nature of Light

Light is a form of electromagnetic radiation that is visible to the human eye. It is a transverse wave that does not need a medium to travel. The speed of light in vacuum is approximately 3 × 10⁸ m/s (the fastest speed in the universe!).

Important Terms in Optics

Ray of Light

A ray is the straight-line path along which light energy travels. It is represented by a straight line with an arrow showing the direction of propagation.

Beam of Light

A beam is a collection (bundle) of rays. It can be parallel, convergent, or divergent.

Normal

The normal is an imaginary line drawn perpendicular (at 90°) to the surface at the point where the ray hits the surface. All angles in optics are measured from the normal.

⭐ Reflection of Light

Definition — Reflection

Reflection is the phenomenon of bouncing back of light into the same medium when it strikes a surface (like a mirror).

Key terms:

  • Incident ray: The ray of light that falls on the surface.
  • Reflected ray: The ray of light that bounces back from the surface.
  • Angle of incidence (i): Angle between the incident ray and the normal.
  • Angle of reflection (r): Angle between the reflected ray and the normal.
  • Point of incidence: The point where the incident ray meets the surface.

Laws of Reflection

Laws of Reflection
  1. First Law: The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence, all lie in the same plane.
  2. Second Law: The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection. i.e., ∠i = ∠r

These laws are valid for all types of reflecting surfaces — plane mirrors, curved mirrors, and even rough surfaces.

⭐ Refraction of Light

Definition — Refraction

Refraction is the phenomenon of change in the direction (bending) of light when it passes from one transparent medium to another (e.g., from air to glass, or from water to air). This bending occurs because the speed of light is different in different media.

Real-life examples of refraction:

  • A stick appears bent when partially dipped in water
  • A coin at the bottom of a water-filled bowl appears raised up
  • Twinkling of stars
  • A swimming pool appears shallower than it actually is
  • Mirage in deserts

Key terms:

  • Incident ray: The ray entering the second medium.
  • Refracted ray: The ray that bends as it enters the second medium.
  • Angle of incidence (i): Angle between incident ray and normal (in medium 1).
  • Angle of refraction (r): Angle between refracted ray and normal (in medium 2).

Laws of Refraction (Snell's Law)

Laws of Refraction
  1. First Law: The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal at the point of incidence, all lie in the same plane.
  2. Second Law (Snell's Law): The ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence to the sine of the angle of refraction is a constant for a given pair of media. This constant is called the refractive index (μ or n).

Formula 2.1 — Snell's Law:

\[ \frac{\sin i}{\sin r} = \mu = \text{constant (refractive index)} \]

Or more generally: \( n_1 \sin i = n_2 \sin r \)

where n₁ = refractive index of medium 1 (where light comes from), n₂ = refractive index of medium 2 (where light goes into).

⭐ Refractive Index

Definition — Refractive Index (μ or n)

The refractive index of a medium is the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum (or air) to the speed of light in that medium.

\[ \mu = \frac{c}{v} = \frac{\text{Speed of light in vacuum}}{\text{Speed of light in medium}} \]

It is a dimensionless quantity (no unit). It is always ≥ 1.

Key Points about Refractive Index
  • Refractive index of vacuum = 1 (exactly)
  • Refractive index of air ≈ 1.0003 ≈ 1 (for practical purposes)
  • Higher μ means light travels slower in that medium (denser medium)
  • A medium with higher μ is called optically denser
  • When light goes from rarer to denser medium → it bends towards the normal (r < i)
  • When light goes from denser to rarer medium → it bends away from the normal (r > i)

Memory Trick

Diamond has the highest refractive index (2.42) among common materials. That's why diamonds sparkle so much — light bends a lot inside and gets trapped by total internal reflection! Water = 4/3 ≈ 1.33, Glass = 3/2 = 1.5 — these fractions are easy to remember.

Common Mistake

Students often confuse: light bends towards normal when entering a denser medium (not rarer!). Remember: Denser = towards, Rarer = away.

Thin Lenses & Image Formation 🔥🔥

What is a Lens?

Definition — Lens

A lens is a transparent optical medium (usually glass) bounded by two curved surfaces (or one curved and one plane surface). A lens refracts light to form images. A thin lens is one whose thickness is very small compared to its radius of curvature.

Types of Lenses

1. Convex Lens (Converging Lens)

Convex Lens

A lens that is thicker at the centre and thinner at the edges. It converges (brings together) parallel rays of light to a point called the focus. Also called a converging lens or positive lens.

Types: Double convex (biconvex), plano-convex, concavo-convex.

2. Concave Lens (Diverging Lens)

Concave Lens

A lens that is thinner at the centre and thicker at the edges. It diverges (spreads out) parallel rays of light. The diverging rays appear to come from a point called the virtual focus. Also called a diverging lens or negative lens.

Types: Double concave (biconcave), plano-concave, convexo-concave.

Important Terms for Lenses

Optical Centre (O)

The central point of the lens. A ray passing through the optical centre goes straight without bending.

Principal Axis

The imaginary straight line passing through the optical centre and perpendicular to the lens surface. It connects the two centres of curvature.

Principal Focus (F)

For a convex lens: The point on the principal axis where parallel rays of light converge after refraction. It is a real focus.

For a concave lens: The point on the principal axis from which parallel rays appear to diverge after refraction. It is a virtual focus.

Every lens has two foci (F₁ and F₂), one on each side.

Focal Length (f)

The distance between the optical centre (O) and the principal focus (F). SI Unit: metre (m).

Convex lens: f is positive. Concave lens: f is negative.

Sign Convention (New Cartesian Convention) 🔥

Sign Convention Rules
  1. All distances are measured from the optical centre (O) of the lens.
  2. Distances measured in the direction of incident light (usually left to right) are positive (+).
  3. Distances measured opposite to the direction of incident light (right to left) are negative (−).
  4. Heights measured above the principal axis are positive (+).
  5. Heights measured below the principal axis are negative (−).

Practical implications:

  • Object distance (u) is always negative (object is on the left of lens).
  • For convex lens: f is positive. For concave lens: f is negative.
  • Real image: v is positive. Virtual image: v is negative.
Most Common Exam Mistake!

Forgetting sign convention is the #1 reason students get wrong answers in optics numericals. ALWAYS apply sign convention before substituting values into the lens formula. Object distance u is always negative!

Rules for Drawing Ray Diagrams (Lenses)

Three Rules for Ray Diagrams
  1. Rule 1: A ray parallel to the principal axis, after refraction, passes through the principal focus F₂ (convex) or appears to come from F₁ (concave).
  2. Rule 2: A ray passing through the optical centre O goes straight without bending.
  3. Rule 3: A ray passing through the focus F₁, after refraction, becomes parallel to the principal axis.

To locate the image, draw any two of these three rays. Where they meet (or appear to meet) is the image point.

Exam Tip

For 5-mark ray diagram questions: Draw a neat, large diagram with all labels (O, F₁, F₂, 2F₁, 2F₂, object, image). Use a ruler. Show at least 2 rays from the top of the object. Mention nature of image (real/virtual, erect/inverted, magnified/diminished). Diagrams carry marks!

Lens Formula, Lens Maker's Formula & Power of Lens 🔥🔥🔥

⭐ Thin Lens Formula

Formula 2.2 — Lens Formula:

\[ \frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f} \]

where:

  • v = image distance (from optical centre to image) (m)
  • u = object distance (from optical centre to object) (m)
  • f = focal length of the lens (m)

Sign convention must be applied!

Most Important Formula of This Unit!

The lens formula 1/v − 1/u = 1/f is used in almost every numerical. Remember: it's 1/v MINUS 1/u (not plus!). The mirror formula is 1/v + 1/u = 1/f — don't confuse them!

⭐ Magnification

Definition — Linear Magnification (m)

Magnification is the ratio of the size (height) of the image to the size (height) of the object. It tells us how many times larger or smaller the image is compared to the object.

Formula 2.3 — Magnification:

\[ m = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = \frac{v}{u} \]

where:

  • m = linear magnification (no unit)
  • hi = height of image (m)
  • ho = height of object (m)
  • v = image distance (m)
  • u = object distance (m)
Interpreting Magnification
  • |m| > 1 → Image is magnified (larger than object)
  • |m| = 1 → Image is same size as object
  • |m| < 1 → Image is diminished (smaller than object)
  • m is positive → Image is erect (virtual)
  • m is negative → Image is inverted (real)

⭐ Lens Maker's Formula (No Derivation — Formula Only)

Formula 2.4 — Lens Maker's Formula:

\[ \frac{1}{f} = (\mu - 1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) \]

where:

  • f = focal length of the lens (m)
  • μ = refractive index of the lens material
  • R₁ = radius of curvature of the first surface (m)
  • R₂ = radius of curvature of the second surface (m)
Why is it called "Lens Maker's Formula"?

This formula is used by lens manufacturers to design lenses. By knowing the required focal length and the refractive index of the glass, they can calculate what radii of curvature to grind the surfaces to. It connects the physical properties of the lens (material and shape) to its optical property (focal length).

Sign Convention for R₁ and R₂

For a convex lens: R₁ is positive (centre of curvature on right) and R₂ is negative (centre of curvature on left).

For a concave lens: R₁ is negative and R₂ is positive.

For a plane surface: R = ∞, so 1/R = 0.

⭐ Power of a Lens

Definition — Power of Lens

The power of a lens is the ability of the lens to converge or diverge rays of light. It is defined as the reciprocal of the focal length (when focal length is in metres).

Formula 2.5 — Power of Lens:

\[ P = \frac{1}{f} \]

where:

  • P = power of lens
  • f = focal length in metres
  • SI Unit: Dioptre (D)
  • 1 D = 1 m⁻¹
Key Points about Power
  • Convex lens: P is positive (converging power). Example: +2D, +5D
  • Concave lens: P is negative (diverging power). Example: −3D, −1.5D
  • Shorter focal length = higher power = stronger lens = more bending
  • Longer focal length = lower power = weaker lens = less bending
  • When your eye doctor prescribes spectacles as "+2.5D" or "−3D", they are specifying the power!

Power of Combination of Lenses

When two thin lenses are placed in contact, the combined power is:

Formula 2.6 — Combination of Lenses:

\[ P = P_1 + P_2 \]

Or equivalently: \[ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2} \]

This works for any number of thin lenses in contact: P = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ...

Quick Conversion

If f is given in cm, convert to m first: f(m) = f(cm)/100. Then P = 1/f(m).

Or directly: P = 100/f(cm) dioptre.

Example: f = 20 cm → P = 100/20 = +5 D (convex)

Example: f = −50 cm → P = 100/(−50) = −2 D (concave)

Exam Favourite

Numericals on lens formula, power, and magnification are asked every year. Always remember:

  • Lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f
  • Magnification: m = v/u = hi/ho
  • Power: P = 1/f (f in metres!) = 100/f(cm)
  • Apply sign convention FIRST, calculate, then interpret

Total Internal Reflection & Critical Angle 🔥🔥🔥

Understanding the Concept Step by Step

When light travels from a denser medium to a rarer medium (e.g., glass to air, water to air), it bends away from the normal (angle of refraction > angle of incidence).

Now imagine increasing the angle of incidence gradually:

  1. Small angle of incidence: Light refracts and goes into the rarer medium, bending away from normal. Some light is also reflected back (partial reflection).
  2. Increasing angle: The refracted ray bends further away from normal. The angle of refraction increases.
  3. At a certain angle (Critical Angle): The refracted ray travels along the surface (angle of refraction = 90°). This is the limit.
  4. Beyond the critical angle: No refraction at all! The light is completely reflected back into the denser medium. This is Total Internal Reflection!

⭐ Critical Angle

Definition — Critical Angle (θc or ic)

The critical angle is the angle of incidence in the denser medium for which the angle of refraction in the rarer medium is exactly 90° (i.e., the refracted ray grazes along the surface boundary).

Relation between Critical Angle and Refractive Index

Applying Snell's law at the critical angle:

\( n_1 \sin \theta_c = n_2 \sin 90° \)

Since sin 90° = 1:

\( n_1 \sin \theta_c = n_2 \)

Formula 2.7 — Critical Angle:

\[ \sin \theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1} = \frac{n_{rarer}}{n_{denser}} \]

If the rarer medium is air (n₂ ≈ 1):

\[ \sin \theta_c = \frac{1}{\mu} \quad \text{or} \quad \theta_c = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{\mu}\right) \]

where μ = refractive index of the denser medium.

Critical Angles of Common Materials (with air)
  • Water (μ = 1.33): θc = sin⁻¹(1/1.33) ≈ 48.8°
  • Glass (μ = 1.5): θc = sin⁻¹(1/1.5) ≈ 41.8°
  • Diamond (μ = 2.42): θc = sin⁻¹(1/2.42) ≈ 24.4°

Note: Higher refractive index → smaller critical angle → easier to achieve TIR!

⭐ Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

Definition — Total Internal Reflection

Total Internal Reflection (TIR) is the phenomenon in which a ray of light travelling from a denser medium to a rarer medium is completely reflected back into the denser medium when the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle.

⭐ Conditions for Total Internal Reflection

Two Necessary Conditions for TIR
  1. Condition 1: Light must travel from an optically denser medium to an optically rarer medium (e.g., glass to air, water to air, glass to water).
  2. Condition 2: The angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle (i > θc) for that pair of media.

BOTH conditions must be satisfied simultaneously for TIR to occur.

Very Important for Exam!

The two conditions for TIR are asked very frequently. Many students write only one condition. You MUST write BOTH: (1) denser to rarer medium, AND (2) angle of incidence > critical angle.

Why "Total"?

In ordinary reflection (like from a mirror), some light is absorbed by the surface. But in TIR, 100% of the light is reflected — no light is lost. That's why it's called "total" internal reflection. This makes it ideal for transmitting light without loss.

Everyday Examples of TIR

  • Sparkling of diamond: Diamond has a very small critical angle (24.4°), so light entering the diamond undergoes multiple TIR inside before coming out, creating intense sparkle.
  • Mirage: On hot roads, layers of air have different densities. Light from sky undergoes TIR near the hot road surface, creating an illusion of water.
  • Shining of air bubble in water: Light hits the air bubble (rarer) from water (denser) at angles > critical angle → TIR → bubble appears shiny.
  • Optical fiber: Uses TIR to transmit light signals over long distances (covered in next section).
  • Prism binoculars: Use TIR in prisms instead of mirrors for reflecting light.

Optical Fiber — Application of TIR 🔥🔥🔥

What is Optical Fiber?

Definition — Optical Fiber

An optical fiber is a thin, flexible, transparent strand (wire) of glass or plastic that can transmit light signals over very long distances with minimal loss by using the principle of Total Internal Reflection (TIR).

Real-world analogy: Think of a water slide at a park. Once you enter the tube, you keep sliding inside until you reach the bottom — you can't escape from the sides. Similarly, once light enters an optical fiber, it keeps bouncing inside the fiber by TIR and travels from one end to the other without escaping!

⭐ Structure of Optical Fiber

An optical fiber has three main parts:

Parts of Optical Fiber
  1. Core: The innermost cylindrical part made of high-purity glass or plastic. It has a higher refractive index (n₁). This is where light actually travels. Diameter: 8–100 μm (micrometres).
  2. Cladding: A layer surrounding the core, also made of glass or plastic but with a lower refractive index (n₂) (n₂ < n₁). The cladding ensures that TIR occurs at the core-cladding boundary. Diameter: ~125 μm.
  3. Jacket (Buffer Coating): An outer protective plastic layer that protects the fiber from physical damage, moisture, and external forces. It does not play any optical role.

Key condition: n₁ (core) > n₂ (cladding) — This is essential for TIR to work!

⭐ Mechanism of Light Propagation in Optical Fiber

Here is how light travels through an optical fiber step by step:

Working Principle
  1. A light signal (usually from a laser or LED) enters one end of the fiber at a suitable angle.
  2. The light enters the core (denser medium, higher refractive index n₁).
  3. When this light hits the core-cladding boundary, it is going from a denser medium (core) to a rarer medium (cladding) — the first condition for TIR is satisfied.
  4. If the light hits the boundary at an angle greater than the critical angle (θ > θc), Total Internal Reflection occurs — the second condition is also satisfied.
  5. The light is completely reflected back into the core. It then hits the opposite side of the core-cladding boundary and again undergoes TIR.
  6. This process repeats continuously — the light keeps zigzagging inside the core by repeated TIR.
  7. The light finally emerges from the other end of the fiber with very little energy loss.

Result: Light (carrying information/data) travels from one end to the other through the fiber, even if the fiber is bent or curved!

⭐ Applications of Optical Fiber

Applications of Optical Fiber
  1. Telecommunication: Optical fibers carry internet data, telephone signals, and cable TV signals at the speed of light over thousands of kilometres. They are the backbone of the internet!
  2. Medical Endoscopy: Thin optical fiber bundles are inserted into the body through mouth, nose, or small cuts. Doctors can see internal organs on a screen without surgery. Used for stomach, intestine, and joint examinations.
  3. Computer Networking (LAN/WAN): Used for high-speed data transfer between computers in offices, data centres, and buildings.
  4. Military & Defence: Used in guided missiles, secure communication systems (hard to tap).
  5. Decorative lighting: Fiber optic lamps and decorative displays use fibers to create beautiful light effects.
  6. Sensors: Optical fiber sensors measure temperature, pressure, strain, and chemical concentrations in industrial and scientific applications.
  7. CCTV & Security: Fiber optic cables transmit security camera signals.
  8. Submarine cables: Undersea optical fiber cables connect continents for global internet connectivity.

Optical Instruments — Simple & Compound Microscope 🔥🔥

What are Optical Instruments?

Optical instruments are devices that use lenses (and/or mirrors) to produce magnified images of objects that are too small or too far away to see clearly with the naked eye. They help us see things better!

Examples: Magnifying glass, microscope, telescope, camera, binoculars, spectacles.

Least Distance of Distinct Vision (D)

Definition — Least Distance of Distinct Vision (D)

The minimum distance from the eye at which an object can be seen clearly and comfortably without strain. For a normal human eye, this distance is approximately 25 cm (0.25 m). This is also called the near point of the eye.

D = 25 cm (standard value used in all formulas)

⭐ Simple Microscope (Magnifying Glass)

Definition — Simple Microscope

A simple microscope is a single convex lens of short focal length used to see magnified, erect, and virtual images of small objects. It is essentially a magnifying glass.

Construction

A simple microscope consists of just a single convex lens of short focal length (typically 2 cm to 10 cm) mounted in a frame with a handle.

Working

  1. The object to be viewed is placed between the optical centre (O) and the focus (F) of the convex lens (i.e., within the focal length: u < f).
  2. When the object is placed this way, the convex lens forms a virtual, erect, and magnified image on the same side as the object.
  3. For comfortable viewing, the image should be formed at the least distance of distinct vision (D = 25 cm) from the eye.
  4. The eye sees this magnified virtual image clearly.

Magnifying Power (Magnification)

Definition — Magnifying Power

The magnifying power (M) of an optical instrument is the ratio of the angle subtended by the image at the eye when seen through the instrument to the angle subtended by the object at the naked eye when placed at the least distance of distinct vision (D = 25 cm).

Formula 2.8 — Magnifying Power of Simple Microscope:

When image is formed at D (25 cm):

\[ M = 1 + \frac{D}{f} \]

When image is formed at infinity (∞) — for relaxed eye:

\[ M = \frac{D}{f} \]

where:

  • M = magnifying power (no unit)
  • D = least distance of distinct vision = 25 cm
  • f = focal length of the convex lens (in same unit as D, usually cm)
Which Formula to Use?

If the question says "image at near point" or "image at D" → use M = 1 + D/f

If the question says "normal adjustment" or "image at infinity" → use M = D/f

If nothing is specified, use M = 1 + D/f (maximum magnification).

Uses of Simple Microscope
  • Reading small print (magnifying glass)
  • Watchmakers examining tiny watch parts
  • Jewellers examining gems and jewellery
  • Biologists examining specimens
  • Stamp and coin collectors examining details
  • Forensic investigators examining evidence

⭐ Compound Microscope 🔥

Definition — Compound Microscope

A compound microscope is an optical instrument that uses two convex lenses — an objective lens and an eyepiece (ocular) lens — to produce a highly magnified image of very small objects that cannot be seen by a simple microscope.

Construction

Parts of Compound Microscope
  1. Objective Lens (O): A convex lens of very short focal length (fo) and small aperture. Placed near the object. It creates the first (intermediate) image.
  2. Eyepiece Lens (E): A convex lens of short focal length (fe) (but larger than fo) and large aperture. Placed near the eye. It magnifies the intermediate image further.
  3. Tube: Both lenses are mounted in a metal tube at fixed positions. The tube length (distance between the lenses) is denoted by L.

Key: fo < fe (objective has shorter focal length than eyepiece)

Working

  1. The object (AB) is placed slightly beyond the focus of the objective lens (between Fo and 2Fo).
  2. The objective lens forms a real, inverted, and magnified intermediate image (A'B') on the other side of the objective lens (inside the tube).
  3. This intermediate image A'B' acts as the object for the eyepiece lens.
  4. The eyepiece is adjusted so that A'B' falls within the focal length of the eyepiece (between O and Fe).
  5. The eyepiece then acts as a simple microscope and forms a final virtual, magnified image (A''B'') at the least distance of distinct vision (D = 25 cm).
  6. The final image is highly magnified, virtual, and inverted (with respect to the original object).

Magnifying Power of Compound Microscope

The total magnification = magnification by objective × magnification by eyepiece.

Formula 2.9 — Magnifying Power of Compound Microscope:

When final image is at D (25 cm):

\[ M = m_o \times M_e = \frac{v_o}{u_o} \times \left(1 + \frac{D}{f_e}\right) \]

Approximately (for large magnification):

\[ M \approx \frac{L}{f_o} \times \frac{D}{f_e} \]

When final image is at infinity (normal adjustment):

\[ M = \frac{L}{f_o} \times \frac{D}{f_e} \]

where:

  • M = total magnifying power
  • mo = magnification of objective = vo/uo
  • Me = magnifying power of eyepiece
  • L = length of the microscope tube (distance between lenses) ≈ vo
  • fo = focal length of objective lens
  • fe = focal length of eyepiece lens
  • D = 25 cm (least distance of distinct vision)
Key Points about Compound Microscope
  • The final image is inverted (upside-down) with respect to the object.
  • The total magnification is the product of the two individual magnifications.
  • To get high magnification, both fo and fe should be small.
  • fo should be much smaller than fe for best results.
  • Typical magnification: 50× to 2000×.
  • Used to observe bacteria, cells, blood samples, tissue structure, etc.

Astronomical Telescope (Refracting) 🔥🔥

What is a Telescope?

Definition — Telescope

A telescope is an optical instrument used to see distant objects (like stars, planets, moon, far-away buildings) that appear too small to the naked eye. It makes distant objects appear closer and larger.

We will study the refracting astronomical telescope (the simplest type), which uses only lenses (no mirrors).

⭐ Construction of Refracting Astronomical Telescope

Parts
  1. Objective Lens (O): A convex lens of large focal length (fo) and large aperture. Placed towards the distant object. It collects light from the distant object and forms the first image.
  2. Eyepiece Lens (E): A convex lens of short focal length (fe) and small aperture. Placed near the eye. It magnifies the first image for viewing.
  3. Tube: Both lenses mounted in two concentric tubes (one slides inside the other for focusing).

Key: fo >> fe (objective has MUCH larger focal length than eyepiece) — this is opposite to the compound microscope!

Don't Confuse!

In a microscope: fo < fe (objective short, eyepiece longer)

In a telescope: fo > fe (objective long, eyepiece short)

This is a common exam trick question!

⭐ Working (Normal Adjustment — Image at Infinity)

  1. Light from a very distant object (at infinity) reaches the objective lens as parallel rays.
  2. The objective lens converges these parallel rays and forms a real, inverted, and diminished image (A'B') at its focus Fo.
  3. The eyepiece is positioned such that this intermediate image A'B' falls at the focus of the eyepiece (Fe).
  4. Since the intermediate image is at the focus of the eyepiece, the eyepiece acts like a magnifying glass and produces the final image at infinity (parallel rays emerge from eyepiece).
  5. The final image is magnified, virtual, and inverted with respect to the object.
  6. In normal adjustment, the distance between the two lenses = fo + fe (length of telescope).

  • For high magnification: fo should be large and fe should be small.
  • The objective has a large aperture to collect maximum light from distant objects (which are faint).
  • The image is inverted — this is acceptable for astronomy (stars look the same upside down!), but for terrestrial use, an additional lens is needed to erect the image.
  • In normal adjustment, the eye is relaxed (rays entering eye are parallel) — most comfortable for extended viewing.

Interference and Diffraction of Light (Qualitative) 🔥

Syllabus Note

The syllabus mentions "Qualitative ideas only" — meaning you need to understand the basic concept, definition, examples, and differences. No mathematical derivations or complex theory is needed for the exam.

⭐ Interference of Light

Definition — Interference

Interference of light is the phenomenon of redistribution of light energy in a medium due to the superposition of two coherent light waves. It results in the formation of alternate bright and dark bands (fringes) on a screen.

Simple explanation: When two light waves from the same source meet at a point, they can either add up (constructive interference → bright) or cancel out (destructive interference → dark). The result is a pattern of alternating bright and dark bands.

Types of Interference

Constructive & Destructive Interference

Constructive Interference:

  • Occurs when crest meets crest (or trough meets trough).
  • Waves are in phase (phase difference = 0, 2π, 4π...)
  • Path difference = nλ (n = 0, 1, 2, 3...)
  • Resultant amplitude is maximum → BRIGHT fringe

Destructive Interference:

  • Occurs when crest meets trough.
  • Waves are out of phase (phase difference = π, 3π, 5π...)
  • Path difference = (2n+1)λ/2 or (n + ½)λ
  • Resultant amplitude is minimum (zero if equal amplitudes) → DARK fringe

Conditions for Sustained Interference

  1. The two light sources must be coherent (same frequency, constant phase difference).
  2. The sources should be monochromatic (single wavelength/colour).
  3. The sources should be close together.
  4. The amplitudes should be equal or nearly equal for best contrast.
Coherent Sources

Coherent sources are two light sources that emit waves of the same frequency (or wavelength) and have a constant phase difference between them. Two independent light bulbs are NOT coherent. Coherent sources can be obtained by splitting light from a single source (e.g., Young's double-slit experiment).

Young's Double Slit Experiment

The most famous demonstration of interference. A single light source illuminates two narrow parallel slits (S₁ and S₂) close together. On a screen placed behind, alternate bright and dark fringes are observed. This proves that light behaves as a wave.

  • Colours seen in thin soap bubbles
  • Colours on an oil film on water (petrol on wet road)
  • Colours in a CD/DVD surface

⭐ Diffraction of Light

Definition — Diffraction

Diffraction of light is the phenomenon of bending of light around the edges of an obstacle or through a narrow opening (slit), causing it to spread into regions where it should not reach as per straight-line propagation (shadow region).

Simple explanation: Light normally travels in straight lines. But when light passes through a very narrow slit (comparable to its wavelength), it spreads out and bends around the edges. This is diffraction. It's like sound going around corners — you can hear someone talking even if they are around the corner of a wall.

Key Points about Diffraction

  • Diffraction is most noticeable when the size of the opening/obstacle is comparable to the wavelength of light.
  • Since the wavelength of visible light is very small (400–700 nm), diffraction of light is observed only through very narrow slits.
  • Diffraction produces a pattern of bright and dark bands, but unlike interference, the central bright band is widest and brightest, and the intensity decreases for higher-order bands.
  • Diffraction is evidence that light is a wave.

Solved Numerical Problems 🔥🔥

Numerical Solving Strategy for Optics
  1. Read problem carefully → identify lens type (convex/concave)
  2. Write Given, To Find
  3. Apply sign convention FIRST — this is crucial!
  4. Choose correct formula
  5. Substitute and solve
  6. Interpret the answer (positive v = real image, negative v = virtual image)

Numerical 1: Lens Formula — Convex Lens

Problem

An object is placed 30 cm from a convex lens of focal length 20 cm. Find the position and nature of the image. Also find the magnification.

Solution

u = −30 cm (negative by sign convention — object on left)

f = +20 cm (positive for convex lens)

Lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f

1/v − 1/(−30) = 1/20

1/v + 1/30 = 1/20

1/v = 1/20 − 1/30 = (3 − 2)/60 = 1/60

v = +60 cm

Positive v → Image is real, formed on the other side of the lens, 60 cm from the lens.

m = v/u = 60/(−30) = −2

m = −2

|m| = 2 → Image is magnified (2 times the object size).

Negative m → Image is inverted.

Nature: Real, inverted, magnified, at 60 cm on the other side.

Numerical 2: Lens Formula — Concave Lens

Problem

An object is placed 20 cm in front of a concave lens of focal length 15 cm. Find the position, nature, and size of the image if the object height is 5 cm.

Solution

u = −20 cm, f = −15 cm (concave lens → negative f)

ho = 5 cm

1/v − 1/u = 1/f

1/v − 1/(−20) = 1/(−15)

1/v + 1/20 = −1/15

1/v = −1/15 − 1/20 = (−4 − 3)/60 = −7/60

v = −60/7 = −8.57 cm

Negative v → Image is virtual, formed on the same side as the object.

m = v/u = (−8.57)/(−20) = +0.4286

hi = m × ho = 0.4286 × 5

hi = +2.14 cm

Positive hi → Image is erect.

|m| = 0.43 < 1 → Image is diminished.

Nature: Virtual, erect, diminished, 8.57 cm from lens on same side as object.

Numerical 3: Power of Lens

Problem

(a) Find the power of a convex lens of focal length 25 cm.

(b) Find the power of a concave lens of focal length 50 cm.

(c) If both lenses are placed in contact, find the combined power and the focal length of the combination.

Solution

f₁ = +25 cm = +0.25 m

P₁ = 1/f₁ = 1/0.25

P₁ = +4 D

f₂ = −50 cm = −0.50 m

P₂ = 1/f₂ = 1/(−0.50)

P₂ = −2 D

P = P₁ + P₂ = +4 + (−2) = +2 D

P = +2 D (acts as convex lens)

f = 1/P = 1/2 = 0.5 m = 50 cm

f = 50 cm (convex)

Numerical 4: Lens Maker's Formula

Problem

A double convex lens is made of glass of refractive index 1.5. Its two surfaces have radii of curvature 20 cm and 30 cm. Find its focal length.

Solution

μ = 1.5, R₁ = +20 cm (first surface convex), R₂ = −30 cm (second surface convex → R₂ negative)

\(\frac{1}{f} = (\mu - 1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right)\)

\(\frac{1}{f} = (1.5 - 1)\left(\frac{1}{20} - \frac{1}{-30}\right)\)

\(\frac{1}{f} = 0.5 \times \left(\frac{1}{20} + \frac{1}{30}\right)\)

\(\frac{1}{f} = 0.5 \times \left(\frac{3 + 2}{60}\right) = 0.5 \times \frac{5}{60} = 0.5 \times \frac{1}{12}\)

\(\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{24}\)

f = 24 cm

Numerical 5: Critical Angle

Problem

The refractive index of glass is 1.5. Calculate the critical angle for glass-air interface.

Solution

μ = 1.5 (glass), rarer medium = air (n = 1)

sin θc = 1/μ = 1/1.5 = 0.6667

θc = sin⁻¹(0.6667)

θc = 41.8° ≈ 42°

Numerical 6: Simple Microscope

Problem

A simple microscope has a focal length of 5 cm. Calculate the magnifying power when (a) image is at the near point (D = 25 cm), (b) image is at infinity.

Solution

f = 5 cm, D = 25 cm

M = 1 + D/f = 1 + 25/5 = 1 + 5

M = 6

M = D/f = 25/5

M = 5

Numerical 7: Compound Microscope

Problem

A compound microscope has an objective of focal length 1 cm and an eyepiece of focal length 5 cm. The tube length is 20 cm. Calculate the magnifying power in normal adjustment. (D = 25 cm)

Solution

fo = 1 cm, fe = 5 cm, L = 20 cm, D = 25 cm

M = (L/fo) × (D/fe) = (20/1) × (25/5)

M = 20 × 5

M = 100

The microscope magnifies the object 100 times.

Numerical 8: Astronomical Telescope

Problem

An astronomical telescope has an objective of focal length 100 cm and an eyepiece of focal length 5 cm. Calculate (a) the magnifying power in normal adjustment, (b) the length of the telescope.

Solution

fo = 100 cm, fe = 5 cm

M = fo/fe = 100/5

M = 20

L = fo + fe = 100 + 5

L = 105 cm

Numerical 9: Snell's Law

Problem

A ray of light passes from air into a glass slab of refractive index 1.5. If the angle of incidence is 30°, find the angle of refraction.

Solution

n₁ = 1 (air), n₂ = 1.5 (glass), i = 30°

n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r

1 × sin 30° = 1.5 × sin r

0.5 = 1.5 × sin r

sin r = 0.5/1.5 = 0.3333

r = sin⁻¹(0.3333)

r = 19.47° ≈ 19.5°

The ray bends towards the normal (r < i) because it goes from rarer to denser medium. ✓

Numerical 10: Magnification & Image Height

Problem

An object 6 cm high is placed at 15 cm from a convex lens of focal length 10 cm. Find the position, nature, and height of the image.

Solution

ho = 6 cm, u = −15 cm, f = +10 cm

1/v − 1/(−15) = 1/10

1/v + 1/15 = 1/10

1/v = 1/10 − 1/15 = (3 − 2)/30 = 1/30

v = +30 cm (real image, on opposite side)

m = v/u = 30/(−15) = −2

hi = m × ho = −2 × 6

hi = −12 cm

Negative height → Image is inverted.

|hi| = 12 cm > 6 cm → Image is magnified (2 times).

Nature: Real, inverted, magnified, 12 cm tall, 30 cm from lens.

Numerical 11: Refractive Index from Critical Angle

Problem

The critical angle for a medium with air is 30°. Find the refractive index of the medium. Also find the speed of light in this medium. (Speed of light in vacuum = 3 × 10⁸ m/s)

Solution

θc = 30°, c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s

sin θc = 1/μ

sin 30° = 1/μ

0.5 = 1/μ

μ = 2

μ = c/v → v = c/μ = (3 × 10⁸)/2

v = 1.5 × 10⁸ m/s

Numerical 12: Combination of Lenses

Problem

Two thin lenses of focal lengths +20 cm and −30 cm are placed in contact. Find the focal length and power of the combination. Is the combination converging or diverging?

Solution

f₁ = +20 cm, f₂ = −30 cm

1/f = 1/f₁ + 1/f₂ = 1/20 + 1/(−30) = 1/20 − 1/30

1/f = (3 − 2)/60 = 1/60

f = +60 cm

P = 100/f(cm) = 100/60

P = +1.67 D

Since f is positive and P is positive, the combination acts as a converging (convex) lens.

Important Definitions

Exam Strategy

Definition questions carry 2 marks. Write the definition, add the formula (if any), mention the SI unit, and give one example if possible. This guarantees full marks!

1. Reflection of Light

The phenomenon of bouncing back of light into the same medium when it strikes a surface. Laws: (i) Incident ray, reflected ray and normal lie in the same plane. (ii) Angle of incidence = angle of reflection (∠i = ∠r).

2. Refraction of Light

The phenomenon of change in direction (bending) of light when it passes from one transparent medium to another, due to the change in speed of light. Governed by Snell's law: n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r.

3. Refractive Index (μ or n)

The ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to the speed of light in the medium. μ = c/v. It is dimensionless and always ≥ 1. Higher μ means optically denser medium. Example: Glass μ = 1.5, Water μ = 1.33.

4. Lens

A transparent optical medium bounded by two curved surfaces (or one curved and one plane surface) that refracts light to form images. Two types: convex (converging) and concave (diverging).

5. Focal Length (f)

The distance between the optical centre and the principal focus of a lens. For convex lens, f is positive; for concave lens, f is negative. SI Unit: metre (m).

6. Power of a Lens

The ability of a lens to converge or diverge light rays. It is the reciprocal of the focal length in metres. P = 1/f. SI Unit: Dioptre (D). Convex → +ve power; Concave → −ve power.

7. Linear Magnification (m)

The ratio of the height (size) of the image to the height (size) of the object. m = hi/ho = v/u. Positive m means erect image; negative m means inverted image. |m| > 1 means magnified.

8. Critical Angle (θc)

The angle of incidence in the denser medium for which the angle of refraction in the rarer medium is exactly 90° (refracted ray grazes along the surface). sin θc = 1/μ. It exists only when light goes from denser to rarer medium.

9. Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

The phenomenon in which a ray of light travelling from a denser medium to a rarer medium is completely reflected back into the denser medium when the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle. Conditions: (1) Light from denser to rarer medium. (2) Angle of incidence > critical angle.

10. Optical Fiber

A thin, flexible strand of glass or plastic that transmits light signals over long distances by the principle of Total Internal Reflection. It has a core (higher RI), cladding (lower RI), and protective jacket.

11. Magnifying Power

The ratio of the angle subtended by the image at the eye (when using the instrument) to the angle subtended by the object at the naked eye when placed at the least distance of distinct vision (D = 25 cm).

12. Least Distance of Distinct Vision (D)

The minimum distance from the eye at which an object can be seen clearly without strain. For a normal human eye, D = 25 cm. Also called the near point of the eye.

13. Simple Microscope

A single convex lens of short focal length used to see magnified, erect, and virtual images of small nearby objects. Magnifying power: M = 1 + D/f (at near point). Also called magnifying glass.

14. Compound Microscope

An optical instrument using two convex lenses (objective: short fo and eyepiece: longer fe) to produce a highly magnified, virtual, inverted image of very small objects. M = (L/fo)(D/fe).

15. Astronomical Telescope

An optical instrument using two convex lenses (objective: large fo and eyepiece: short fe) to see magnified images of distant objects like stars and planets. M = fo/fe (normal adjustment). Tube length L = fo + fe.

16. Interference of Light

The phenomenon of redistribution of light energy due to superposition of two coherent light waves, producing alternate bright (constructive) and dark (destructive) fringes. Example: Young's double-slit experiment, soap film colours.

17. Diffraction of Light

The phenomenon of bending of light around the edges of an obstacle or through a narrow opening (comparable to wavelength), causing it to spread into the shadow region. Example: Colours on CD surface.

18. Coherent Sources

Two light sources that emit waves of the same frequency and maintain a constant phase difference between them. Required for sustained interference pattern. Obtained by splitting light from a single source.

Short Answer Questions (2 Marks)

Q1. State the laws of refraction of light.

First Law: The incident ray, refracted ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.

Second Law (Snell's Law): The ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence to the sine of the angle of refraction is a constant for a given pair of media. n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r.

Q2. Define refractive index. What is its SI unit?

Refractive index (μ) of a medium is the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to the speed of light in that medium. μ = c/v. It is a dimensionless quantity (no unit). It is always ≥ 1. Higher μ means optically denser medium (light travels slower).

Q3. Define power of a lens and give its unit.

Power of a lens is the ability of the lens to converge or diverge light rays. It is defined as the reciprocal of the focal length in metres: P = 1/f. Its SI unit is Dioptre (D), where 1 D = 1 m⁻¹. Convex lens has positive power, concave lens has negative power.

Q4. State the two conditions for Total Internal Reflection.

Condition 1: Light must travel from an optically denser medium to a rarer medium (e.g., glass to air).

Condition 2: The angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle (i > θc) for that pair of media.

Both conditions must be satisfied simultaneously for TIR to occur.

Q5. What is critical angle? How is it related to refractive index?

Critical angle (θc) is the angle of incidence in the denser medium for which the angle of refraction in the rarer medium is exactly 90° (the refracted ray grazes along the surface).

Relation: sin θc = 1/μ, where μ is the refractive index of the denser medium with respect to the rarer medium. Higher μ → smaller θc.

Q6. What is the difference between the objective and eyepiece in a compound microscope?

Objective: Convex lens of very short focal length (fo) and small aperture. Placed near the object. Creates the first real, inverted, magnified intermediate image.

Eyepiece: Convex lens of short focal length (fe) but fe > fo, with larger aperture. Placed near the eye. Magnifies the intermediate image further to produce the final virtual image.

Q7. Define interference of light. Give two examples.

Interference is the phenomenon of redistribution of light energy due to superposition of two coherent light waves, resulting in alternate bright and dark fringes.

Examples: (1) Colours seen in thin soap bubbles. (2) Colours of oil film on water surface. (3) Young's double-slit experiment.

Q8. What is an optical fiber? Name its three parts.

Optical fiber is a thin, flexible strand of glass or plastic that transmits light signals over long distances using Total Internal Reflection.

Three parts: (1) Core — innermost, higher refractive index, carries light. (2) Cladding — surrounds core, lower refractive index, causes TIR. (3) Jacket — outer protective plastic covering.

Q9. Why does a diamond sparkle so much?

Diamond has a very high refractive index (μ = 2.42), which means its critical angle is very small (about 24.4°). When light enters a well-cut diamond, it undergoes multiple total internal reflections inside the diamond before finally emerging. This repeated TIR causes the diamond to sparkle brilliantly.

Q10. What is the difference between interference and diffraction?

Interference: Caused by superposition of waves from two coherent sources. All fringes have equal width and equal brightness. Needs two sources.

Diffraction: Caused by bending of waves around edges of an obstacle or through a narrow slit. Central fringe is widest and brightest. Needs only one source and a slit/obstacle.

Long Answer Questions (5–7 Marks)

Q1. What is Total Internal Reflection? State its conditions. Explain with diagram. Give three examples. [7 marks] 🔥🔥

Total Internal Reflection (TIR) is the complete reflection of a light ray back into the denser medium when it hits the boundary of a rarer medium at an angle greater than the critical angle.

Conditions:

  1. Light must travel from an optically denser medium to a rarer medium.
  2. The angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle (i > θc).

Critical angle (θc): The angle of incidence for which the angle of refraction is 90°. sin θc = 1/μ.

Explanation: When light goes from denser to rarer medium, it bends away from normal. As angle of incidence increases, angle of refraction increases faster. At critical angle, refracted ray travels along the surface (r = 90°). Beyond critical angle, no refraction occurs — all light reflects back into the denser medium.

[Draw diagram showing 3 cases: (a) i < θc: partial refraction, (b) i = θc: ray along surface, (c) i > θc: total internal reflection]

Examples: (1) Sparkling of diamond (2) Optical fiber communication (3) Mirage in deserts (4) Shining of air bubble in water.

Q2. Explain the principle and working of an optical fiber with a neat diagram. State its applications. [7 marks] 🔥🔥

Principle: Optical fiber works on the principle of Total Internal Reflection (TIR).

Structure: Three parts — (1) Core: high RI (n₁), carries light; (2) Cladding: low RI (n₂ < n₁), causes TIR; (3) Jacket: protective outer covering.

Working:

  1. Light enters the core from one end at a suitable angle.
  2. At the core-cladding boundary, light goes from denser (core) to rarer (cladding) → first condition for TIR satisfied.
  3. If the angle of incidence at the boundary > critical angle → TIR occurs.
  4. Light bounces back into the core, hits the other side, again TIR.
  5. This zigzag path by repeated TIR continues along the entire fiber.
  6. Light emerges from the other end with minimal loss.

[Draw diagram: cylindrical fiber with core (shaded) and cladding, showing zigzag light path inside core by repeated TIR]

Applications: (1) Telecommunication/Internet (2) Medical endoscopy (3) Computer networking (4) CCTV and security (5) Decorative lighting (6) Sensors for temperature/pressure.

Q3. Draw a neat ray diagram of a compound microscope and derive the expression for its magnifying power. [7 marks] 🔥

Construction: Two convex lenses — Objective (short fo, small aperture, near object) and Eyepiece (fe > fo, larger aperture, near eye).

Working:

  1. Object AB placed slightly beyond Fo of objective.
  2. Objective forms a real, inverted, magnified intermediate image A'B' inside the tube.
  3. A'B' falls within focal length of eyepiece.
  4. Eyepiece acts as a simple microscope and forms final virtual, magnified image A''B'' at D = 25 cm.

[Draw ray diagram showing: Object AB → Objective lens → Intermediate image A'B' (real, inverted, magnified) → Eyepiece lens → Final image A''B'' (virtual, magnified)]

Magnifying Power:

M = mo × Me

mo (magnification of objective) = vo/uo ≈ L/fo

Me (magnifying power of eyepiece) = 1 + D/fe (at D) or D/fe (at ∞)

At D: M = (L/fo)(1 + D/fe)

At ∞: M = (L/fo)(D/fe)

For high M: both fo and fe should be small.

Q4. Draw a neat ray diagram of an astronomical refracting telescope in normal adjustment. Derive expression for magnifying power and tube length. [7 marks] 🔥

Construction: Two convex lenses — Objective (large fo, large aperture, towards distant object) and Eyepiece (small fe, small aperture, near eye). fo >> fe.

Normal Adjustment (image at ∞):

  1. Parallel rays from distant object enter the objective.
  2. Objective forms a real, inverted, diminished image A'B' at its focus Fo.
  3. Eyepiece is positioned so that A'B' lies at Fe (focus of eyepiece).
  4. Since object for eyepiece is at its focus, final image is formed at infinity (parallel rays emerge).
  5. Eye sees a magnified, virtual, inverted image at infinity.

[Draw diagram: Parallel rays → Objective → converge at Fo = Fe → Eyepiece → Parallel rays to eye]

Magnifying Power:

M = angle subtended by image / angle subtended by object = β/α

tan α ≈ α = A'B'/fo and tan β ≈ β = A'B'/fe

M = fo/fe

Length of telescope: L = fo + fe

For high M: fo should be large and fe should be small.

Q5. State the lens formula and lens maker's formula. Explain each term. Solve: An object is placed 40 cm from a convex lens of focal length 15 cm. Find image position, nature, and magnification. [7 marks] 🔥

Lens Formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f

v = image distance, u = object distance, f = focal length. Apply sign convention.

Lens Maker's Formula: 1/f = (μ − 1)(1/R₁ − 1/R₂)

μ = refractive index, R₁ and R₂ = radii of curvature. Used by lens manufacturers.

Numerical Solution:

Given: u = −40 cm, f = +15 cm (convex)

1/v − 1/(−40) = 1/15

1/v + 1/40 = 1/15

1/v = 1/15 − 1/40 = (8 − 3)/120 = 5/120 = 1/24

v = +24 cm (positive → real image, on opposite side)

m = v/u = 24/(−40) = −0.6

|m| < 1 → diminished. Negative m → inverted.

Image: Real, inverted, diminished, at 24 cm from lens on opposite side.

[Draw shapes of both lenses and their action on parallel rays]

Concave Lens: Thinner at centre, diverges light, virtual focus, −ve focal length, always forms virtual, erect, diminished images. Used in correcting myopia (short-sightedness), peepholes in doors.

Q7. Explain constructive and destructive interference. What conditions are needed for sustained interference? [5 marks]

Constructive Interference: When two waves meet in phase (crest with crest), they reinforce each other. Resultant amplitude = sum of individual amplitudes. Produces bright fringe. Path difference = nλ.

Destructive Interference: When two waves meet out of phase (crest with trough), they cancel each other. Resultant amplitude = difference. Produces dark fringe. Path difference = (n + ½)λ.

Conditions for sustained interference:

  1. Sources must be coherent (same frequency, constant phase difference).
  2. Sources should be monochromatic (single wavelength).
  3. Sources should be close together.
  4. Amplitudes should be equal or nearly equal.

Example: Young's double slit experiment demonstrates sustained interference.

Q8. Describe a simple microscope. Draw its ray diagram and derive the formula for magnifying power when the image is at the near point. [5 marks]

Simple Microscope: A single convex lens of short focal length used as a magnifying glass.

Working: Object is placed between O and F of the convex lens. A virtual, erect, and magnified image is formed on the same side as the object, at D = 25 cm for comfortable viewing.

[Draw ray diagram: Object between O and F, two refracted rays diverging, extended back to form virtual image at D on the same side]

Derivation:

Magnifying power M = angle with instrument / angle without instrument = β/α

Without instrument: α ≈ h/D (object at D)

With instrument: β ≈ h/u (object at u from lens)

M = (h/u)/(h/D) = D/u

Using lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f, with v = −D (virtual image at near point):

1/(−D) − 1/u = 1/f → 1/u = −1/D − 1/f = −(1/D + 1/f)

M = D/u = D × (1/D + 1/f) = 1 + D/f

M = 1 + D/f (when image is at near point D)

L = fo + fe = 130 ... (ii)

P6. A biconvex lens has radii of curvature 25 cm and 50 cm. The refractive index of glass is 1.5. Find its focal length and power. [4 marks]

R₁ = +25 cm, R₂ = −50 cm (biconvex), μ = 1.5

1/f = (1.5 − 1)(1/25 − 1/(−50)) = 0.5 × (1/25 + 1/50)

= 0.5 × (2 + 1)/50 = 0.5 × 3/50 = 3/100

f = 100/3 = 33.33 cm = 0.3333 m

P = 1/f = 1/0.3333 = +3 D

f = 10 cm, D = 25 cm

P8. A ray of light enters water (μ = 4/3) from air at an angle of incidence of 45°. Find the angle of refraction. [3 marks]

n₁ = 1 (air), n₂ = 4/3 (water), i = 45°

n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r

1 × sin 45° = (4/3) × sin r

0.7071 = 1.333 × sin r

sin r = 0.7071/1.333 = 0.5303

r = sin⁻¹(0.5303) = 32.03° ≈ 32°

Light bends towards normal (r < i) as it enters denser medium. ✓
