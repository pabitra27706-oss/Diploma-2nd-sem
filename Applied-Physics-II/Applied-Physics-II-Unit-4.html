Basic Concepts: Current, Resistance, Conductance, Color Coding

Electric Current (I)

I = dQ/dt (rate of flow of charge). Unit: Ampere (A).

Direct Current (DC): constant direction. Alternating Current (AC): periodically reverses.

Resistance (R)

R = V/I. Unit: Ohm (Ω).

Specific resistance (ρ): ρ = R A / l, unit Ω·m.

Conductance (G) & Specific Conductance (σ)

G = 1/R (Siemens, S). σ = 1/ρ (S/m).

Factors Affecting Resistance
  • Length (R ∝ l)
  • Cross-sectional area (R ∝ 1/A)
  • Material (ρ)
  • Temperature (R increases with temp for metals)
Carbon Resistance & Colour Coding

4-band code: Band1 (1st digit), Band2 (2nd digit), Band3 (multiplier), Band4 (tolerance).

Brown(1), Black(0), Red(×100) = 10×100 = 1000Ω = 1kΩ, Gold(±5%)

Color Code Example

Find resistance for Yellow-Violet-Orange-Gold.

Yellow=4, Violet=7, Orange=×1000 = 47×1000 = 47 kΩ, Gold=±5%

Ohm's Law · Kirchhoff's Laws

Ohm's Law

At constant temperature, current through a conductor is proportional to voltage across it.

V = I R

Series Combination

R_eq = R₁ + R₂ + ...

Current same, voltage divides.

Parallel Combination

1/R_eq = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ...

Voltage same, current divides.

Kirchhoff's Laws

KCL (Current Law): Sum of currents entering a junction = sum leaving.

KVL (Voltage Law): Sum of EMFs = sum of IR drops in any closed loop.

Series-Parallel Problem

Three resistors 10Ω, 20Ω, 30Ω: first two in parallel, then series with third. Find equivalent resistance.

R_parallel = (10×20)/(10+20) = 200/30 = 6.67Ω
R_eq = 6.67 + 30 = 36.67Ω

Wheatstone Bridge · Carrey Foster Bridge

Wheatstone Bridge

Used to measure unknown resistance. Balance condition:

P/Q = R/S

where P, Q are known ratio arms, R unknown, S variable standard.

Carrey Foster Bridge

Modified Wheatstone bridge to measure small resistance differences. Used to compare nearly equal resistances and determine specific resistance.

Applications: Measuring low resistances, temperature coefficient, calibration of wires.

Wheatstone Bridge Problem

In a Wheatstone bridge, P=100Ω, Q=200Ω, S=150Ω at balance. Find R.

R = (P/Q) × S = (100/200) × 150 = 0.5 × 150 = 75Ω

EMF · Terminal PD · Heating Effect · Power · Energy

EMF & Terminal Potential

EMF (ε): energy supplied per coulomb by source (open circuit voltage).

Terminal potential difference (V): voltage across terminals when current flows. V = ε - I r (r = internal resistance).

Heating Effect (Joule's Law)

Heat produced: H = I² R t (Joules).

Electric Power: P = V I = I² R = V²/R (Watts).

Electric Energy: E = P × t. Unit: Joule or kWh (1 kWh = 3.6×10⁶ J).

Numerical Problem 1

A 220V heater draws 5A. Find power and energy consumed in 2 hours.

P = V I = 220 × 5 = 1100 W
Energy = P × t = 1.1 kW × 2 h = 2.2 kWh
Numerical Problem 2

A battery of emf 12V, internal resistance 0.5Ω delivers 2A. Find terminal voltage.

V = ε - I r = 12 - 2×0.5 = 12 - 1 = 11 V
Numerical Problem 3

A 50Ω resistor carries 0.2A. Find heat in 5 minutes.

H = I² R t = (0.2)² × 50 × (5×60) = 0.04 × 50 × 300 = 2 × 300 = 600 J

Thermoelectric Effect: Seebeck · Peltier

Seebeck Effect

When two dissimilar metals are joined at two junctions at different temperatures, an EMF is generated (thermocouple).

Used in thermocouple thermometers.

Peltier Effect

When current flows through a junction of two dissimilar metals, heat is absorbed or released (reverse of Seebeck).

Used in thermoelectric cooling (Peltier modules).

Difference: Seebeck converts temperature difference to voltage; Peltier converts current to temperature difference.
