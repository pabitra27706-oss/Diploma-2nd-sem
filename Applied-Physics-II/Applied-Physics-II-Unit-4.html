Electric Current & Its Units 🔥

⭐ Electric Current

Definition — Electric Current

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor. It tells us how much charge passes through a cross-section of the conductor per unit time.

Formula 4.1 — Electric Current:

\[ I = \frac{Q}{t} \]

where:

  • I = electric current (Ampere, A)
  • Q = charge flowing (Coulomb, C)
  • t = time (second, s)

SI Unit: Ampere (A). 1 Ampere = 1 Coulomb per second.

Analogy: Electric current is like water flowing through a pipe. The amount of water passing per second is the "current." The pressure pushing the water is like voltage. The pipe's narrowness is like resistance.

Important Facts about Current
  • Current is a scalar quantity (though we assign direction by convention).
  • Conventional current direction: From positive terminal to negative terminal (outside the cell). This is the direction positive charges would move.
  • Actual electron flow: From negative to positive (opposite to conventional current).
  • Current flows only when there is a potential difference (voltage) across the conductor AND a closed circuit.
  • Ampere is a base unit in SI system.

⭐ Direct Current (DC) vs Alternating Current (AC)

Direct Current (DC)

Direct current is the electric current that flows in one direction only and has a constant magnitude (or changes magnitude but not direction). Source: Battery, cell, DC generator, solar cell.

Alternating Current (AC)

Alternating current is the electric current that changes its direction periodically (flows forward, then backward, then forward again). Its magnitude also varies sinusoidally. Source: AC generator, mains power supply (230V, 50Hz in India).

Resistance, Specific Resistance, Conductance 🔥🔥🔥

⭐ Resistance (R)

Definition — Resistance

Resistance is the opposition offered by a conductor to the flow of electric current through it. It is the property of a material that resists (hinders) the movement of electrons.

SI Unit: Ohm (Ω). Symbol: R.

Formula 4.2 — Resistance (from Ohm's law):

\[ R = \frac{V}{I} \]

1 Ohm = 1 Volt / 1 Ampere

Analogy: Resistance is like the narrowness of a water pipe. A narrow pipe resists water flow (high resistance). A wide pipe allows easy flow (low resistance).

⭐ Factors Affecting Resistance of a Wire

Factors (Very Important!)

The resistance of a wire depends on:

  1. Length (l): R ∝ l — longer wire = more resistance. (Longer pipe = more friction.)
  2. Cross-sectional Area (A): R ∝ 1/A — thicker wire = less resistance. (Wider pipe = less friction.)
  3. Material: Different materials have different resistivities. Copper has low resistance (good conductor). Nichrome has high resistance (used in heaters).
  4. Temperature: For metals, resistance increases with temperature. For semiconductors, resistance decreases with temperature.

Formula 4.3 — Resistance of a Wire:

\[ R = \rho \frac{l}{A} \]

where:

  • R = resistance (Ω)
  • ρ (rho) = resistivity / specific resistance (Ω·m)
  • l = length of wire (m)
  • A = cross-sectional area of wire (m²)

⭐ Specific Resistance / Resistivity (ρ)

Definition — Specific Resistance (Resistivity)

Specific resistance (ρ) is the resistance offered by a conductor of unit length and unit cross-sectional area. It is a property of the material itself, not its dimensions.

From R = ρl/A: \(\rho = \frac{RA}{l}\)

SI Unit: Ohm-metre (Ω·m).

⭐ Conductance (G)

Definition — Conductance

Conductance is the reciprocal of resistance. It measures how easily a material allows current to flow through it.

\[ G = \frac{1}{R} \]

SI Unit: Siemens (S) or Mho (Ω⁻¹ or ℧).

⭐ Specific Conductance / Conductivity (σ)

Definition — Specific Conductance (Conductivity)

Specific conductance (σ) is the reciprocal of specific resistance (resistivity).

\[ \sigma = \frac{1}{\rho} \]

SI Unit: Siemens per metre (S/m).

High σ = good conductor. Low σ = poor conductor (insulator).

⭐ How to Read a 4-Band Resistor

Reading Steps
  1. 1st Band (nearest to one end): First digit of resistance value.
  2. 2nd Band: Second digit of resistance value.
  3. 3rd Band: Multiplier (number of zeros to add, or power of 10).
  4. 4th Band (slightly separated): Tolerance (accuracy).

Resistance = (1st digit)(2nd digit) × Multiplier ± Tolerance

A resistor has colour bands: Red, Violet, Orange, Gold. Find its resistance.

2nd band: Violet = 7

3rd band: Orange = ×10³ = ×1000

4th band: Gold = ±5%

A resistor has colour bands: Yellow, Violet, Red, Silver. Find its resistance.

Solution

Yellow = 4, Violet = 7, Red = ×10² = ×100, Silver = ±10%

R = 47 × 100 = 4700 Ω = 4.7 kΩ ± 10%

Series & Parallel Combination of Resistances 🔥🔥🔥

⭐ Resistors in Series

Series Combination

Resistors are in series when connected end to end so that the same current flows through each resistor, but the voltage divides among them.

Fig. 4.3: Resistors R₁, R₂, R₃ in series

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Formula 4.4 — Resistors in Series:

\[ R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \ldots \]

Properties:

  • Current: Same through all → I = I₁ = I₂ = I₃
  • Voltage: Divides → V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃
  • Req > largest individual resistance (total increases)

⭐ Resistors in Parallel

Parallel Combination

Resistors are in parallel when connected across the same two points so that the same voltage is across each, but the current divides among them.

Fig. 4.4: Resistors R₁, R₂, R₃ in parallel

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Formula 4.5 — Resistors in Parallel:

\[ \frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \ldots \]

For two resistors in parallel:

\[ R_{eq} = \frac{R_1 \times R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \]

Properties:

  • Voltage: Same across all → V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃
  • Current: Divides → I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃
  • Req < smallest individual resistance
Don't Confuse with Capacitors!

Resistors: Series → ADD, Parallel → reciprocal

Capacitors: Series → reciprocal, Parallel → ADD

They are opposite! This is the #1 exam trap.

Ohm's Law 🔥🔥🔥

⭐ Statement of Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law states that the current (I) flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference (V) applied across its ends, provided the temperature and other physical conditions remain constant.

Formula 4.6 — Ohm's Law:

\[ V = IR \quad \text{or} \quad I = \frac{V}{R} \quad \text{or} \quad R = \frac{V}{I} \]

where:

  • V = potential difference / voltage (Volt, V)
  • I = current (Ampere, A)
  • R = resistance (Ohm, Ω) — constant of proportionality
The Most Important Formula!

V = IR is the single most used formula in electricity. Learn all three forms: V = IR, I = V/R, R = V/I. Every numerical starts from here!

V-I Characteristics (Graph)

If we plot voltage (V) on the x-axis and current (I) on the y-axis, the graph is a straight line passing through the origin. This shows that V and I are directly proportional — confirming Ohm's law.

Fig. 4.5: V-I characteristic of an ohmic conductor — straight line through origin

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Key Points
  • The slope of the V-I graph gives the resistance (R = V/I).
  • Steeper slope = higher resistance.
  • Materials that obey Ohm's law are called ohmic conductors (e.g., metals like copper, aluminium at constant temperature).
  • Materials that don't obey Ohm's law are called non-ohmic conductors (e.g., diodes, transistors, filament bulbs). Their V-I graph is not a straight line.

Limitations of Ohm's Law

Limitations
  1. Not valid for non-ohmic devices (diodes, LEDs, transistors).
  2. Not valid at very high temperatures (resistance changes with temperature).
  3. Not valid for semiconductors.
  4. Not valid for electrolytes and gases.
  5. Valid only when physical conditions (temperature, pressure) remain constant.
Applications of Ohm's Law
  • Designing electrical circuits
  • Calculating required resistance for a circuit
  • Determining current or voltage in a circuit
  • Power calculations (P = VI = I²R = V²/R)
  • Fuse rating calculations
  • Voltage divider circuits

Kirchhoff's Laws 🔥🔥🔥

Ohm's law works for simple circuits with one loop. But for complex circuits with multiple loops and branches, we need Kirchhoff's Laws — two powerful rules that can solve ANY circuit!

⭐ Kirchhoff's First Law — KCL (Kirchhoff's Current Law)

KCL — Junction Rule

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) states that the algebraic sum of all currents meeting at a junction (node) in a circuit is zero.

Or equivalently: The sum of currents entering a junction = Sum of currents leaving the junction.

\[ \sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out} \]

Analogy: Think of a water pipe junction. The total water flowing INTO the junction must equal the total water flowing OUT. Water doesn't accumulate or disappear at the junction. Similarly, charge doesn't accumulate at a circuit junction.

KCL is based on Conservation of Charge

Charge cannot be created or destroyed. So whatever charge enters a junction per second (current in) must leave per second (current out).

Fig. 4.6: KCL at a junction — currents entering = currents leaving

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Quick Example — KCL

At a junction, currents of 3 A and 5 A flow in, and currents of 2 A and I flow out. Find I.

Solution

ΣIin = ΣIout

3 + 5 = 2 + I

8 = 2 + I

I = 6 A

⭐ Kirchhoff's Second Law — KVL (Kirchhoff's Voltage Law)

KVL — Loop Rule

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL) states that the algebraic sum of all the potential differences (voltages) around any closed loop in a circuit is zero.

\[ \sum V = 0 \quad \text{(around any closed loop)} \]

Or equivalently: Sum of EMFs = Sum of voltage drops (IR products) in a loop.

\[ \sum \varepsilon = \sum IR \]

Analogy: Imagine walking around a circular hill path. You go up some hills (EMF = voltage rise) and down some slopes (IR drop = voltage fall). When you return to your starting point, your net altitude change is zero. Similarly, going around a closed loop, the total voltage rise must equal the total voltage drop.

KVL is based on Conservation of Energy

Energy supplied by the source (EMF) must equal energy consumed by the resistances (IR drops) in a closed loop.

Fig. 4.7: KVL — sum of voltages around a closed loop = 0

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Sign Conventions for KVL

Rules for Traversing a Loop
  1. Resistor: If you traverse in the direction of current → voltage drop = −IR. If against current → voltage rise = +IR.
  2. Battery/EMF: If you traverse from − to + (through battery) → voltage rise = +ε. If from + to − → voltage drop = −ε.

Wheatstone Bridge 🔥🔥🔥

What is a Wheatstone Bridge?

Definition — Wheatstone Bridge

A Wheatstone bridge is an electrical circuit used to accurately measure an unknown resistance by comparing it with known resistances. It consists of four resistances arranged in a diamond (bridge) shape, a galvanometer, and a battery.

Construction

The Wheatstone bridge has:

  • Four resistors: P, Q, R, and S arranged in four arms of a diamond shape.
  • Galvanometer (G): Connected between junctions B and D (the "bridge").
  • Battery (E): Connected between junctions A and C.

Fig. 4.8: Wheatstone bridge — four arms P, Q, R, S with galvanometer G and battery E

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

⭐ Balanced Condition

Wheatstone Bridge Balance Condition

The bridge is said to be balanced when the galvanometer shows zero deflection (no current flows through the galvanometer). This happens when:

Formula 4.7 — Wheatstone Bridge Balance Condition:

\[ \frac{P}{Q} = \frac{R}{S} \]

where P, Q, R, S are the four resistances in the arms of the bridge.

If S is unknown: \( S = \frac{QR}{P} \)

Why Zero Current in Galvanometer?

At balance, the potential at point B equals the potential at point D. Since there is no potential difference between B and D, no current flows through the galvanometer (bridge). The circuit behaves as if the galvanometer wire is absent.

Working Principle

  1. Connect the unknown resistance S in one arm of the bridge.
  2. Adjust the known resistances P, Q, R until the galvanometer shows zero deflection (null point).
  3. At balance: P/Q = R/S, so S = QR/P.
  4. This method is very accurate because it is a null method — the measurement doesn't depend on the accuracy of the galvanometer.
Applications of Wheatstone Bridge
  • Accurate measurement of unknown resistance
  • Strain gauge measurements (structural engineering)
  • Temperature measurement using RTDs (Resistance Temperature Detectors)
  • Pressure measurement
  • Light measurement (using LDRs in bridge configuration)
Exam Favourite

"Draw the circuit diagram of a Wheatstone bridge, state the balanced condition, and find the unknown resistance" — this is asked almost every year! Always draw the diamond-shaped diagram neatly with labels P, Q, R, S, G, E.

Carey Foster Bridge 🔥🔥

What is a Carey Foster Bridge?

Definition — Carey Foster Bridge

A Carey Foster bridge is a modified form of the Wheatstone bridge used to compare two nearly equal resistances or to find the difference between two resistances with high accuracy. It uses a uniform resistance wire (bridge wire) along which a jockey can slide to find the balance point.

Construction

  • A uniform wire of length 100 cm (usually constantan or manganin) stretched on a meter scale.
  • Two resistances P and Q connected in two gaps of the bridge.
  • Two known resistances R and S (or one known + one unknown) in the other two gaps.
  • A galvanometer connected between the junction of P, Q and a sliding jockey on the wire.
  • A battery to supply current.

Fig. 4.9: Carey Foster Bridge — circuit diagram with bridge wire, gaps, galvanometer

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Working Principle

The Carey Foster bridge works on the Wheatstone bridge principle. The jockey is slid along the wire until the galvanometer shows zero deflection (balance point). The balance point position is noted.

Formula 4.8 — Carey Foster Bridge:

If the balance point is at distance l₁ from one end with resistances in one arrangement, and at l₂ after interchanging the resistances:

\[ S - R = (l_1 - l_2) \times \rho \]

where:

  • S − R = difference of the two resistances
  • l₁, l₂ = balance lengths (in cm)
  • ρ = resistance per unit length of the bridge wire (Ω/cm)

⭐ Applications of Carey Foster Bridge

Applications
  1. Comparing two nearly equal resistances: By finding the difference between them accurately.
  2. Finding resistance per unit length (ρ) of the bridge wire using known resistance standards.
  3. Finding the specific resistance (resistivity) of the wire material.
  4. Temperature coefficient of resistance measurement.
  5. Calibration of resistance standards.

EMF & Terminal Potential Difference 🔥🔥

⭐ Electromotive Force (EMF)

Definition — EMF

Electromotive Force (EMF or ε) is the total potential difference between the terminals of a cell (or battery) when no current is drawn from it (i.e., in open circuit condition). It is the maximum voltage a cell can provide.

It represents the work done per unit charge by the cell in moving a charge through the complete circuit (including inside the cell).

SI Unit: Volt (V).

Misleading Name!

Despite its name, EMF is NOT a force — it is a voltage (potential difference). The name is historical and stuck. Always remember: EMF is measured in Volts, not Newtons!

⭐ Terminal Potential Difference (TPD)

Definition — Terminal Potential Difference

Terminal Potential Difference (V) is the potential difference between the terminals of a cell when current is flowing (i.e., in closed circuit / when the cell is in use).

It is always less than the EMF when current flows (because some voltage is "lost" inside the cell due to internal resistance).

Internal Resistance (r)

Definition — Internal Resistance

Internal resistance (r) is the resistance offered by the electrolyte and electrodes inside the cell to the flow of current. Every real cell has some internal resistance.

⭐ Relationship between EMF, Terminal PD, and Internal Resistance

Formula 4.9 — EMF, TPD & Internal Resistance:

\[ \varepsilon = V + Ir = IR + Ir = I(R + r) \]

Or: \[ V = \varepsilon - Ir \]

where:

  • ε = EMF of the cell (V)
  • V = terminal potential difference (V)
  • I = current flowing (A)
  • r = internal resistance of cell (Ω)
  • R = external resistance (Ω)
  • Ir = voltage drop across internal resistance (lost voltage)
Key Points
  • When I = 0 (open circuit): V = ε. Terminal PD = EMF.
  • When I > 0 (closed circuit): V < ε. Terminal PD is less than EMF.
  • The difference (ε − V) = Ir = voltage drop across internal resistance.
  • If internal resistance r = 0 (ideal cell): V = ε always.
  • Larger internal resistance → more voltage lost inside → less voltage available externally.

Heating Effect, Electric Power & Energy 🔥🔥🔥

⭐ Heating Effect of Current (Joule's Law of Heating)

Heating Effect

When electric current flows through a conductor, the conductor gets heated. This is because the moving electrons collide with the atoms/ions of the conductor, transferring their kinetic energy as heat energy. This is called the heating effect of current or Joule heating.

Formula 4.10 — Joule's Law of Heating:

\[ H = I^2 R t \]

where:

  • H = heat produced (Joule, J)
  • I = current (Ampere, A)
  • R = resistance (Ohm, Ω)
  • t = time (second, s)

In calories: H = I²Rt / 4.2 (since 1 cal = 4.2 J)

Examples of heating effect: Electric heater, electric iron, electric kettle, toaster, incandescent bulb, fuse wire, electric geyser.

⭐ Electric Power (P)

Definition — Electric Power

Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed (or converted to other forms of energy) in a circuit.

Formula 4.11 — Electric Power:

\[ P = \frac{W}{t} = VI = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R} \]

where:

  • P = power (Watt, W)
  • V = voltage (V)
  • I = current (A)
  • R = resistance (Ω)
  • W = energy consumed (J)
  • t = time (s)

SI Unit: Watt (W). 1 Watt = 1 Joule / 1 second = 1 Volt × 1 Ampere.

Larger units: 1 kilowatt (kW) = 1000 W. 1 horsepower (HP) = 746 W.

⭐ Electric Energy (W)

Definition — Electric Energy

Electric energy is the total work done (or total energy consumed) by the current in a given time.

Formula 4.12 — Electric Energy:

\[ W = Pt = VIt = I^2Rt = \frac{V^2}{R} \times t \]

SI Unit: Joule (J).

Commercial unit: kilowatt-hour (kWh) = 1 unit of electricity.

\[ 1 \text{ kWh} = 1000 \times 3600 = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J} = 3.6 \text{ MJ} \]

Very Important for Exam!

1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J. This conversion is asked in every exam. Also remember: P = VI = I²R = V²/R — four forms of the same formula!

Electricity Bill Calculation

Electricity bill is calculated in units (kWh):

Energy consumed = Power (kW) × Time (hours)

Example: A 100 W bulb running for 10 hours = 0.1 kW × 10 h = 1 kWh = 1 unit.

If cost per unit = ₹8, then bill = 1 × 8 = ₹8.

Thermoelectric Effects — Seebeck & Peltier 🔥🔥

What is Thermoelectricity?

Thermoelectricity deals with the direct conversion of heat energy into electrical energy (and vice versa) at the junction of two different metals. There are two main effects:

⭐ Seebeck Effect

Definition — Seebeck Effect

The Seebeck effect states that when the two junctions of a circuit made of two different metals (called a thermocouple) are maintained at different temperatures, an EMF (voltage) is generated and a current flows in the circuit. This EMF is called thermo-EMF.

Discovered by Thomas Seebeck in 1821.

How Seebeck Effect Works
  1. Take two different metal wires (e.g., copper and iron).
  2. Join their ends to form two junctions — creating a loop (thermocouple).
  3. Keep one junction hot and the other cold.
  4. Due to the temperature difference, free electrons in the two metals move at different rates, creating a potential difference.
  5. This causes a small current to flow in the circuit.
  6. The magnitude of thermo-EMF depends on:
    • The temperature difference between the two junctions
    • The nature (type) of the two metals

Fig. 4.11: Seebeck effect — thermocouple with hot and cold junctions, current flow

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Applications of Seebeck Effect
  1. Thermocouple thermometer: Used to measure temperatures (especially high temperatures in furnaces, industrial processes)
  2. Thermoelectric generator (TEG): Converts waste heat directly into electricity
  3. Power generation in space: Radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) used in space probes
  4. Temperature sensing in industrial and scientific applications

⭐ Peltier Effect

Definition — Peltier Effect

The Peltier effect is the reverse of the Seebeck effect. It states that when an electric current is passed through a junction of two different metals (thermocouple), heat is either absorbed or released at the junction, depending on the direction of current.

One junction gets heated and the other gets cooled.

Discovered by Jean Peltier in 1834.

How Peltier Effect Works
  • Pass current through a thermocouple (two different metals joined).
  • At one junction, heat is absorbed (cooling effect).
  • At the other junction, heat is released (heating effect).
  • If the current direction is reversed, the hot and cold junctions swap.
  • The heat absorbed/released is proportional to the current and time.

Fig. 4.12: Peltier effect — current through thermocouple causing one junction to cool and other to heat

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Applications of Peltier Effect
  1. Thermoelectric cooler (Peltier cooler): Used in small refrigerators, portable coolers, CPU coolers in computers
  2. Cooling of electronic components: Laser diodes, CCD sensors in cameras
  3. Mini-fridges: in hotel rooms, cars (cigarette lighter powered)
  4. Medical: Cooling of biological samples, temperature control in lab equipment
  5. Dehumidifiers: Small Peltier-based dehumidifiers

Numerical 2: Resistance of a Wire

Problem

A copper wire is 2 m long and has a cross-sectional area of 1 mm². If the resistivity of copper is 1.7 × 10⁻⁸ Ω·m, find its resistance.

Solution

l = 2 m, A = 1 mm² = 1 × 10⁻⁶ m², ρ = 1.7 × 10⁻⁸ Ω·m

R = (1.7 × 10⁻⁸ × 2) / (1 × 10⁻⁶)

R = 3.4 × 10⁻⁸ / 10⁻⁶ = 3.4 × 10⁻²

R = 0.034 Ω

Numerical 3: Resistors in Series & Parallel

Problem

Three resistors of 4 Ω, 6 Ω, and 12 Ω are connected (a) in series, (b) in parallel. Find the equivalent resistance in each case.

Solution

Req = 4 + 6 + 12 = 22 Ω

1/Req = 1/4 + 1/6 + 1/12 = 3/12 + 2/12 + 1/12 = 6/12 = 1/2

Req = 2 Ω

Numerical 4: Wheatstone Bridge ⭐

Problem

In a Wheatstone bridge, P = 100 Ω, Q = 200 Ω, R = 40 Ω. Find the value of S for the bridge to be balanced.

Solution

At balance: P/Q = R/S

100/200 = 40/S

S = (200 × 40)/100 = 8000/100

S = 80 Ω

Numerical 5: EMF & Internal Resistance ⭐

Problem

A cell of EMF 2 V and internal resistance 0.5 Ω is connected to an external resistance of 4.5 Ω. Find (a) the current, (b) the terminal potential difference.

Solution

I = ε/(R + r) = 2/(4.5 + 0.5) = 2/5

I = 0.4 A

V = ε − Ir = 2 − (0.4 × 0.5) = 2 − 0.2

V = 1.8 V

(Note: V < ε because of internal resistance drop.)

Numerical 6: Electric Power ⭐

Problem

An electric heater rated 1000 W, 220 V is used for 3 hours. Calculate (a) the current drawn, (b) the resistance, (c) the energy consumed in kWh and Joules.

Solution

P = VI → I = P/V = 1000/220

I = 4.545 A ≈ 4.55 A

R = V²/P = (220)²/1000 = 48400/1000

R = 48.4 Ω

In kWh: W = P × t = 1 kW × 3 h = 3 kWh = 3 units

In Joules: W = 3 × 3.6 × 10⁶ = 10.8 × 10⁶ J = 10.8 MJ

A current of 5 A flows through a resistance of 20 Ω for 10 minutes. Calculate the heat generated in Joules and calories.

At a junction, currents of 5 A, 3 A, and 2 A flow in. If two currents of 4 A and I flow out, find I.

Important Definitions

1. Electric Current

The rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor. I = Q/t. SI Unit: Ampere (A). 1 A = 1 C/s.

2. Direct Current (DC)

Current that flows in one direction only and generally has constant magnitude. Source: battery, cell, solar cell.

3. Alternating Current (AC)

Current that changes direction periodically and whose magnitude varies sinusoidally with time. Source: mains supply, AC generator.

4. Resistance

The opposition offered by a conductor to the flow of electric current through it. R = V/I. SI Unit: Ohm (Ω).

5. Specific Resistance / Resistivity

The resistance of a material of unit length and unit cross-sectional area. ρ = RA/l. SI Unit: Ohm-metre (Ω·m).

6. Conductance

The reciprocal of resistance. G = 1/R. It measures how easily current flows. SI Unit: Siemens (S).

7. Specific Conductance / Conductivity

The reciprocal of resistivity. σ = 1/ρ. SI Unit: Siemens per metre (S/m).

8. Ohm's Law

At constant temperature, the current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across its ends. V = IR.

9. Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

The algebraic sum of all currents meeting at a junction is zero. Sum of currents entering = sum of currents leaving. Based on conservation of charge.

10. Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

The algebraic sum of all potential differences around any closed loop in a circuit is zero. Based on conservation of energy.

11. Wheatstone Bridge

An arrangement of four resistances in a diamond shape used to measure an unknown resistance accurately. Balanced condition: P/Q = R/S, when galvanometer shows zero deflection.

12. Carey Foster Bridge

A modified Wheatstone bridge used to compare two nearly equal resistances or to find their difference using a uniform wire and sliding contact (jockey).

13. EMF

The total potential difference between the terminals of a cell when no current flows through it (open circuit). SI Unit: Volt (V).

14. Terminal Potential Difference

The potential difference between the terminals of a cell when current is being drawn from it (closed circuit). V = ε − Ir.

15. Internal Resistance

The resistance offered to the flow of current by the electrolyte and electrodes inside a cell. Symbol: r. SI Unit: Ohm (Ω).

16. Heating Effect of Current

The production of heat in a conductor when current flows through it due to collision of electrons with atoms. H = I²Rt.

17. Electric Power

The rate at which electric energy is consumed or converted in a circuit. P = VI = I²R = V²/R. SI Unit: Watt (W).

18. Electric Energy

The total electrical work done or energy consumed in a given time. W = Pt = VIt. SI Unit: Joule (J). Commercial unit: kilowatt-hour (kWh).

19. Seebeck Effect

When two junctions of two dissimilar metals are kept at different temperatures, a thermo-EMF is produced and current flows in the circuit.

20. Peltier Effect

When current is passed through a junction of two dissimilar metals, heat is absorbed at one junction and released at the other. It is the reverse of the Seebeck effect.

Short Answer Questions (2 Marks)

Q1. Define electric current and state its SI unit.

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor. I = Q/t. Its SI unit is Ampere (A). 1 A = 1 C/s. Conventional current flows from positive to negative terminal, though electrons actually move from negative to positive terminal.

Q2. State Ohm's law and mention one limitation.

Ohm's law states that the current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across its ends, provided temperature and other physical conditions remain constant. V = IR.

Limitation: It is not valid for non-ohmic devices like diodes, transistors, electrolytes, or when temperature changes significantly.

Q3. Define resistivity and state its unit.

Resistivity (specific resistance) is the resistance of a conductor of unit length and unit cross-sectional area. It is a property of the material. ρ = RA/l. SI Unit: Ohm-metre (Ω·m). Low resistivity means good conductor; high resistivity means poor conductor.

Q4. State Kirchhoff's two laws.

KCL: The sum of currents entering a junction equals the sum leaving it (ΣI = 0). Based on conservation of charge.

KVL: The algebraic sum of all voltages around a closed loop is zero (ΣV = 0). Based on conservation of energy.

Q5. What is the balance condition of a Wheatstone bridge?

A Wheatstone bridge is balanced when the galvanometer shows zero deflection, meaning no current flows through it. The balance condition is:

P/Q = R/S

where P, Q, R, and S are the four resistances in the bridge arms. At balance, potential at the two galvanometer points is equal.

Q6. Distinguish between EMF and terminal potential difference.

EMF (ε): Voltage of a cell when no current flows (open circuit). It is the maximum voltage.

Terminal PD (V): Actual voltage across cell terminals when current flows (closed circuit). It is less than EMF due to internal resistance. Relation: V = ε − Ir.

Q7. State Joule's law of heating.

Joule's law of heating states that the heat produced in a conductor carrying current is directly proportional to the square of current, resistance of the conductor, and time for which current flows. H = I²Rt. SI unit of heat is Joule (J).

Q8. What is the commercial unit of electric energy? Convert it into Joules.

The commercial unit of electric energy is kilowatt-hour (kWh), also called one "unit" of electricity. It is the energy consumed by a 1 kW device in 1 hour.

1 kWh = 1000 × 3600 = 3.6 × 10⁶ J.

Q9. What is Seebeck effect?

Seebeck effect is the phenomenon in which an EMF is produced in a circuit of two dissimilar metals when their two junctions are maintained at different temperatures. This thermo-EMF causes a current to flow in the circuit. It is used in thermocouples and thermoelectric generators.

Q10. What is Peltier effect?

Peltier effect is the reverse of Seebeck effect. When current is passed through a junction of two dissimilar metals, heat is absorbed at one junction and released at the other. One junction becomes cold, the other hot. It is used in Peltier coolers and mini-fridges.

Long Answer Questions (5–7 Marks)

Q1. State and explain Ohm's law with V-I characteristics. Mention its limitations. [7 marks] 🔥🔥

Ohm's Law: At constant temperature and other physical conditions, current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across its ends.

V ∝ I → V = IR, where R is the constant of proportionality called resistance.

Explanation: If voltage doubles, current doubles (for same R). If resistance increases, current decreases.

V-I Graph: A straight line passing through the origin. Slope gives resistance R = V/I. A steeper slope means higher resistance.

[Draw graph with V on x-axis, I on y-axis, straight line through origin]

Limitations:

  1. Not valid for non-ohmic devices like diodes and transistors.
  2. Not valid if temperature changes.
  3. Not valid for electrolytes and gases.
  4. Not valid for semiconductors.

Ohm's law is one of the most fundamental laws in electricity and is used in almost all circuit calculations.

Q2. State Kirchhoff's laws and explain them with examples. [7 marks] 🔥🔥

Kirchhoff's First Law (KCL): The algebraic sum of currents at a junction is zero. Sum entering = sum leaving. Based on conservation of charge.

Example: If 3 A and 5 A enter a junction and 2 A and I leave, then 3+5 = 2+I → I = 6 A.

Kirchhoff's Second Law (KVL): The algebraic sum of all potential differences around a closed loop is zero. Sum of EMF = sum of IR drops. Based on conservation of energy.

Example: In a loop with battery 12 V and resistors 2 Ω and 4 Ω carrying current I: 12 = 2I + 4I → I = 2 A.

Sign Conventions:

  • Through resistor in direction of current → −IR
  • Across battery from − to + → +E

These laws are used to solve complex multi-loop circuits where Ohm's law alone is insufficient.

Q3. Explain Wheatstone bridge with circuit diagram and derive the balance condition. [7 marks] 🔥🔥🔥

Wheatstone Bridge: An arrangement of four resistances P, Q, R, S in a diamond shape used to measure unknown resistance accurately.

[Draw diamond circuit with A, B, C, D; P in AB, Q in BC, R in AD, S in DC, galvanometer between B and D, battery between A and C]

Balanced Condition: When galvanometer shows zero deflection, no current flows through BD. Therefore, potential at B = potential at D.

Let current through P and Q branch = I₁ and through R and S branch = I₂.

Potential drop from A to B = I₁P, from A to D = I₂R

At balance: I₁P = I₂R ... (1)

Potential drop from B to C = I₁Q, from D to C = I₂S

At balance: I₁Q = I₂S ... (2)

Dividing (1) by (2): P/Q = R/S

Balance Condition: P/Q = R/S

If S is unknown: S = QR/P

Used for measuring unknown resistance, strain gauges, RTDs, pressure sensors.

Q4. Explain the concept of EMF, terminal potential difference, and internal resistance. Derive the relation between them. [5 marks]

EMF (ε): The potential difference between the terminals of a cell when no current flows (open circuit). Maximum voltage of the cell.

Terminal Potential Difference (V): The actual potential difference across the terminals of a cell when current is drawn.

Internal Resistance (r): The resistance inside the cell due to electrolyte and electrodes.

When current I flows through the cell, a voltage drop Ir occurs inside the cell due to internal resistance.

Therefore, EMF = Terminal PD + internal voltage drop

ε = V + Ir

Or: V = ε − Ir

If external resistance = R, then V = IR, so:

ε = I(R + r)

When I = 0 (open circuit), V = ε. When current flows, V < ε.

Q5. State Joule's law of heating and derive expressions for electric power and electric energy. [7 marks] 🔥🔥

Joule's Law: Heat produced in a conductor carrying current is proportional to the square of current, resistance, and time.

H = I²Rt

Derivation of Power:

Electrical work done = charge × potential difference = QV

Since Q = It, work W = VIt

Power = W/t = VIt/t = VI

Using Ohm's law:

P = VI = (IR)I = I²R

Also, P = VI = V(V/R) = V²/R

So, P = VI = I²R = V²/R

Electric Energy:

Energy consumed in time t = Power × time

W = Pt = VIt = I²Rt = (V²/R)t

SI unit: Joule (J). Commercial unit: kWh. 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J.

Q6. Explain the carbon resistor colour coding system with examples. [5 marks] 🔥🔥

Carbon resistors use coloured bands to indicate their resistance value and tolerance.

4-band system:

  • 1st band → first digit
  • 2nd band → second digit
  • 3rd band → multiplier
  • 4th band → tolerance

Colour values: Black 0, Brown 1, Red 2, Orange 3, Yellow 4, Green 5, Blue 6, Violet 7, Grey 8, White 9.

Tolerance: Gold ±5%, Silver ±10%, No band ±20%

Example 1: Red, Violet, Orange, Gold → 27 × 10³ = 27 kΩ ±5%

Example 2: Yellow, Violet, Red, Silver → 47 × 10² = 4.7 kΩ ±10%

Mnemonic: "BB ROY of Great Britain has a Very Good Wife"

Q7. Explain Seebeck and Peltier effects with applications. [5 marks]

Seebeck Effect: When two junctions of two dissimilar metals are kept at different temperatures, a thermo-EMF is produced and current flows. This converts heat into electricity. Applications: thermocouple thermometer, thermoelectric generator, temperature sensing.

Peltier Effect: Reverse of Seebeck effect. When current is passed through a junction of two dissimilar metals, one junction gets heated and the other cooled. Applications: Peltier cooler, mini-fridge, CPU cooling, cooling of electronic components.

Difference: Seebeck = heat to electricity, Peltier = electricity to heat/cooling.

Q8. Compare series and parallel combinations of resistances. [5 marks]

Series: Connected end to end. Current same through all. Voltage divides. R = R₁ + R₂ + ... Equivalent resistance greater than largest resistor. Used in voltage division.

Parallel: Connected across same two points. Voltage same across all. Current divides. 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ... Equivalent resistance less than smallest. Used in house wiring because each appliance gets full voltage and can work independently.

🔵 Kirchhoff's Laws

  • KCL: Sum current in = sum current out (charge conservation)
  • KVL: Sum of voltages in loop = 0 (energy conservation)

🔵 Wheatstone Bridge

  • Used to measure unknown resistance
  • Balance condition: P/Q = R/S 🔥🔥
  • Galvanometer shows zero deflection at balance

🔵 EMF & Internal Resistance

  • EMF = open circuit voltage
  • Terminal PD = actual voltage when current flows
  • ε = V + Ir, so V = ε − Ir

🔵 Heating, Power, Energy

  • H = I²Rt (Joule heating) 🔥
  • P = VI = I²R = V²/R 🔥🔥
  • W = Pt = VIt
  • 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J = 1 unit

🔵 Thermoelectric Effects

  • Seebeck: heat → electricity
  • Peltier: electricity → heat/cooling
  • Peltier is reverse of Seebeck
