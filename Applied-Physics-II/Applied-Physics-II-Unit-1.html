Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) — Definition & Concepts 🔥

What is Periodic Motion?

Before understanding SHM, let's understand periodic motion. When any object repeats its motion after a fixed interval of time, it is called periodic motion.

Examples: Earth going around the Sun (period ≈ 365 days), a clock's pendulum swinging back and forth, the hands of a clock, heartbeat, etc.

What is Oscillatory (Vibratory) Motion?

When a body moves to and fro (back and forth) about a fixed point (called the mean or equilibrium position), it is called oscillatory motion or vibratory motion.

Examples: A swing moving back and forth, a guitar string vibrating, a pendulum.

Quick Tip

All oscillatory motions are periodic, but all periodic motions are NOT oscillatory. (Example: Earth revolving around the Sun is periodic but not oscillatory — it doesn't go back and forth.)

⭐ Definition of Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

Definition — SHM

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is a special type of oscillatory motion in which the restoring force acting on the body is directly proportional to the displacement of the body from its mean (equilibrium) position and is always directed towards the mean position.

Mathematically: F = −kx

where F = restoring force, k = spring constant (or force constant), x = displacement from mean position. The negative sign shows force is opposite to displacement.

Real-world analogy: Imagine a ball in a bowl. Push it to one side — it rolls back, overshoots, goes to the other side, comes back again. The bowl's curved surface always pushes the ball back towards the center (lowest point). This is like SHM!

Key Terms in SHM

Mean Position (Equilibrium Position)

The central position where the body naturally rests when no external force acts on it. At this position, the restoring force is zero and the velocity is maximum.

Displacement (x or y)

The distance of the vibrating body from its mean position at any instant of time. It can be positive or negative depending on direction. SI Unit: metre (m).

Amplitude (A)

The maximum displacement of the vibrating body from its mean position. It is always positive. SI Unit: metre (m).

Time Period (T)

The time taken by the body to complete one full oscillation (one complete to-and-fro motion). SI Unit: second (s).

Frequency (f or ν)

The number of complete oscillations made by the body in one second. SI Unit: Hertz (Hz) = cycles per second.

Relationship: \( f = \frac{1}{T} \) and \( T = \frac{1}{f} \)

Angular Frequency (ω)

The rate of change of phase angle. It tells how fast the oscillation happens in terms of angle.

\( \omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T} \)

SI Unit: radian per second (rad/s).

Phase (φ)

The phase of an oscillating body tells us the state of the body (position and direction of motion) at any given instant. The term (ωt + φ₀) in the SHM equation is called the phase. The constant φ₀ is the initial phase or epoch.

Restoring Force

The force that always tries to bring the oscillating body back to its mean position. In SHM, this force is proportional to displacement: F = −kx.

Examples of SHM

  1. Simple Pendulum — a bob swinging with small amplitude
  2. Mass on a spring — a mass attached to a spring oscillating up and down
  3. Tuning fork prongs — vibrating when struck
  4. A floating body — bobbing up and down in water (small amplitude)
  5. Guitar/Sitar string — vibrating after being plucked
  6. Atoms in a solid — vibrating about their equilibrium positions

Conditions for SHM

Necessary Conditions for SHM
  1. The motion must be oscillatory (to and fro about a fixed point).
  2. The restoring force must be directly proportional to displacement from the mean position (F ∝ x).
  3. The restoring force must always be directed towards the mean position (opposite to displacement).
  4. Mathematically: F = −kx, which gives acceleration a = −ω²x

SHM as Projection of Uniform Circular Motion

This is one of the best ways to understand SHM. Imagine a particle moving in a circle with constant speed. Now shine a light from one side and look at the shadow of the particle on the diameter. That shadow moves back and forth — exactly like SHM!

Consider a particle P moving in a circle of radius A with angular velocity ω. At time t, the angle made by the radius OP with the x-axis = ωt. The projection of P on the y-axis (let's call it point M) is at a distance y from the center:

\[ y = A \sin(\omega t) \]

This is the displacement equation of SHM!

Remember for Exam

SHM is defined by: a = −ω²x (acceleration proportional to displacement and directed towards mean position). If any question asks "show that the motion is SHM", you need to prove that acceleration ∝ −displacement.

SHM — Displacement, Velocity & Acceleration 🔥🔥

1. Displacement in SHM

The displacement of a particle executing SHM at any time t is given by:

Formula 1.1 — Displacement:

\[ x = A \sin(\omega t + \phi_0) \]

where:

  • x = displacement from mean position at time t (in metre)
  • A = amplitude (maximum displacement) (in metre)
  • ω = angular frequency = 2πf = 2π/T (in rad/s)
  • t = time (in second)
  • φ₀ = initial phase or epoch (in radian)
  • (ωt + φ₀) = phase at time t
Choosing sin or cos

If at t = 0, the particle is at the mean position (x = 0), we use: x = A sin(ωt)

If at t = 0, the particle is at the extreme position (x = A), we use: x = A cos(ωt)

Both are correct — they differ only in initial phase (φ₀). For exams, use x = A sin(ωt) unless told otherwise.

2. Velocity in SHM ⭐

Velocity is the rate of change of displacement. Differentiating the displacement equation:

\[ x = A\sin(\omega t) \]

\[ v = \frac{dx}{dt} = A\omega\cos(\omega t) \]

Since \(\sin^2(\omega t) + \cos^2(\omega t) = 1\), we get \(\cos(\omega t) = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2(\omega t)} = \sqrt{1 - x^2/A^2}\)

Formula 1.2 — Velocity:

\[ v = \omega\sqrt{A^2 - x^2} \]

where:

  • v = velocity at displacement x (in m/s)
  • ω = angular frequency (in rad/s)
  • A = amplitude (in m)
  • x = displacement from mean position (in m)
Special Cases of Velocity

At mean position (x = 0):

\[ v_{max} = \omega\sqrt{A^2 - 0} = \omega A \]

✅ Velocity is MAXIMUM at mean position.


At extreme position (x = A):

\[ v = \omega\sqrt{A^2 - A^2} = 0 \]

✅ Velocity is ZERO at extreme position.

Most Important for Exam

vmax = ωA — This formula is asked in almost every WBSCTE paper!

3. Acceleration in SHM ⭐

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. Differentiating the velocity equation:

\[ v = A\omega\cos(\omega t) \]

\[ a = \frac{dv}{dt} = -A\omega^2\sin(\omega t) = -\omega^2 x \]

Formula 1.3 — Acceleration:

\[ a = -\omega^2 x \]

where:

  • a = acceleration (in m/s²)
  • ω = angular frequency (in rad/s)
  • x = displacement from mean position (in m)
  • The negative sign indicates acceleration is directed towards the mean position (opposite to displacement)
Special Cases of Acceleration

At mean position (x = 0):

\[ a = -\omega^2 \times 0 = 0 \]

✅ Acceleration is ZERO at mean position.


At extreme position (x = ±A):

\[ a_{max} = \omega^2 A \]

✅ Acceleration is MAXIMUM at extreme positions.

Common Exam Mistake

Students often confuse: velocity max at mean but acceleration max at extreme. Remember:

  • 🏃 Velocity → MAX at mean, ZERO at extreme
  • 💪 Acceleration → ZERO at mean, MAX at extreme

Think of it this way: At the extreme, the body stops momentarily (v = 0) but the restoring force (and hence acceleration) is maximum, pulling it back.

Extreme Position ±A (maximum) 0 (minimum) ω²A (maximum) kA (maximum)

4. Time Period and Frequency of SHM

From \(a = -\omega^2 x\), we get:

\[ \omega^2 = \frac{|a|}{x} = \frac{\text{acceleration}}{\text{displacement}} \]

\[ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{|a|}{x}} \]

Formula 1.4 — Time Period of SHM:

\[ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{x}{|a|}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{\text{displacement}}{\text{acceleration}}} \]

Formula 1.5 — Frequency of SHM:

\[ f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{|a|}{x}} \]

5. Time Period of a Simple Pendulum (for reference)

Formula 1.6:

\[ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{l}{g}} \]

where l = length of pendulum, g = acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²)

6. Time Period of a Spring-Mass System

Formula 1.7:

\[ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} \]

where m = mass attached to spring (kg), k = spring constant (N/m)

7. Energy in SHM

A body executing SHM possesses two types of energy:

Kinetic Energy (KE):

\[ KE = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2(A^2 - x^2) \]

KE is maximum at mean position (x=0) and zero at extreme position (x=A).

Potential Energy (PE):

\[ PE = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2 \]

PE is zero at mean position and maximum at extreme position.

Total Energy (TE):

\[ TE = KE + PE = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2 = \text{constant} \]

Total energy remains constant throughout the motion (for ideal SHM with no friction).

  • At mean position: KE = max, PE = 0
  • At extreme: KE = 0, PE = max
  • At every point: KE + PE = Total Energy = constant

Vibrations of Cantilever 🔥

What is a Cantilever?

Definition — Cantilever

A cantilever is a beam (or rod or bar) that is fixed at one end and free at the other end. When the free end is displaced and released, the cantilever vibrates up and down about its equilibrium position.

Real-world examples:

  • A diving board at a swimming pool (fixed at the platform, free at the end)
  • A ruler held on the edge of a table with one end extending out
  • A tree branch (fixed at trunk, free at tip)
  • Balcony projecting from a building wall

Vibration of a Loaded Cantilever

When a mass M is placed at the free end of a cantilever and the free end is given a small displacement and released, the cantilever vibrates. This vibration is approximately Simple Harmonic for small displacements.

Depression of a Cantilever

When a load (mass M) is placed at the free end of a cantilever of length l, the free end bends downward. This bending (called depression) is given by:

Formula 1.8 — Depression of Cantilever:

\[ \delta = \frac{Mgl^3}{3YI} \]

where:

  • δ (delta) = depression at the free end (in m)
  • M = mass of the load (in kg)
  • g = acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²)
  • l = length of cantilever (in m)
  • Y = Young's modulus of the material (in N/m² or Pa)
  • I = moment of inertia of cross-section (in m⁴)

For a rectangular cross-section cantilever:

If the beam has breadth b and thickness (depth) d:

\[ I = \frac{bd^3}{12} \]

So: \[ \delta = \frac{4Mgl^3}{Ybd^3} \]

For a circular cross-section cantilever:

If the beam has radius r:

\[ I = \frac{\pi r^4}{4} \]

So: \[ \delta = \frac{4Mgl^3}{3\pi Y r^4} \]

⭐ Time Period of Vibration of a Loaded Cantilever

Since the restoring force is proportional to displacement (depression), the vibration is SHM. The time period is:

Formula 1.9 — Time Period of Cantilever:

\[ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{\delta}{g}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{Ml^3}{3YIg}} \]

For a rectangular cross-section:

\[ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{4Ml^3}{Ybd^3 g}} \]

Key Points about Cantilever Vibration
  1. The vibration of a loaded cantilever is approximately SHM for small displacements.
  2. Time period increases with increase in length (l) and mass (M).
  3. Time period decreases with increase in Young's modulus (Y) — stiffer materials vibrate faster.
  4. Time period depends on the geometry (shape and dimensions) of the cross-section.
  5. This principle is used in vibration analysis of structures, bridges, and buildings.

Experimental Determination of Time Period

In the laboratory, we can determine the time period of a cantilever by:

  1. Clamp a thin metallic strip (or meter scale) firmly at one end of a table.
  2. Let a known length project beyond the table edge.
  3. Attach a known mass M at the free end.
  4. Pull the free end slightly and release it.
  5. Count the number of oscillations (N) in a measured time (t seconds).
  6. Time period T = t/N
  7. Repeat with different lengths and masses to verify the formula.
Engineering Applications of Cantilever
  • Bridges: Cantilever bridges use this principle
  • Aircraft wings: Behave like cantilevers during flight
  • MEMS sensors: Micro-cantilevers in sensors and accelerometers
  • Diving boards: Classic cantilever example
  • Building balconies: Act as cantilevers
  • Atomic Force Microscope (AFM): Uses micro-cantilever to scan surfaces

Free, Damped & Forced Vibrations 🔥

Types of Vibrations

Based on the presence of external forces and resistance, vibrations are classified into three types:

1. Free Vibrations (Natural Vibrations)

Definition — Free Vibrations

When a body is set into vibration and then left to vibrate on its own without any external force, the vibrations are called free vibrations. The body vibrates at its natural frequency.

Characteristics:

  • No external periodic force acts on the body after the initial displacement.
  • The body vibrates at its own natural frequency (f₀).
  • In an ideal case (no friction/resistance), the amplitude remains constant forever.
  • In practice, amplitude gradually decreases due to friction and air resistance.
  • The natural frequency depends on the physical properties (mass, stiffness, length, etc.).

Examples:

  1. A pendulum pulled and released in vacuum
  2. A tuning fork struck and allowed to vibrate
  3. A guitar string plucked and left to vibrate
  4. A child on a swing given one push and left swinging
  5. A spring-mass system displaced and released

2. Damped Vibrations

Definition — Damped Vibrations

When the amplitude of vibration gradually decreases with time due to the presence of resistive forces (friction, air resistance, viscosity), the vibrations are called damped vibrations.

Characteristics:

  • Amplitude continuously decreases with time and eventually becomes zero.
  • Energy is lost to the surroundings as heat due to friction or air resistance.
  • The frequency of damped vibration is slightly less than the natural frequency.
  • The resistive force is called the damping force.
  • The vibration eventually dies out (stops).

Damping force is usually proportional to velocity: Fdamping = −bv, where b = damping coefficient.

Examples:

  1. A pendulum vibrating in air (amplitude decreases slowly)
  2. A tuning fork gradually becoming quieter
  3. Shock absorbers in vehicles (designed damping)
  4. A door closer mechanism
  5. A bell that gradually stops ringing
  6. Vibrations of a bridge after a vehicle passes

Types of Damping
  1. Underdamped: Body oscillates with decreasing amplitude (most common case). Example: pendulum in air.
  2. Critically damped: Body returns to mean position in the shortest time without oscillating. Example: car shock absorbers.
  3. Overdamped: Body returns to mean position very slowly without oscillating. Example: door closer (heavy oil damper).

3. Forced Vibrations

Definition — Forced Vibrations

When a body is made to vibrate by applying a continuous external periodic force, the vibrations are called forced vibrations. The body vibrates with the frequency of the external force (not its own natural frequency).

Characteristics:

  • An external periodic force continuously acts on the body.
  • The body vibrates with the frequency of the driving force, not its own natural frequency.
  • The amplitude remains constant as long as the external force is maintained (energy is being supplied).
  • The amplitude depends on the difference between driving frequency and natural frequency.
  • When the driving frequency equals the natural frequency, resonance occurs — amplitude becomes very large!

Examples:

  1. A child being pushed on a swing at regular intervals
  2. Vibrations of a factory floor due to running machines
  3. Vibration of a bridge when soldiers march in step
  4. Sound board of a guitar vibrating when strings are plucked
  5. Vibrations of window panes when a truck passes
  6. Radio receiver tuned to a station (electrical resonance)

⭐ Resonance — A Special Case of Forced Vibration

Definition — Resonance

Resonance occurs when the frequency of the external driving force becomes equal to the natural frequency of the body. At resonance, the amplitude of vibration becomes maximum.

Examples of Resonance:

  • Breaking of a glass by a singer's voice at the right pitch
  • Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse (1940) — wind matched bridge's natural frequency
  • Tuning a radio to a station (electrical resonance)
  • Soldiers breaking step while crossing a bridge (to avoid resonance)
  • MRI machines in hospitals
Danger of Resonance

Resonance can be destructive! If the natural frequency of a bridge matches the marching frequency of soldiers, the bridge can collapse. That's why soldiers are ordered to break step while crossing a bridge.

Wave Motion — Types & Properties 🔥🔥

What is Wave Motion?

Definition — Wave Motion

Wave motion is a form of disturbance that travels through a medium (or space) transferring energy from one point to another without the actual transfer of matter (particles).

Real-world analogy: Imagine you are sitting at one end of a swimming pool and your friend throws a stone at the other end. You see ripples (waves) coming towards you. But the water itself doesn't move from that end to your end — only the disturbance (energy) travels. The water particles just move up and down at their own positions.

Another example: In a cricket stadium, when fans do a "Mexican wave" (standing and sitting in sequence), each person stays in their seat but the wave pattern moves around the stadium. Energy and pattern move, people don't!

Important Characteristics of Wave Motion

  • Waves transfer energy and momentum without transferring matter.
  • Waves require a source of disturbance.
  • Most waves require a medium to travel (exceptions: electromagnetic waves like light).
  • Waves have properties like reflection, refraction, diffraction, interference.
  • The speed of a wave depends on the properties of the medium.

Types of Waves

Waves can be classified in different ways:

A. Based on Medium Requirement:

  1. Mechanical Waves: Need a medium to travel (e.g., sound waves, water waves, seismic waves)
  2. Electromagnetic Waves: Do not need a medium (e.g., light, radio waves, X-rays)

B. Based on Direction of Vibration (Most Important! 🔥):

1. Transverse Waves

Definition — Transverse Wave

A transverse wave is a wave in which the particles of the medium vibrate perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction of propagation of the wave.

Think of it like this: the wave moves forward (horizontal), but particles move up-and-down (vertical). Like shaking a rope up and down — the wave travels along the rope but the rope particles move up and down.

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Key features:

  • Forms crests (peaks/high points) and troughs (low points).
  • Can travel through solids and on the surface of liquids.
  • Cannot travel through gases or inside liquids (because these media cannot sustain shear stress).
  • Can be polarized (vibration restricted to one plane).

Examples of Transverse Waves:

  1. Light waves (electromagnetic — transverse in nature)
  2. Waves on a string or rope
  3. Water surface waves (partially transverse)
  4. S-waves (secondary seismic waves) in earthquakes
  5. Radio waves, microwaves, X-rays (all electromagnetic)

2. Longitudinal Waves

Definition — Longitudinal Wave

A longitudinal wave is a wave in which the particles of the medium vibrate parallel (along) the direction of propagation of the wave.

Think of it like this: both the wave and the particles move in the same direction (back and forth along the same line). Like pushing and pulling a slinky (spring toy) — compressions and rarefactions travel along the slinky.

Fig. 1.10: Longitudinal wave showing compressions and rarefactions

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Key features:

  • Forms compressions (where particles are close together, high pressure) and rarefactions (where particles are spread apart, low pressure).
  • Can travel through solids, liquids, and gases — all three states of matter.
  • Cannot be polarized.
  • Speed is generally highest in solids, lower in liquids, and lowest in gases.

Examples of Longitudinal Waves:

  1. Sound waves (the most important example!)
  2. Waves in a slinky (when pushed and pulled)
  3. P-waves (primary seismic waves) in earthquakes
  4. Ultrasonic waves
  5. Pressure waves in a pipe
Very Important for Exam

Sound is a longitudinal wave — particles vibrate parallel to direction of wave travel. Light is a transverse wave — electric and magnetic fields vibrate perpendicular to direction of wave travel. This is asked almost every year!

Wave Parameters (Definitions) 🔥

Wavelength (λ — lambda)

The distance between two consecutive points in the same phase of vibration (e.g., two consecutive crests, or two consecutive compressions). SI Unit: metre (m).

Frequency (f or ν — nu)

The number of complete waves (oscillations) produced in one second. SI Unit: Hertz (Hz) = s⁻¹.

Wave Velocity (v)

The distance travelled by the wave in one second. It is the speed at which the wave disturbance propagates through the medium. SI Unit: m/s.

Time Period (T)

The time taken to complete one full wave (or one complete oscillation). T = 1/f. SI Unit: second (s).

Amplitude (A)

The maximum displacement of a particle from its mean position. SI Unit: metre (m).

⭐ Relationship between Wave Velocity, Frequency and Wavelength

In one time period T, the wave travels a distance equal to one wavelength λ.

Therefore:

Formula 1.10 — Wave Equation (Fundamental):

\[ v = f\lambda \]

where:

  • v = wave velocity (m/s)
  • f = frequency (Hz)
  • λ = wavelength (m)

Also: \( v = \frac{\lambda}{T} \) (since f = 1/T)

Must Remember!

v = fλ is the most fundamental wave equation. It applies to ALL types of waves — sound, light, water waves, radio waves. This formula is used in almost every numerical problem on waves!

Equation of a Plane Progressive Wave 🔥🔥

What is a Plane Progressive Wave?

Definition — Plane Progressive Wave

A plane progressive wave (or travelling wave) is a wave that travels continuously in a medium in a definite direction without any change in amplitude. Each particle of the medium executes SHM about its mean position as the wave passes through it.

⭐ Derivation of the Equation of a Plane Progressive Wave

Consider a transverse wave travelling in the positive x-direction with velocity v.

Step 1: At the origin (x = 0), the displacement of the particle at time t is:

\[ y_0 = A\sin(\omega t) \]

Step 2: Now consider a particle at a distance x from the origin. The wave takes time \(\frac{x}{v}\) to reach this particle from the origin. So this particle starts vibrating later than the origin by time \(\frac{x}{v}\).

Step 3: The displacement of the particle at position x at time t is the same as the displacement of the particle at the origin at an earlier time \((t - \frac{x}{v})\):

\[ y = A\sin\left[\omega\left(t - \frac{x}{v}\right)\right] \]

Formula 1.11 — Equation of Plane Progressive Wave (travelling in +x direction):

\[ y = A\sin(\omega t - kx) \]

where:

  • y = displacement of particle at position x at time t (m)
  • A = amplitude (maximum displacement) (m)
  • ω = angular frequency = 2πf = 2π/T (rad/s)
  • k = wave number (propagation constant) = 2π/λ = ω/v (rad/m)
  • t = time (s)
  • x = position of the particle from origin (m)

Alternative Forms of the Wave Equation

Different Forms (All Equivalent)

Form 1: \( y = A\sin(\omega t - kx) \)

Form 2: \( y = A\sin\left[2\pi\left(\frac{t}{T} - \frac{x}{\lambda}\right)\right] \)

Form 3: \( y = A\sin\left[\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}(vt - x)\right] \)

Form 4: \( y = A\sin\left[2\pi(ft - \frac{x}{\lambda})\right] \)

All of these represent the same wave — just expressed using different parameters.

Wave Travelling in Negative x-direction

If the wave travels in the negative x-direction (towards left), the equation becomes:

\[ y = A\sin(\omega t + kx) \]

(Note: the sign between ωt and kx changes from minus to plus)

Common Exam Mistake

Students often get confused between the signs:

  • y = A sin(ωt − kx) → wave travelling in +x direction (to the right)
  • y = A sin(ωt + kx) → wave travelling in −x direction (to the left)

Memory trick: If the signs of ωt and kx are opposite, wave goes in +x direction. If same sign, wave goes in −x direction.

Important Relationships

Wave Number: \( k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} = \frac{\omega}{v} \) (Unit: rad/m)

Angular Frequency: \( \omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T} = kv \) (Unit: rad/s)

Wave Velocity: \( v = \frac{\omega}{k} = f\lambda = \frac{\lambda}{T} \) (Unit: m/s)

Particle Velocity vs Wave Velocity

Don't Confuse These!

Wave velocity (v): The speed at which the wave pattern moves through the medium. It is constant for a given medium. \(v = f\lambda\)

Particle velocity (vp): The speed at which individual particles of the medium vibrate. It varies — maximum at mean position, zero at extreme position.

\[ v_p = \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = A\omega\cos(\omega t - kx) \]

Maximum particle velocity = Aω

Exam Favourite

Questions often ask you to: "Write the equation of a progressive wave" given amplitude, frequency, wavelength, and direction. Just substitute values into: y = A sin(ωt − kx). Calculate ω = 2πf and k = 2π/λ.

Superposition of Waves & Beat Formation 🔥

Principle of Superposition of Waves

Definition — Principle of Superposition

When two or more waves pass through the same point in a medium simultaneously, the resultant displacement at that point is equal to the algebraic (vector) sum of the individual displacements caused by each wave separately.

Mathematically: \( y = y_1 + y_2 + y_3 + \ldots \)

Analogy: Imagine two people throwing stones into a pond at different points. The ripples from both stones meet and overlap. At the meeting point, the water surface rises higher when both crests meet (constructive) and becomes flat when a crest meets a trough (destructive). After crossing, each wave continues as if the other didn't exist!

Results of Superposition

Types of Superposition Effects
  1. Constructive Interference: When crests meet crests (or troughs meet troughs) — resultant amplitude = sum of individual amplitudes. The waves reinforce each other.
  2. Destructive Interference: When crest meets trough — resultant amplitude = difference of individual amplitudes. The waves cancel each other (partially or fully).

These effects lead to important phenomena like interference, beats, and stationary waves.

⭐ Beats

Definition — Beats

Beats is the phenomenon of periodic rise and fall (waxing and waning) in the intensity of sound when two sound waves of slightly different frequencies travel in the same direction and superpose on each other.

Analogy: Imagine two clocks ticking at slightly different rates. Sometimes both tick together (loud — constructive), sometimes one ticks when the other is between ticks (soft — destructive). The sound alternates between loud and soft regularly. This is like beats!

How Beats are Formed?

Consider two sound waves with frequencies f₁ and f₂ (where f₁ ≈ f₂ but not exactly equal):

  • At some moments, the crests of both waves arrive together → constructive interference → sound is loud (maximum intensity) — this is called WAXING.
  • At other moments, the crest of one wave coincides with the trough of the other → destructive interference → sound is soft/silent (minimum intensity) — this is called WANING.
  • This alternating loud-soft pattern repeats periodically.

Mathematical Analysis of Beats

Let two waves have the same amplitude A but different frequencies f₁ and f₂:

\[ y_1 = A\sin(2\pi f_1 t) \quad \text{and} \quad y_2 = A\sin(2\pi f_2 t) \]

By superposition: y = y₁ + y₂

Using the trigonometric identity: sin C + sin D = 2 cos\(\frac{C-D}{2}\) sin\(\frac{C+D}{2}\)

\[ y = 2A\cos\left[2\pi\left(\frac{f_1 - f_2}{2}\right)t\right] \cdot \sin\left[2\pi\left(\frac{f_1 + f_2}{2}\right)t\right] \]

Interpretation

The resultant wave has:

  • Apparent frequency = \(\frac{f_1 + f_2}{2}\) (average of the two frequencies)
  • Amplitude that varies as: \(2A\cos\left[2\pi\left(\frac{f_1 - f_2}{2}\right)t\right]\)
  • The amplitude oscillates between 0 and 2A

⭐ Beat Frequency

Formula 1.12 — Beat Frequency:

\[ f_{beat} = |f_1 - f_2| \]

where:

  • fbeat = number of beats heard per second (beat frequency)
  • f₁ = frequency of first sound wave (Hz)
  • f₂ = frequency of second sound wave (Hz)

One beat = one cycle of waxing (loud) and waning (soft).

Formula 1.13 — Beat Period:

\[ T_{beat} = \frac{1}{f_{beat}} = \frac{1}{|f_1 - f_2|} \]

Key Points for Exam
  • Beats can be heard only when the difference in frequencies is small (usually less than about 10 Hz). If the difference is too large, the ear cannot distinguish individual beats.
  • Human ear can detect beats up to about 10 beats per second.
  • Beat frequency = |f₁ − f₂| — always take the positive (absolute) value.

Applications of Beats

Practical Applications of Beats
  1. Tuning musical instruments: Musicians tune their instruments by listening to beats. When beats disappear (fbeat = 0), the instrument is perfectly tuned.
  2. Determining unknown frequency: By producing beats between a known and unknown frequency source, the unknown frequency can be calculated.
  3. Doppler radar: Uses beat frequency to measure speed of objects.
  4. Radio receivers: Heterodyne receivers use beat principle.
  5. Detecting dangerous gases in mines: Change in beat frequency indicates change in gas composition.
Quick Example — Beats

Q: Two tuning forks have frequencies 256 Hz and 260 Hz. How many beats per second are heard?

Solution

f₁ = 256 Hz, f₂ = 260 Hz

Beat frequency = |f₁ − f₂| = |256 − 260| = 4 beats per second

This means you will hear 4 cycles of loud-soft-loud-soft in one second.

Acoustics of Buildings 🔥🔥

What is Acoustics?

Definition — Acoustics

Acoustics is the branch of physics that deals with the study of sound — its production, transmission, reception, and effects. Architectural acoustics (or building acoustics) specifically deals with the design of buildings (halls, auditoriums, theatres, cinemas) for good quality sound.

Have you ever noticed that your voice sounds different in a bathroom versus a carpeted bedroom? That's acoustics! In a tiled bathroom, sound bounces off hard walls and you hear echoes. In a carpeted room, soft materials absorb sound and you hear clearly without echoes.

⭐ Reverberation

Definition — Reverberation

Reverberation is the phenomenon of persistence (prolongation) of sound in a closed space even after the original sound source has stopped, due to multiple reflections from walls, ceiling, floor, and other surfaces.

Explanation: When a speaker speaks in a hall, the sound reaches the listener in two ways:

  1. Direct sound: Travels straight from speaker to listener.
  2. Reflected sound: Bounces off walls, ceiling, floor, and then reaches the listener.

The reflected sound arrives slightly later than the direct sound. If the hall is large and has hard surfaces, the reflected sound takes a long time to die out. This continuous overlapping of direct and reflected sound is called reverberation.

Effects of Reverberation
  • Too much reverberation: Sound becomes unclear and blurry. Words overlap with their echoes. Speech becomes difficult to understand.
  • Too little reverberation: Sound dies quickly and feels "dead." The hall feels uncomfortably quiet. Sound doesn't reach the back rows.
  • Optimum reverberation: Sound is clear, pleasant, sufficiently loud, and reaches all corners of the hall. This is the goal of good acoustic design!

⭐ Reverberation Time

Definition — Reverberation Time

Reverberation time (T) is the time taken for the sound intensity to fall to one-millionth (10⁻⁶) of its original intensity after the source is stopped. In terms of sound level, it is the time for the sound to decrease by 60 decibels (dB).

⭐ Sabine's Formula for Reverberation Time

Prof. W.C. Sabine (considered the father of architectural acoustics) derived the following formula:

Formula 1.14 — Sabine's Formula:

\[ T = \frac{0.161 \times V}{\sum aS} = \frac{0.161V}{A} \]

where:

  • T = reverberation time (in seconds)
  • V = volume of the room/hall (in m³)
  • a = absorption coefficient of the surface material (dimensionless, 0 to 1)
  • S = area of the absorbing surface (in m²)
  • A = ΣaS = total absorption of the room (in sabins or m²)
  • 0.161 = Sabine's constant (in seconds/metre)

If a room has multiple surfaces with different materials:

\[ A = a_1S_1 + a_2S_2 + a_3S_3 + \ldots \]

Most Important Formula!

Sabine's formula is the most frequently asked formula from this unit in WBSCTE exams. Both in theory and numerical problems. Memorize: T = 0.161V / A

⭐ Coefficient of Absorption of Sound

Definition — Absorption Coefficient (a)

The absorption coefficient of a surface is the ratio of sound energy absorbed by the surface to the total sound energy incident on it.

\[ a = \frac{\text{Sound energy absorbed}}{\text{Total sound energy incident}} \]

Value ranges from 0 to 1:

  • a = 0 → perfect reflector (no absorption — like marble/glass)
  • a = 1 → perfect absorber (100% absorption — like an open window)

⭐ Methods to Control Reverberation Time

According to Sabine's formula T = 0.161V/A:

  • To decrease T → increase total absorption (A) or decrease volume (V)
  • To increase T → decrease absorption or increase volume
Practical Methods to Control Reverberation Time
  1. Use sound-absorbing materials on walls and ceilings: Acoustic tiles, perforated boards, fibreboard, mineral wool panels. These absorb sound and reduce reflections.
  2. Heavy curtains and drapes: Hanging thick curtains on walls and windows significantly increases absorption.
  3. Carpets and rugs on floors: Soft floor covering absorbs sound that would otherwise reflect off hard floors.
  4. Cushioned/upholstered seats: Padded seats absorb more sound than wooden benches. (Helps maintain consistent acoustics whether hall is full or empty.)
  5. Audience (people): The audience itself is a major absorber. An empty hall has different acoustics than a full one.
  6. Proper shape design: Curved walls and domed ceilings can cause focusing of sound. Use flat or broken-up surfaces to scatter sound evenly.
  7. Avoid large flat parallel walls: These cause flutter echoes. Use angled or irregular surfaces.
  8. Use of false ceilings: A perforated false ceiling with absorbing material above it.
  9. Windows: Opening windows (a = 1) dramatically increases absorption.

Noise

Definition — Noise

Noise is an unwanted, unpleasant, and disturbing sound that causes discomfort and interferes with the desired sound (signal). Noise reduces the clarity and intelligibility of speech and music in a hall.

Types of noise in buildings:

  1. Airborne noise: Transmitted through air — traffic noise, aircraft noise, loudspeakers outside.
  2. Structure-borne noise: Transmitted through the building structure — footsteps on upper floor, vibrations from machines, lifts.
  3. Internal noise: From inside the building — air conditioning units, fans, chattering audience.

Methods to reduce noise:

  • Use soundproof doors and double-glazed windows.
  • Use sound insulation in walls (cavity walls with absorbing material).
  • Place noisy machines on vibration-absorbing mounts.
  • Use acoustic enclosures around noisy equipment.
  • Plant trees around the building (act as sound barriers).
  • Use thick walls and heavy materials that don't transmit sound easily.

What are Ultrasonic Waves?

Definition — Ultrasonic Waves

Ultrasonic waves (or ultrasonics) are sound waves with frequency greater than 20,000 Hz (20 kHz), which is above the upper limit of human hearing. They have the same nature as ordinary sound waves (longitudinal, mechanical) but cannot be heard by human ears.

Interesting fact: Dogs can hear up to about 40 kHz, and bats can hear and produce ultrasonic waves up to 100 kHz for navigation (echolocation)!

⭐ Properties of Ultrasonic Waves

Properties of Ultrasonics
  1. Not audible: Cannot be heard by human ears (frequency > 20 kHz).
  2. Very short wavelength: Due to high frequency (λ = v/f), the wavelength is very small. This gives them beam-like directional properties.
  3. High energy: Carry more energy than audible sound due to high frequency.
  4. Can be focused: Like light, they can be focused into narrow beams using reflectors.
  5. Follow laws of reflection and refraction: Just like ordinary sound and light.
  6. Can travel long distances: Especially in water, with less absorption than audible sound.
  7. High penetrating power: Can penetrate through solids, liquids, and can detect internal defects.
  8. Produce heating effect: When ultrasonic waves pass through a medium, some energy is absorbed, causing local heating.
  9. Cause cavitation: In liquids, intense ultrasonic waves create tiny bubbles that collapse violently — this is called cavitation. Useful for cleaning.
  10. Can produce chemical effects: Can accelerate certain chemical reactions.
  11. Can produce biological effects: Can destroy bacteria and break cells (used in medicine).

Production of Ultrasonic Waves (Brief)

Ultrasonic waves are produced mainly by two methods:

  1. Magnetostriction method: Uses a ferromagnetic rod (nickel) placed in an alternating magnetic field. The rod vibrates at its natural frequency and produces ultrasonic waves. (Frequency up to ~300 kHz)
  2. Piezoelectric method: Uses a piezoelectric crystal (like quartz) placed between two metal plates with an AC voltage. The crystal vibrates at the frequency of the applied voltage and produces ultrasonic waves. (Frequency up to several MHz)

⭐ Engineering Applications of Ultrasonic Waves

Engineering Applications
  1. Non-Destructive Testing (NDT) / Flaw Detection: Ultrasonic waves are sent through metal parts (castings, welds, railway tracks). If there is an internal crack or defect, the wave reflects back from the flaw. By analyzing the reflected signal, the location and size of the defect can be found without breaking the material. This is called ultrasonic flaw detection.
  2. Ultrasonic Cleaning: Objects like jewellery, electronic components, lenses, surgical instruments are placed in a liquid bath. Ultrasonic waves create cavitation (tiny bubbles that collapse violently) which removes dirt, grease, and contaminants from every tiny crevice.
  3. Ultrasonic Welding: Used to weld plastics and thin metal sheets. High-frequency vibrations generate heat at the joint surface, melting and fusing the materials together.
  4. Ultrasonic Drilling: Very hard and brittle materials (glass, ceramics, semiconductors) can be drilled using ultrasonic vibrations with an abrasive slurry.
  5. SONAR (Sound Navigation And Ranging): Used in ships and submarines to detect underwater objects (other submarines, icebergs, sea floor depth, fish schools). Ultrasonic pulse is sent into water, and the echo is received. Distance = (v × t)/2.
  6. Ultrasonic Soldering: For soldering aluminium and other difficult-to-solder metals.
  7. Depth of Sea Measurement: By sending ultrasonic pulses and timing the echo from the sea bed. This is called echo sounding or SONAR depth finding.
  8. Emulsification: Used to mix immiscible liquids (oil and water) to form emulsions (used in pharmaceutical and food industries).

⭐ Medical Applications of Ultrasonic Waves

Medical Applications
  1. Ultrasonography (Ultrasound Imaging): The most common medical application. Ultrasonic waves are sent into the body and reflected signals from internal organs create images on a screen. Used for:
    • Pregnancy monitoring (fetal imaging)
    • Examining liver, kidney, gallbladder, heart
    • Detecting tumours and cysts
    Safe because it uses no harmful radiation (unlike X-rays).
  2. Echocardiography: Ultrasound imaging of the heart to detect heart valve problems, chamber sizes, blood flow patterns.
  3. Lithotripsy: High-intensity focused ultrasonic waves are used to break kidney stones and gallstones into small pieces without surgery. The fragments are then naturally passed out.
  4. Physiotherapy / Diathermy: Ultrasonic waves are used to provide deep heating to muscles, tendons, and joints. This reduces pain, increases blood flow, and promotes healing of injured tissues.
  5. Ultrasonic Surgery: High-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU) can destroy diseased tissue (like tumours) without cutting.
  6. Dental Cleaning: Ultrasonic scalers remove plaque and tartar from teeth.
  7. Sterilization: Ultrasonic waves can destroy bacteria in medical instruments and liquid medicines.
  8. Blood Flow Measurement (Doppler Ultrasound): Uses Doppler effect with ultrasonics to measure blood flow velocity in arteries and veins.

Numerical 1: SHM — Velocity & Acceleration

Problem

A particle executes SHM with an amplitude of 5 cm and a frequency of 10 Hz. Find:

  1. The maximum velocity
  2. The maximum acceleration
  3. The velocity when displacement is 3 cm
Solution

Amplitude, A = 5 cm = 0.05 m

Frequency, f = 10 Hz

Angular frequency: ω = 2πf = 2 × 3.14 × 10 = 62.8 rad/s

Maximum velocity: vmax = ωA = 62.8 × 0.05

vmax = 3.14 m/s

Maximum acceleration: amax = ω²A = (62.8)² × 0.05 = 3943.84 × 0.05

amax = 197.19 m/s² ≈ 197.2 m/s²

Velocity at x = 3 cm = 0.03 m:

v = ω√(A² − x²) = 62.8 × √(0.05² − 0.03²)

v = 62.8 × √(0.0025 − 0.0009) = 62.8 × √0.0016

v = 62.8 × 0.04

v = 2.512 m/s ≈ 2.51 m/s

Numerical 2: SHM — Time Period

Problem

A body executing SHM has an acceleration of 8 m/s² when its displacement is 2 m from the mean position. Calculate the time period of oscillation.

Solution

Acceleration, |a| = 8 m/s²

Displacement, x = 2 m

In SHM: |a| = ω²x

ω² = |a|/x = 8/2 = 4

ω = √4 = 2 rad/s

T = 2π/ω = 2 × 3.14 / 2 = 3.14 s

T = 3.14 seconds ≈ π seconds

Problem

A sound wave has a frequency of 500 Hz and travels through air with a speed of 340 m/s. Calculate:

  1. The wavelength
  2. The time period

f = 500 Hz, v = 340 m/s

v = fλ

λ = v/f = 340/500

λ = 0.68 m = 68 cm

T = 1/f = 1/500

T = 0.002 s = 2 × 10⁻³ s = 2 ms

Problem

A hall has a volume of 5000 m³. The total absorption in the hall is 200 sabins (m²). Calculate the reverberation time. Also, if the hall is to have a reverberation time of 1.5 s, what additional absorption is needed?

Numerical 8: SONAR — Depth Measurement

Problem

An ultrasonic signal is sent from a ship towards the sea bed. The echo is received after 1.5 seconds. If the speed of sound in sea water is 1500 m/s, calculate the depth of the sea.

Solution

Total time for echo, t = 1.5 s

Speed of sound in sea water, v = 1500 m/s

The signal travels to the sea bed and back. So total distance = 2d (where d = depth).

Total distance = v × t = 1500 × 1.5 = 2250 m

Depth d = Total distance / 2 = 2250 / 2

Depth of sea = 1125 m = 1.125 km

Important Definitions

Exam Strategy

Definition-type questions carry 2 marks each. Learn these definitions word-by-word. Write the definition first, then add the formula (if any), and finally the SI unit. This guarantees full marks!

1. Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

SHM is a type of oscillatory motion in which the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement from the mean position and always directed towards the mean position. F = −kx, or equivalently, a = −ω²x.

2. Amplitude

The maximum displacement of a vibrating body from its mean (equilibrium) position. SI Unit: metre (m). Symbol: A.

3. Time Period

The time taken by a vibrating body to complete one full oscillation. SI Unit: second (s). Symbol: T. T = 1/f.

4. Frequency

The number of complete oscillations made by a vibrating body in one second. SI Unit: Hertz (Hz). Symbol: f or ν. f = 1/T.

5. Angular Frequency

The rate of change of phase angle per unit time. ω = 2πf = 2π/T. SI Unit: radian per second (rad/s).

6. Cantilever

A beam or rod that is rigidly fixed at one end and free at the other end. When the free end is displaced and released, it vibrates with SHM.

7. Free Vibrations

Vibrations of a body which vibrates freely at its natural frequency without any external periodic force after the initial displacement. The amplitude remains constant (ideal case).

8. Damped Vibrations

Vibrations in which the amplitude gradually decreases with time due to resistive forces like friction, air resistance, or viscosity. The vibration eventually dies out.

9. Forced Vibrations

Vibrations of a body caused by an external periodic force applied continuously. The body vibrates with the frequency of the driving force, not its natural frequency.

10. Resonance

The phenomenon in which the amplitude of forced vibrations becomes maximum when the frequency of the external driving force equals the natural frequency of the vibrating body.

11. Wave Motion

A disturbance that travels through a medium (or space) transferring energy from one point to another without the actual transfer of matter.

12. Transverse Wave

A wave in which the particles of the medium vibrate perpendicular to the direction of propagation of the wave. It forms crests and troughs. Example: Light wave.

13. Longitudinal Wave

A wave in which the particles of the medium vibrate parallel to the direction of propagation of the wave. It forms compressions and rarefactions. Example: Sound wave.

14. Wavelength (λ)

The distance between two consecutive points in the same phase of vibration (e.g., two consecutive crests or compressions). SI Unit: metre (m).

15. Wave Velocity

The distance travelled by the wave disturbance in one second through the medium. v = fλ. SI Unit: m/s.

16. Principle of Superposition

When two or more waves meet at a point, the resultant displacement at that point is equal to the algebraic sum of the displacements caused by individual waves. y = y₁ + y₂ + ...

17. Beats

The periodic rise and fall (waxing and waning) in the intensity of sound caused by the superposition of two sound waves of slightly different frequencies travelling in the same direction. Beat frequency = |f₁ − f₂|.

18. Reverberation

The persistence (prolongation) of sound in a closed room/hall after the source of sound has stopped, due to multiple reflections of sound from walls, ceiling, and other surfaces.

19. Reverberation Time

The time taken for the sound intensity in a hall to fall to one-millionth (10⁻⁶) of its original intensity after the source stops. Equivalently, the time for sound to decrease by 60 dB. Given by Sabine's formula: T = 0.161V/ΣaS.

20. Absorption Coefficient

The ratio of sound energy absorbed by a surface to the total sound energy incident on it. It is a dimensionless quantity ranging from 0 (perfect reflector) to 1 (perfect absorber like an open window).

21. Ultrasonic Waves

Sound waves having frequency greater than 20,000 Hz (20 kHz), which is above the upper limit of human hearing. They are longitudinal mechanical waves with very short wavelength and high energy.

22. Noise

An unwanted, unpleasant, and disturbing sound that interferes with the desired sound and causes discomfort to the listener.

Q1. Define SHM and give two examples.

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is a type of oscillatory motion in which the restoring force acting on the body is directly proportional to the displacement from the mean position and is always directed towards the mean position. Mathematically: F = −kx, or a = −ω²x.

Examples: (1) Vibration of a simple pendulum with small amplitude. (2) Oscillation of a mass attached to a spring.

Q2. What is the difference between time period and frequency?

Time Period (T): The time taken to complete one full oscillation. Unit: second (s).

Frequency (f): The number of complete oscillations in one second. Unit: Hertz (Hz).

They are reciprocals of each other: f = 1/T and T = 1/f.

Q3. State the principle of superposition of waves.

The principle of superposition states that when two or more waves pass through the same point simultaneously, the resultant displacement at that point is equal to the algebraic sum of the individual displacements caused by each wave independently. i.e., y = y₁ + y₂ + y₃ + ... This principle leads to phenomena like interference and beats.

Q4. Define beats and write the formula for beat frequency.

Beats: The periodic rise and fall (waxing and waning) in the intensity of sound when two sound waves of slightly different frequencies superpose while travelling in the same direction.

Beat frequency: fbeat = |f₁ − f₂|, where f₁ and f₂ are the frequencies of the two sound waves. For example, if f₁ = 256 Hz and f₂ = 260 Hz, then beat frequency = 4 beats/second.

Q5. What is reverberation? Is too much reverberation desirable?

Reverberation: The persistence of sound in a closed hall after the source of sound has stopped, caused by multiple reflections from walls, ceiling, and other surfaces.

No, too much reverberation is not desirable because it causes overlapping of successive sounds, making speech unclear. The sound becomes blurred and unintelligible. An optimum reverberation time is needed for good acoustics.

Q6. Define absorption coefficient of sound. What is its range?

Absorption coefficient (a): It is the ratio of sound energy absorbed by a surface to the total sound energy incident on it. a = Energy absorbed / Energy incident.

Range: 0 to 1. When a = 0, the surface is a perfect reflector (no absorption). When a = 1, the surface is a perfect absorber (e.g., an open window). Soft, porous materials have high absorption coefficient.

Q7. What are ultrasonic waves? Why can't humans hear them?

Ultrasonic waves are sound waves having frequency greater than 20,000 Hz (20 kHz). They are mechanical longitudinal waves like ordinary sound but have very high frequency and very short wavelength.

Humans cannot hear them because the human ear can only detect sound in the range of 20 Hz to 20 kHz. Frequencies above 20 kHz do not stimulate the auditory mechanism of the human ear.

Q8. What is a cantilever? Give two examples.

Cantilever: A beam or rod that is rigidly fixed (clamped) at one end and free at the other end. When the free end is displaced and released, the cantilever vibrates with SHM (for small displacements).

Examples: (1) A diving board at a swimming pool. (2) A ruler clamped at the edge of a table with one end projecting out.

Q9. State the relationship v = fλ and explain each term.

The fundamental wave equation is: v = fλ

Where: v = wave velocity (speed at which wave travels), unit m/s; f = frequency (number of waves per second), unit Hz; λ = wavelength (distance between two successive crests or compressions), unit m.

This means: in one second, f waves pass a point, each of length λ, so total distance covered = fλ = velocity.

Q10. Distinguish between constructive and destructive interference.

Constructive interference: When crest of one wave meets crest of another (or trough meets trough), the resultant amplitude increases (sum of individual amplitudes). The waves reinforce each other. Sound becomes louder.

Destructive interference: When crest of one wave meets trough of another, the resultant amplitude decreases (difference of amplitudes). The waves cancel each other. Sound becomes softer or silent (if equal amplitudes).

Q3. What is reverberation? State Sabine's formula and explain each term. How can reverberation time be controlled? [7 marks] 🔥🔥

  3. Use carpets on floors.

Where:

  • y = displacement of particle at position x at time t (m)
  • A = amplitude — maximum displacement (m)
  • ω = angular frequency = 2πf = 2π/T (rad/s)
  • t = time instant (s)
  • k = wave number = 2π/λ = ω/v (rad/m)

Q6. What are beats? Explain how beats are formed. Derive the expression for beat frequency. State applications of beats. [7 marks]

Beats: The periodic rise (waxing) and fall (waning) in the intensity of sound when two sound waves of slightly different frequencies travel in the same direction and superpose.

Formation: When two sound waves with frequencies f₁ and f₂ (f₁ ≈ f₂) meet at a point:

  • When crests of both waves coincide → constructive interference → loud sound (waxing).
  • When crest of one meets trough of other → destructive interference → soft/silent (waning).
  • This alternating loud-soft pattern repeats periodically.

Derivation:

Let y₁ = A sin(2πf₁t) and y₂ = A sin(2πf₂t)

By superposition: y = y₁ + y₂ = A[sin(2πf₁t) + sin(2πf₂t)]

Using sin C + sin D = 2cos[(C−D)/2]sin[(C+D)/2]:

y = 2A cos[2π((f₁−f₂)/2)t] × sin[2π((f₁+f₂)/2)t]

The resultant has varying amplitude = 2A cos[2π((f₁−f₂)/2)t]

The amplitude becomes maximum twice in each cycle of the cosine term.

Beat frequency = |f₁ − f₂|

Applications: (1) Tuning musical instruments, (2) Determining unknown frequency, (3) Doppler radar, (4) Radio receivers (heterodyne principle).

Damped Vibrations: When amplitude gradually decreases with time due to resistive forces (friction, air resistance). Energy is lost as heat. Example: Pendulum in air, shock absorber in car.

Forced Vibrations: When body vibrates under a continuous external periodic force. Vibrates at the frequency of driving force. Amplitude stays constant. When driving frequency = natural frequency, resonance occurs (maximum amplitude). Example: Child pushed on swing repeatedly.

P4. The equation of a wave is y = 0.03 sin(400πt − 4πx) metre. Find amplitude, frequency, wavelength and wave velocity. [4 marks]

Comparing with y = A sin(ωt − kx):

A = 0.03 m = 3 cm

ω = 400π, so f = ω/(2π) = 400π/(2π) = 200 Hz

k = 4π, so λ = 2π/k = 2π/(4π) = 0.5 m = 50 cm

v = fλ = 200 × 0.5 = 100 m/s

(Or: v = ω/k = 400π/4π = 100 m/s ✓)

P5. Two tuning forks produce 5 beats per second. If the frequency of one fork is 480 Hz, what are the possible frequencies of the other fork? [2 marks]

Given: f₁ = 480 Hz, fbeat = 5 Hz

fbeat = |f₁ − f₂|

5 = |480 − f₂|

f₂ = 480 + 5 = 485 Hz OR f₂ = 480 − 5 = 475 Hz

(To determine which one, additional information like wax loading test is needed.)

P6. An ultrasonic pulse is sent from a ship and its echo from the sea bed is received after 0.8 seconds. If the speed of sound in sea water is 1450 m/s, find the depth of the sea. [2 marks]

Given: t = 0.8 s, v = 1450 m/s

d = vt/2 = (1450 × 0.8)/2 = 1160/2

d = 580 m

P7. Find the time period and frequency of a simple pendulum of length 0.25 m. (g = 9.8 m/s²) [3 marks]

Given: l = 0.25 m, g = 9.8 m/s²

T = 2π√(l/g) = 2 × 3.14 × √(0.25/9.8)

T = 6.28 × √(0.02551) = 6.28 × 0.1597

T = 1.003 s ≈ 1.0 s

f = 1/T = 1/1.003 = 0.997 Hz ≈ 1.0 Hz

P8. A hall of volume 8000 m³ has a reverberation time of 2.5 seconds. If the reverberation time is to be reduced to 1.5 s, how much additional absorption is needed? [4 marks]

Given: V = 8000 m³, T₁ = 2.5 s, T₂ = 1.5 s

Step 1: Find existing absorption A₁:

T₁ = 0.161V/A₁ → A₁ = 0.161 × 8000/2.5 = 1288/2.5 = 515.2 m²

Step 2: Find required absorption A₂:

T₂ = 0.161V/A₂ → A₂ = 0.161 × 8000/1.5 = 1288/1.5 = 858.67 m²

Step 3: Additional absorption = A₂ − A₁ = 858.67 − 515.2

Additional absorption needed = 343.47 m² ≈ 343.5 sabins

Quick Revision — Unit Summary 🎯

Last-Minute Revision Points

🔵 SHM — Simple Harmonic Motion

  • SHM: Restoring force ∝ displacement, directed towards mean position: F = −kx, a = −ω²x
  • Displacement: x = A sin(ωt + φ₀)
  • Velocity: v = ω√(A² − x²) → Max at mean (ωA), Zero at extreme
  • Acceleration: a = −ω²x → Zero at mean, Max at extreme (ω²A)
  • Time Period: T = 2π/ω = 2π√(x/a)
  • Pendulum: T = 2π√(l/g) | Spring-mass: T = 2π√(m/k)
  • Energy: KE = ½mω²(A²−x²), PE = ½mω²x², TE = ½mω²A² = constant
  • KE max at mean, PE max at extreme, TE always constant

🔵 Cantilever

  • Fixed at one end, free at other end
  • Depression: δ = Mgl³/(3YI)
  • Time period: T = 2π√(δ/g)

🔵 Types of Vibrations

  • Free: No external force, natural frequency, constant amplitude (ideal)
  • Damped: Friction present, amplitude decreases, dies out
  • Forced: External periodic force, driving frequency, constant amplitude
  • Resonance: When driving freq = natural freq → amplitude maximum

🔵 Wave Motion

  • Waves transfer energy, NOT matter
  • Transverse: Vibration ⊥ wave direction, crests & troughs, light waves
  • Longitudinal: Vibration ∥ wave direction, compressions & rarefactions, sound waves
  • Fundamental equation: v = fλ
  • Sound: longitudinal, needs medium; Light: transverse, no medium needed

🔵 Progressive Wave Equation

  • y = A sin(ωt − kx) → wave in +x direction
  • y = A sin(ωt + kx) → wave in −x direction
  • k = 2π/λ (wave number), ω = 2πf, v = ω/k = fλ

🔵 Beats

  • Caused by superposition of two waves of slightly different frequencies
  • Beat frequency = |f₁ − f₂|
  • Waxing (loud) + Waning (soft) = 1 beat
  • Application: tuning instruments, finding unknown frequency

🔵 Acoustics

  • Reverberation: Persistence of sound due to multiple reflections
  • Reverberation time: Time for sound to decrease by 60 dB (to 10⁻⁶)
  • Sabine's formula: T = 0.161V/ΣaS 🔥🔥
  • Absorption coefficient (a): 0 = perfect reflector, 1 = open window
  • Control methods: acoustic tiles, carpets, curtains, cushioned seats
  • Optimum T: Lecture = 0.5-1.5s, Concert = 1.5-2.5s

🔵 Ultrasonics

  • Frequency > 20 kHz, not audible to humans
  • Short wavelength → can be focused, high energy, high penetration
  • Engineering: NDT/flaw detection, SONAR, cleaning, welding, drilling
  • Medical: Ultrasonography, echocardiography, lithotripsy, physiotherapy
  • SONAR depth: d = vt/2
