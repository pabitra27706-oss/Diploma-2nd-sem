Unit Overview

What This Unit Covers

This unit explains Simple Lifting Machines — machines used to lift heavy loads by applying comparatively smaller effort. In everyday life, we use screw jacks to lift cars, pulleys in wells and cranes, winches for lifting materials, and worm gears in hoists.

The main idea is simple: a machine helps us multiply force. We apply a small effort over a larger distance, and the machine lifts a big load through a smaller distance.

Why This Unit Matters in Engineering
  • Used in garages, workshops, warehouses, construction sites, and factories
  • Helps understand practical machines like jacks, pulley systems, cranes, hoists, and winches
  • Very important for diploma exams — many direct formula-based numericals come from this unit
  • Builds the concept of machine efficiency, machine friction, and reversibility

Basics of Lifting Machines

2.1 Definition of Lifting Machine

Definition

A lifting machine is a mechanical device used to raise, lower, or move a heavy load by applying a comparatively small force called effort.

Simple Meaning

If a 1000 N load is lifted by applying only 100 N effort, then the machine is helping us. This is the function of a lifting machine — to make difficult work easier.

2.2 Applications of Lifting Machines

Garage

Car lifting by screw jack or hydraulic jack

Construction

Cranes and pulley systems for lifting cement, bricks, steel

Warehouse

Hoists and winches for moving goods

Well

Pulley block for drawing water

Shipping

Crab winch for loading and unloading cargo

Maintenance

Worm gear hoist and chain pulley blocks

2.3 Advantages of Lifting Machines

Advantages
  1. They reduce the effort required to lift heavy loads.
  2. They make work safer and faster.
  3. They can lift loads to great heights.
  4. They improve efficiency in workshops and industries.
  5. They help a single worker perform work that otherwise needs many people.

2.4 Basic Terms — Load and Effort

Load

Load (W) is the resistance or weight lifted by the machine.

Effort

Effort (P) is the force applied to the machine to lift the load.

Exam Tip

In many books, load is denoted by W and effort by P. In numericals, always write clearly:

  • Load = W
  • Effort = P

Mechanical Advantage, Velocity Ratio & Efficiency 🔥

3.1 Mechanical Advantage (M.A.)

Definition

Mechanical Advantage (M.A.) is the ratio of load lifted by the machine to the effort applied.

Formula

M.A. = LoadEffort = WP

If M.A. is large, a small effort can lift a large load. That means the machine is effective in multiplying force.

3.2 Velocity Ratio (V.R.)

Definition

Velocity Ratio (V.R.) is the ratio of the distance moved by the effort to the distance moved by the load in the same time.

Formula

V.R. = Distance moved by effortDistance moved by load

Physical Meaning

In a lifting machine, the effort usually moves through a longer distance than the load. That is why a small effort can lift a large load.

3.3 Efficiency of Machine (η)

Definition

Efficiency of a machine is the ratio of useful work output to work input. It is also the ratio of mechanical advantage to velocity ratio.

Formula

η = M.A.V.R.

or in percentage:

η% = M.A.V.R. × 100

3.4 Relationship Among M.A., V.R., and Efficiency ⭐

Derivation

Input work = Effort × distance moved by effort

Output work = Load × distance moved by load

Efficiency = Output work / Input work

η = W × distance moved by loadP × distance moved by effort

Rearranging,

η = W/Pdistance moved by effort / distance moved by load

η = M.A.V.R.

Therefore: M.A. = η × V.R.

Important Formula Triangle
  • M.A. = W / P
  • V.R. = distance by effort / distance by load
  • η = M.A. / V.R.
  • M.A. = η × V.R.

3.5 Solved Examples

Example 6.3

A machine lifts a load of 1200 N by an effort of 150 N. Find the mechanical advantage.

Solution 6.3

M.A. = W / P = 1200 / 150 = 8

M.A. = 8

Example 6.4

The effort moves 2 m while the load rises by 0.25 m. Find the velocity ratio.

Solution 6.4

V.R. = distance moved by effort / distance moved by load

V.R. = 2 / 0.25 = 8

V.R. = 8

Example 6.5

A machine has M.A. = 6 and V.R. = 10. Find its efficiency.

Solution 6.5

η = M.A. / V.R. = 6 / 10 = 0.6

η% = 0.6 × 100 = 60%

Efficiency = 60%

Example 6.6

A lifting machine raises a load of 3000 N with an effort of 500 N. The effort moves 4 m while the load moves 0.5 m. Find M.A., V.R., and efficiency.

Solution 6.6

Mechanical Advantage:

M.A. = W / P = 3000 / 500 = 6

Velocity Ratio:

V.R. = 4 / 0.5 = 8

Efficiency:

η = M.A. / V.R. = 6 / 8 = 0.75

η% = 0.75 × 100 = 75%

M.A. = 6, V.R. = 8, Efficiency = 75%

Law of Machine 🔥

4.1 Definition

Law of Machine

The law of machine gives the relation between the effort applied and the load lifted by the machine.

Standard Equation

P = mW + C

Where:

  • P = Effort applied
  • W = Load lifted
  • m = Constant of the machine (slope of line)
  • C = Effort required to overcome machine friction at no load
Meaning of the Equation

The effort needed to lift a load is not only for lifting the load, but also for overcoming machine friction. So even if load is zero, some effort is still needed. That no-load effort is C.

4.2 Graph of Law of Machine

If we plot P on Y-axis and W on X-axis, we get a straight line.

  • Slope = m
  • Y-intercept = C

Fig. 6.3: Graph of law of machine showing straight line P = mW + C, slope m and intercept C

4.3 Why is Law of Machine Important?

Importance
  • Helps predict effort for any given load
  • Helps determine frictional resistance in the machine
  • Used to find maximum mechanical advantage and efficiency
  • Very common in WBSCTE numerical questions

4.4 Solved Examples

Example 6.7

The law of a machine is P = 0.04W + 20. Find the effort required to lift a load of 1000 N.

Solution 6.7

Given: P = 0.04W + 20, W = 1000 N

P = 0.04 × 1000 + 20 = 40 + 20 = 60 N

Effort required = 60 N

Example 6.8

A machine follows the law P = 0.02W + 15. Find the effort needed to raise a load of 2500 N.

Solution 6.8

P = 0.02 × 2500 + 15 = 50 + 15 = 65 N

Effort = 65 N

Example 6.9

For a machine, the effort needed to lift 1000 N and 3000 N loads are 60 N and 140 N respectively. Find the law of the machine.

Solution 6.9

Let law be: P = mW + C

For W = 1000 N, P = 60 N:

60 = 1000m + C ... (1)

For W = 3000 N, P = 140 N:

140 = 3000m + C ... (2)

Subtract (1) from (2):

80 = 2000m

m = 80/2000 = 0.04

Substitute in (1):

60 = 1000 × 0.04 + C = 40 + C

C = 20

Law of machine: P = 0.04W + 20

Example 6.10

The law of machine is P = 0.05W + 25. If the machine lifts 2000 N, find its mechanical advantage.

Solution 6.10

P = 0.05 × 2000 + 25 = 100 + 25 = 125 N

M.A. = W / P = 2000 / 125 = 16

M.A. = 16

Ideal Machine, Friction, Maximum M.A., Reversible & Non-Reversible Machines

5.1 Ideal Machine

Ideal Machine

An ideal machine is a machine in which there is no loss of energy due to friction. Therefore, input work equals output work.

Condition for Ideal Machine

Efficiency η = 100%

M.A. = V.R.

5.2 Friction in Machine

Friction in Machine

In actual machines, some effort is wasted in overcoming friction between moving parts. So actual efficiency is always less than 100%.

Practical Fact

No real machine is ideal. Every practical machine has friction, wear, and some energy loss as heat and sound.

5.3 Maximum Mechanical Advantage

Formula

From law of machine:

P = mW + C

Mechanical Advantage:

M.A. = WP = WmW + C

When W becomes very large, C becomes negligible.

Maximum M.A. = 1 / m

5.4 Maximum Efficiency

Formula

Efficiency = M.A. / V.R.

Using maximum M.A. = 1/m,

Maximum Efficiency = 1 / (m × V.R.)

In percentage:

Maximum η% = [1 / (m × V.R.)] × 100

5.5 Reversible Machine

Reversible Machine

A reversible machine is one in which, when effort is removed, the load can move downward and the machine can run backward.

5.6 Non-Reversible or Self-Locking Machine

Non-Reversible Machine

A non-reversible machine is one in which, when effort is removed, the load does not move downward by itself. Such a machine is also called a self-locking machine.

5.7 Condition for Reversibility ⭐

Important Condition
  • If Efficiency > 50% → Machine is generally reversible
  • If Efficiency < 50% → Machine is generally non-reversible / self-locking

5.8 Solved Examples

Example 6.11

The law of a machine is P = 0.02W + 10. Find the maximum mechanical advantage.

Solution 6.11

Here, m = 0.02

Maximum M.A. = 1 / m = 1 / 0.02 = 50

Maximum M.A. = 50

Example 6.12

A machine has law P = 0.04W + 20 and velocity ratio 25. Find maximum efficiency.

Solution 6.12

Given: m = 0.04, V.R. = 25

Maximum efficiency = 1 / (m × V.R.)

= 1 / (0.04 × 25) = 1 / 1 = 1

η% = 1 × 100 = 100%

Maximum efficiency = 100%

Example 6.13

A machine has efficiency of 40%. State whether it is reversible or non-reversible.

Solution 6.13

Efficiency = 40% < 50%

Therefore, the machine is non-reversible (self-locking).

Machine is non-reversible

Simple Axle and Wheel

6.1 Construction and Working Principle

Simple Axle and Wheel

A simple axle and wheel consists of a large wheel rigidly fixed to a smaller axle, both rotating together about a common axis. The effort is applied at the rim of the wheel and the load is lifted by a rope wound around the axle.

Working

When effort is applied at the larger wheel, the wheel and axle rotate together. Since the wheel radius is larger than axle radius, a small effort can lift a larger load.

Fig. 6.5: Simple axle and wheel showing wheel radius R, axle radius r, effort P and load W

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

6.2 Velocity Ratio of Simple Axle and Wheel

Derivation

In one revolution:

  • Distance moved by effort = circumference of wheel = 2πR
  • Distance moved by load = circumference of axle = 2πr

Therefore,

V.R. = 2πR2πr

V.R. = R / r = D / d

Where:

  • R = radius of wheel
  • r = radius of axle
  • D = diameter of wheel
  • d = diameter of axle

6.3 Solved Examples

Example 6.14

In a simple axle and wheel, the diameter of the wheel is 600 mm and the diameter of the axle is 100 mm. Find the velocity ratio.

Solution 6.14

V.R. = D / d = 600 / 100 = 6

Velocity Ratio = 6

Example 6.15

A simple axle and wheel lifts a load of 1500 N by an effort of 300 N. If the diameter ratio is 8, find M.A. and efficiency.

Solution 6.15

M.A. = W / P = 1500 / 300 = 5

V.R. = 8

η = M.A. / V.R. = 5 / 8 = 0.625

η% = 62.5%

M.A. = 5, Efficiency = 62.5%

Differential Axle and Wheel

7.1 Construction and Working

Differential Axle and Wheel

A differential axle and wheel has one large wheel and two axles of different diameters fixed on the same shaft. The load is suspended by a rope wound in opposite directions on the two axles.

Why "Differential"?

Because the two axles have different diameters, the rope wound and unwound in one revolution is different. The effective lift depends on the difference of the two circumferences.

Fig. 6.6: Differential axle and wheel showing wheel diameter D and two axle diameters d1 and d2

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

7.2 Velocity Ratio

Derivation

In one revolution:

  • Distance moved by effort = circumference of wheel = πD
  • Rope wound on larger axle = πd1
  • Rope unwound from smaller axle = πd2

Net shortening of rope = π(d1 − d2)

Since load is supported by two segments of rope, load rises by half of this distance:

Distance moved by load = π(d1 − d2)2

Therefore,

V.R. = πDπ(d1 − d2) / 2

V.R. = 2D / (d1 − d2)

7.3 Solved Examples

Example 6.16

A differential axle and wheel has wheel diameter 500 mm, and axle diameters 120 mm and 80 mm. Find the velocity ratio.

Solution 6.16

V.R. = 2D / (d₁ − d₂)

= 2 × 500 / (120 − 80)

= 1000 / 40 = 25

Velocity Ratio = 25

Example 6.17

A differential axle and wheel has V.R. = 20. It lifts a load of 4000 N with an effort of 250 N. Find M.A. and efficiency.

Solution 6.17

M.A. = W / P = 4000 / 250 = 16

η = M.A. / V.R. = 16 / 20 = 0.8

η% = 80%

M.A. = 16, Efficiency = 80%

Worm and Worm Wheel

8.1 Construction and Working

Worm and Worm Wheel

A worm and worm wheel machine consists of a worm (screw-like gear) mounted on a shaft and a worm wheel (gear wheel) meshing with it. When the effort wheel is rotated, the worm turns and rotates the worm wheel, thereby lifting the load.

Working Principle

One complete revolution of the worm generally moves the worm wheel by one tooth (for single-thread worm). Because of this slow motion, a very high velocity ratio is obtained.

Fig. 6.7: Worm and worm wheel lifting machine showing effort wheel, worm, worm wheel, load drum, and load

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

8.2 Velocity Ratio

Formula

Let:

  • D = diameter of effort wheel
  • r = radius of load drum
  • T = number of teeth on worm wheel

In one revolution of effort wheel:

  • Distance moved by effort = πD
  • Worm rotates once and worm wheel advances by one tooth
  • So worm wheel turns by 1/T revolution
  • Distance moved by load = circumference of drum × 1/T = 2πr / T

Therefore,

V.R. = πD2πr / T

V.R. = DT / 2r

8.3 Solved Examples

Example 6.18

In a worm and worm wheel machine, the effort wheel diameter is 400 mm, the load drum radius is 50 mm, and the worm wheel has 40 teeth. Find the velocity ratio.

Solution 6.18

V.R. = DT / 2r

= 400 × 40 / (2 × 50)

= 16000 / 100 = 160

Velocity Ratio = 160

Example 6.19

A worm and worm wheel machine of V.R. 120 lifts 6000 N load by an effort of 100 N. Find M.A. and efficiency.

Solution 6.19

M.A. = W / P = 6000 / 100 = 60

η = M.A. / V.R. = 60 / 120 = 0.5

η% = 50%

M.A. = 60, Efficiency = 50%

Single Purchase and Double Purchase Crab Winch 🔥

9.1 Single Purchase Crab Winch

Single Purchase Crab Winch

In a single purchase crab winch, the effort applied at the handle rotates a pinion, which meshes with a larger gear. The gear is attached to the load drum, so the drum rotates and lifts the load.

Fig. 6.8: Single purchase crab winch showing handle length L, pinion teeth T1, gear teeth T2, drum radius r, effort and load

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

9.2 Velocity Ratio of Single Purchase Crab Winch

Formula

Let:

  • L = length of handle
  • r = radius of load drum
  • T₁ = number of teeth on pinion
  • T₂ = number of teeth on gear

In one revolution of handle:

  • Distance moved by effort = 2πL
  • Pinion makes 1 revolution, gear makes T₁ / T₂ revolution
  • Drum also makes T₁ / T₂ revolution
  • Distance moved by load = 2πr × (T₁ / T₂)

Therefore,

V.R. = 2πL2πr × (T₁/T₂)

V.R. = LT₂ / rT₁

9.3 Double Purchase Crab Winch

Double Purchase Crab Winch

In a double purchase crab winch, there are two gear pairs instead of one. This gives a much larger velocity ratio and allows very heavy loads to be lifted with small effort.

Fig. 6.9: Double purchase crab winch showing two gear-pinion pairs, handle, load drum and load

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

9.4 Velocity Ratio of Double Purchase Crab Winch

Formula

Let:

  • L = handle length
  • r = radius of load drum
  • T₁, T₂ = teeth on first pinion and first gear
  • T₃, T₄ = teeth on second pinion and second gear

The overall speed reduction is product of both gear ratios.

V.R. = LT₂T₄ / rT₁T₃

9.5 Solved Examples

Example 6.20

A single purchase crab has handle length 300 mm, drum radius 50 mm, pinion teeth 12, and gear teeth 48. Find the velocity ratio.

Solution 6.20

V.R. = LT₂ / rT₁

= 300 × 48 / (50 × 12)

= 14400 / 600 = 24

Velocity Ratio = 24

Example 6.21

A double purchase crab winch has handle length 400 mm, drum radius 80 mm, teeth: T₁ = 10, T₂ = 50, T₃ = 12, T₄ = 60. Find the velocity ratio.

Solution 6.21

V.R. = LT₂T₄ / rT₁T₃

= 400 × 50 × 60 / (80 × 10 × 12)

= 1200000 / 9600 = 125

Velocity Ratio = 125

Example 6.22

A single purchase crab winch lifts 2400 N load with 150 N effort. If V.R. = 20, find M.A. and efficiency.

Solution 6.22

M.A. = W / P = 2400 / 150 = 16

η = 16 / 20 = 0.8 = 80%

M.A. = 16, Efficiency = 80%

Example 6.23

A double purchase crab has V.R. = 100. If its efficiency is 70%, find the mechanical advantage.

Solution 6.23

η = M.A. / V.R.

M.A. = η × V.R. = 0.70 × 100 = 70

M.A. = 70

Simple Screw Jack

10.1 Construction and Working

Simple Screw Jack

A simple screw jack consists of a square-threaded screw passing through a threaded nut. A handle is used to rotate the screw and lift the load placed on the top of the screw.

Common Use

The screw jack is widely used in garages for lifting cars. It gives very high mechanical advantage with simple construction.

Fig. 6.10: Simple screw jack showing handle length L, pitch p, screw, and load W

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

10.2 Velocity Ratio of Screw Jack

Formula

In one revolution of handle:

  • Distance moved by effort = circumference of handle circle = 2πL
  • Distance moved by load = pitch of screw = p

Therefore,

V.R. = 2πL / p

Where:

  • L = length of handle
  • p = pitch of screw

10.3 Solved Examples

Example 6.24

A screw jack has handle length 300 mm and screw pitch 6 mm. Find its velocity ratio.

Solution 6.24

V.R. = 2πL / p

= 2 × 3.1416 × 300 / 6

= 1884.96 / 6 = 314.16

Velocity Ratio = 314.16

Example 6.25

A screw jack lifts a load of 5000 N by an effort of 100 N. If its velocity ratio is 250, find M.A. and efficiency.

Solution 6.25

M.A. = W / P = 5000 / 100 = 50

η = 50 / 250 = 0.2

η% = 20%

M.A. = 50, Efficiency = 20%

Simple Pulley Block

11.1 Construction and Working

Simple Pulley Block

A simple pulley block consists of two sets of pulleys — one fixed block and one movable block — connected by a rope. The load is attached to the movable block and effort is applied at the free end of the rope.

Working Principle

The load is supported by several rope segments. Therefore, the effort required becomes smaller. The velocity ratio depends mainly on the number of rope segments supporting the movable block.

Fig. 6.11: Simple pulley block showing fixed pulleys, movable pulleys, rope path, effort and load

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

11.2 Velocity Ratio

Formula

For a simple pulley block,

V.R. = n

Where n = number of rope segments supporting the load

In many exam problems, if there are n pulleys, the commonly used relation is:

V.R. = number of supporting rope segments

Important Clarification

For diploma exams, the velocity ratio of a pulley block is usually taken as the number of rope segments supporting the movable block. If the diagram is given, count carefully.

11.3 Solved Examples

Example 6.26

A simple pulley block has 4 supporting rope segments. Find its velocity ratio.

Solution 6.26

V.R. = number of supporting rope segments = 4

Velocity Ratio = 4

Example 6.27

A pulley block of velocity ratio 6 lifts a load of 1800 N with an effort of 400 N. Find its M.A. and efficiency.

Solution 6.27

M.A. = W / P = 1800 / 400 = 4.5

η = 4.5 / 6 = 0.75

η% = 75%

M.A. = 4.5, Efficiency = 75%

Numerical 1

A machine lifts a load of 2500 N with an effort of 125 N. The effort moves 3 m while the load moves 0.2 m. Find M.A., V.R., and efficiency.

Solution

M.A. = W / P = 2500 / 125 = 20

V.R. = 3 / 0.2 = 15

η = 20 / 15 = 1.333

η% = 133.3%

This is not physically possible for an actual machine. Therefore, the given data is unrealistic or may represent a theoretical error.

M.A. = 20, V.R. = 15, Efficiency = 133.3% (not possible practically)

If efficiency comes out more than 100%, the data is not physically possible for a real machine. In exams, if this happens, mention it clearly.

Important Definitions

Lifting Machine

A lifting machine is a device used to lift heavy loads by applying comparatively smaller effort.

Load

The resistance or weight lifted by the machine is called load.

Effort

The force applied to operate the machine is called effort.

Mechanical Advantage

Mechanical advantage is the ratio of load lifted to effort applied.

Velocity Ratio

Velocity ratio is the ratio of distance moved by effort to distance moved by load in the same time.

Efficiency

Efficiency is the ratio of useful work output to work input. It is also equal to M.A./V.R.

Law of Machine

The law of machine gives the relation between effort and load, usually expressed as P = mW + C.

Ideal Machine

An ideal machine is one in which input work equals output work and efficiency is 100%.

Reversible Machine

A reversible machine is one in which the load can descend by itself when the effort is removed.

Self-locking Machine

A self-locking or non-reversible machine is one in which the load does not descend by itself when effort is removed.

Long Answer Questions

Q1. Define lifting machine and explain load, effort, mechanical advantage, velocity ratio, and efficiency. [5–7 Marks]

A lifting machine is a device used to lift heavy loads by applying comparatively smaller effort.

  • Load: The weight lifted by the machine.
  • Effort: The force applied to operate the machine.
  • Mechanical Advantage: Ratio of load to effort, M.A. = W/P.
  • Velocity Ratio: Ratio of distance moved by effort to distance moved by load.
  • Efficiency: Ratio of M.A. to V.R., η = M.A./V.R.

These quantities help us judge the performance of the machine.

Q2. State and explain the law of machine. [5 Marks] 🔥

The law of machine gives the relation between effort and load and is expressed as:

P = mW + C

Where:

  • P = effort
  • W = load
  • m = constant of machine
  • C = frictional effort at no load

This equation shows that effort increases linearly with load. When load is zero, effort is still C due to friction. The graph of P vs W is a straight line.

Q3. Explain ideal machine, reversible machine, and self-locking machine. [5 Marks]

Ideal Machine: A machine with no friction and 100% efficiency. In it, M.A. = V.R.

Reversible Machine: A machine in which load descends when effort is removed. Its efficiency is usually more than 50%.

Self-Locking Machine: A machine in which load does not descend when effort is removed. It is also called non-reversible. Its efficiency is usually less than 50%.

Q4. Derive the velocity ratio of a simple axle and wheel. [5 Marks]

Let R be radius of wheel and r be radius of axle.

In one revolution:

  • Distance moved by effort = circumference of wheel = 2πR
  • Distance moved by load = circumference of axle = 2πr

Therefore,

V.R. = 2πR / 2πr = R/r

If diameters are used, then V.R. = D/d

Hence proved.

Q5. Derive the velocity ratio of a simple screw jack. [5 Marks]

Let L be the handle length and p be the pitch of screw.

In one revolution of handle:

  • Distance moved by effort = circumference of handle circle = 2πL
  • Distance moved by load = pitch of screw = p

Therefore,

V.R. = 2πL / p

This is the velocity ratio of a simple screw jack.

Practice Numericals

P1. A machine lifts 1800 N load by 300 N effort. Find M.A.

M.A. = W/P = 1800/300 = 6

P2. The effort moves 5 m while load moves 0.25 m. Find V.R.

V.R. = 5 / 0.25 = 20

P3. M.A. = 8 and V.R. = 10. Find efficiency.

η = 8/10 = 0.8

η% = 80%

P4. Law of machine is P = 0.05W + 10. Find effort for W = 1000 N.

P = 0.05 × 1000 + 10 = 50 + 10 = 60 N

P5. In a simple axle and wheel, D = 500 mm and d = 100 mm. Find V.R.

V.R. = D/d = 500/100 = 5

P6. For a screw jack, L = 250 mm and p = 4 mm. Find V.R.

V.R. = 2πL/p = 2 × 3.1416 × 250 / 4

= 1570.8 / 4 = 392.7

P7. A worm and worm wheel has D = 300 mm, T = 50, r = 30 mm. Find V.R.

V.R. = DT/2r = 300 × 50 / (2 × 30)

= 15000 / 60 = 250

P8. A machine has efficiency 45%. Is it reversible?

Since efficiency = 45% < 50%, machine is non-reversible / self-locking.
