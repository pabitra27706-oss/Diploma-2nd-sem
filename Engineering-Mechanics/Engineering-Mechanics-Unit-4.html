Friction — Definition, Relevance & Types

2.1 What is Friction? 🔥

Definition: Friction

Friction is the resisting force that acts between two surfaces in contact when one body moves or tends to move over the other. It always acts tangentially (along the surface) at the point of contact and in a direction opposite to the direction of motion (or tendency of motion).

Understanding Friction — Real-World Analogy

Imagine pushing a heavy almirah (cupboard) on a rough floor:

  • When you push gently, the almirah doesn't move → friction balances your push exactly
  • As you push harder, the almirah still doesn't move → friction increases to match your push
  • At some point, you push hard enough and the almirah just starts to slide → friction has reached its maximum value (this is limiting friction)
  • Once moving, you notice it's slightly easier to keep pushing → kinetic friction is less than limiting friction

2.2 Why Does Friction Occur?

Cause of Friction

No surface is perfectly smooth — even surfaces that look smooth have tiny microscopic irregularities (bumps and valleys) called asperities. When two surfaces are in contact, these irregularities interlock with each other. When we try to slide one surface over the other, these interlocking asperities resist the motion.

Additionally, at the points of contact, there are molecular adhesion forces (attraction between molecules of the two surfaces) which also contribute to friction.

2.3 Relevance of Friction in Engineering ⭐

Engineering Applications of Friction

Friction is both useful and harmful in engineering:

✅ Advantages (Where Friction is Useful):

  1. Walking: We can walk only because of friction between shoes and ground
  2. Braking: Vehicle brakes work by friction between brake pads and disc/drum
  3. Belt drives: Power transmission through belts relies on friction
  4. Clutch: Automobile clutch uses friction to transmit engine power to wheels
  5. Nuts and bolts: Stay tight due to friction in threads
  6. Writing: Pen/pencil writes on paper due to friction
  7. Ladder: A ladder rests against a wall due to friction
  8. Grinding/Polishing: Material removal happens due to friction

❌ Disadvantages (Where Friction is Harmful):

  1. Wear and tear: Machine parts wear out due to friction
  2. Energy loss: Friction converts useful energy into heat (waste)
  3. Overheating: Bearings and moving parts heat up
  4. Reduced efficiency: Machines become less efficient due to friction losses
  5. Noise: Friction can cause unwanted noise and vibration

🛠️ Methods to Reduce Friction:

  • Lubrication (oil, grease)
  • Use of ball bearings and roller bearings
  • Polishing surfaces
  • Using wheels instead of sliding
  • Streamlining (for air friction)

2.4 Types of Friction 🔥

Classification of Friction

Friction is classified into the following types:

1. Static Friction

The friction that acts on a body at rest when an external force tends to move it but hasn't succeeded yet. The body remains stationary.

  • Acts when body tends to move but hasn't moved
  • Its value varies from zero to a maximum value
  • Self-adjusting — equals the applied force up to the limit
  • Direction: Opposite to the tendency of motion

2. Dynamic (Kinetic) Friction

The friction that acts on a body when it is actually moving (sliding) over a surface.

  • Acts when body is in motion
  • Has a constant value (doesn't change with speed much)
  • Always less than limiting static friction
  • Two sub-types: Sliding friction and Rolling friction

3. Rolling Friction

The friction that acts when a body rolls over a surface (like a wheel or ball bearing).

  • Acts during rolling motion
  • Much less than sliding friction
  • That's why wheels are used instead of dragging
  • Example: Ball bearings in machines

2.5 Limiting Friction ⭐

Definition: Limiting Friction

Limiting friction is the maximum value of static friction that can act between two surfaces. When the applied force equals the limiting friction, the body is on the verge of motion (about to move but hasn't started moving yet). Any additional force beyond this will cause the body to start sliding.

Fig. 4.3: Graph showing friction force vs applied force — friction increases linearly up to limiting value (FL), then drops to kinetic friction (Fk) when motion starts

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Key Understanding
  • When applied force P = 0 → Friction F = 0
  • As P increases → F increases equally (F = P) → body doesn't move
  • When P reaches limiting value FL → F = FL → body is about to move
  • When P > FL → body starts moving → friction drops to kinetic value Fk
  • Fk < FL always (kinetic friction < limiting friction)
Exam Note

In most diploma-level problems, unless specifically stated otherwise, we work with limiting friction. The phrase "the body is about to move" or "the body is on the verge of sliding" means we should use limiting friction. The formula F = μN gives the limiting friction.

Laws of Friction 🔥

3.1 Laws of Static Friction ⭐

Laws of Static (Dry) Friction
  1. Law 1: The force of friction always acts in a direction opposite to the direction in which the body tends to move.
  2. Law 2: The magnitude of friction is exactly equal to the applied force as long as the body is at rest (self-adjusting friction). At limiting condition, F = μsN.
  3. Law 3: The limiting friction is directly proportional to the normal reaction between the surfaces.

    FL ∝ N or FL = μs × N

  4. Law 4: The limiting friction is independent of the area of contact between the surfaces (for given normal reaction).
  5. Law 5: The friction depends on the nature (material and roughness) of the surfaces in contact.

3.2 Laws of Kinetic (Dynamic) Friction

Laws of Kinetic Friction
  1. Law 1: The kinetic friction is directly proportional to the normal reaction.

    Fk = μk × N

  2. Law 2: Kinetic friction is independent of the velocity of sliding (for moderate speeds).
  3. Law 3: Kinetic friction is independent of the area of contact.
  4. Law 4: Kinetic friction is always less than limiting static friction for the same surfaces.

    Fk < FL or μk < μs

Common Exam Mistake

Students often confuse: "Friction is independent of area of contact" — this seems counter-intuitive! But it's true for dry friction. A wider block and a narrower block of the same weight on the same surface experience the same friction force. The wider block has more contact area but less pressure per unit area, and these effects cancel out.

Summary of Laws — Exam Quick Points
  • ✅ Friction ∝ Normal Reaction (F = μN)
  • ✅ Friction is independent of contact area
  • ✅ Friction depends on nature of surfaces
  • ✅ Friction opposes motion (or tendency of motion)
  • ✅ Static friction is self-adjusting (0 to FL)
  • ✅ Kinetic friction < Limiting static friction
  • ✅ Kinetic friction is approximately constant at all speeds

Key Concepts — μ, φ, Cone, Repose & Relations 🔥

4.1 Coefficient of Friction (μ) ⭐

Definition: Coefficient of Friction

The coefficient of friction (μ) is the ratio of the limiting friction force to the normal reaction between two surfaces. It is a dimensionless number (no unit) that depends on the nature of the two surfaces in contact.

Formula: Coefficient of Friction

μ = FN

Or: F = μ × N

Where:

  • μ = Coefficient of friction (no unit, dimensionless)
  • F = Limiting friction force (N)
  • N = Normal reaction (N)
Typical Values of μ
Surface Pairμs (Static)μk (Kinetic)
Steel on Steel0.740.57
Wood on Wood0.25–0.500.20–0.40
Rubber on Concrete1.00.80
Leather on Metal0.600.50
Teflon on Steel0.040.04
Ice on Ice0.100.03

Note: μk is always less than μs for the same surface pair.

4.2 Angle of Friction (φ) 🔥

Definition: Angle of Friction

The angle of friction (φ) is the angle which the resultant of the limiting friction force (F) and the normal reaction (N) makes with the normal reaction. It is denoted by φ (phi).

Fig. 4.4: Angle of friction φ — showing the resultant R of friction force F and normal reaction N, with angle φ between R and N

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Formula: Angle of Friction

From the right triangle formed by F, N, and their resultant R:

tan φ = FN = μ

Therefore:

φ = tan⁻¹(μ)

Or equivalently: μ = tan φ

4.3 Relation Between Coefficient of Friction and Angle of Friction ⭐

Derivation of the Relation

Consider a body resting on a rough horizontal surface. Let:

  • N = Normal reaction (perpendicular to surface)
  • F = Limiting friction force (along the surface)
  • R = Resultant of F and N
  • φ = Angle between R and N

From the right triangle (F, N, R):

tan φ = OppositeAdjacent = FN

But we know that F = μN, so:

tan φ = μNN = μ

∴ μ = tan φ

This is the fundamental relation between coefficient of friction (μ) and angle of friction (φ).

4.4 Cone of Friction

Definition: Cone of Friction

If the direction of the applied force (and hence the direction of friction) is gradually changed through 360° while keeping the body on the verge of motion, the resultant reaction R traces out a cone. This cone is called the Cone of Friction.

The half-angle of this cone equals the angle of friction φ. The axis of the cone is the normal reaction N.

Fig. 4.5: Cone of Friction — Normal N as axis, half-angle = φ (angle of friction), resultant R traces the surface of the cone

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Significance of Cone of Friction
  • If the resultant of all forces applied on the body falls inside the cone, the body will NOT move (friction is sufficient to prevent motion)
  • If the resultant falls on the surface of the cone, the body is on the verge of motion
  • If the resultant falls outside the cone, the body will move (friction cannot prevent motion)

4.5 Angle of Repose (α) 🔥

Definition: Angle of Repose

The angle of repose is the maximum angle of an inclined plane with the horizontal at which a body placed on it just begins to slide down on its own (without any external force). It is denoted by α (alpha).

In other words, if you gradually tilt a surface from horizontal, the angle at which a block placed on it starts sliding is the angle of repose.

4.6 Proof: Angle of Repose = Angle of Friction (α = φ) 🔥⭐

Proof that α = φ

Consider a body of weight W placed on an inclined plane making angle α with the horizontal. The body is on the verge of sliding down (limiting equilibrium).

Forces acting on the body:

  1. Weight W acting vertically downward
  2. Normal reaction N perpendicular to the inclined surface
  3. Limiting friction F acting up the incline (opposing tendency to slide down)

Resolving W along and perpendicular to the incline:

Component along the incline (causing sliding) = W sin α

Component perpendicular to incline = W cos α

Equilibrium conditions:

Along the incline: F = W sin α ... (i)

Perpendicular to incline: N = W cos α ... (ii)

Dividing (i) by (ii):

FN = W sin αW cos α = tan α

But at limiting condition, FN = μ = tan φ

Therefore: tan α = tan φ

∴ α = φ (Angle of Repose = Angle of Friction)

Also: μ = tan α = tan φ

Super Important for Exam!

The three quantities are related as:

μ = tan φ = tan α

Where μ = coefficient of friction, φ = angle of friction, α = angle of repose.

This relation is asked in almost every WBSCTE paper — either as a proof (5 marks) or as a statement (2 marks).

4.7 Solved Examples on Key Concepts

Example 4.1

A body weighing 500 N is placed on a rough horizontal surface. If the coefficient of friction is 0.3, find: (a) Limiting friction, (b) Angle of friction, (c) Angle of repose.

Solution 4.1
Given: W = 500 N, μ = 0.3
On horizontal surface: N = W = 500 N
(a) Limiting Friction:
F = μN = 0.3 × 500

F = 150 N

(b) Angle of Friction:
tan φ = μ = 0.3
φ = tan⁻¹(0.3)

φ = 16.7°

(c) Angle of Repose:
α = φ = 16.7°

α = 16.7°

Example 4.2

A body starts sliding down an inclined plane when the angle of inclination reaches 25°. Find the coefficient of friction and the angle of friction.

Solution 4.2
Given: Body starts sliding at angle = 25° → This is the angle of repose → α = 25°
Coefficient of friction:
μ = tan α = tan 25° = 0.4663

μ = 0.466

Angle of friction:
φ = α = 25°

φ = 25°

Body on Horizontal Surface — Force Parallel to Plane

5.1 Setup and Free Body Diagram

Scenario

A block of weight W rests on a rough horizontal surface. A horizontal force P is applied to push (or pull) the block along the surface. The body is on the verge of motion (limiting equilibrium).

Fig. 4.7: FBD of block on horizontal surface with horizontal applied force P — showing Weight W (↓), Normal Reaction N (↑), Applied force P (→), and Friction F (←)

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

5.2 Forces Acting

  1. W = Weight of the body (acting vertically downward) — known
  2. N = Normal reaction (acting vertically upward, perpendicular to surface)
  3. P = Applied horizontal force (in the direction of desired motion)
  4. F = Friction force (opposing motion, opposite to P)

5.3 Equilibrium Equations

Deriving the Equations

Resolving vertically (↑ = +ve):

ΣFy = 0: N − W = 0

N = W ... (1)

Resolving horizontally (→ = +ve):

ΣFx = 0: P − F = 0

P = F = μN = μW ... (2)

Key Results:

  • Normal reaction equals the weight of the body
  • Applied force equals the friction force
  • Minimum force to just move the body: P = μW
This is the Simplest Case!

When force is horizontal on a horizontal surface:

  • N = W (normal reaction equals weight)
  • P = μW (applied force equals μ times weight)

This is the base case. All other cases (inclined force, inclined plane) are variations of this.

5.4 Solved Examples

Example 4.3

A block weighing 800 N is placed on a rough horizontal surface. The coefficient of friction is 0.25. Find the horizontal force required to just move the block.

Solution 4.3
Given: W = 800 N, μ = 0.25
Normal reaction: N = W = 800 N (horizontal surface, horizontal force)
Force to just move:
P = μN = 0.25 × 800

P = 200 N

Example 4.4

A body of mass 50 kg is resting on a horizontal surface. A horizontal force of 120 N is needed to just start the body moving. Find: (a) coefficient of friction, (b) angle of friction. Take g = 9.81 m/s².

Solution 4.4
Given: m = 50 kg, P = 120 N, g = 9.81 m/s²
W = mg = 50 × 9.81 = 490.5 N
N = W = 490.5 N (horizontal surface, horizontal force)
(a) Coefficient of friction:
At limiting condition: P = μN
120 = μ × 490.5
μ = 120 / 490.5

μ = 0.245

(b) Angle of friction:
φ = tan⁻¹(μ) = tan⁻¹(0.245)

φ = 13.74°

Example 4.5

A block weighing 1000 N rests on a rough horizontal floor (μ = 0.4). A horizontal force of 350 N is applied. Will the block move? If yes, find the net force causing motion.

Solution 4.5
Given: W = 1000 N, μ = 0.4, P = 350 N
Limiting friction:
FL = μN = μW = 0.4 × 1000 = 400 N
Comparison:
Applied force P = 350 N
Limiting friction FL = 400 N
Since P < FL, the block will NOT move.

The block will NOT move. The friction force is 350 N (= applied force, self-adjusting).

Note: Friction in this case is only 350 N (not 400 N), because the block is at rest and friction is self-adjusting.

Body on Horizontal Surface — Force Inclined to Plane

6.1 Introduction

Now the applied force is not horizontal. It acts at an angle θ to the horizontal. This changes the normal reaction, and therefore the friction also changes. This is a very important exam case.

Main Idea

If the force is pulling upward, then it reduces the normal reaction, so friction becomes less and less force is needed to move the body.

If the force is pushing downward, then it increases the normal reaction, so friction becomes more and more force is needed to move the body.

6.2 Case 1: Pulling Force Inclined Upward

Fig. 4.8: Block on rough horizontal surface pulled by force P at angle θ above horizontal. Shows W, N, friction F, and components P cosθ and P sinθ.

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Derivation for Pulling at Angle θ

Let:

  • W = weight of block
  • P = applied force at angle θ above horizontal
  • N = normal reaction
  • F = limiting friction = μN

Resolve P into components:

  • Horizontal component = P cos θ
  • Vertical upward component = P sin θ

Vertical equilibrium:

N + P sin θ - W = 0

N = W - P sin θ ... (1)

Horizontal equilibrium:

P cos θ - F = 0

But F = μN

P cos θ = μ(W - P sin θ)

P cos θ = μW - μP sin θ

P(cos θ + μ sin θ) = μW

P = μWcos θ + μ sin θ

This is the formula for the force required to pull the body on a rough horizontal surface.

6.3 Case 2: Pushing Force Inclined Downward

Fig. 4.9: Block on rough horizontal surface pushed by force P at angle θ below horizontal. Shows W, N, friction F, and components P cosθ and P sinθ.

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Derivation for Pushing at Angle θ

Resolve P into components:

  • Horizontal component = P cos θ
  • Vertical downward component = P sin θ

Vertical equilibrium:

N - W - P sin θ = 0

N = W + P sin θ ... (2)

Horizontal equilibrium:

P cos θ = F = μN = μ(W + P sin θ)

P cos θ = μW + μP sin θ

P(cos θ - μ sin θ) = μW

P = μWcos θ - μ sin θ

This is the formula for the force required to push the body on a rough horizontal surface.

Very Important Difference
  • Pulling upward: N = W − P sin θ
  • Pushing downward: N = W + P sin θ

Students often interchange these. Always check whether the vertical component of P acts upward or downward.

6.4 Which is Better — Pulling or Pushing?

Comparison

For the same block and same angle θ:

  • Pulling reduces normal reaction → friction becomes less → less force required
  • Pushing increases normal reaction → friction becomes more → more force required

Therefore, pulling is always easier than pushing on a rough horizontal surface.

6.5 Solved Examples

Example 4.6

A block weighing 600 N is pulled on a rough horizontal surface by a force inclined at 30° to the horizontal. If the coefficient of friction is 0.25, find the force required to just move the block.

Solution 4.6
Given: W = 600 N, μ = 0.25, θ = 30°
Formula for pulling:
P = μW / (cos θ + μ sin θ)
Substitute values:
P = (0.25 × 600) / (cos 30° + 0.25 × sin 30°)
P = 150 / (0.866 + 0.25 × 0.5)
P = 150 / (0.866 + 0.125)
P = 150 / 0.991

P = 151.36 N

Example 4.7

The coefficient of friction between a block and horizontal floor is 0.3. The weight of the block is 1000 N. Find the least force required to push the block when the push is inclined at 20° below the horizontal.

Solution 4.7
Given: μ = 0.3, W = 1000 N, θ = 20°
Formula for pushing:
P = μW / (cos θ − μ sin θ)
Substitute values:
P = (0.3 × 1000) / (cos 20° − 0.3 × sin 20°)
P = 300 / (0.9397 − 0.3 × 0.342)
P = 300 / (0.9397 − 0.1026)
P = 300 / 0.8371

P = 358.38 N

Example 4.8

A block of weight 500 N is pulled on a rough horizontal plane by a force of 200 N acting at 25° above the horizontal. If the coefficient of friction is 0.35, determine whether the block will move.

Solution 4.8
Given: W = 500 N, P = 200 N, θ = 25°, μ = 0.35
Normal reaction:
N = W − P sin θ
N = 500 − 200 sin 25°
N = 500 − 200 × 0.4226
N = 500 − 84.52
N = 415.48 N
Limiting friction:
FL = μN = 0.35 × 415.48 = 145.42 N
Horizontal component of pull:
P cos θ = 200 cos 25° = 200 × 0.9063 = 181.26 N
Since horizontal pull 181.26 N > limiting friction 145.42 N,

The block WILL MOVE.

Body on Inclined Plane — Force Parallel to Plane

7.1 Introduction

This is the most important friction problem in diploma exams. A body rests on a rough plane inclined at angle α to the horizontal. An external force P acts parallel to the plane.

Here, the weight W must be resolved into:

  • W sin α → along the plane
  • W cos α → perpendicular to the plane

Fig. 4.10: Block on rough inclined plane with force P acting parallel to plane. Shows W, components W sinα and W cosα, N, and friction F.

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

7.2 Case 1: Force P Acts Up the Plane and Body is About to Move Up

Direction of Friction

If the body tends to move up the plane, friction will act down the plane because friction always opposes the tendency of motion.

Derivation — Body About to Move Up the Plane

Perpendicular to plane:

N = W cos α

Friction force:

F = μN = μW cos α

Along the plane (upward positive):

The applied force P acts up the plane.

Opposing forces down the plane are:

  • Component of weight = W sin α
  • Friction = μW cos α

So, equilibrium gives:

P = W sin α + μW cos α

P = W(sin α + μ cos α)

7.3 Case 2: Force P Acts Up the Plane and Body is About to Move Down

Direction of Friction

If the body tends to move down the plane, friction will act up the plane.

Derivation — Body About to Move Down the Plane

Again,

N = W cos α

F = μW cos α

Now, if the body tends to move down, the downward force is W sin α.

The forces acting up the plane are:

  • Applied force P
  • Friction F = μW cos α

Therefore:

W sin α = P + μW cos α

P = W sin α − μW cos α

P = W(sin α − μ cos α)

Memory Trick

For force parallel to incline:

  • To move up: P = W(sin α + μ cos α)
  • To move down: P = W(sin α − μ cos α)

Just remember: friction changes sign depending on direction of impending motion.

7.4 Solved Examples

Example 4.9

A block weighing 1000 N rests on a rough plane inclined at 20° to the horizontal. If the coefficient of friction is 0.3, find the force acting parallel to the plane required to just move the block upward.

Solution 4.9
Given: W = 1000 N, α = 20°, μ = 0.3
Formula for moving up:
P = W(sin α + μ cos α)
Substitute values:
P = 1000(sin 20° + 0.3 cos 20°)
P = 1000(0.342 + 0.3 × 0.9397)
P = 1000(0.342 + 0.2819)
P = 1000 × 0.6239

P = 623.9 N

Example 4.10

A body of weight 800 N is on a rough plane inclined at 25°. If μ = 0.25, find the force acting parallel to the plane required to prevent the body from sliding down.

Solution 4.10
Given: W = 800 N, α = 25°, μ = 0.25
Since the body tends to move downward, friction acts upward.
Force required up the plane is:

P = W(sin α − μ cos α)

P = 800(sin 25° − 0.25 cos 25°)
P = 800(0.4226 − 0.25 × 0.9063)
P = 800(0.4226 − 0.2266)
P = 800 × 0.196

P = 156.8 N

Example 4.11

A block of weight 500 N is placed on a rough plane inclined at 15°. The coefficient of friction is 0.2. Will the block slide down on its own?

Solution 4.11
Given: W = 500 N, α = 15°, μ = 0.2
Component of weight down the plane:
W sin α = 500 sin 15° = 500 × 0.2588 = 129.4 N
Normal reaction:
N = W cos α = 500 cos 15° = 500 × 0.9659 = 482.95 N
Limiting friction:
FL = μN = 0.2 × 482.95 = 96.59 N
Since component down plane (129.4 N) > limiting friction (96.59 N),

The block WILL slide down.

Body on Inclined Plane — Force Inclined to Plane

8.1 Introduction

This is the most general case. The plane is inclined at angle α, and the applied force P itself is inclined at angle θ to the plane.

Now we must resolve both:

  • Weight W into components along and perpendicular to the plane
  • Applied force P into components along and perpendicular to the plane

Fig. 4.11: Block on rough inclined plane with applied force P at angle θ to the plane, showing weight components, normal reaction, and friction.

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

8.2 Case 1: Force Inclined Upward from Plane, Body About to Move Up the Plane

Derivation — Motion Up the Plane

Let P be applied at angle θ above the plane.

Resolve P:

  • Along plane = P cos θ
  • Perpendicular outward from plane = P sin θ

Resolve W:

  • Along plane downward = W sin α
  • Perpendicular into plane = W cos α

Normal reaction:

N + P sin θ = W cos α

N = W cos α - P sin θ

Friction:

F = μN = μ(W cos α - P sin θ)

Along the plane:

For impending upward motion, friction acts downward.

P cos θ = W sin α + μ(W cos α - P sin θ)

P cos θ = W sin α + μW cos α - μP sin θ

P(cos θ + μ sin θ) = W(sin α + μ cos α)

P = W(sin α + μ cos α)cos θ + μ sin θ

8.3 Case 2: Force Inclined Upward from Plane, Body About to Move Down the Plane

Derivation — Motion Down the Plane

Again,

N = W cos α - P sin θ

For impending downward motion: friction acts upward.

Along the plane:

W sin α = P cos θ + μ(W cos α - P sin θ)

W sin α = P cos θ + μW cos α - μP sin θ

W(sin α - μ cos α) = P(cos θ - μ sin θ)

P = W(sin α - μ cos α)cos θ - μ sin θ

Formula Summary
  • Body about to move UP:
    P = W(sin α + μ cos α) / (cos θ + μ sin θ)
  • Body about to move DOWN:
    P = W(sin α − μ cos α) / (cos θ − μ sin θ)

8.4 Solved Examples

Example 4.12

A block weighing 1200 N rests on a rough plane inclined at 30°. It is pulled by a force at 20° above the plane. If μ = 0.25, find the force required to just move the block upward.

Solution 4.12
Given: W = 1200 N, α = 30°, θ = 20°, μ = 0.25

Example 4.14

A 700 N block is on an incline of 20°. It is pulled by a force of 250 N at 10° above the plane. If μ = 0.2, determine whether it will move up, move down, or remain at rest.

Solution 4.14
Given: W = 700 N, α = 20°, P = 250 N, θ = 10°, μ = 0.2
Normal reaction:
N = W cos α - P sin θ
N = 700 cos 20° - 250 sin 10°
N = 700 × 0.9397 - 250 × 0.1736
N = 657.79 - 43.4
N = 614.39 N
Limiting friction:
FL = μN = 0.2 × 614.39 = 122.88 N
Force component up the plane:
P cos θ = 250 cos 10° = 250 × 0.9848 = 246.2 N
Weight component down the plane:
W sin α = 700 sin 20° = 700 × 0.342 = 239.4 N
Compare the driving tendency:
Upward component = 246.2 N
Downward resisting due to weight = 239.4 N
Difference = 6.8 N upward

Friction can oppose this by 122.88 N, which is much larger than 6.8 N.
Therefore the block will NOT move.

The block remains at rest.

Important Definitions

1. Friction

Friction is the resisting force that acts between two surfaces in contact when one body moves or tends to move over the other. It acts tangentially to the contact surface and opposite to the direction of motion or tendency of motion.

2. Static Friction

Static friction is the frictional force acting between two surfaces in contact when there is no actual relative motion, but there is a tendency of motion. It is self-adjusting and varies from zero to the limiting friction.

3. Limiting Friction

Limiting friction is the maximum value of static friction that acts when the body is just about to move. At this condition, friction reaches its maximum value and is given by F = μN.

4. Kinetic Friction

Kinetic or dynamic friction is the frictional force acting between two surfaces when there is actual relative motion between them. It is less than limiting friction.

5. Rolling Friction

Rolling friction is the frictional resistance that acts when a body rolls over a surface. It is much smaller than sliding friction.

6. Coefficient of Friction

The coefficient of friction is the ratio of limiting friction to the normal reaction between the surfaces. It is denoted by μ and has no unit. μ = F/N.

7. Angle of Friction

The angle of friction is the angle between the resultant reaction and the normal reaction when the body is on the verge of motion. It is denoted by φ and satisfies tan φ = μ.

8. Cone of Friction

The cone of friction is the cone generated by the resultant reaction when the direction of friction changes through 360° under limiting condition. Its semi-vertical angle is equal to the angle of friction.

9. Angle of Repose

The angle of repose is the maximum inclination of a plane with the horizontal at which a body just begins to slide down under its own weight. It is equal to the angle of friction.

10. Normal Reaction

Normal reaction is the force exerted by the surface on the body, acting perpendicular to the surface of contact.

Long Answer Questions (5–7 Mark Style)

Q1. Define friction. Explain its types and engineering applications. [5–7 Marks]

Definition: Friction is the resisting force that acts between two surfaces in contact when one body moves or tends to move over the other.

Types:

  1. Static friction: Acts when body is at rest but tends to move.
  2. Limiting friction: Maximum value of static friction just before motion starts.
  3. Kinetic or dynamic friction: Acts when the body is actually moving.
  4. Rolling friction: Acts when a body rolls over a surface and is much smaller than sliding friction.

Engineering applications:

  • Walking and movement of vehicles
  • Braking systems
  • Clutches and brakes
  • Belt drives
  • Nuts and bolts
  • Grinding and polishing operations

Disadvantages: wear and tear, loss of power, heating, noise, reduced efficiency.

Q2. State and explain the laws of friction. [5 Marks] 🔥

The laws of friction are:

  1. Friction acts opposite to motion or tendency of motion.
  2. Limiting friction is directly proportional to normal reaction: F = μN.
  3. Friction depends on the nature of surfaces in contact.
  4. Friction is independent of the apparent area of contact.
  5. Static friction is self-adjusting up to limiting value.
  6. Kinetic friction is less than limiting static friction.

These laws are valid for dry friction between rigid bodies.

Q3. Define angle of friction and angle of repose. Prove that they are equal. [5–7 Marks] 🔥

Angle of friction (φ): The angle between the resultant reaction and the normal reaction at limiting condition.

Angle of repose (α): The maximum inclination of a plane with the horizontal at which a body just begins to slide down.

Proof:

Consider a body on a rough inclined plane at angle α, just about to slide down.

Forces:

  • Weight W vertically downward
  • Normal reaction N perpendicular to plane
  • Friction F acting up the plane

Resolving along plane: F = W sin α

Resolving perpendicular to plane: N = W cos α

Dividing, F/N = tan α

But F/N = μ = tan φ

Therefore tan α = tan φ

Hence, α = φ

Q4. Derive the expression for force required to pull a body on a rough horizontal plane by a force inclined at angle θ to the horizontal. [5 Marks]

Let W = weight, P = pull at angle θ above horizontal, N = normal reaction, μ = coefficient of friction.

Resolve P:

  • Horizontal component = P cos θ
  • Vertical component = P sin θ (upward)

Vertical equilibrium:

N + P sin θ = W

N = W − P sin θ

Friction F = μN = μ(W − P sin θ)

Horizontal equilibrium at limiting condition:

P cos θ = F = μ(W − P sin θ)

P cos θ = μW − μP sin θ

P(cos θ + μ sin θ) = μW

P = μW / (cos θ + μ sin θ)

Q5. Derive the force required to move a body upward on a rough inclined plane when the force acts parallel to the plane. [5 Marks]

Let W be the weight of body and α be the angle of inclination.

Resolve weight:

  • Along plane downward = W sin α
  • Perpendicular to plane = W cos α

Normal reaction: N = W cos α

Friction: F = μN = μW cos α

Since body is about to move upward, friction acts downward.

Along plane, upward force P must balance:

  • Weight component W sin α
  • Friction μW cos α

Therefore:

P = W sin α + μW cos α

P = W(sin α + μ cos α)
