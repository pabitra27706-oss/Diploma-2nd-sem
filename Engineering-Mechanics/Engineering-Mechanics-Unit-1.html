Basic Concepts of Engineering Mechanics

2.1 What is Engineering Mechanics? 🔥

Definition: Engineering Mechanics

Engineering Mechanics is the branch of science that deals with the study of the effect of forces on bodies that are either at rest or in motion.

In simple words: When you push a table, lift a bag, or a crane lifts a heavy load — forces are acting. Engineering Mechanics helps us understand, calculate, and predict what happens when forces act on objects.

Branches of Engineering Mechanics

Engineering Mechanics is divided into two main branches:

  1. Statics — Study of bodies at rest (or in equilibrium)
  2. Dynamics — Study of bodies in motion

Dynamics is further divided into:

  • Kinematics — Study of motion without considering the forces causing it (only displacement, velocity, acceleration)
  • Kinetics — Study of motion considering the forces that cause the motion

2.2 Fundamental Concepts 🔥

Before studying mechanics, you need to understand these basic terms:

2.2.1 Space

Definition: Space

Space is the geometric region in which events take place. It is associated with the concept of position. A point in space can be described by three coordinates (x, y, z) in a three-dimensional system.

Real-world analogy: Think of a room. The room is "space." Any object in the room has a position — you can describe where a table is by saying "2 metres from the wall, 3 metres from the door."

2.2.2 Time

Definition: Time

Time is the measure of the sequence of events. It is a fundamental quantity used mainly in Dynamics to describe when events occur, how long they take, and the rate of change of position (velocity) or velocity (acceleration).

Note: In Statics, time does not play a significant role because bodies are at rest — nothing is changing with time.

2.2.3 Mass

Definition: Mass

Mass is the quantity of matter contained in a body. It is a measure of the inertia of the body (i.e., resistance to change in motion). SI unit of mass is kilogram (kg).

Real-world analogy: A heavy iron ball is harder to push than a light plastic ball — the iron ball has more mass (more inertia). Mass does NOT change whether you are on Earth or Moon.

Mass vs. Weight — Don't Confuse!
  • Mass = Amount of matter = constant everywhere = measured in kg
  • Weight = Force of gravity on the mass = W = mg = changes with location = measured in Newton (N)

A 10 kg body has weight = 10 × 9.81 = 98.1 N on Earth, but only about 16.3 N on the Moon!

2.2.4 Particle

Definition: Particle

A particle is an idealized body that has mass but has negligible (very small) dimensions. All the mass is assumed to be concentrated at a single point.

When do we use this concept? When the size of the body is very small compared to the distances involved. For example, when studying the motion of Earth around the Sun, we can treat Earth as a particle because Earth's diameter (12,742 km) is tiny compared to its distance from Sun (150 million km).

2.2.5 Flexible Body

Definition: Flexible Body

A flexible body is a body that changes its shape when forces are applied to it. Examples include ropes, chains, cables, belts, and springs.

2.2.6 Rigid Body 🔥

Definition: Rigid Body

A rigid body is an idealized body in which the distance between any two points remains constant (unchanged) no matter what forces are applied to it. In other words, a rigid body does not deform (does not change shape or size) under the action of forces.

⭐ Important: In reality, every body deforms slightly under forces. But in Engineering Mechanics, we assume bodies to be perfectly rigid for simplicity. This is one of the fundamental assumptions of this subject. The study of deformable bodies is done in "Strength of Materials."

Real-world analogy: Think of a steel beam — when you put load on it, it bends a tiny bit. But in Mechanics, we assume it stays perfectly straight. This simplification makes calculations manageable.

Scalar & Vector Quantities

3.1 Scalar Quantity

Definition: Scalar Quantity

A scalar quantity is a physical quantity that has only magnitude (numerical value with unit) but no direction.

Examples of Scalars: Mass (5 kg), Temperature (25°C), Time (10 seconds), Length (3 m), Area (12 m²), Volume (5 m³), Speed (60 km/hr), Energy (100 J), Work (500 J), Density (7850 kg/m³).

Real-world analogy: If someone says "I have 5 kg of rice," that's complete information. You don't need to ask "in which direction?" because mass has no direction.

3.2 Vector Quantity 🔥

Definition: Vector Quantity

A vector quantity is a physical quantity that has both magnitude and direction. It also obeys the laws of vector addition (like the parallelogram law).

Examples of Vectors: Force (10 N towards east), Velocity (20 m/s at 30° upward), Displacement (5 m north), Acceleration (9.81 m/s² downward), Momentum, Weight.

Real-world analogy: If someone says "Push with 50 N force," you will ask "In which direction?" A force of 50 N pushing left is completely different from 50 N pushing right. So force needs both magnitude AND direction — it is a vector.

3.4 Representation of a Vector

A vector is represented by a directed line segment (an arrow):

  • The length of the arrow represents the magnitude (drawn to a suitable scale)
  • The arrowhead shows the direction (sense)
  • The starting point is called the tail
  • The ending point (arrow tip) is called the head

For example, a force of 50 N acting at 30° to the horizontal can be drawn as an arrow of length 5 cm (scale: 1 cm = 10 N) at 30° to the horizontal line.

3.5 Addition of Vectors 🔥

Vectors CANNOT be added like ordinary numbers. If two forces of 3 N and 4 N act on a body, the resultant is NOT always 7 N — it depends on the angle between them!

Methods of Vector Addition
  1. Triangle Law of Vector Addition
  2. Parallelogram Law of Vector Addition
  3. Polygon Law of Vector Addition (for more than 2 vectors)

These laws are explained in detail in the "Composition of Forces" section below.

3.5.1 Triangle Law of Vector Addition

Triangle Law of Vector Addition

If two vectors are represented in magnitude and direction by the two sides of a triangle taken in order (head to tail), then their resultant is represented in magnitude and direction by the third side of the triangle taken in the opposite order (from the tail of the first to the head of the second).

3.6 Subtraction of Vectors

Vector Subtraction

To subtract vector B from vector A:

\(\vec{A} - \vec{B} = \vec{A} + (-\vec{B})\)

Steps:

  1. Reverse the direction of vector B to get −B
  2. Add vector A and −B using the parallelogram or triangle law

So vector subtraction is simply vector addition with the second vector reversed!

Force — Definition, Representation & Properties

5.1 Definition of Force 🔥

Definition: Force

Force is an external agent (push or pull) that changes or tends to change the state of rest or state of uniform motion of a body, or changes the direction of motion, or changes the shape of a body.

Real-world examples:

  • Pushing a car → changes state of rest to motion
  • Braking a bicycle → changes state of motion to rest
  • Hitting a cricket ball → changes direction of motion
  • Squeezing a rubber ball → changes shape of the body
Unit of Force

SI Unit: Newton (N)

\(1 \text{ N} = 1 \text{ kg} \times 1 \text{ m/s}^2\)

One Newton is the force required to accelerate a mass of 1 kg at a rate of 1 m/s².

Other units: kN (kilonewton) = 1000 N, MN (meganewton) = 10⁶ N

5.2 Characteristics (Elements) of a Force 🔥

A force is completely specified by its four characteristics:

Four Characteristics of a Force
  1. Magnitude: The size or value of the force (e.g., 100 N, 5 kN)
  2. Direction: The line along which the force acts and the sense (push/pull, towards/away). Defined by the angle it makes with a reference line.
  3. Point of Application: The exact point on the body where the force is applied.
  4. Line of Action: An imaginary infinite line extending in both directions along the direction of the force through the point of application.
⭐ Exam Tip: "State the characteristics of a force" is a very common 2-mark question. Always mention all four: Magnitude, Direction, Point of Application, Line of Action.

5.3 Effects of a Force 🔥

When a force acts on a body, it may produce one or more of the following effects:

Effects of a Force
  1. It may change the state of rest of a body — A stationary body starts moving (e.g., kicking a football)
  2. It may change the state of uniform motion — A moving body stops or changes speed (e.g., braking a car)
  3. It may change the direction of motion — A moving body turns (e.g., hitting a cricket ball changes its path)
  4. It may change the shape of a body — The body gets deformed (e.g., compressing a spring, stretching a rubber band)
  5. It may produce turning effect (rotation) — The body rotates about a point or axis (e.g., opening a door by pushing its handle)

5.4 Representation of a Force

5.4.1 As a Vector

Since force has both magnitude and direction, it is a vector quantity. It is represented graphically by a directed line segment (arrow).

  • Draw a line to a suitable scale (e.g., 1 cm = 10 N)
  • The length represents the magnitude
  • The direction of the arrow shows the direction of force
  • The starting point represents the point of application
Example

A force of 40 N acts at 45° to the horizontal. Draw its vector representation.

Solution: Choose scale: 1 cm = 10 N. So 40 N = 4 cm. Draw a line of 4 cm at 45° to the horizontal with an arrowhead at the end.

5.4.2 Bow's Notation 🔥

Definition: Bow's Notation

Bow's Notation is a method of labelling forces in which the spaces between the forces are labeled with capital letters (A, B, C, D...). Each force is then identified by the two letters of the spaces on either side of it.

How Bow's Notation Works
  1. Label the spaces between forces (and beyond the extreme forces) with capital letters A, B, C, D... going clockwise
  2. Each force is then named by the two letters on its sides. For example, the force between spaces A and B is called force AB
  3. In the vector diagram (force polygon), these same letters are used in lowercase (a, b, c...) to denote the corresponding points

Bow's Notation is especially useful in the graphical method of finding resultants and is essential for constructing vector diagrams (force polygons).

5.5 Principle of Transmissibility of Force 🔥

Principle of Transmissibility

The Principle of Transmissibility states that the external effect of a force on a rigid body remains unchanged if the force is moved (shifted) along its line of action to any other point on the body.

In simpler words: You can slide a force along its line of action, and the effect on the rigid body (whether it moves, rotates, or stays still) remains the same.

Understanding with Example

Imagine pushing a rigid box along a smooth floor. Whether you push at the front face, or you pull from the back face (along the same line), the box moves in the same way. The external effect (motion of the box) is the same.

Important Limitations:

  • This principle applies ONLY to rigid bodies
  • It applies ONLY to the external effects (motion/equilibrium), NOT to internal effects (stress, deformation)
  • The force can be moved ONLY along its line of action, NOT in any other direction

Force Systems & Classification

6.1 What is a Force System?

Definition: Force System

When two or more forces act on a body simultaneously, the group of forces is called a Force System (or System of Forces).

6.2 Classification of Force Systems 🔥

Force systems are classified based on two criteria:

  1. Whether the forces lie in one plane or not → Coplanar vs. Non-coplanar
  2. Whether the lines of action meet at a point or not → Concurrent vs. Non-concurrent
Complete Classification

A. Coplanar Force Systems (All forces in ONE plane)

  1. Coplanar Concurrent: All forces lie in one plane AND their lines of action pass through a single common point.
    Example: Forces acting on a pin joint, forces on a hook where multiple ropes meet.
  2. Coplanar Non-concurrent: All forces lie in one plane BUT their lines of action do NOT pass through a single point.
    Example: Forces on a beam (weight, reactions at supports).
  3. Coplanar Parallel: All forces lie in one plane AND are parallel to each other (a special case of non-concurrent).
    Example: Weight distributed along a beam, load on a simply supported beam.
  4. Coplanar Collinear: All forces lie in one plane AND act along the same line (same line of action).
    Example: Tug of war — all forces along the same rope.

B. Non-coplanar Force Systems (Forces in MORE than one plane — 3D)

  1. Non-coplanar Concurrent: Forces in 3D space, all meeting at one point.
    Example: Guy wires attached to a communication tower top.
  2. Non-coplanar Non-concurrent: Forces in 3D space, not meeting at one point.
    Example: Forces on a 3D structure or machine frame.
  3. Non-coplanar Parallel: Forces in 3D space, all parallel.
    Example: Column loads in a multi-storey building.

6.3 Key Terms for Force Systems

Resultant Force

The Resultant is a single force that produces the same effect as the combined effect of all the forces in a system. Finding the resultant is called Composition of Forces.

Equilibrant Force

The Equilibrant is a single force that, when added to a force system, produces equilibrium (i.e., the body remains at rest). The equilibrant is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction to the resultant.

Component Forces

Components are two or more forces into which a single force can be split. The process of splitting a force into components is called Resolution of Forces.

Composition of Forces — Parallelogram, Triangle & Polygon Laws

7.1 What is Composition of Forces? 🔥

Definition: Composition of Forces

Composition of Forces (also called Compounding of Forces) is the process of finding a single force (called the Resultant) which produces the same effect as a given system of forces acting on a body.

Real-world analogy: Imagine three people pushing a heavy box in slightly different directions. Composition tells us: "What single push would move the box in exactly the same way as those three pushes combined?"

Two Methods of Finding Resultant
  1. Analytical Method — Using mathematical formulas and calculations (more accurate)
  2. Graphical Method — Using drawings to scale with ruler and protractor (less accurate but visual)

7.2 Parallelogram Law of Forces 🔥🔥

Parallelogram Law of Forces

If two forces acting simultaneously at a point are represented in magnitude and direction by the two adjacent sides of a parallelogram, then their resultant is represented in magnitude and direction by the diagonal of the parallelogram passing through that point.

7.2.1 Analytical Formula (Derivation) 🔥🔥

Derivation of Resultant using Parallelogram Law

Let two forces P and Q act at a point O, making an angle α (alpha) between them.

Step 1: Draw OA = P and OB = Q as adjacent sides of a parallelogram OACB.

Step 2: Complete the parallelogram. The diagonal OC = R is the resultant.

Step 3: From C, drop a perpendicular CD on OA (extended if needed).

Step 4: In triangle OCD:

  • OD = OA + AD = P + Q cos α
  • CD = Q sin α

Step 5: Applying Pythagoras theorem in triangle OCD:

\[R^2 = OD^2 + CD^2\]

\[R^2 = (P + Q\cos\alpha)^2 + (Q\sin\alpha)^2\]

\[R^2 = P^2 + 2PQ\cos\alpha + Q^2\cos^2\alpha + Q^2\sin^2\alpha\]

\[R^2 = P^2 + 2PQ\cos\alpha + Q^2(\cos^2\alpha + \sin^2\alpha)\]

Since \(\cos^2\alpha + \sin^2\alpha = 1\):

Formula 1: Magnitude of Resultant (Parallelogram Law)

\[\boxed{R = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2 + 2PQ\cos\alpha}}\]

Where:

  • R = Magnitude of the resultant force (N)
  • P = Magnitude of first force (N)
  • Q = Magnitude of second force (N)
  • α (alpha) = Angle between the two forces P and Q
Direction of Resultant

Step 6: Let the resultant R make an angle θ (theta) with force P.

From triangle OCD:

\[\tan\theta = \frac{CD}{OD} = \frac{Q\sin\alpha}{P + Q\cos\alpha}\]

Formula 2: Direction of Resultant

\[\boxed{\tan\theta = \frac{Q\sin\alpha}{P + Q\cos\alpha}}\]

Where:

  • θ (theta) = Angle of resultant R with force P
  • α (alpha) = Angle between forces P and Q
🔥🔥 MOST IMPORTANT FORMULAS OF THIS UNIT: These two formulas (magnitude and direction of resultant) are used in almost every numerical problem. Memorize them thoroughly!

7.2.2 Special Cases of Parallelogram Law 🔥

Special Cases — Very Important for Exams

Case 1: When α = 0° (Forces in the same direction — same sense)

\(R = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2 + 2PQ\cos 0°} = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2 + 2PQ}\)

\(R = \sqrt{(P+Q)^2}\)

\[\boxed{R_{max} = P + Q}\]

The resultant is maximum when forces act in the same direction. Direction: along the same line.

Case 2: When α = 180° (Forces in opposite directions)

\(R = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2 + 2PQ\cos 180°} = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2 - 2PQ}\)

\(R = \sqrt{(P-Q)^2}\)

\[\boxed{R_{min} = P - Q} \quad \text{(along the direction of greater force)}\]

The resultant is minimum when forces act in opposite directions.

Case 3: When α = 90° (Forces at right angles)

\(R = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2 + 2PQ\cos 90°} = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2 + 0}\)

\[\boxed{R = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2}} \quad \text{(Pythagoras theorem!)}\]

\(\tan\theta = \frac{Q}{P}\)

Case 4: When P = Q (Two equal forces)

\(R = \sqrt{P^2 + P^2 + 2P^2\cos\alpha} = P\sqrt{2(1 + \cos\alpha)}\)

Using identity: \(1 + \cos\alpha = 2\cos^2\frac{\alpha}{2}\)

\[\boxed{R = 2P\cos\frac{\alpha}{2}}\]

Direction: \(\theta = \frac{\alpha}{2}\) (bisects the angle between the forces)

Case 5: When P = Q and α = 60°

\(R = 2P\cos 30° = 2P \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = P\sqrt{3}\)

Case 6: When P = Q and α = 90°

\(R = 2P\cos 45° = 2P \times \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = P\sqrt{2}\)

Case 7: When P = Q and α = 120°

\(R = 2P\cos 60° = 2P \times \frac{1}{2} = P\)

(Resultant equals each force when two equal forces act at 120°!)

Golden Rule to Remember

The resultant of two forces always lies between:

(P − Q) ≤ R ≤ (P + Q)

This is very useful in MCQs! If a question gives P = 5 N and Q = 3 N, the resultant must be between 2 N and 8 N. Any option outside this range is wrong!

7.3 Triangle Law of Forces 🔥

Triangle Law of Forces

If two forces acting at a point are represented in magnitude and direction by the two sides of a triangle taken in order (i.e., head-to-tail arrangement), then the resultant is represented in magnitude and direction by the third side of the triangle taken in the reverse order (closing side, from the tail of the first force to the head of the second force).

How Triangle Law Works — Step by Step
  1. Choose a suitable scale (e.g., 1 cm = 10 N)
  2. Draw the first force P as a vector (arrow) from point a to point b
  3. From the head of P (point b), draw the second force Q as a vector from b to point c
  4. The closing side of the triangle, from a to c, represents the resultant R in magnitude and direction

Note: The two forces are placed head-to-tail, and the resultant goes from the tail of the first to the head of the last.

7.3.1 Applying Sine Rule to the Force Triangle

Once you have the force triangle, you can use the Sine Rule of trigonometry to find unknown sides (forces) or angles:

Sine Rule for Force Triangle

\[\frac{P}{\sin\beta} = \frac{Q}{\sin\gamma} = \frac{R}{\sin\delta}\]

Where β, γ, δ are the angles of the triangle opposite to sides P, Q, R respectively.

7.4 Polygon Law of Forces 🔥

Polygon Law of Forces

If a number of forces acting at a point are represented in magnitude and direction by the sides of a polygon taken in order (head-to-tail), then the resultant is represented in magnitude and direction by the closing side of the polygon taken in the reverse order.

Understanding the Polygon Law

The Polygon Law is simply the extension of the Triangle Law for more than two forces.

For 3 forces: Triangle (3 sides)

For 4 forces: Quadrilateral (4 sides)

For 5 forces: Pentagon (5 sides)

For n forces: n-sided polygon

Steps:

  1. Choose a suitable scale
  2. Draw the first force as a vector from point a to b
  3. From the head of the first (b), draw the second force from b to c
  4. From the head of the second (c), draw the third force from c to d
  5. Continue for all forces...
  6. The closing side (from the tail of the first force to the head of the last force) is the resultant

Important Note

If the polygon closes (i.e., the head of the last force coincides with the tail of the first force), then the resultant is zero — the forces are in equilibrium!

7.5 Vector Diagram (Graphical Method) 🔥

Definition: Vector Diagram

A Vector Diagram (also called a Force Polygon) is a graphical diagram drawn to scale in which forces are represented as vectors arranged head-to-tail. The closing side gives the resultant. It is used with Bow's Notation for systematic force identification.

Steps for Drawing a Vector Diagram (Graphical Method)
  1. Draw the Space Diagram: Show all the forces acting at the point with their correct directions and angles. Label using Bow's Notation (spaces labeled A, B, C, D... clockwise).
  2. Choose a Scale: e.g., 1 cm = 10 N
  3. Start the Vector Diagram: Pick any force (say AB). Draw it to scale in its correct direction. Label the start point as 'a' and end as 'b'.
  4. Draw the next force: From point 'b', draw force BC in its correct direction. Mark endpoint as 'c'.
  5. Continue for all forces (head-to-tail).
  6. Close the polygon: The line from the first point 'a' to the last point gives the resultant.
  7. Measure: Measure the closing side length (convert using scale → magnitude of R) and its angle (→ direction of R).

7.6 Analytical Method for Multiple Concurrent Forces 🔥🔥

General Analytical Method (For Any Number of Concurrent Forces)

When there are more than two concurrent forces, the most efficient analytical method uses Resolution (splitting each force into X and Y components) and then combining the components.

Steps:

  1. Choose X-axis (horizontal) and Y-axis (vertical) as reference directions
  2. Resolve each force into X-component and Y-component:
    • \(F_x = F\cos\theta\) (horizontal component)
    • \(F_y = F\sin\theta\) (vertical component)
    where θ is the angle the force makes with the X-axis
  3. Find the algebraic sum of all X-components: \(\Sigma F_x\)
  4. Find the algebraic sum of all Y-components: \(\Sigma F_y\)
  5. Calculate the Resultant:
Formula 3: Resultant of Multiple Concurrent Forces

\[\boxed{R = \sqrt{(\Sigma F_x)^2 + (\Sigma F_y)^2}}\]

Formula 4: Direction of Resultant

\[\boxed{\tan\theta = \frac{\Sigma F_y}{\Sigma F_x}}\]

Where θ is the angle of the resultant with the X-axis (horizontal).

Sign Convention — Very Important!
  • → (Right / Positive X direction): Positive (+)
  • ← (Left / Negative X direction): Negative (−)
  • ↑ (Upward / Positive Y direction): Positive (+)
  • ↓ (Downward / Negative Y direction): Negative (−)

Always be careful with signs! A wrong sign will give a completely wrong answer.

Finding the Correct Quadrant for θ

After finding tan θ, you must check the signs of ΣFx and ΣFy to determine which quadrant the resultant lies in:

  • ΣFx > 0, ΣFy > 0: First quadrant (0° to 90°)
  • ΣFx < 0, ΣFy > 0: Second quadrant (90° to 180°)
  • ΣFx < 0, ΣFy < 0: Third quadrant (180° to 270°)
  • ΣFx > 0, ΣFy < 0: Fourth quadrant (270° to 360°)

Example 7.1 — Two Forces (Parallelogram Law)

Resolution of Forces — Orthogonal Components

8.1 What is Resolution of Forces? 🔥

Definition: Resolution of Forces

Resolution of Forces is the process of splitting (breaking) a single force into two or more forces (called components) which together produce the same effect as the original force. It is the reverse process of composition.

Real-world analogy: Composition is like mixing blue and yellow paint to get green (two forces → one resultant). Resolution is like separating green paint back into blue and yellow (one force → two components).

Composition vs. Resolution
  • Composition: Many forces → One single resultant (combining)
  • Resolution: One force → Two or more components (splitting)

They are opposite (reverse) processes.

8.2 Orthogonal Components (Rectangular Components) 🔥🔥

Definition: Orthogonal (Rectangular) Components

When a force is resolved into two components that are mutually perpendicular (at 90° to each other), these components are called orthogonal components or rectangular components. Usually, one is along the horizontal (X-axis) and the other along the vertical (Y-axis).

Consider a force F making an angle θ with the horizontal (X-axis):

Fig. 1.15: Resolution of Force F into horizontal (F cos θ) and vertical (F sin θ) components

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Formula 5: Rectangular Components of a Force

\[\boxed{F_x = F\cos\theta}\]

\[\boxed{F_y = F\sin\theta}\]

Where:

  • F = magnitude of the original force
  • θ = angle of force with the horizontal (X-axis)
  • Fx = horizontal component (along X-axis)
  • Fy = vertical component (along Y-axis)
Easy Way to Remember: "Cos is Close, Sin is Sky"
  • cos θ → The component that is "close" to the angle θ (i.e., along the side that makes the angle)
  • sin θ → The component that is "going to the sky" (i.e., perpendicular to the side that makes the angle)

If θ is measured from horizontal: Fx = F cos θ (horizontal is "close" to the angle), Fy = F sin θ (vertical goes "up to sky").

⚠️ But if θ is measured from the vertical: Then Fx = F sin θ and Fy = F cos θ. Always check from where the angle is measured!

Most Common Mistake in Exams!

Students often confuse whether to use sin θ or cos θ. The rule is simple:

  • The component along the side from which angle is measured → use cos θ
  • The component perpendicular to the side from which angle is measured → use sin θ

If the angle is from horizontal: Horizontal = cos θ, Vertical = sin θ

If the angle is from vertical: Vertical = cos θ, Horizontal = sin θ

8.3 Verification: Components Back to Original Force

You can always verify by composing the components back:

Formula 6: Verifying Resolution

\[\boxed{F = \sqrt{F_x^2 + F_y^2}}\]

\[\boxed{\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{F_y}{F_x}\right)}\]

8.5 Non-rectangular Resolution

Resolution into Non-perpendicular Components

A force can also be resolved into two components that are NOT at 90° to each other. However, in most diploma-level problems, we use orthogonal (rectangular) resolution only.

If a force F is to be resolved along two directions making angles α and β with the force (where α + β is the angle between the two component directions), then by the sine rule:

\[\frac{F}{\sin(\alpha + \beta)} = \frac{F_1}{\sin\beta} = \frac{F_2}{\sin\alpha}\]

This is rarely asked in WBSCTE exams but good to know.

Important Definitions — Exam Ready

1. Engineering Mechanics

Engineering Mechanics is the branch of science that deals with the study of the effect of forces on bodies that are at rest or in motion.

2. Statics

Statics is the branch of mechanics that deals with the study of bodies at rest (or in equilibrium) under the action of forces.

3. Dynamics

Dynamics is the branch of mechanics that deals with the study of bodies in motion and the forces causing that motion.

4. Kinematics

Kinematics is the branch of dynamics that deals with the study of motion of bodies without considering the forces causing the motion.

5. Kinetics

Kinetics is the branch of dynamics that deals with the study of motion of bodies taking into account the forces causing the motion.

6. Particle

A particle is an idealized body that has mass but negligible dimensions (size). Its mass is assumed to be concentrated at a single point.

7. Rigid Body

A rigid body is an idealized body in which the distance between any two points within the body remains constant regardless of the forces applied to it. It does not deform under the action of forces.

8. Force

Force is an external agent (push or pull) that changes or tends to change the state of rest or state of uniform motion of a body, or changes the direction of motion, or changes the shape of a body. SI unit: Newton (N).

9. Scalar Quantity

A scalar quantity is a physical quantity that has only magnitude (numerical value with unit) but no direction. Examples: mass, time, temperature, speed.

10. Vector Quantity

A vector quantity is a physical quantity that has both magnitude and direction and obeys the laws of vector addition. Examples: force, velocity, displacement, acceleration.

11. Resultant Force

The resultant is a single force that can replace a system of forces and produces the same effect on the body as the original system of forces.

12. Equilibrant Force

The equilibrant is a single force which, when added to a system of forces, produces equilibrium. It is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction to the resultant.

13. Composition of Forces

Composition of forces is the process of finding a single resultant force which produces the same effect as a given system of forces acting on a body.

14. Resolution of Forces

Resolution of forces is the process of splitting a single force into two or more components which together produce the same effect as the original force.

15. Principle of Transmissibility

The principle of transmissibility states that the external effect of a force on a rigid body remains unchanged if the force is moved along its line of action to any other point on the body.

16. Force System

When two or more forces act on a body simultaneously, the group of forces is called a force system.

17. Coplanar Forces

Forces whose lines of action lie in the same plane are called coplanar forces.

18. Concurrent Forces

Forces whose lines of action pass through a single common point are called concurrent forces.

19. Collinear Forces

Forces whose lines of action lie along the same straight line are called collinear forces.

20. Bow's Notation

Bow's Notation is a method of labelling forces in which the spaces between the forces are labeled with capital letters (A, B, C, D...) and each force is identified by the two letters of the spaces on either side of it.

Short Answer & Long Answer Questions

📝 Short Answer Questions (2 Marks Each)

SAQ 1: Define Engineering Mechanics and state its two main branches. ⭐

Engineering Mechanics is the branch of science that deals with the study of the effect of forces on bodies that are at rest or in motion.

Its two main branches are:

  1. Statics — deals with bodies at rest (in equilibrium)
  2. Dynamics — deals with bodies in motion (further divided into Kinematics and Kinetics)

SAQ 2: Differentiate between scalar and vector quantities with examples. ⭐

Scalar quantity: A physical quantity that has only magnitude (numerical value) but no direction. Examples: mass, time, temperature, speed, energy.

Vector quantity: A physical quantity that has both magnitude and direction and obeys the laws of vector addition. Examples: force, velocity, displacement, acceleration, momentum.

Key difference: Scalars are added algebraically (3 + 4 = 7 always), while vectors are added using parallelogram or triangle law (resultant depends on the angle between them).

SAQ 3: State the four characteristics of a force. 🔥

A force is completely specified by the following four characteristics:

  1. Magnitude — The size or value of the force (e.g., 100 N)
  2. Direction — The line along which the force acts and its sense (e.g., 30° to horizontal, acting upward)
  3. Point of Application — The exact point on the body where the force is applied
  4. Line of Action — An imaginary infinite line extending along the direction of the force through the point of application

SAQ 4: State the Principle of Transmissibility of Force. 🔥

The Principle of Transmissibility states that the external effect of a force on a rigid body remains unchanged if the force is moved (shifted) to any other point along its line of action.

Conditions:

  • Applicable only to rigid bodies
  • Only external effects remain unchanged (not internal stresses)
  • Force can be moved only along its line of action, not in any other direction

SAQ 5: Define resultant and equilibrant of a force system. ⭐

Resultant: The resultant is a single force that can replace a system of forces and produces the same effect on the body as all the forces acting together.

Equilibrant: The equilibrant is a single force which, when added to a system of forces, brings the body into equilibrium (state of rest). It is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction to the resultant.

SAQ 6: What is Bow's Notation? Why is it used?

Bow's Notation is a method of labelling forces in which the spaces between the forces are labeled with capital letters (A, B, C, D...) going clockwise. Each force is then identified by the two letters of the spaces on either side of it (e.g., force AB, force BC).

Why is it used?

  • It provides a systematic way of identifying forces in a space diagram
  • It helps in drawing the vector diagram (force polygon) accurately
  • It avoids confusion when dealing with multiple forces in graphical methods

SAQ 7: Define composition and resolution of forces. ⭐

Composition of Forces: It is the process of finding a single force (called the resultant) which produces the same effect as a given system of forces. (Many forces → One resultant)

Resolution of Forces: It is the process of splitting (breaking) a single force into two or more forces (called components) which together produce the same effect as the original force. (One force → Multiple components)

They are reverse processes of each other.

SAQ 8: State the effects of a force on a body. 🔥

A force may produce the following effects on a body:

  1. It may change the state of rest (start motion)
  2. It may change the state of uniform motion (stop or change speed)
  3. It may change the direction of motion
  4. It may change the shape of the body (deformation)
  5. It may produce a turning effect (rotation about a point)

SAQ 9: Define a particle and a rigid body. How are they different?

Particle: An idealized body that has mass but negligible (very small) dimensions. All mass is concentrated at a single point.

Rigid Body: An idealized body in which the distance between any two points remains constant (unchanged) regardless of applied forces. It has definite shape and size but does not deform.

Difference: A particle has no size (point mass), while a rigid body has definite shape and size. A particle cannot rotate, but a rigid body can rotate about an axis.

SAQ 10: What is the range of resultant of two forces P and Q? When is the resultant maximum and minimum?

The resultant R of two forces P and Q always lies in the range:

|P − Q| ≤ R ≤ (P + Q)

  • Maximum resultant = P + Q — when the forces act in the same direction (α = 0°)
  • Minimum resultant = |P − Q| — when the forces act in opposite directions (α = 180°)

LAQ 1: State and prove the Parallelogram Law of Forces. Derive the expressions for the magnitude and direction of the resultant. 🔥🔥

Statement: If two forces acting simultaneously at a point are represented in magnitude and direction by the two adjacent sides of a parallelogram, then their resultant is represented in magnitude and direction by the diagonal of the parallelogram passing through that point.

Proof / Derivation:

Let two forces P and Q act at point O, with angle α between them.

Fig: Parallelogram OACB with diagonal OC as resultant R

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Construction:

  • Draw OA = P along horizontal
  • Draw OB = Q at angle α to OA
  • Complete parallelogram OACB
  • Diagonal OC = Resultant R
  • From C, draw CD perpendicular to OA extended

In triangle ACD:

∠CAD = α (since AC is parallel to OB)

AC = OB = Q

AD = AC cos α = Q cos α

CD = AC sin α = Q sin α

In right triangle OCD:

OD = OA + AD = P + Q cos α

By Pythagoras theorem:

\[OC^2 = OD^2 + CD^2\]

\[R^2 = (P + Q\cos\alpha)^2 + (Q\sin\alpha)^2\]

\[R^2 = P^2 + 2PQ\cos\alpha + Q^2\cos^2\alpha + Q^2\sin^2\alpha\]

\[R^2 = P^2 + 2PQ\cos\alpha + Q^2(\cos^2\alpha + \sin^2\alpha)\]

Since cos²α + sin²α = 1:

\[\boxed{R = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2 + 2PQ\cos\alpha}}\]

Direction: Let θ be the angle of R with force P.

\[\tan\theta = \frac{CD}{OD} = \frac{Q\sin\alpha}{P + Q\cos\alpha}\]

\[\boxed{\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{Q\sin\alpha}{P + Q\cos\alpha}\right)}\]

(Hence proved.)

LAQ 2: Classify force systems with neat diagrams. Give examples of each type. 🔥

Force systems are classified based on two criteria: (a) Whether forces lie in one plane or not, and (b) Whether their lines of action meet at a point or not.

A. Coplanar Force Systems (All forces in one plane):

  1. Coplanar Concurrent: All forces lie in one plane and their lines of action pass through a single common point.
    Example: Forces on a hook where multiple ropes meet at one point.
  2. Coplanar Non-concurrent: All forces lie in one plane but their lines of action do NOT meet at one point.
    Example: Forces acting on a loaded beam (weight, support reactions).
  3. Coplanar Parallel: All forces lie in one plane and are parallel to each other.
    Example: Weights placed along a horizontal beam.
  4. Coplanar Collinear: All forces lie along the same straight line.
    Example: Tug-of-war (all forces along the rope).

B. Non-Coplanar Force Systems (Forces in 3D space):

  1. Non-coplanar Concurrent: Forces in 3D space, all meeting at one point.
    Example: Guy wires attached to top of a tower.
  2. Non-coplanar Non-concurrent: Forces in 3D, not meeting at one point.
    Example: Forces on a 3D frame structure.
  3. Non-coplanar Parallel: Forces in 3D, all parallel to each other.
    Example: Column loads in a building.

LAQ 3: State and explain the Parallelogram Law, Triangle Law, and Polygon Law of Forces. How are they related? 🔥

1. Parallelogram Law of Forces:

"If two forces acting simultaneously at a point are represented in magnitude and direction by the two adjacent sides of a parallelogram, then their resultant is represented in magnitude and direction by the diagonal of the parallelogram passing through that point."

Formula: \(R = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2 + 2PQ\cos\alpha}\)

2. Triangle Law of Forces:

"If two forces acting at a point are represented in magnitude and direction by the two sides of a triangle taken in order (head-to-tail), then their resultant is represented in magnitude and direction by the third side (closing side) taken in the reverse order."

3. Polygon Law of Forces:

"If a number of forces acting at a point are represented in magnitude and direction by the sides of a polygon taken in order (head-to-tail), then their resultant is represented in magnitude and direction by the closing side of the polygon taken in the reverse order."

Relationship between the three laws:

  • The Triangle Law is derived from the Parallelogram Law — the triangle is simply half of the parallelogram.
  • The Polygon Law is an extension of the Triangle Law — it applies the triangle law repeatedly for more than two forces.
  • All three are consistent and give the same resultant.
  • Parallelogram and Triangle laws work for two forces only. Polygon law works for any number of forces.
  • If the polygon closes completely, the resultant is zero and the forces are in equilibrium.

LAQ 4: Explain the analytical method of finding the resultant of a coplanar concurrent force system using the method of resolution. 🔥

Resolution Method (Analytical Method for Multiple Concurrent Forces):

This is the most powerful and commonly used method for finding the resultant of any number of concurrent coplanar forces.

Steps:

  1. Set up reference axes: Choose a convenient X-axis (horizontal) and Y-axis (vertical) passing through the point of concurrence.
  2. Resolve each force: For each force Fi making angle θi with the X-axis:
    Horizontal component: Fix = Fi cos θi
    Vertical component: Fiy = Fi sin θi
  3. Apply sign convention:
    → and ↑ are positive (+)
    ← and ↓ are negative (−)
  4. Sum up all components:
    ΣFx = sum of all horizontal components
    ΣFy = sum of all vertical components
  5. Calculate resultant magnitude:
    \(R = \sqrt{(\Sigma F_x)^2 + (\Sigma F_y)^2}\)
  6. Calculate resultant direction:
    \(\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\Sigma F_y}{\Sigma F_x}\right)\)
    Determine the quadrant based on signs of ΣFx and ΣFy.

Advantages of this method:

  • Works for any number of forces
  • Gives accurate (exact) numerical answers
  • Systematic and easy to tabulate
  • No drawing/scaling errors

LAQ 5: Discuss the special cases of the Parallelogram Law when (i) α = 0°, (ii) α = 90°, (iii) α = 180°, and (iv) P = Q.

The general formula for resultant: \(R = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2 + 2PQ\cos\alpha}\)

(i) When α = 0° (Same direction):

cos 0° = 1

R = √(P² + Q² + 2PQ) = √(P + Q)² = P + Q

This is the maximum possible resultant. The resultant acts along the same line as the forces.

(ii) When α = 90° (Perpendicular):

cos 90° = 0

R = √(P² + Q² + 0) = √(P² + Q²)

This is the Pythagoras theorem. Direction: tan θ = Q/P

(iii) When α = 180° (Opposite direction):

cos 180° = −1

R = √(P² + Q² − 2PQ) = √(P − Q)² = |P − Q|

This is the minimum possible resultant. The resultant acts along the direction of the larger force.

(iv) When P = Q (Equal forces):

R = √(P² + P² + 2P² cos α) = P√(2 + 2cos α) = P√(2(1 + cos α))

Using identity: 1 + cos α = 2cos²(α/2)

R = P × √(4cos²(α/2)) = 2P cos(α/2)

Direction: θ = α/2 (resultant bisects the angle between equal forces)

Special sub-cases for P = Q:

  • α = 0°: R = 2P
  • α = 60°: R = P√3
  • α = 90°: R = P√2
  • α = 120°: R = P
  • α = 180°: R = 0

LAQ 6: Explain the graphical method of finding the resultant of concurrent forces using vector diagram and Bow's Notation.

Graphical Method Using Vector Diagram and Bow's Notation:

The graphical method is a visual technique where forces are drawn to scale as vectors, and the resultant is measured directly from the drawing.

Step 1 — Draw the Space Diagram:

  • Draw all forces acting at the point with their correct directions and angles
  • Label the spaces between the forces using capital letters (A, B, C, D...) going clockwise — this is Bow's Notation
  • Each force is now identified by two letters (e.g., force AB, force BC, force CD)

Step 2 — Choose a Scale:

  • Select a suitable scale (e.g., 1 cm = 10 N or 1 cm = 20 N)
  • The scale should be such that the diagram fits the paper and is large enough for accurate measurement

Step 3 — Draw the Vector Diagram (Force Polygon):

  • Start with any force. For force AB, draw a line from point 'a' to point 'b' to scale, in the correct direction
  • From point 'b', draw force BC from 'b' to 'c' (head-to-tail)
  • Continue for all forces (c to d, d to e, etc.)
  • The closing line from the first point 'a' to the last point represents the Resultant

Step 4 — Measure the Resultant:

  • Measure the length of the closing line and convert using the scale → magnitude of R
  • Measure the angle of the closing line with reference direction → direction of R

Note: If the vector diagram closes (last point coincides with first point), the resultant is zero and the forces are in equilibrium.

📌 Composition of Forces (Finding Resultant)

  • Parallelogram Law: \(R = \sqrt{P^2 + Q^2 + 2PQ\cos\alpha}\)
  • Direction: \(\tan\theta = \frac{Q\sin\alpha}{P + Q\cos\alpha}\)
  • Triangle Law: Two vectors head-to-tail → closing side = resultant
  • Polygon Law: Multiple vectors head-to-tail → closing side = resultant
  • If polygon closes → Resultant = 0 → Equilibrium!

📌 Special Cases (MEMORIZE!) 🔥

  • α = 0°: R = P + Q (maximum)
  • α = 90°: R = √(P² + Q²)
  • α = 180°: R = |P − Q| (minimum)
  • P = Q: R = 2P cos(α/2), θ = α/2
  • P = Q, α = 120°: R = P (very commonly asked!)
  • Range: (P−Q) ≤ R ≤ (P+Q) — use for MCQ elimination

📌 Resolution of Forces

  • Fx = F cos θ (component along the side from which θ is measured)
  • Fy = F sin θ (component perpendicular to that side)
  • For multiple forces: ΣFx, ΣFy → R = √(ΣFx² + ΣFy²)
  • Sign convention: → and ↑ = positive, ← and ↓ = negative
  • Determine quadrant from signs of ΣFx and ΣFy

📌 Analytical vs. Graphical Method

  • Analytical = formulas (more accurate) | Graphical = scale drawing (visual)
  • Graphical uses Bow's Notation + Vector Diagram (Force Polygon)
