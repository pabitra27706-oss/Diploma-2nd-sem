Unit Overview

What This Unit Covers

This unit deals with the location of the geometric center and the gravitational center of engineering shapes and bodies. In engineering drawing, machine design, structures, fabrication, and mechanics, it is very important to know where the centroid or center of gravity lies.

This unit includes:

  • Concept of Centroid
  • Concept of Centre of Gravity
  • Centroid of standard plane figures:
    • Triangle
    • Rectangle
    • Circle
    • Semicircle
    • Quadrant of a circle
  • Centroid of composite sections
  • Centre of gravity of simple solids like cube, cuboid, cylinder and sphere
  • Centre of gravity of composite solids
Why This Unit Matters

When engineers design beams, channels, machine parts, supports, tanks, brackets, and fabricated sections, they must know the centroid. The centroid helps in:

  • Finding beam bending behavior
  • Designing balanced machine parts
  • Locating neutral axis
  • Determining stability of structures
  • Finding centre of gravity for safe support and lifting

Basic Concepts: Centroid and Centre of Gravity

1. Concept of Centroid 🔥

Definition: Centroid

The centroid of a plane figure is the point at which the entire area of the figure may be assumed to be concentrated.

In simple words, the centroid is the geometrical center of a plane area.

Easy Understanding

If a thin cardboard shape of uniform thickness is cut out, then the point where it balances perfectly on a pin is its centroid.

Important: Centroid is associated with area, not with mass directly.

2. Concept of Centre of Gravity 🔥

Definition: Centre of Gravity

The centre of gravity (C.G.) of a body is the point through which the whole weight of the body may be assumed to act, whatever may be the position of the body.

In simple language, the center of gravity is the point at which the entire weight of the body appears to act.

Real Life Meaning
  • A cricket bat balances at its C.G.
  • A ruler balances at its center if material is uniform
  • A truck with high C.G. is less stable and can topple more easily
  • A hanging object always comes to rest with its C.G. vertically below the point of suspension

3. Centroid of Area vs Centre of Gravity of Body

For a uniform thin lamina (thin plate of uniform density and thickness), the centroid and centre of gravity are at the same point.

⭐ Very Important: For a uniform lamina:
Centroid = Centre of Gravity
This is why many diploma problems use centroid formulas for uniform sections.

4. Why These Concepts Are Needed in Engineering

Engineering Importance
  • To locate the line of action of weight
  • To find bending behavior in beams
  • To determine stability of structures and vehicles
  • To design machine parts properly
  • To calculate moment of inertia in later subjects
  • To ensure proper balancing and support location

5. Coordinate Method for Centroid 🔥🔥

For composite sections, we use the coordinate method. If a figure is divided into several simple figures, then centroid coordinates are found using:

Formula for Centroid of Composite Area

\[\boxed{\bar{x} = \frac{\Sigma (a x)}{\Sigma a}}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{y} = \frac{\Sigma (a y)}{\Sigma a}}\]

Where:

  • \(a\) = area of each component part
  • \(x\) = x-coordinate of centroid of each part from reference axis
  • \(y\) = y-coordinate of centroid of each part from reference axis
  • \(\bar{x}\), \(\bar{y}\) = coordinates of centroid of whole section
Shortcut Memory Tip

Centroid formula is exactly like a weighted average.

Each area pulls the centroid toward itself according to its size.

6. Important Note About Negative Area

Cut-out Sections

If any part is removed from the section (hole or cut-out), then that area is taken as negative in centroid calculation.

That means:

  • Area of cut-out = negative
  • \(ax\) of cut-out = negative
  • \(ay\) of cut-out = negative

Centroid of a Uniform Triangular Lamina

1. Position of Centroid of Triangle 🔥

Centroid of Triangle

The centroid of a triangle lies at the intersection of its three medians.

It is located at a distance:

  • \(h/3\) from the base
  • 2h/3 from the opposite vertex

Centroid Position of Triangle

\[\boxed{\bar{y} = \frac{h}{3} \text{ from base}}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{y} = \frac{2h}{3} \text{ from vertex}}\]

Triangles are used frequently in composite sections, especially in:

  • Tapered plates
  • Triangular cut-outs
  • Trusses and gusset plates
  • Built-up fabricated sections

Centroid of Circle

The centroid of a complete circular lamina lies at its center.

Centroid of a Uniform Semi-circular Lamina

1. Position of Centroid of Semicircle 🔥🔥

Centroid of Semicircle

The centroid of a semicircular lamina lies on its axis of symmetry at a distance of:

\(\frac{4r}{3\pi}\) from the diameter.

Fig. 5.6: Centroid of semicircle at 4r/3π from the diameter along axis of symmetry

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Centroid Position of Semicircle

\[\boxed{\bar{y} = \frac{4r}{3\pi} \text{ from diameter}}\]

Numerically,

\[\boxed{\frac{4r}{3\pi} \approx 0.424r}\]

2. Area of Semicircle

Area of Semicircle

\[\boxed{A = \frac{1}{2}\pi r^2}\]

⭐ Exam Trap: Students often write centroid of semicircle at \(r/2\), which is wrong. The correct value is:
\(4r/3\pi\)

Centroid of a Quadrant of a Circle

1. Position of Centroid of Quadrant 🔥

Centroid of Quadrant

The centroid of a quadrant of a circle is located at equal distances from both bounding radii, and each distance is:

\(\frac{4r}{3\pi}\)

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Centroid Coordinates of Quadrant

\[\boxed{\bar{x} = \frac{4r}{3\pi}, \quad \bar{y} = \frac{4r}{3\pi}}\]

2. Area of Quadrant

Area of Quadrant of Circle

\[\boxed{A = \frac{1}{4}\pi r^2}\]

This shape is very commonly used in composite section problems involving cut corners, curved plates, and built-up sections.

Solved Examples on Basic Standard Figures

Example 5.1

Find the distance of centroid of a triangular lamina of height 12 cm from its base.

Solution

For a triangle, centroid lies at:

\[\bar{y} = \frac{h}{3}\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{12}{3} = 4 \text{ cm}\]

\[\boxed{\text{Centroid is } 4 \text{ cm from the base}}\]

Example 5.2

Find the centroid of a rectangle of width 80 mm and height 50 mm measured from one corner.

Solution

For a rectangle:

\[\bar{x} = \frac{b}{2} = \frac{80}{2} = 40 \text{ mm}\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{h}{2} = \frac{50}{2} = 25 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{(\bar{x}, \bar{y}) = (40 \text{ mm}, 25 \text{ mm})}\]

Example 5.3

Find the centroid of a semicircular lamina of radius 70 mm from its diameter.

Solution

For a semicircle:

\[\bar{y} = \frac{4r}{3\pi}\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{4 \times 70}{3 \times 3.1416} = \frac{280}{9.4248} = 29.71 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{\text{Centroid is } 29.71 \text{ mm from the diameter}}\]

Example 5.4

Find the centroid coordinates of a quadrant of radius 60 mm from its bounding axes.

Solution

For a quadrant:

\[\bar{x} = \bar{y} = \frac{4r}{3\pi}\]

\[\bar{x} = \bar{y} = \frac{4 \times 60}{3 \times 3.1416} = \frac{240}{9.4248} = 25.46 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{x} = \bar{y} = 25.46 \text{ mm}}\]

Introduction to Composite Sections

1. What is a Composite Section? 🔥

Definition: Composite Section

A composite section is a section made up of two or more simple geometrical shapes such as rectangles, triangles, circles, semicircles, etc.

Since most engineering sections like T-section, I-section, channel, angle, and built-up sections are not simple shapes, we divide them into standard shapes and use the centroid formula.

Fig. 5.8: Typical composite sections used in centroid calculation

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
General Method of Solving Composite Centroid Problems
  1. Divide the section into simple figures
  2. Choose convenient reference axes
  3. Find area of each simple figure
  4. Locate centroid of each simple figure from the chosen axes
  5. Prepare table with \(a\), \(ax\), \(ay\)
  6. Use:
    \(\bar{x} = \Sigma ax / \Sigma a\)
    \(\bar{y} = \Sigma ay / \Sigma a\)

T-section, I-section, angle section, channel section, Z-section, cut-out sections, and built-up sections.

Composite Sections — Full Method

1. General Formula for Composite Sections 🔥🔥

Composite Area Centroid Formula

\[\boxed{\bar{x} = \frac{\Sigma (a x)}{\Sigma a}}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{y} = \frac{\Sigma (a y)}{\Sigma a}}\]

Where:

  • \(a\) = area of each component part
  • \(x\) = x-coordinate of centroid of each part from reference Y-axis
  • \(y\) = y-coordinate of centroid of each part from reference X-axis
  • \(\bar{x}, \bar{y}\) = coordinates of centroid of whole section
Standard Procedure for Solving Composite Centroid Problems
  1. Draw the complete section clearly.
  2. Split it into simple shapes like rectangles, triangles, circles, semicircles, etc.
  3. Choose suitable reference axes:
    • Usually left edge as Y-axis
    • Usually bottom edge as X-axis
  4. Find area of each part.
  5. Locate centroid of each part from the selected axes.
  6. Prepare a table of \(a\), \(x\), \(y\), \(ax\), and \(ay\).
  7. Find \(\Sigma a\), \(\Sigma ax\), and \(\Sigma ay\).
  8. Calculate \(\bar{x}\) and \(\bar{y}\).
Common Mistakes
  • Wrong reference axis selection
  • Using wrong centroid of semicircle/quadrant
  • Forgetting to use negative area for holes/cut-outs
  • Using total dimension instead of local centroid distance
  • Arithmetic mistakes in \(ax\) and \(ay\)

2. Symmetry Rule — Important Shortcut 🔥

Symmetry Shortcut

If a section is symmetrical about a vertical axis, then centroid lies on that axis. So only \(\bar{y}\) needs to be calculated.

If a section is symmetrical about a horizontal axis, then centroid lies on that axis. So only \(\bar{x}\) needs to be calculated.

If a section is symmetrical about both axes, the centroid lies at the geometric center.

⭐ Exam Trick: Before starting calculation, always check symmetry. It can save a lot of time.

Centroid of T-Section

1. What is a T-Section?

T-Section

A T-section is a section shaped like the capital letter T, usually made of one flange and one web.

A T-section can be split into:

  • One rectangular flange
  • One rectangular web

Fig. 5.9: T-section divided into flange and web rectangles for centroid calculation

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

2. Symmetry of T-section

Most T-sections are symmetrical about the vertical axis. Therefore:

\[\boxed{\bar{x} = \text{center line of section}}\]

Only \(\bar{y}\) generally needs to be calculated.

3. Solved Example — T-Section

Example 5.5

Find the centroid of a T-section having:

  • Flange = 100 mm × 20 mm
  • Web = 20 mm × 80 mm

Take bottom of the section as reference X-axis.

Solution

Split the section into two rectangles:

  • Rectangle 1 = flange = \(100 \times 20\)
  • Rectangle 2 = web = \(20 \times 80\)
Areas:

\[a_1 = 100 \times 20 = 2000 \text{ mm}^2\]

\[a_2 = 20 \times 80 = 1600 \text{ mm}^2\]

Centroid distances from bottom:

Web is below flange.

Centroid of web from bottom:

\[y_2 = \frac{80}{2} = 40 \text{ mm}\]

Flange is on top of web, so centroid of flange from bottom:

\[y_1 = 80 + \frac{20}{2} = 90 \text{ mm}\]

Calculate \(ay\):

\[a_1y_1 = 2000 \times 90 = 180000\]

\[a_2y_2 = 1600 \times 40 = 64000\]

Use centroid formula:

\[\bar{y} = \frac{\Sigma ay}{\Sigma a}\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{180000 + 64000}{2000 + 1600}\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{244000}{3600} = 67.78 \text{ mm}\]

By symmetry,

\[\bar{x} = \frac{100}{2} = 50 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{x} = 50 \text{ mm},\quad \bar{y} = 67.78 \text{ mm from bottom}}\]

Centroid of Angle Sections

1. What is an Angle Section?

Angle Section

An angle section is an L-shaped section formed by two legs placed at right angles.

Angle sections may be:

  • Equal angle section — both legs equal
  • Unequal angle section — legs of different lengths

Fig. 5.10: Equal and unequal angle sections split into rectangles

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

2. How to Solve

Angle sections are usually divided into two rectangles. But the overlapping square at the corner gets counted twice, so:

⭐ Important: For angle sections:
Either divide carefully into two non-overlapping rectangles
or use:
horizontal rectangle + vertical rectangle − overlapping square

3. Solved Example — Equal Angle Section

Example 5.6

Find the centroid of an equal angle section of size 80 mm × 80 mm × 10 mm.

Solution

Take reference axes along outer edges.

Divide into:

  • Rectangle 1: vertical leg = \(80 \times 10\)
  • Rectangle 2: horizontal leg excluding overlap = \(70 \times 10\)
Areas:

\[a_1 = 80 \times 10 = 800 \text{ mm}^2\]

\[a_2 = 70 \times 10 = 700 \text{ mm}^2\]

Centroid coordinates:

Rectangle 1 centroid:

\[x_1 = 5,\quad y_1 = 40\]

Rectangle 2 centroid:

\[x_2 = 10 + \frac{70}{2} = 45,\quad y_2 = 5\]

Compute \(ax\) and \(ay\):

\[a_1x_1 = 800 \times 5 = 4000\]

\[a_2x_2 = 700 \times 45 = 31500\]

\[a_1y_1 = 800 \times 40 = 32000\]

\[a_2y_2 = 700 \times 5 = 3500\]

\[\Sigma a = 800 + 700 = 1500\]

\[\bar{x} = \frac{4000 + 31500}{1500} = \frac{35500}{1500} = 23.67 \text{ mm}\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{32000 + 3500}{1500} = \frac{35500}{1500} = 23.67 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{x} = \bar{y} = 23.67 \text{ mm}}\]

Why \( \bar{x} = \bar{y} \)?

Because this is an equal angle section and it is symmetrical about the 45° line, the centroid coordinates from both outer edges are equal.

Centroid of I-Sections

1. What is an I-Section?

I-Section

An I-section consists of two flanges and one vertical web, giving the shape of the letter I.

I-sections may be:

  • Equal I-section
  • Unequal I-section

Fig. 5.11: I-section divided into top flange, web, and bottom flange

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

2. Symmetry of Equal I-Section

If top and bottom flanges are equal and web is central, then the section is symmetrical about both vertical and horizontal center lines.

Then centroid lies at geometric center.

⭐ Shortcut: For equal I-sections with full symmetry, centroid is at the center of section. No lengthy calculation needed unless asked to show.

3. Solved Example — Equal I-Section

Example 5.7

An equal I-section has overall depth 150 mm, flange width 100 mm, flange thickness 20 mm, and web thickness 20 mm. Find the centroid.

Solution

This is an equal I-section symmetrical about both vertical and horizontal axes.

Horizontal centroid coordinate:

\[\bar{x} = \frac{100}{2} = 50 \text{ mm}\]

Vertical centroid coordinate:

\[\bar{y} = \frac{150}{2} = 75 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{(\bar{x}, \bar{y}) = (50 \text{ mm}, 75 \text{ mm})}\]

Solved Examples on Composite Sections

Example 5.8 — Channel Section

Problem

Find the centroid of a channel section of overall depth 120 mm, flange width 60 mm, flange thickness 10 mm, and web thickness 10 mm. Take left outer edge as Y-axis and bottom as X-axis.

Solution

Divide the channel into three rectangles:

  • Top flange = \(60 \times 10\)
  • Bottom flange = \(60 \times 10\)
  • Web = \(10 \times 100\) (between flanges)
Areas:

\[a_1 = 60 \times 10 = 600\]

\[a_2 = 60 \times 10 = 600\]

\[a_3 = 10 \times 100 = 1000\]

Centroid coordinates of each rectangle:

Bottom flange:

\[x_1 = 30,\quad y_1 = 5\]

Top flange:

\[x_2 = 30,\quad y_2 = 115\]

Web (on left side):

\[x_3 = 5,\quad y_3 = 10 + \frac{100}{2} = 60\]

Compute \(ax\) and \(ay\):

\[a_1x_1 = 600 \times 30 = 18000\]

\[a_2x_2 = 600 \times 30 = 18000\]

\[a_3x_3 = 1000 \times 5 = 5000\]

\[a_1y_1 = 600 \times 5 = 3000\]

\[a_2y_2 = 600 \times 115 = 69000\]

\[a_3y_3 = 1000 \times 60 = 60000\]

\[\Sigma a = 600 + 600 + 1000 = 2200\]

\[\Sigma ax = 18000 + 18000 + 5000 = 41000\]

\[\Sigma ay = 3000 + 69000 + 60000 = 132000\]

\[\bar{x} = \frac{41000}{2200} = 18.64 \text{ mm}\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{132000}{2200} = 60 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{x} = 18.64 \text{ mm},\quad \bar{y} = 60 \text{ mm}}\]

Observation

The section is symmetrical about the horizontal axis, so \(\bar{y} = 60\) mm comes naturally at mid-depth.

Example 5.9 — Rectangle with Circular Hole (Cut-out Section) 🔥

Problem

A rectangular plate of size 120 mm × 80 mm has a circular hole of diameter 40 mm at its center. Find the centroid of the remaining section.

Solution

The hole is at the center of the rectangle. So the remaining section is symmetrical about both axes.

Centroid of outer rectangle is at:

\[\bar{x} = \frac{120}{2} = 60 \text{ mm},\quad \bar{y} = \frac{80}{2} = 40 \text{ mm}\]

The circular hole is also centered at the same point.

Due to full symmetry, centroid of remaining area stays at the same point.

\[\boxed{(\bar{x}, \bar{y}) = (60 \text{ mm}, 40 \text{ mm})}\]

Example 5.10 — Rectangle with Semicircular Cut-out

Problem

A rectangular plate of width 100 mm and height 120 mm has a semicircular cut-out of radius 25 mm removed from the top center edge. Find the centroid of the remaining section from the bottom edge.

Solution

Since the cut-out is centrally placed, the section is symmetrical about vertical axis.

So only \(\bar{y}\) is needed.

Outer rectangle:

\[a_1 = 100 \times 120 = 12000 \text{ mm}^2\]

\[y_1 = \frac{120}{2} = 60 \text{ mm}\]

Semicircular cut-out:

\[a_2 = -\frac{1}{2}\pi r^2 = -\frac{1}{2}\pi (25)^2\]

\[a_2 = -981.75 \text{ mm}^2\]

The diameter of semicircle lies on top edge, so centroid from top edge:

\[\frac{4r}{3\pi} = \frac{4 \times 25}{3 \times 3.1416} = 10.61 \text{ mm}\]

Therefore centroid of semicircle from bottom edge:

\[y_2 = 120 - 10.61 = 109.39 \text{ mm}\]

Compute \(ay\):

\[a_1y_1 = 12000 \times 60 = 720000\]

\[a_2y_2 = -981.75 \times 109.39 = -107409.68\]

\[\Sigma a = 12000 - 981.75 = 11018.25\]

\[\Sigma ay = 720000 - 107409.68 = 612590.32\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{612590.32}{11018.25} = 55.60 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{y} = 55.60 \text{ mm from bottom}}\]

Also by symmetry:

\[\boxed{\bar{x} = 50 \text{ mm}}\]

Example 5.11 — Built-up Section

Problem

A built-up section is made by joining two rectangular plates:
Plate 1 = 120 mm × 20 mm
Plate 2 = 20 mm × 100 mm
The second plate is centrally joined below the first plate to form a T-section. Find centroid from bottom.

Solution

This is effectively a T-section.

\[a_1 = 120 \times 20 = 2400\]

\[a_2 = 20 \times 100 = 2000\]

Take bottom as reference.

Web centroid:

\[y_2 = \frac{100}{2} = 50\]

Flange centroid:

\[y_1 = 100 + \frac{20}{2} = 110\]

\[a_1y_1 = 2400 \times 110 = 264000\]

\[a_2y_2 = 2000 \times 50 = 100000\]

\[\Sigma a = 2400 + 2000 = 4400\]

\[\Sigma ay = 264000 + 100000 = 364000\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{364000}{4400} = 82.73 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{y} = 82.73 \text{ mm from bottom}}\]

Example 5.12 — Unequal Rectangular Composite Section

Problem

An L-shaped section is formed by removing a rectangle of 60 mm × 40 mm from the top-right corner of a larger rectangle of 100 mm × 80 mm. Find the centroid of the remaining section from the left and bottom edges.

Solution
Outer rectangle:

\[a_1 = 100 \times 80 = 8000\]

\[x_1 = 50,\quad y_1 = 40\]

Removed rectangle (cut-out):

\[a_2 = -(60 \times 40) = -2400\]

The cut-out lies at top-right corner, so:

\[x_2 = 100 - \frac{60}{2} = 70\]

\[y_2 = 80 - \frac{40}{2} = 60\]

\[a_1x_1 = 8000 \times 50 = 400000\]

\[a_2x_2 = -2400 \times 70 = -168000\]

\[a_1y_1 = 8000 \times 40 = 320000\]

\[a_2y_2 = -2400 \times 60 = -144000\]

\[\Sigma a = 8000 - 2400 = 5600\]

\[\Sigma ax = 400000 - 168000 = 232000\]

\[\Sigma ay = 320000 - 144000 = 176000\]

\[\bar{x} = \frac{232000}{5600} = 41.43 \text{ mm}\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{176000}{5600} = 31.43 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{x} = 41.43 \text{ mm},\quad \bar{y} = 31.43 \text{ mm}}\]

Centre of Gravity of Composite Solids

1. Concept of Composite Solids

Composite Solids

A composite solid is formed by joining two or more simple solids such as a cylinder and hemisphere, cube and cuboid, etc.

In this syllabus, only the concept is required clearly. Usually composite solids are made of not more than two simple solids.

2. Basic Principle

The centre of gravity of a composite solid lies closer to the part having greater volume/weight.

Principle

If the solids are made of the same material, then weight is proportional to volume. Hence, the C.G. can be found by replacing area with volume:

\[\bar{x} = \frac{\Sigma(Vx)}{\Sigma V}\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{\Sigma(Vy)}{\Sigma V}\]

\[\bar{z} = \frac{\Sigma(Vz)}{\Sigma V}\]

⭐ Diploma Note: Even if numerical problems are not required for composite solids, you should know that the method is exactly similar to composite centroid of areas — only area is replaced by volume.

3. Example Concepts

Examples of Composite Solids
  • Cylinder surmounted by a hemisphere
  • Cuboid with a cylinder placed on top
  • Two cylinders joined together
  • Cone attached to a cylinder

Fig. 5.20: Example of composite solid and location of combined center of gravity

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

4. Important Observation

Practical Understanding

If two solids are joined:

  • The combined C.G. lies on the line joining the individual C.G.s
  • It lies nearer to the heavier/larger-volume solid
  • If both are equal and symmetrically placed, the combined C.G. lies at the midpoint

Important Definitions

Centroid

The centroid of a plane figure is the point at which the whole area may be assumed to be concentrated.

Centre of Gravity

The centre of gravity of a body is the point through which the total weight of the body acts.

Centre of Mass

The centre of mass is the point through which the total mass of a body may be assumed to act.

Composite Section

A composite section is a section made up of two or more standard geometrical shapes.

Cut-out Section

A cut-out section is formed when a portion is removed from a larger area.

Built-up Section

A built-up section is made by joining two or more structural elements or plates.

Uniform Lamina

A uniform lamina is a thin plate having uniform thickness and uniform density throughout.

Symmetrical Section

A symmetrical section is a section whose shape is identical on both sides of the axis of symmetry.

Long Answer Questions

LAQ 5.1: Define centroid and centre of gravity. Distinguish between them. 🔥

Centroid: The centroid of a plane figure is the point at which the whole area may be assumed to be concentrated.

Centre of Gravity: The centre of gravity of a body is the point through which the total weight of the body acts.

Difference:

  • Centroid relates to area and geometry.
  • Centre of gravity relates to weight.
  • Centroid is used mainly for plane figures.
  • C.G. is used mainly for physical bodies.
  • For uniform lamina, both coincide.

LAQ 5.2: Explain the method of finding centroid of composite sections. 🔥🔥

To find centroid of a composite section:

  1. Divide the section into simple standard figures
  2. Select suitable reference axes
  3. Calculate area of each part
  4. Locate centroid coordinates of each part
  5. Prepare table of \(a, x, y, ax, ay\)
  6. Find \(\Sigma a, \Sigma ax, \Sigma ay\)
  7. Apply:
    \(\bar{x}=\Sigma ax/\Sigma a\)
    \(\bar{y}=\Sigma ay/\Sigma a\)

For holes or cut-outs, removed area is taken as negative.

LAQ 5.3: State the centroid positions of standard plane figures used in engineering mechanics. 🔥

Triangle: \(h/3\) from base, at intersection of medians

Rectangle: At center, \((b/2, h/2)\)

Circle: At center

Semicircle: \(4r/3\pi\) from diameter along axis of symmetry

Quadrant: \(4r/3\pi\) from both axes

LAQ 5.4: Explain the centroid of T-section with a suitable numerical procedure.

A T-section is divided into two rectangles — flange and web. Area and centroid distance of each rectangle from a reference axis are found. Then using:

\(\bar{y} = \Sigma ay/\Sigma a\)

If the T-section is vertically symmetric, \(\bar{x}\) lies on the center line. Thus only \(\bar{y}\) needs detailed calculation.

LAQ 5.5: Explain the concept of cut-out section and how centroid is found for it. 🔥

In a cut-out section, a part is removed from a larger section, such as a hole or notch. The removed portion is treated as negative area.

Method:

  1. Take the whole outer figure as positive area
  2. Take removed part as negative area
  3. Find centroid coordinates of each
  4. Use:
    \(\bar{x}=\Sigma ax/\Sigma a\)
    \(\bar{y}=\Sigma ay/\Sigma a\)

LAQ 5.6: State the centre of gravity positions of simple solids. 🔥

Cube: At geometrical center

Cuboid: At geometrical center

Cylinder: On axis at mid-height

Sphere: At center

These positions arise because the solids are uniform and symmetric.

P 5.1: Find the centroid from the base of a triangle of height 18 cm.

\[\bar{y} = \frac{h}{3} = \frac{18}{3} = 6 \text{ cm}\]

\[\boxed{6 \text{ cm from base}}\]

P 5.2: Find the centroid coordinates of a rectangle 120 mm × 60 mm.

\[\bar{x} = \frac{120}{2} = 60 \text{ mm}\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{60}{2} = 30 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{(60 \text{ mm}, 30 \text{ mm})}\]

P 5.3: Find the centroid of a semicircle of radius 42 mm from its diameter.

\[\bar{y} = \frac{4r}{3\pi} = \frac{4 \times 42}{3 \times 3.1416} = \frac{168}{9.4248} = 17.83 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{17.83 \text{ mm from diameter}}\]

P 5.4: Find the centroid coordinates of a quadrant of radius 35 mm.

\[\bar{x}=\bar{y}=\frac{4r}{3\pi}=\frac{4\times35}{3\times3.1416}=14.86 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{x}=\bar{y}=14.86 \text{ mm}}\]

P 5.5: A T-section has flange 80 × 10 mm and web 10 × 70 mm. Find centroid from bottom.

Areas:

\[a_1 = 80 \times 10 = 800\]

\[a_2 = 10 \times 70 = 700\]

Take bottom as reference.

Web centroid: \(y_2 = 35\)

Flange centroid: \(y_1 = 70 + 5 = 75\)

\[\Sigma ay = 800 \times 75 + 700 \times 35 = 60000 + 24500 = 84500\]

\[\Sigma a = 1500\]

\[\bar{y} = \frac{84500}{1500} = 56.33 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{\bar{y}=56.33 \text{ mm from bottom}}\]

P 5.6: Find the centroid of a circle of radius 25 mm.

The centroid of a circle lies at its center.

\[\boxed{\text{At center of the circle}}\]

P 5.7: Find the centroid of a rectangle 100 mm × 60 mm with a central circular hole of diameter 20 mm.

The hole is central, so symmetry about both axes remains unchanged.

\[\bar{x} = 100/2 = 50 \text{ mm}\]

\[\bar{y} = 60/2 = 30 \text{ mm}\]

\[\boxed{(\bar{x},\bar{y})=(50 \text{ mm},30 \text{ mm})}\]

P 5.8: State the C.G. coordinates of a cuboid of dimensions 120 mm × 80 mm × 60 mm from one corner.

For a cuboid, C.G. lies at geometrical center:

\[\left(\frac{120}{2}, \frac{80}{2}, \frac{60}{2}\right) = (60, 40, 30)\]

\[\boxed{(60 \text{ mm}, 40 \text{ mm}, 30 \text{ mm})}\]
