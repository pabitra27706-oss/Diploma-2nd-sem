Unit Overview

What This Unit Covers

This unit studies the condition of equilibrium of bodies under different force systems. In simple words, equilibrium means the body does not move from its present condition. It may remain at rest or continue in uniform motion, but there is no unbalanced force and no unbalanced turning effect.

You will learn:

  • Meaning and conditions of equilibrium
  • Equilibrium of coplanar concurrent force systems
  • Lami’s theorem
  • Triangle law and polygon law of equilibrium
  • Free Body Diagram (FBD)
  • Equilibrium of coplanar non-concurrent parallel forces
  • Types of beams, supports and loads
  • Calculation of beam reactions for simply supported and overhanging beams
Why This Unit Matters

This is one of the most practical units in Engineering Mechanics. Bridges, roofs, cranes, machine frames, electric poles, brackets, and beams all must remain in equilibrium. If reactions or forces are wrongly calculated, the structure can fail. This unit directly supports later topics like Strength of Materials, Structural Mechanics, and Machine Design.

Meaning of Equilibrium & Basic Conditions

1. What is Equilibrium? 🔥

Definition: Equilibrium

A body is said to be in equilibrium when the resultant of all the forces acting on it is zero and the resultant moment (turning effect) about any point is also zero.

In simple language, equilibrium means there is no unbalanced push/pull and no unbalanced turning effect.

What happens in equilibrium?
  • If the body is at rest, it remains at rest.
  • If the body is moving with uniform velocity, it continues to move uniformly.
  • There is no acceleration.
  • There is no tendency to rotate due to unbalanced moment.
⭐ Exam Point: For a body to be in complete equilibrium, both conditions must be satisfied:
1. Translational equilibrium → no unbalanced force
2. Rotational equilibrium → no unbalanced moment

2. Types of Equilibrium

Type Meaning Condition Example
Translational Equilibrium No net force along any direction \(\Sigma F_x = 0\), \(\Sigma F_y = 0\) A box resting on floor
Rotational Equilibrium No net turning effect \(\Sigma M = 0\) A beam staying balanced
Complete Equilibrium Both above satisfied together \(\Sigma F_x=0\), \(\Sigma F_y=0\), \(\Sigma M=0\) A loaded beam at rest

3. Conditions of Equilibrium for Coplanar Forces 🔥

General Equilibrium Conditions for a Coplanar Force System

\[\boxed{\Sigma F_x = 0}\]

\[\boxed{\Sigma F_y = 0}\]

\[\boxed{\Sigma M = 0}\]

Where:

  • \(\Sigma F_x\) = algebraic sum of all horizontal components
  • \(\Sigma F_y\) = algebraic sum of all vertical components
  • \(\Sigma M\) = algebraic sum of moments about any convenient point
When are all 3 equations needed?
  • For a particle or concurrent force system, usually \(\Sigma F_x = 0\) and \(\Sigma F_y = 0\) are enough because all forces pass through one point, so moment condition becomes automatically satisfied.
  • For a rigid body with non-concurrent forces, all three equations are needed.

4. Sign Convention

Standard Sign Convention
  • Rightward horizontal force = positive (+)
  • Leftward horizontal force = negative (−)
  • Upward vertical force = positive (+)
  • Downward vertical force = negative (−)
  • Clockwise moment = one sign
  • Anticlockwise moment = opposite sign

You may take clockwise positive and anticlockwise negative, or vice versa — but be consistent throughout the problem.

Equilibrium of Coplanar Concurrent Force System

1. What is a Coplanar Concurrent Force System?

Definition

A coplanar concurrent force system is a system in which all forces lie in the same plane and their lines of action pass through one common point.

Examples: Forces acting at a pin joint, hook, ring, or knot where several strings meet.

Fig. 3.2: Coplanar concurrent force system — all lines of action meeting at one point

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

2. Condition of Equilibrium for Concurrent Forces 🔥

Since all forces pass through one point, there is no separate turning effect to consider about that common point. Therefore, equilibrium can be checked by balancing the horizontal and vertical components.

Equilibrium Conditions for Coplanar Concurrent Forces

\[\boxed{\Sigma F_x = 0}\]

\[\boxed{\Sigma F_y = 0}\]

⭐ Important: For concurrent force problems, the main method is:
1. Resolve all forces into horizontal and vertical components
2. Apply \(\Sigma F_x = 0\)
3. Apply \(\Sigma F_y = 0\)
4. Solve for unknowns

3. Analytical Method for Concurrent Equilibrium

Step-by-step Method
  1. Draw the figure clearly and show all forces.
  2. Assume unknown directions if not given.
  3. Resolve each force into X and Y components.
  4. Use correct signs for right/left and up/down directions.
  5. Apply \(\Sigma F_x = 0\)
  6. Apply \(\Sigma F_y = 0\)
  7. Find the unknown force(s) or angle(s).

4. Example Situation

Suppose three forces act at a point and the body is in equilibrium:

  • Tension in rope 1 = T1
  • Tension in rope 2 = T2
  • Weight = W downward

Then:
\(\Sigma F_x = 0\) → horizontal components balance
\(\Sigma F_y = 0\) → vertical components balance

6. Solved Example — Basic Concurrent Equilibrium

Example 3.1

Two forces of 100 N and 80 N act on a point at angles 0° and 180° respectively along the same horizontal line. What additional vertical force is needed to keep the body in equilibrium?

Solution

Given: One force = 100 N rightward, another = 80 N leftward.

Horizontal resultant = \(100 - 80 = 20\) N rightward.

A vertical force cannot balance a horizontal resultant.

So no vertical force alone can produce equilibrium.

To keep equilibrium, an additional force of 20 N leftward is required.

\[\boxed{\text{Required equilibrant} = 20 \text{ N leftward}}\]

Concept Check

This example teaches an important idea: the equilibrant must be equal and opposite to the resultant. Direction matters.

7. Solved Example — Using \(\Sigma F_x = 0\) and \(\Sigma F_y = 0\)

Example 3.2

A load of 200 N is suspended by two strings. One string makes 30° with the horizontal and the other makes 60° with the horizontal. Find the tensions T1 and T2 in the two strings.

Solution

Fig. 3.4: Load suspended by two strings making 30° and 60° with horizontal

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Let T1 be the tension in the 30° string and T2 in the 60° string.

Apply horizontal equilibrium:

\[\Sigma F_x = 0\]

Rightward component = Leftward component

\[T_1 \cos 30^\circ = T_2 \cos 60^\circ\]

\[T_1 (0.866) = T_2 (0.5)\]

\[0.866T_1 = 0.5T_2\]

\[T_2 = 1.732T_1\]

Apply vertical equilibrium:

\[\Sigma F_y = 0\]

Upward components = downward force

\[T_1 \sin 30^\circ + T_2 \sin 60^\circ = 200\]

\[T_1(0.5) + T_2(0.866) = 200\]

Substitute \(T_2 = 1.732T_1\):

\[0.5T_1 + 0.866(1.732T_1) = 200\]

\[0.5T_1 + 1.5T_1 = 200\]

\[2T_1 = 200\]

\[T_1 = 100 \text{ N}\]

\[T_2 = 1.732 \times 100 = 173.2 \text{ N}\]

\[\boxed{T_1 = 100 \text{ N}, \quad T_2 = 173.2 \text{ N}}\]

Lami’s Theorem

1. Statement of Lami’s Theorem 🔥🔥

Lami’s Theorem

If a body is in equilibrium under the action of three coplanar, concurrent and non-collinear forces, then each force is proportional to the sine of the angle between the other two forces.

Formula: Lami’s Theorem

\[\boxed{\frac{F_1}{\sin \alpha} = \frac{F_2}{\sin \beta} = \frac{F_3}{\sin \gamma}}\]

Where:

  • \(F_1\), \(F_2\), \(F_3\) are the three forces
  • \(\alpha\) is the angle between \(F_2\) and \(F_3\)
  • \(\beta\) is the angle between \(F_3\) and \(F_1\)
  • \(\gamma\) is the angle between \(F_1\) and \(F_2\)
🔥 Very Important: In Lami’s theorem, the angle used in the denominator is the angle between the other two forces, not the angle opposite to itself in the ordinary geometry sense. Students often make mistakes here.

2. Conditions for Applying Lami’s Theorem

Lami’s theorem can be used only when:
  1. Exactly three forces act on the body
  2. The three forces are coplanar
  3. The three forces are concurrent
  4. The body is in equilibrium
  5. The forces are non-parallel / non-collinear
When NOT to Use Lami’s Theorem
  • If more than 3 forces act
  • If forces are not concurrent
  • If forces are not in equilibrium
  • If there are parallel force systems like beam load problems

3. Why Lami’s Theorem Works

If three concurrent forces keep a body in equilibrium, then their vector polygon forms a closed triangle. From the triangle of forces and applying the sine rule, we get Lami’s theorem.

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

4. Understanding the Angles in Lami’s Theorem

Smart Trick

Suppose the three forces are \(P\), \(Q\), and \(R\).

  • Write force \(P\) over sine of angle between Q and R
  • Write force \(Q\) over sine of angle between R and P
  • Write force \(R\) over sine of angle between P and Q

This is the safest way to avoid confusion.

5. Standard Applications of Lami’s Theorem

Engineering Uses
  • Load supported by two inclined strings
  • Ring or hook in equilibrium under 3 forces
  • Ladder problems (in simplified 3-force form)
  • Crane hook load support
  • Suspended electric cable support point

6. Solved Example — Basic Lami’s Theorem

Example 3.3

A body is in equilibrium under three forces of which two are 100 N and 150 N. The angle between these two forces is 120°. Find the third force using Lami’s theorem.

Solution

Let the third force be \(F_3\).

The angle between 100 N and 150 N is 120°.

In equilibrium under three forces, the third force will be equal and opposite to the resultant of the other two forces. But since this is a Lami’s theorem section, we relate using force triangle understanding.

For direct result, the third force corresponds to the angle between the first two forces.

Use resultant of 100 N and 150 N first:

\[R = \sqrt{100^2 + 150^2 + 2(100)(150)\cos 120^\circ}\]

\[R = \sqrt{10000 + 22500 + 30000(-0.5)}\]

\[R = \sqrt{32500 - 15000} = \sqrt{17500}\]

\[R = 132.29 \text{ N}\]

Therefore, for equilibrium, the third force must be equal and opposite to this resultant.

\[\boxed{F_3 = 132.29 \text{ N}}\]

Exam Note: Some Lami problems are easier by direct resolution or resultant method. Use the method that is simplest and most accurate.

7. Solved Example — Proper 3-force Lami Problem

Example 3.4

A load of 500 N is suspended by two strings making angles of 30° and 60° with the horizontal. Find the tensions in the strings using Lami’s theorem.

Solution

Fig. 3.6: Load supported by two strings — application of Lami’s theorem

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Let the tensions be \(T_1\) and \(T_2\).

The load \(W = 500\) N acts vertically downward.

Angle between \(T_1\) and \(W\) = \(90^\circ + 30^\circ = 120^\circ\)

Angle between \(T_2\) and \(W\) = \(90^\circ + 60^\circ = 150^\circ\)

Angle between \(T_1\) and \(T_2\) = \(180^\circ - (30^\circ + 60^\circ) = 90^\circ\)

Apply Lami’s theorem:

\[\frac{T_1}{\sin 150^\circ} = \frac{T_2}{\sin 120^\circ} = \frac{500}{\sin 90^\circ}\]

For \(T_1\):

\[\frac{T_1}{\sin 150^\circ} = \frac{500}{1}\]

\[T_1 = 500 \times \sin 150^\circ = 500 \times 0.5\]

\[T_1 = 250 \text{ N}\]

For \(T_2\):

\[\frac{T_2}{\sin 120^\circ} = \frac{500}{1}\]

\[T_2 = 500 \times \sin 120^\circ = 500 \times 0.866\]

\[T_2 = 433 \text{ N}\]

\[\boxed{T_1 = 250 \text{ N}, \quad T_2 = 433 \text{ N}}\]

Triangle Law & Polygon Law of Equilibrium

1. Triangle Law of Equilibrium 🔥

Triangle Law of Equilibrium

If three coplanar concurrent forces acting on a body are in equilibrium, then they can be represented in magnitude and direction by the three sides of a triangle taken in order.

This is just the equilibrium form of the triangle law of forces. If the three forces form a closed triangle, their resultant is zero, so the body is in equilibrium.

Meaning in Simple Language

If you draw the three forces head-to-tail one after another, and the last point comes back to the starting point, then the triangle closes. That means the total resultant is zero, so equilibrium exists.

Fig. 3.7: Triangle law of equilibrium — three forces forming a closed triangle

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
⭐ Important Relation: If three forces are in equilibrium:
Resultant of any two forces = equal and opposite to the third force.

2. Mathematical Interpretation

Suppose three forces \(P\), \(Q\), and \(R\) act at a point and keep the body in equilibrium.

Then their vector sum is zero:

Vector Condition of Equilibrium

\[\boxed{\vec{P} + \vec{Q} + \vec{R} = 0}\]

That means when drawn head-to-tail, the force polygon closes into a triangle.

3. Use of Triangle Law in Problems

When to Use
  • When exactly three forces act
  • When the body is in equilibrium
  • When graphical or geometric approach is easier
  • When force triangle angles are easier to identify than components

4. Polygon Law of Equilibrium 🔥

Polygon Law of Equilibrium

If a number of coplanar concurrent forces acting on a body are in equilibrium, then they can be represented in magnitude and direction by the sides of a polygon taken in order.

In simple language: if many forces act at a point and you draw them one after another head-to-tail, and the shape closes, then the body is in equilibrium.

Fig. 3.8: Polygon law of equilibrium — several forces forming a closed force polygon

6. Solved Example — Triangle Law of Equilibrium

Example 3.5

Three forces acting at a point are in equilibrium. Two of them are 300 N and 400 N and the angle between them is 90°. Find the third force.

Solution

Since the forces are in equilibrium, the third force must be equal and opposite to the resultant of the first two forces.

Resultant of 300 N and 400 N at 90°:

\[R = \sqrt{300^2 + 400^2} = \sqrt{90000 + 160000} = \sqrt{250000}\]

\[R = 500 \text{ N}\]

Therefore the third force required for equilibrium is 500 N in the opposite direction.

\[\boxed{\text{Third force} = 500 \text{ N}}\]

7. Solved Example — Polygon Equilibrium Idea

Example 3.6

Four forces act at a point in sequence as 3 N east, 4 N north, 3 N west, and 4 N south. Is the body in equilibrium?

Solution

Horizontal forces: 3 N east and 3 N west cancel each other.

Vertical forces: 4 N north and 4 N south cancel each other.

The force polygon closes into a rectangle, so resultant is zero.

\[\boxed{\text{Yes, the body is in equilibrium}}\]

Free Body Diagram (FBD)

1. What is a Free Body Diagram? 🔥🔥

Definition: Free Body Diagram

A Free Body Diagram (FBD) is a diagram of a body isolated from its surroundings, showing all external forces, reactions, and moments acting on it.

In simple words, FBD means: separate the body from everything touching it and then show all forces acting on it.

🔥 Most Important Skill in Mechanics: If you can draw the correct FBD, most problems become easy. If your FBD is wrong, the whole answer becomes wrong.

2. Why Free Body Diagram is Necessary

Importance of FBD
  • Helps identify all forces acting on the body
  • Shows unknown reactions clearly
  • Makes equilibrium equations easy to write
  • Prevents missing any force
  • Very useful in beams, ladders, blocks, frames, and pulley systems

3. Steps to Draw a Free Body Diagram

Step-by-step Procedure
  1. Select the body or part of structure to be analyzed.
  2. Imagine the body cut free from all supports, strings, surfaces, and connections.
  3. Replace each support/contact by its reaction force(s).
  4. Show all applied loads such as weight, point load, UDL, pull, push, etc.
  5. Mark dimensions and angles if needed.
  6. Choose reference axes if resolution of forces is required.

4. Common Forces to Show in FBD

Situation Force / Reaction to Show Direction
Body under gravityWeight \(W\)Vertically downward through CG
String / ropeTension \(T\)Along string, away from body
Smooth surfaceNormal reaction \(R\)Perpendicular to surface
Rough surfaceNormal reaction + frictionNormal + along surface
Roller supportOne reactionNormal to supporting surface
Hinge / pin supportTwo componentsHorizontal and vertical
Fixed supportHorizontal, vertical, and momentAll restraining actions

7. Common Mistakes in FBD

Common Exam Mistakes
  • Forgetting to show self-weight or given load
  • Showing force in wrong direction
  • Not replacing supports with proper reactions
  • Showing both support and reaction together after isolation
  • Missing dimensions needed for moment calculation
  • Using wrong reaction type at roller / hinge / fixed support

8. Solved Example — FBD Concept

Example 3.7

A block rests on a smooth horizontal floor and is pulled by a force \(P\) inclined at angle \(\theta\) above the horizontal. What forces should appear in its FBD?

Solution

The FBD of the block will contain:

  1. Weight \(W\) of the block acting vertically downward
  2. Normal reaction \(R\) from floor acting vertically upward
  3. Pulling force \(P\) acting at angle \(\theta\) above horizontal

Since the floor is smooth, no friction force is present.

\[\boxed{\text{FBD includes } W, R, \text{ and } P}\]

Equilibrium of Coplanar Non-concurrent Parallel Forces

1. Meaning of Non-concurrent Parallel Force System 🔥

Definition

A coplanar non-concurrent parallel force system is a system in which all forces lie in the same plane, are parallel to each other, but their lines of action do not pass through a common point.

This type of force system is very common in beams, ladders, brackets, and loaded structures.

2.1 Like Parallel Forces

Like Parallel Forces

Parallel forces acting in the same direction are called like parallel forces.

Example: Several downward loads acting on a beam.

Unlike Parallel Forces

Parallel forces acting in opposite directions are called unlike parallel forces.

Example: Upward support reactions and downward external loads on a beam.

3. Conditions of Equilibrium for Coplanar Parallel Forces 🔥🔥

Since all forces are parallel, only one force equation and one moment equation are generally needed.

Equilibrium Conditions for Coplanar Parallel Force System

\[\boxed{\Sigma F_y = 0}\]

\[\boxed{\Sigma M = 0}\]

Usually, forces are vertical. So:

  • Sum of upward forces = sum of downward forces
  • Sum of clockwise moments = sum of anticlockwise moments
⭐ Most Important for Beam Problems:
\(\Sigma V = 0\) → total upward reaction = total downward load
\(\Sigma M = 0\) → moment balance about any support or point

4. Resultant of Like Parallel Forces

Like Parallel Forces Resultant

If two like parallel forces \(P\) and \(Q\) act at distances apart, their resultant:

  • Acts in the same direction as the forces
  • Magnitude = \(P + Q\)
  • Its position is found by taking moments

5. Resultant of Unlike Parallel Forces

Unlike Parallel Forces Resultant

If two unlike parallel forces \(P\) and \(Q\) act:

  • Magnitude of resultant = difference of forces = \(|P - Q|\)
  • Direction of resultant = direction of the larger force
  • Position is found from moment balance

6. Method to Solve Parallel Force Equilibrium Problems

Standard Procedure
  1. Draw the FBD properly
  2. Show all vertical loads and vertical reactions
  3. Apply \(\Sigma F_y = 0\)
  4. Take moment about one point/support
  5. Apply \(\Sigma M = 0\)
  6. Solve for unknown reactions or loads

7. Solved Example — Parallel Force Equilibrium

Example 3.8

Two downward parallel forces of 40 N and 60 N act at points 2 m apart on a rigid bar. Find the magnitude and position of the resultant from the 40 N force.

Solution

Since both forces act downward, they are like parallel forces.

Magnitude of resultant:

\[R = 40 + 60 = 100 \text{ N}\]

Let the resultant act at distance \(x\) from the 40 N force.

Take moments about the 40 N force:

Moment of 60 N force = Moment of resultant

\[60 \times 2 = 100 \times x\]

\[120 = 100x\]

\[x = 1.2 \text{ m}\]

\[\boxed{R = 100 \text{ N downward, at } 1.2 \text{ m from the 40 N force}}\]

8. Solved Example — Unlike Parallel Forces

Example 3.9

Two parallel forces of 80 N upward and 50 N downward act 3 m apart. Find their resultant.

Solution

These are unlike parallel forces.

Resultant magnitude:

\[R = 80 - 50 = 30 \text{ N}\]

Direction of resultant is in the direction of the larger force, i.e., upward.

Position can be found by moments, but for unlike forces the resultant may lie outside the two forces.

Taking moments about 80 N force:

\[50 \times 3 = 30 \times x\]

\[150 = 30x\]

\[x = 5 \text{ m}\]

\[\boxed{R = 30 \text{ N upward}}\]

The line of action lies outside the two forces, at 5 m from the 80 N force on the side away from the 50 N force.

Types of Beams, Supports and Loads

1. What is a Beam? 🔥

Definition: Beam

A beam is a long structural member designed to carry loads acting transverse (generally perpendicular) to its length.

Beams carry loads from slabs, roofs, floors, machines, walls, etc., and transfer them to supports such as columns or walls.

Practical Examples of Beams
  • Roof beam in a house
  • Bridge girder
  • Lintel over door or window
  • Railway sleeper type support member
  • Crane runway beam
  • Machine support frame member

2. Main Types of Beams 🔥

2.1 Simply Supported Beam

A beam supported at both ends, usually one end hinged and the other on roller support.

Used in exam most frequently.

2.2 Cantilever Beam

A beam fixed at one end and free at the other end.

2.3 Overhanging Beam

A simply supported beam with one or both ends extending beyond the supports.

2.4 Fixed Beam

A beam fixed at both ends.

2.5 Continuous Beam

A beam resting on more than two supports.

4. Types of Supports 🔥🔥

4.1 Roller Support

Roller Support

A roller support allows horizontal movement and rotation, but resists motion perpendicular to the supporting surface. Therefore it gives only one reaction.

For a horizontal beam on a horizontal roller support, the reaction is usually vertical upward.

4.2 Hinged / Pin Support

Hinged (Pin) Support

A hinged or pin support prevents movement in both horizontal and vertical directions, but allows rotation. Therefore it gives two reaction components: horizontal and vertical.

4.3 Fixed Support

Fixed Support

A fixed support prevents horizontal movement, vertical movement, and rotation. Therefore it gives three reactions: horizontal reaction, vertical reaction, and fixing moment.

4.4 Simple Support

Simple Support

In many diploma-level beam problems, a simple support means the beam is just resting on a support without rigid fixing. Practically, beam problems usually model the two supports as one hinged and one roller.

6. Types of Loads on Beams 🔥🔥

6.1 Point Load (Concentrated Load)

Point Load

A point load is a load assumed to act at a single point on the beam.

Example: Wheel load on bridge, machine load at one point, hanging load.

6.2 Uniformly Distributed Load (UDL)

Uniformly Distributed Load (UDL)

A uniformly distributed load is a load spread uniformly over a length of the beam at a constant rate, usually expressed in N/m or kN/m.

Example: Brick wall on beam, floor load, self-weight distributed along beam.

Equivalent Point Load of a UDL

\[\boxed{W = w \times L}\]

Where:

  • \(W\) = total load due to UDL
  • \(w\) = intensity of UDL (kN/m or N/m)
  • \(L\) = length over which UDL acts

This resultant acts at the midpoint of the loaded length.

6.3 Combination of Loads

In many beam problems, both point loads and UDL act together. Then:

  • Convert UDL into equivalent point load
  • Keep point loads as they are
  • Use total load and moments to find reactions

8. Solved Example — Equivalent Point Load of UDL

Example 3.10

A beam carries a UDL of 5 kN/m over a length of 4 m. Find the equivalent concentrated load and its point of action.

Solution

Use formula:

\[W = wL = 5 \times 4 = 20 \text{ kN}\]

This equivalent point load acts at the midpoint of the 4 m loaded length.

Midpoint = \(4/2 = 2\) m from either end of the UDL region.

\[\boxed{W = 20 \text{ kN, acting at the center of the 4 m loaded length}}\]

9. Beam Terminology Useful in Reactions

Common Terms
  • Span: Distance between two supports
  • Overhang: Portion of beam extending beyond a support
  • Reaction: Force developed by support on beam
  • Load intensity: Load per unit length for UDL
  • Resultant load: Single equivalent load of distributed loading

Beam Reactions for Simply Supported and Overhanging Beams

1. What is a Support Reaction? 🔥

Definition: Support Reaction

A support reaction is the force or moment developed at a support in order to keep the beam in equilibrium under the action of applied loads.

Whenever a beam carries external loads, the supports push back on the beam. These upward or horizontal balancing forces are called reactions.

⭐ Core Idea: A beam under load remains at rest only if:
\(\Sigma F_y = 0\) and \(\Sigma M = 0\)

2. Standard Beam Reaction Method 🔥🔥

Step-by-Step Procedure for Beam Reaction Problems
  1. Draw the beam neatly with all dimensions.
  2. Replace supports by proper reaction forces:
    • Roller → one vertical reaction
    • Hinge/pin → vertical and horizontal reaction (if needed)
  3. Convert every UDL into equivalent point load:
    \(W = wL\), acting at the midpoint of loaded length
  4. Apply vertical force equilibrium:
    \(\Sigma F_y = 0\)
  5. Take moments about one support:
    \(\Sigma M = 0\)
  6. Find one reaction from moment equation, then the other from force equation.
  7. Check the result by substituting back.

3. Simply Supported Beam with Point Load

Fig. 3.17: Simply supported beam with a point load and end reactions

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Consider a simply supported beam AB carrying a point load \(W\) at a distance \(a\) from support A and \(b\) from support B. Total span \(L = a+b\).

Reactions for a Single Point Load

\[\boxed{R_A = \frac{Wb}{L}}\]

\[\boxed{R_B = \frac{Wa}{L}}\]

Where:

  • \(R_A\) = reaction at support A
  • \(R_B\) = reaction at support B
  • \(W\) = point load
  • \(a\) = distance of load from A
  • \(b\) = distance of load from B
  • \(L = a+b\) = span
Memory Trick

The reaction at one support depends on the distance of the load from the other support.

  • \(R_A\) uses distance from B
  • \(R_B\) uses distance from A

\[W = wL\]

It acts at the center of the beam.

Reactions for Full-span UDL

\[\boxed{R_A = R_B = \frac{wL}{2}}\]

Because the load is symmetric, both reactions are equal.

7. Solved Example — Simply Supported Beam with One Point Load

Example 3.11

A simply supported beam of span 6 m carries a point load of 12 kN at a distance of 2 m from support A. Find the reactions at supports A and B.

Solution

Given: Span AB = 6 m, load \(W = 12\) kN, load distance from A = 2 m, so from B = 4 m.

Apply vertical force equilibrium:

\[\Sigma F_y = 0\]

\[R_A + R_B = 12\]

Take moment about A:

\[R_B \times 6 - 12 \times 2 = 0\]

\[6R_B = 24\]

\[R_B = 4 \text{ kN}\]

From \(\Sigma F_y = 0\):

\[R_A + 4 = 12\]

\[R_A = 8 \text{ kN}\]

\[\boxed{R_A = 8 \text{ kN}, \quad R_B = 4 \text{ kN}}\]

8. Solved Example — Simply Supported Beam with UDL

Example 3.12

A simply supported beam of span 5 m carries a UDL of 4 kN/m over the entire span. Find the support reactions.

Solution

Equivalent point load of UDL:

\[W = wL = 4 \times 5 = 20 \text{ kN}\]

This acts at the center of span, i.e., 2.5 m from each support.

By symmetry, reactions are equal:

\[R_A = R_B = \frac{20}{2} = 10 \text{ kN}\]

\[\boxed{R_A = 10 \text{ kN}, \quad R_B = 10 \text{ kN}}\]

9. Solved Example — Simply Supported Beam with Point Load and UDL

Example 3.13

A simply supported beam of span 8 m carries a point load of 10 kN at 2 m from A and a UDL of 3 kN/m over a length of 4 m starting from A. Find reactions at A and B.

Solution

Fig. 3.21: Beam with one point load and partial UDL

🔍 Click to view on Google Images

Equivalent point load of UDL:

\[W_{UDL} = wL = 3 \times 4 = 12 \text{ kN}\]

This acts at the midpoint of the 4 m loaded region, i.e., 2 m from A.

Total downward load:

\[10 + 12 = 22 \text{ kN}\]

So:

\[R_A + R_B = 22\]

Take moments about A:

\[R_B \times 8 - 10 \times 2 - 12 \times 2 = 0\]

\[8R_B - 20 - 24 = 0\]

\[8R_B = 44\]

\[R_B = 5.5 \text{ kN}\]

\[R_A = 22 - 5.5 = 16.5 \text{ kN}\]

\[\boxed{R_A = 16.5 \text{ kN}, \quad R_B = 5.5 \text{ kN}}\]

10. Solved Example — Overhanging Beam

Example 3.14

A beam AB of total length 7 m is supported at A and B, where the distance between supports is 5 m. It overhangs 2 m beyond B. A point load of 6 kN acts at the free overhanging end. Find the support reactions.

Solution

Let support A be at the left end and support B be 5 m from A. Overhang beyond B = 2 m.

Vertical force equilibrium:

\[R_A + R_B = 6\]

Take moments about A:

The 6 kN load acts at 7 m from A.

\[R_B \times 5 - 6 \times 7 = 0\]

\[5R_B = 42\]

\[R_B = 8.4 \text{ kN}\]

\[R_A + 8.4 = 6\]

\[R_A = -2.4 \text{ kN}\]

The negative sign means the actual reaction at A acts downward, opposite to the assumed upward direction.

\[\boxed{R_A = 2.4 \text{ kN downward}, \quad R_B = 8.4 \text{ kN upward}}\]

⭐ Important Concept: In overhanging beams, one support reaction can become negative. That means the actual direction is opposite to the assumed direction.

Additional Solved Numerical Problems

Example 3.15 — Lami’s Theorem

A small ring is in equilibrium under three forces: a weight of 300 N downward and two string tensions. One string makes 45° with horizontal and the other 30° with horizontal. Find the tensions.

Solution

Let tensions be \(T_1\) and \(T_2\).

Angle between \(T_1\) and weight = \(90+45=135^\circ\)

Angle between \(T_2\) and weight = \(90+30=120^\circ\)

Angle between \(T_1\) and \(T_2\) = \(180-(45+30)=105^\circ\)

\[\frac{T_1}{\sin 120^\circ} = \frac{T_2}{\sin 135^\circ} = \frac{300}{\sin 105^\circ}\]

For \(T_1\):

\[T_1 = \frac{300\sin120^\circ}{\sin105^\circ} = \frac{300(0.866)}{0.966} = 268.94 \text{ N}\]

For \(T_2\):

\[T_2 = \frac{300\sin135^\circ}{\sin105^\circ} = \frac{300(0.707)}{0.966} = 219.57 \text{ N}\]

\[\boxed{T_1 = 268.94 \text{ N}, \quad T_2 = 219.57 \text{ N}}\]

Example 3.16 — Support Reactions of a Symmetric Beam

A simply supported beam of span 10 m carries two equal point loads of 8 kN each, placed 2 m from each end. Find reactions.

Solution

Total load = \(8 + 8 = 16\) kN

Since the loading is symmetric, reactions are equal:

\[R_A = R_B = 16/2 = 8 \text{ kN}\]

\[\boxed{R_A = 8 \text{ kN}, \quad R_B = 8 \text{ kN}}\]

Example 3.17 — Partial UDL Reaction Problem

A simply supported beam of span 6 m carries a UDL of 2 kN/m over the left 3 m only. Find reactions.

Solution

Equivalent load of UDL:

\[W = 2 \times 3 = 6 \text{ kN}\]

This acts at midpoint of loaded 3 m region = 1.5 m from A.

\[R_A + R_B = 6\]

Taking moments about A:

\[R_B \times 6 - 6 \times 1.5 = 0\]

\[6R_B = 9\]

\[R_B = 1.5 \text{ kN}\]

\[R_A = 6 - 1.5 = 4.5 \text{ kN}\]

\[\boxed{R_A = 4.5 \text{ kN}, \quad R_B = 1.5 \text{ kN}}\]

Important Definitions

Equilibrium

A body is in equilibrium when the resultant of all forces and resultant moment on it are zero.

Concurrent Forces

Forces whose lines of action pass through one common point are called concurrent forces.

Non-concurrent Forces

Forces whose lines of action do not pass through one common point are called non-concurrent forces.

Like Parallel Forces

Parallel forces acting in the same direction are called like parallel forces.

Unlike Parallel Forces

Parallel forces acting in opposite directions are called unlike parallel forces.

Free Body Diagram

A free body diagram is a diagram of a body isolated from surroundings showing all external forces and reactions acting on it.

Beam

A beam is a structural member designed to carry transverse loads.

Roller Support

A support that provides only one reaction normal to the supporting surface and allows rotation and horizontal movement.

Hinged Support

A support that provides horizontal and vertical reactions but allows rotation.

Fixed Support

A support that prevents horizontal movement, vertical movement and rotation, giving two force reactions and one moment reaction.

Point Load

A load assumed to act at a single point of a beam.

Uniformly Distributed Load

A load spread uniformly over a certain length of beam at a constant rate per unit length.

Overhang

The portion of a beam extending beyond a support is called overhang.

Support Reaction

The force or moment developed at a support to maintain equilibrium of the loaded beam is called support reaction.

Long Answer Questions

LAQ 3.1: Explain equilibrium and state the conditions of equilibrium for concurrent and non-concurrent coplanar force systems. 🔥

Equilibrium is the condition when the body remains at rest or moves with uniform velocity because the resultant force and resultant moment are zero.

For concurrent coplanar forces:

\(\Sigma F_x = 0\), \(\Sigma F_y = 0\)

Because all forces meet at a common point, the turning effect about that common point is generally not considered separately.

For non-concurrent coplanar forces:

\(\Sigma F_x = 0\), \(\Sigma F_y = 0\), \(\Sigma M = 0\)

Here the lines of action do not pass through one point, so moment balance is also necessary.

LAQ 3.2: State and explain Lami’s theorem with condition of applicability. 🔥

Lami’s theorem states that if a body is in equilibrium under the action of three coplanar concurrent forces, then each force is proportional to the sine of the angle between the other two forces.

\[\frac{F_1}{\sin\alpha} = \frac{F_2}{\sin\beta} = \frac{F_3}{\sin\gamma}\]

Applicability:

  • Exactly 3 forces
  • Forces must be coplanar
  • Forces must be concurrent
  • Body must be in equilibrium

It is useful in string, ring, hook, and suspended load problems.

LAQ 3.3: What is a Free Body Diagram? Explain the procedure of drawing FBD with examples. 🔥

A free body diagram is a diagram of a body isolated from surroundings showing all external forces, reactions, and moments acting on it.

Procedure:

  1. Select the body to isolate
  2. Cut it free from all supports/connections
  3. Replace supports by their reaction forces
  4. Show all applied loads and weight
  5. Mark dimensions and directions clearly

Examples: suspended load, beam with supports, block on rough surface, ladder against wall.

LAQ 3.4: Explain types of supports with reactions produced by each. 🔥

Roller Support: gives one reaction normal to the surface. Allows horizontal movement and rotation.

Hinged/Pin Support: gives two reactions, horizontal and vertical. Prevents translation but allows rotation.

Fixed Support: gives three reactions — horizontal, vertical, and moment. Prevents translation and rotation.

These support reactions are used to analyze beams and structures in equilibrium.

LAQ 3.5: Explain beam reaction calculation for a simply supported beam carrying point load and UDL. 🔥🔥

To calculate beam reactions:

  1. Draw the beam and free body diagram
  2. Replace supports by reactions \(R_A\) and \(R_B\)
  3. Convert UDL into equivalent point load \(W=wL\) acting at midpoint
  4. Apply \(\Sigma F_y = 0\): \(R_A + R_B = \text{total downward load}\)
  5. Take moments about one support to find the other reaction
  6. Substitute back to find remaining reaction

This method is used for point loads, UDL, and combination loading on simply supported beams.

LAQ 3.6: Explain the difference between simply supported beam, cantilever beam, and overhanging beam.

Simply Supported Beam: supported at two points, usually one hinge and one roller. No overhang.

Cantilever Beam: fixed at one end and free at the other end.

Overhanging Beam: beam supported at two points but one or both ends extend beyond the supports.

Difference: support arrangement and end conditions. Cantilever has one fixed end; simply supported has two supports within the beam ends; overhanging beam extends beyond support.
