Basic Concepts of Friction

What is Friction?

Friction is the resisting force that acts along the tangent between two surfaces in contact when one tends to slide over the other.

Relevance in engineering: Friction is essential for motion (walking, braking) and also causes energy loss (wear, heat).

Types of Friction

  • Static friction: between surfaces at rest
  • Kinetic (dynamic) friction: between sliding surfaces
  • Rolling friction: for rolling objects

Laws of Friction

  • Friction acts opposite to motion
  • Friction is independent of area of contact
  • Limiting friction ∝ normal reaction (F = μN)
  • Kinetic friction < static friction

Limiting Friction · μ · Angle of Friction · Cone · Repose

Limiting Friction

The maximum friction force that can be developed before motion starts. It is proportional to normal reaction.

Fmax = μs N

where μs = coefficient of static friction, N = normal reaction.

Coefficient of Friction (μ)

μ = F / N (ratio of friction force to normal reaction). It is dimensionless.

Angle of Friction (φ)

The angle between the normal reaction and the resultant of normal and limiting friction forces.

tan φ = μ

φ = angle of friction.

Cone of Friction

A right circular cone with semi-vertex angle equal to angle of friction. If the resultant force lies inside this cone, no sliding occurs.

Angle of Repose (θ)

The maximum angle of an inclined plane on which a body can rest without sliding.

tan θ = μ

Relation: Angle of repose = Angle of friction (θ = φ).

Relation Example

If coefficient of friction is 0.3, find angle of friction and angle of repose.

φ = tan⁻¹(0.3) = 16.7°
Angle of repose = φ = 16.7°

Equilibrium on Horizontal Surface

Force Parallel to Plane

For a body on horizontal surface with force P parallel to surface:

  • Normal reaction N = mg
  • Friction force F = P (if in equilibrium)
  • Limiting condition: P = μN = μmg
Example: Parallel Force

A block of 50N weight is on horizontal surface. μ = 0.4. Find force required to just move it.

N = 50N
Fmax = μN = 0.4 × 50 = 20N
Force Inclined to Plane

If force P acts at angle θ to horizontal:

  • P cosθ = F (friction)
  • N = mg - P sinθ (if force pulls upward)
  • Limiting condition: P cosθ = μ (mg - P sinθ)
Example: Inclined Force

A 100N block on horizontal surface, μ=0.3. Force applied at 30° above horizontal. Find P to just move.

Limiting: P cos30° = μ (mg - P sin30°)
P × 0.866 = 0.3 (100 - 0.5P)
0.866P = 30 - 0.15P
1.016P = 30 ⇒ P = 29.53 N

Equilibrium on Inclined Plane

↓ mg
→ P
Force Parallel to Plane

Body of weight W on incline angle θ:

  • Component down plane = W sinθ
  • Normal reaction N = W cosθ
  • For equilibrium with force P up plane: P = W sinθ + μN (if impending up) or P = W sinθ - μN (if impending down)
Example: Parallel Force on Incline

A 200N block rests on 20° incline. μ=0.25. Find force parallel to plane to just push it up.

N = 200 cos20° = 200×0.94 = 188N
Friction down = μN = 0.25×188 = 47N
Component down = 200 sin20° = 200×0.342 = 68.4N
P = 68.4 + 47 = 115.4 N
Force Inclined to Plane

Force P applied at angle α to the incline:

Components: P cosα along plane, P sinα perpendicular to plane.

Normal reaction N = W cosθ - P sinα

Friction F = μN

Equilibrium: P cosα = W sinθ + F (for impending up)

Example: Inclined Force on Incline

Block 500N on 25° incline. μ=0.3. Force P applied at 15° to the incline (above the incline). Find P to just move up.

W sinθ = 500 sin25° = 500×0.423 = 211.5N
W cosθ = 500 cos25° = 500×0.906 = 453N
Let P be the force.
N = W cosθ - P sinα = 453 - P sin15° = 453 - 0.259P
F = μN = 0.3(453 - 0.259P) = 135.9 - 0.0777P
Equilibrium along plane: P cosα = W sinθ + F
P cos15° = 211.5 + 135.9 - 0.0777P
0.966P = 347.4 - 0.0777P
1.0437P = 347.4 ⇒ P = 332.8 N

Problem 1: A 80kg crate rests on horizontal floor. μ=0.4. Find force required at 20° above horizontal to just start motion.

W = 80×9.81 = 784.8N
P cos20° = μ(784.8 - P sin20°)
0.94P = 0.4(784.8 - 0.342P) = 313.92 - 0.1368P
1.0768P = 313.92 ⇒ P = 291.5 N

Problem 2: A 300N block is on 30° incline. μ=0.2. Find force parallel to incline to prevent sliding down.

N = 300 cos30° = 300×0.866 = 259.8N
Friction max up = μN = 0.2×259.8 = 52N
Component down = 300 sin30° = 150N
Since 150 > 52, it will slide. Force needed up = 150 - 52 = 98N
