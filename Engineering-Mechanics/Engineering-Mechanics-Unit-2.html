Moment of a Force About a Point

2.1 Introduction — What is a Moment? 🔥

When you apply a force on a body, two things can happen:

  1. Translation — the body moves in a straight line (linear motion)
  2. Rotation — the body turns about a fixed point or axis

The tendency of a force to rotate a body about a point or axis is called the Moment of the Force.

Definition: Moment of a Force

The moment of a force about a point is defined as the product of the force and the perpendicular distance of the line of action of the force from that point.

The point about which the moment is taken is called the moment centre, and the perpendicular distance is called the moment arm.

Formula: Moment of a Force

M = F × d

Where:

  • M = Moment of the force (N·m or kN·m)
  • F = Magnitude of the force (N or kN)
  • d = Perpendicular distance from the moment centre to the line of action of force (m)

2.2 Understanding the Concept — Real-World Analogy

Door Analogy

Imagine opening a door:

  • The hinge of the door is the moment centre (O)
  • You push at the handle — this is the force (F)
  • The distance from the hinge to the handle is the moment arm (d)

Now think about this:

  • If you push near the handle (far from hinge) → door opens easily → moment is large
  • If you push near the hinge (close to hinge) → door is hard to open → moment is small
  • If you push at the hinge itself (d = 0) → door doesn't rotate at all → moment is zero

This is why door handles are placed as far from the hinge as possible!

2.3 Important Points About Moment

  1. Moment is a vector quantity — it has both magnitude and direction (clockwise or anticlockwise)
  2. SI Unit of moment: Newton-metre (N·m) or kilo-Newton-metre (kN·m)
  3. Moment depends on two factors: the magnitude of force AND the perpendicular distance
  4. If the line of action of force passes through the moment centre, then d = 0, so M = 0
  5. Moment is also called "Torque" when dealing with rotating shafts and machines

2.4 Sign Convention for Moments ⭐

Sign Convention — Very Important for Exam!

In engineering mechanics, we follow this standard sign convention:

  • Anticlockwise (ACW) moment = Positive (+) ↺
  • Clockwise (CW) moment = Negative (−) ↻

Some textbooks use the opposite convention. Always state your sign convention at the start of every problem in the exam!

2.5 Moment When Force is Inclined

When a force F acts at an angle θ to a line, we cannot directly see the perpendicular distance. In such cases, we resolve the force into components and find the moment.

Method for Inclined Forces

If force F acts at point A, making angle θ with the horizontal, and we want moment about point O:

  1. Resolve F into horizontal component Fx = F cos θ and vertical component Fy = F sin θ
  2. Find perpendicular distance of each component from O
  3. Calculate moment of each component separately
  4. Add the moments algebraically (considering sign convention)

MO = Fx × dy + Fy × dx

(Where dy = vertical distance and dx = horizontal distance of force application point from O)

2.6 Solved Examples on Moment of a Force

Example 2.1

A force of 50 N acts at a point on a body. The perpendicular distance of the line of action of the force from a point O is 3 m. Find the moment of the force about point O.

Solution 2.1
Given: F = 50 N, d = 3 m
Formula: M = F × d
Calculation:
M = 50 × 3

M = 150 N·m

Example 2.2 🔥

Three forces act on a body as shown. Force of 20 N acts at point A which is 4 m to the right of O. Force of 30 N acts at point B which is 2 m to the left of O. Force of 10 N acts at point C which is 5 m to the right of O. All forces act vertically. Find the resultant moment about point O. (20 N acts downward, 30 N acts upward, 10 N acts downward)

Solution 2.2
Sign Convention: Taking anticlockwise as positive (+)
Moment of 20 N (at A, 4 m right, downward):
20 N downward at right of O → tends to rotate clockwise → Negative
M₁ = −20 × 4 = −80 N·m
Moment of 30 N (at B, 2 m left, upward):
30 N upward at left of O → tends to rotate clockwise → Negative
M₂ = −30 × 2 = −60 N·m
Moment of 10 N (at C, 5 m right, downward):
10 N downward at right of O → tends to rotate clockwise → Negative
M₃ = −10 × 5 = −50 N·m
Resultant Moment:
M = M₁ + M₂ + M₃
M = (−80) + (−60) + (−50)

M = −190 N·m (Clockwise)

Example 2.3 — Inclined Force

A force of 100 N acts at angle 30° to the horizontal at point A. Point A is located 2 m to the right and 1 m above point O. Find the moment of this force about point O.

Solution 2.3
Given: F = 100 N, θ = 30°, horizontal distance = 2 m, vertical distance = 1 m
Resolve the force:
Fx = F cos 30° = 100 × 0.866 = 86.6 N (horizontal, →)
Fy = F sin 30° = 100 × 0.5 = 50 N (vertical, ↑)
Moment of Fx about O:
Fx = 86.6 N acts horizontally at a vertical distance of 1 m above O
This component tends to rotate clockwise about O
M₁ = −86.6 × 1 = −86.6 N·m
Moment of Fy about O:
Fy = 50 N acts vertically upward at horizontal distance of 2 m to the right of O
This component tends to rotate anticlockwise about O
M₂ = +50 × 2 = +100 N·m
Total Moment:
M = M₁ + M₂ = (−86.6) + (+100)

M = +13.4 N·m (Anticlockwise)

Physical Significance of Moment

3.1 What Does Moment Physically Mean?

Physical Meaning

The moment of a force represents the turning effect or rotational tendency of a force about a point. It tells us:

  • How much a force tends to rotate the body (magnitude of moment)
  • In which direction it tends to rotate (clockwise or anticlockwise)

A larger moment means a stronger tendency to rotate. A zero moment means no rotation tendency.

3.2 Practical Examples of Moment in Engineering

Real-World Applications of Moment
  1. Spanner/Wrench: When you tighten a nut with a spanner, you apply a moment. A longer spanner requires less force (greater moment arm d, so less F needed for same moment)
  2. See-saw: Children balance on a see-saw using the principle of moments — heavier child sits closer to the fulcrum
  3. Crane: The load lifted by a crane creates a moment about the base. The crane must be designed to resist this moment to prevent toppling
  4. Beam design: Engineers calculate moments to determine where a beam might bend or break
  5. Steering wheel: A larger steering wheel is easier to turn because it provides a greater moment arm
  6. Door hinge: As discussed earlier, the handle is far from the hinge to maximize moment

3.3 The Principle of Moments (Lever Principle)

Principle of Moments

When a body is in rotational equilibrium (not rotating), the sum of clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of anticlockwise moments about the same point.

ΣMclockwise = ΣManticlockwise

Or equivalently: ΣM = 0 (algebraic sum of all moments = 0)

See-Saw Example

A child weighing 40 kg sits 3 m from the fulcrum. Where should a child weighing 60 kg sit to balance the see-saw?

Using Principle of Moments:

Clockwise moment = Anticlockwise moment

60 × d = 40 × 3

d = 120/60 = 2 m

So the heavier child must sit 2 m from the fulcrum — closer than the lighter child!

Key Takeaway

The moment concept tells us that a small force at a large distance can produce the same moment as a large force at a small distance. This is the lever principle — one of the oldest and most important principles in engineering!

"Give me a lever long enough and a fulcrum on which to place it, and I shall move the world." — Archimedes

Resultant of System of Parallel and Inclined Forces

4.1 What are Parallel Forces?

Definition: Parallel Forces

Parallel forces are forces whose lines of action are parallel to each other — they never meet at a point (unlike concurrent forces). They are of two types:

  • Like Parallel Forces: Forces acting in the same direction (both upward or both downward)
  • Unlike Parallel Forces: Forces acting in opposite directions (one upward, one downward)

4.2 Resultant of Two Like Parallel Forces 🔥

Two Like Parallel Forces

Let two like parallel forces P and Q act at points A and B respectively. The distance between A and B is d.

Properties of the Resultant:

  1. Magnitude: R = P + Q (sum of the two forces)
  2. Direction: Same as the direction of P and Q
  3. Position: The resultant passes through a point C between A and B such that it divides AB internally in the inverse ratio of the forces
Formulas: Two Like Parallel Forces

R = P + Q

P × AC = Q × BC

Or equivalently:

ACBC = QP

Where:

  • R = Resultant force
  • AC = Distance of resultant from force P
  • BC = Distance of resultant from force Q
  • AC + BC = d (total distance between the forces)

Fig. 2.7: Resultant R of two like parallel forces P and Q, passing through point C between A and B (internal division in inverse ratio)

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Memory Trick

"Resultant is closer to the larger force."

If P > Q, then the resultant R is closer to P (AC < BC). Think of it like a see-saw — the heavier side pulls the balance point closer to it.

4.3 Resultant of Two Unlike Parallel Forces ⭐

Two Unlike Parallel Forces

Let two unlike parallel forces P and Q act at points A and B respectively, where P > Q. They act in opposite directions.

Properties of the Resultant:

  1. Magnitude: R = P − Q (difference of the two forces)
  2. Direction: Same as the direction of the larger force
  3. Position: The resultant passes through a point C outside AB, on the side of the larger force. It divides AB externally in the inverse ratio of the forces
Formulas: Two Unlike Parallel Forces

R = P − Q (where P > Q)

P × AC = Q × BC

Or equivalently:

ACBC = QP

Where point C lies outside segment AB, on the side of force P (the larger force).

Special Case: Equal Unlike Parallel Forces

When P = Q (two equal unlike parallel forces), the resultant R = P − Q = 0, but the moment is NOT zero. This system is called a COUPLE, which we will study in detail in the next sections.

A couple has no resultant force but has a resultant moment. It cannot be balanced by a single force.

4.4 Resultant of a System of Parallel Forces

General Method for Multiple Parallel Forces

When more than two parallel forces act on a body:

  1. Choose a reference point (usually one end or a convenient point)
  2. Assign signs: Take one direction as positive (e.g., upward = +, downward = −)
  3. Find magnitude of resultant: R = algebraic sum of all forces = ΣF
  4. Find position of resultant: Take moments about the reference point. Use the principle: R × x̄ = ΣM
Formula: Position of Resultant

x̄ = ΣMR = Σ(Fi × xi)ΣFi

Where:

  • x̄ = Distance of resultant from the reference point
  • Fi = Each individual force (with proper sign)
  • xi = Distance of each force from the reference point

4.5 Solved Examples on Parallel Forces

Example 2.4 — Like Parallel Forces

Two like parallel forces of 80 N and 120 N act at points A and B which are 5 m apart. Find the magnitude, direction, and position of the resultant.

Solution 2.4
Given: P = 80 N, Q = 120 N, d = AB = 5 m (Like parallel forces)
Magnitude of Resultant:
R = P + Q = 80 + 120
R = 200 N
Direction: Same as the direction of P and Q (both act in same direction)
Position of Resultant (from A):
Using P × AC = Q × BC
80 × AC = 120 × BC ... (i)
Also, AC + BC = 5 m ... (ii)
From (ii): BC = 5 − AC
Substituting in (i): 80 × AC = 120 × (5 − AC)
80 AC = 600 − 120 AC
200 AC = 600
AC = 3 m
BC = 5 − 3 = 2 m

Resultant R = 200 N, acting 3 m from A (or 2 m from B)

Note: Resultant is closer to the larger force (120 N at B). ✓

Example 2.5 — Unlike Parallel Forces

Two unlike parallel forces of 150 N (downward) at A and 90 N (upward) at B act on a beam. A and B are 4 m apart. Find the resultant and its position.

Solution 2.5
Given: P = 150 N (↓), Q = 90 N (↑), d = AB = 4 m (Unlike parallel forces, P > Q)
Magnitude of Resultant:
R = P − Q = 150 − 90
R = 60 N
Direction: Same as the larger force P → Downward
Position of Resultant (from A):
The resultant lies outside AB, on the side of larger force (A side).
Using P × AC = Q × BC
150 × AC = 90 × BC ... (i)
Since C is outside AB on side of A: BC = AC + 4 (C is beyond A, away from B)
Wait — let's reconsider. C is outside on the side of the larger force. Since P acts at A and P > Q, C is on the side of A but beyond A (away from B).
So: BC − AC = AB = 4 m → BC = AC + 4 ... (ii)
Substituting (ii) in (i):
150 × AC = 90 × (AC + 4)
150 AC = 90 AC + 360
60 AC = 360
AC = 6 m
BC = 6 + 4 = 10 m

Resultant R = 60 N (downward), acting 6 m from A on the side of A (away from B)

Verification: P × AC = 150 × 6 = 900 N·m; Q × BC = 90 × 10 = 900 N·m ✓
Example 2.6 — System of Parallel Forces 🔥

Four parallel forces of 10 N, 20 N, 30 N, and 40 N act vertically downward at distances of 1 m, 3 m, 6 m, and 10 m respectively from point A on a horizontal beam. Find the magnitude and position of the resultant.

Solution 2.6
Given: All forces act downward (like parallel forces)
F₁ = 10 N at x₁ = 1 m from A
F₂ = 20 N at x₂ = 3 m from A
F₃ = 30 N at x₃ = 6 m from A
F₄ = 40 N at x₄ = 10 m from A
Magnitude of Resultant:
R = F₁ + F₂ + F₃ + F₄ = 10 + 20 + 30 + 40
R = 100 N (downward)
Taking moments about A:
ΣMA = F₁ × x₁ + F₂ × x₂ + F₃ × x₃ + F₄ × x₄
ΣMA = (10 × 1) + (20 × 3) + (30 × 6) + (40 × 10)
ΣMA = 10 + 60 + 180 + 400
ΣMA = 650 N·m
Position of Resultant from A:
x̄ = ΣMAR = 650100

x̄ = 6.5 m from A

Result: The resultant is 100 N acting vertically downward at a distance of 6.5 m from A.

4.7 Resultant of Inclined Forces Using Moments

Method for Inclined (Non-Parallel, Non-Concurrent) Forces

When forces are inclined (not parallel, not concurrent), the resultant is found by combining two approaches:

  1. Resolve each force into horizontal (X) and vertical (Y) components
  2. Find resultant components: Rx = ΣFx, Ry = ΣFy
  3. Find magnitude: R = √(Rx² + Ry²)
  4. Find direction: tan α = Ry / Rx
  5. Find position: Use Varignon's Theorem (next section) — take moments about a point
Resultant of Inclined Force System

R = √(Rx² + Ry²)

α = tan−1(RyRx)

R × d = ΣMO (for position, using Varignon's Theorem)

Varignon's Theorem 🔥

5.1 Introduction

Varignon's Theorem is one of the most important theorems in engineering mechanics. It is named after the French mathematician Pierre Varignon (1654–1722). This theorem provides a very powerful shortcut for finding moments, especially when forces are inclined. It is asked in almost every WBSCTE exam paper.

Statement of Varignon's Theorem ⭐🔥

Varignon's Theorem states that:

"The algebraic sum of the moments of a system of coplanar forces about any point in their plane is equal to the moment of their resultant about the same point."

In simple words: Moment of the Resultant = Sum of Moments of Individual Forces

MR = M₁ + M₂ + M₃ + ... = ΣMi

Exam Must-Know

Varignon's Theorem is asked as:

  • "State and prove Varignon's Theorem" — 5 to 7 marks
  • "State Varignon's Theorem" — 2 marks
  • Numerical problems based on Varignon's Theorem — 5 to 8 marks

Learn both the statement AND the proof!

5.2 Proof of Varignon's Theorem (For Two Concurrent Forces) 🔥

Proof

Given: Two forces P and Q act at point A. Their resultant is R. Let O be any point in the plane of these forces.

To Prove: Moment of R about O = Moment of P about O + Moment of Q about O

Construction:

  • Let O be the moment centre (any arbitrary point).
  • From O, draw a line OY parallel to the forces direction.
  • Draw OA and let it represent the reference line.
  • Let the perpendicular distance from O to force P be d₁, to force Q be d₂, and to the resultant R be d.

Proof using components:

Let us choose a coordinate system with O as origin. Let point A have coordinates (x, y) from O.

Let force P make angle α₁ with x-axis, Q make angle α₂, and R make angle α with x-axis.

The y-component of resultant R equals sum of y-components of P and Q:

R sin α = P sin α₁ + Q sin α₂ ... (i)

Multiplying both sides by x (the x-coordinate of point A from O):

R · x · sin α = P · x · sin α₁ + Q · x · sin α₂ ... (ii)

Now, the moment of any force about O = Force × perpendicular distance from O.

For a force F acting at point A(x, y) making angle θ with x-axis:

Moment about O = Fy · x − Fx · y = F sin θ · x − F cos θ · y

Similarly, the x-component of R equals sum of x-components:

R cos α = P cos α₁ + Q cos α₂ ... (iii)

Multiplying both sides of (iii) by y:

R · y · cos α = P · y · cos α₁ + Q · y · cos α₂ ... (iv)

Subtracting (iv) from (ii):

R(x sin α − y cos α) = P(x sin α₁ − y cos α₁) + Q(x sin α₂ − y cos α₂)

But (x sin θ − y cos θ) for any force at point A is nothing but the perpendicular distance × some factor, which equals the moment of that force about O.

Therefore:

Moment of R about O = Moment of P about O + Moment of Q about O

MR = MP + MQ (Hence Proved)

Simplified Proof for Exam (Alternative Method)

For exams, you can also prove Varignon's Theorem using a simpler graphical approach:

  1. Let forces P and Q act at point A, and their resultant is R (by parallelogram law).
  2. Let O be the moment centre. Draw perpendicular from O to each force line.
  3. The moment of R about O = R × d (perpendicular distance to R)
  4. By geometric proof (using properties of similar triangles from the parallelogram), we show that R × d = P × d₁ + Q × d₂
  5. Hence MR = MP + MQ

Key point: The theorem extends to any number of forces — the moment of the resultant = algebraic sum of individual moments.

5.3 Applications of Varignon's Theorem

Practical Uses
  1. Finding the position of resultant of parallel forces — instead of using the formula P × AC = Q × BC, we can use Varignon's Theorem directly
  2. Finding moment of an inclined force — resolve into components and find moment of each component (this is actually an application of Varignon's Theorem!)
  3. Beam reaction calculation — in Unit 3, we use this theorem extensively to find support reactions
  4. Determining position of centroid — in Unit 5, centroid calculations use the same principle
  5. Structural analysis — engineers use this to find moments at any section of a structure

Example 2.8 — Finding Position of Resultant Using Varignon's Theorem

Three parallel forces of 100 N, 200 N, and 300 N act vertically downward at points A, B, and C respectively. AB = 2 m and BC = 3 m. Using Varignon's Theorem, find the position of the resultant from A.

Solution 2.8
Given:
F₁ = 100 N at A (x₁ = 0 m from A)
F₂ = 200 N at B (x₂ = 2 m from A)
F₃ = 300 N at C (x₃ = 2 + 3 = 5 m from A)
All forces act downward.
Resultant:
R = 100 + 200 + 300 = 600 N (downward)
Applying Varignon's Theorem about point A:
Moment of Resultant about A = Sum of moments of individual forces about A
R × x̄ = F₁ × x₁ + F₂ × x₂ + F₃ × x₃
600 × x̄ = (100 × 0) + (200 × 2) + (300 × 5)
600 × x̄ = 0 + 400 + 1500
600 × x̄ = 1900
x̄ = 1900 / 600

x̄ = 3.167 m from A

The resultant of 600 N acts 3.167 m from A, which is between B and C — closer to C since the largest force is at C. ✓

Example 2.9 — Mixed Parallel Forces

Forces of 50 N (↑), 80 N (↓), 40 N (↑), and 100 N (↓) act at distances of 0, 2, 5, and 8 m from left end A respectively. Find the resultant and its position from A.

Solution 2.9
Sign Convention: Upward = +ve, Downward = −ve

F₁ = +50 N at x₁ = 0 m
F₂ = −80 N at x₂ = 2 m
F₃ = +40 N at x₃ = 5 m
F₄ = −100 N at x₄ = 8 m
Resultant:
R = +50 − 80 + 40 − 100 = −90 N
R = 90 N (Downward)
Moments about A (Varignon's Theorem):
ΣMA = (50 × 0) + (−80 × 2) + (40 × 5) + (−100 × 8)
ΣMA = 0 − 160 + 200 − 800
ΣMA = −760 N·m
Position:
R × x̄ = ΣMA
−90 × x̄ = −760
x̄ = 760 / 90

x̄ = 8.44 m from A (downward direction)

The resultant acts 8.44 m from A. Since this is beyond the last force at 8 m, the resultant lies outside the force system — this makes sense because downward forces dominate.

Common Mistakes in Varignon's Theorem Problems
  • Mistake 1: Forgetting to assign proper signs (+ or −) to forces and moments
  • Mistake 2: Taking wrong perpendicular distances — always measure PERPENDICULAR distance from moment centre to line of action of force
  • Mistake 3: Confusing the direction of rotation — draw the diagram, imagine removing all forces except one, and see which way the body would rotate

Couples — Definition, Properties, and Equivalent Couples 🔥

6.1 Definition of a Couple

Definition: Couple

A couple consists of two equal, parallel, and opposite forces whose lines of action do not coincide (are separated by a distance).

The two forces are equal in magnitude, parallel to each other, and act in opposite directions. The perpendicular distance between their lines of action is called the arm of the couple.

6.2 Moment of a Couple ⭐

Definition: Moment of a Couple

The moment of a couple is the product of one of the forces and the perpendicular distance between the two forces (the arm of the couple).

Formula: Moment of a Couple

M = F × d

Where:

  • M = Moment of the couple (N·m)
  • F = Magnitude of each force (N) — note: only ONE force's magnitude, not the sum
  • d = Perpendicular distance between the two forces = arm of the couple (m)
Very Important

The moment of a couple is the same about any point in the plane. Unlike the moment of a single force (which changes when you change the moment centre), the moment of a couple is independent of the moment centre.

This is a unique and very important property of a couple!

6.3 Proof: Moment of a Couple is Independent of the Moment Centre

Proof

Given: A couple consists of two forces, each of magnitude F, acting at points A and B. The arm of the couple (perpendicular distance) = d.

To Prove: The moment of the couple about ANY point O in the plane is always F × d.

Case 1: Point O is outside AB

Let O be a point at distance x from A (and x + d from B, or x − d depending on position).

Let O be on the left of A. Distance from O to A = x, distance from O to B = x + d.

Moment about O = F × (x + d) − F × x = Fx + Fd − Fx = Fd ✓

Case 2: Point O is between A and B

Let distance from O to A = x, distance from O to B = (d − x).

Both forces create moments in the same rotational direction about O.

Moment about O = F × x + F × (d − x) = Fx + Fd − Fx = Fd ✓

Case 3: Point O coincides with A or B

If O is at A: Moment = 0 + F × d = Fd ✓

If O is at B: Moment = F × d + 0 = Fd ✓

In all cases, Moment of Couple = F × d (constant). Hence Proved.

6.4 Physical Significance of Couples

What a Couple Does

A couple produces pure rotation without any translation. Since the two forces are equal and opposite, they cancel each other's translational effect (ΣF = 0). But they don't cancel each other's rotational effect — they add up to produce a net moment.

Key Points:

  • A couple has no resultant force (R = F − F = 0)
  • A couple has a resultant moment (M = F × d ≠ 0)
  • A couple can only be balanced by another couple, never by a single force
  • A couple produces pure rotation
Real-World Examples of Couples
  1. Steering wheel: Your two hands apply equal and opposite forces on opposite sides → couple → wheel rotates
  2. Opening a water tap: Fingers apply opposite forces on the tap handle → couple → tap rotates
  3. Screwdriver: Fingers apply couple to rotate the screwdriver
  4. Key in a lock: Turning a key applies a couple to the lock mechanism
  5. Compass needle: Earth's magnetic field creates a couple on the needle, making it rotate to align North-South
  6. Electric motor: The electromagnetic force creates a couple on the rotor, causing it to rotate
  7. Bicycle handlebar: Two hands apply a couple to steer the bicycle

6.5 Properties of a Couple ⭐

Important Properties (Exam-Ready List)
  1. The algebraic sum of forces constituting a couple is zero (ΣF = 0)
  2. The algebraic sum of moments of the two forces about any point in their plane is constant and equals F × d
  3. The moment of a couple is independent of the moment centre
  4. A couple cannot be balanced by a single force — it can only be balanced by another couple of equal and opposite moment
  5. A couple produces pure rotation (no translation)
  6. A couple can be moved anywhere in its plane without changing its effect (called a free vector)
  7. Two couples are equivalent if they have the same moment (magnitude and sense of rotation)

6.6 Equivalent Couples 🔥

Definition: Equivalent Couples

Two couples are said to be equivalent if they produce the same moment (same magnitude and same sense of rotation — both CW or both ACW).

Equivalent couples have the same rotational effect on the body, even though the individual forces and arm lengths may be different.

Conditions for Equivalent Couples

Two couples are equivalent if:

F₁ × d₁ = F₂ × d₂

AND both have the same sense of rotation (both CW or both ACW)

Example:

  • Couple 1: Two forces of 10 N with arm = 3 m → M = 30 N·m (ACW)
  • Couple 2: Two forces of 15 N with arm = 2 m → M = 30 N·m (ACW)

These two couples are equivalent — they produce exactly the same rotational effect!

Key Implication

An equivalent couple means you can replace one couple with another that has the same moment. You can change the force magnitude or the arm length, as long as the product F × d remains the same. This is very useful in structural analysis.

Resultant of Any Number of Coplanar Couples

7.1 How to Combine Multiple Couples

Resultant of Multiple Couples

When several coplanar couples act on a body simultaneously, their combined effect is found by algebraic addition of their individual moments.

This is because:

  • Each couple has zero resultant force (F − F = 0)
  • Each couple has a moment (F × d)
  • Moments are scalar quantities (in 2D) that can be added algebraically
Formula: Resultant of Coplanar Couples

MR = M₁ + M₂ + M₃ + ... = ΣMi

Where:

  • MR = Resultant couple moment
  • M₁, M₂, M₃, ... = Moments of individual couples (with sign convention: ACW = +, CW = −)

The resultant is itself a couple (not a force). Its moment equals the algebraic sum of all couple moments.

Key Points
  • The resultant of any number of coplanar couples is always a couple (never a single force)
  • If MR = 0, the system of couples is in equilibrium
  • If MR > 0 → resultant couple is anticlockwise
  • If MR < 0 → resultant couple is clockwise

Replacement of a Force by a Force and a Couple 🔥

8.1 The Concept

Why Do We Need This?

Sometimes in engineering analysis, we need to shift a force from its actual point of application to a different point. But by the principle of transmissibility, we can only move a force along its line of action, not to a parallel line.

However, we CAN shift a force to any parallel position if we add a couple to compensate for the change in moment. This is called force-couple replacement or transfer of a force to a parallel position.

8.2 The Process — Step by Step

Statement

A force acting at a point can be replaced by an equal like parallel force acting at another point, together with a couple whose moment equals the moment of the original force about the new point.

Step-by-Step Procedure

Suppose force F acts at point A, and we want to shift it to point B.

  1. Step 1: At point B, introduce two equal and opposite forces, each equal to F — one in the same direction as the original force (F↑) and one in the opposite direction (F↓). These two cancel each other, so the system hasn't changed.
  2. Step 2: Now we have three forces: the original F at A, and F↑ and F↓ at B.
  3. Step 3: The original F at A and F↓ at B form a couple (equal, opposite, parallel). The moment of this couple = F × d (where d is the distance between A and B).
  4. Step 4: We are left with F↑ at B (single force) + the couple (F × d).

Result: Force F at point A = Force F at point B + Couple of moment M = F × d

Formula: Force-Couple Replacement

Force F at A ≡ Force F at B + Couple M

M = F × d

Where d = perpendicular distance from B to the line of action of the original force at A

8.3 Reverse Process — Couple to Force Shift

Reverse: Force + Couple → Single Force

The reverse is also true. If you have a force F at a point B and a couple M, you can replace them with a single force F acting at a new point A, such that:

d = M / F

Where d = distance from B to A (the new position of the force).

The direction of shift depends on the sense of the couple — if the couple is ACW, the force shifts in such a direction that it would create an ACW moment about B.

So the couple is clockwise.

Equivalent system: 200 N (↓) at B + Clockwise couple of 600 N·m

1. Moment of a Force

The moment of a force about a point is the product of the force and the perpendicular distance of the line of action of the force from that point. It is the measure of the turning tendency of the force about that point. SI Unit: N·m.

2. Moment Centre

The moment centre is the fixed point about which the moment of a force is calculated. It is the point about which the body tends to rotate.

3. Moment Arm

The moment arm (or lever arm) is the perpendicular distance from the moment centre to the line of action of the force. A larger moment arm means a larger moment for the same force.

4. Couple

A couple is a system of two equal, parallel, and opposite forces whose lines of action do not coincide. A couple produces pure rotation without any translation.

5. Arm of a Couple

The arm of a couple is the perpendicular distance between the lines of action of the two forces forming the couple.

6. Moment of a Couple

The moment of a couple is the product of one of the forces and the perpendicular distance (arm) between them. It is independent of the point about which it is taken. M = F × d.

7. Equivalent Couples

Two couples are said to be equivalent if they have the same moment (same magnitude and same sense of rotation), even though the individual forces and arm lengths may be different.

8. Like Parallel Forces

Like parallel forces are two or more parallel forces that act in the same direction. Their resultant equals the sum of all forces and acts between them.

9. Unlike Parallel Forces

Unlike parallel forces are two parallel forces that act in opposite directions. Their resultant equals the difference of the forces and acts on the side of the larger force, outside the two forces.

10. Varignon's Theorem

Varignon's Theorem states that the algebraic sum of the moments of a system of coplanar forces about any point in their plane is equal to the moment of their resultant about the same point. MR = ΣMi.

11. Principle of Moments

The Principle of Moments states that when a body is in rotational equilibrium, the sum of clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of anticlockwise moments about the same point. ΣMCW = ΣMACW.

12. Force-Couple System

A force-couple system is an equivalent system where a force acting at one point is replaced by the same force at another point plus a couple whose moment equals the moment of the original force about the new point.

Short Answer Questions (2-Mark Style)

Q1. Define moment of a force about a point.

The moment of a force about a point is the product of the force (F) and the perpendicular distance (d) of the line of action of the force from that point. M = F × d. Its SI unit is Newton-metre (N·m). It represents the turning tendency of the force about that point.

Q2. What is a couple? Give one example.

A couple is a system of two equal, parallel, and opposite forces whose lines of action do not coincide. A couple produces pure rotation without translation. Example: The two forces applied by hands on a steering wheel to turn it form a couple.

Q3. State Varignon's Theorem.

Varignon's Theorem states that the algebraic sum of the moments of a system of coplanar forces about any point in their plane is equal to the moment of their resultant about the same point. Mathematically: MR = M₁ + M₂ + M₃ + ... = ΣMi

Q4. Differentiate between like and unlike parallel forces.

Like parallel forces act in the same direction. Their resultant R = P + Q and lies between them. Unlike parallel forces act in opposite directions. Their resultant R = P − Q and lies outside them, on the side of the larger force. When two unlike parallel forces are equal, they form a couple.

Q5. What is the physical significance of moment?

The moment represents the turning effect or rotational tendency of a force about a point. It tells us how much a force tends to rotate a body and in which direction (clockwise or anticlockwise). A larger moment means stronger turning tendency. This concept is used in designing levers, beams, and all rotating structures.

Q6. Why is the moment of a couple independent of the moment centre?

Because in a couple, the two forces are equal and opposite. When we take moments about any point, the extra distance added to one force is subtracted from the other, and the terms cancel out. The result always equals F × d (where d is the arm of the couple), regardless of which point we choose as the moment centre.

Q7. What are equivalent couples?

Two couples are called equivalent if they produce the same moment (same magnitude and same sense of rotation). For example, a couple of 20 N forces with 3 m arm (M = 60 N·m ACW) is equivalent to a couple of 30 N forces with 2 m arm (M = 60 N·m ACW). The condition is: F₁ × d₁ = F₂ × d₂ with same rotational direction.

Q8. State the Principle of Moments.

The Principle of Moments states that when a body is in rotational equilibrium (not rotating), the sum of clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of anticlockwise moments about the same point. Mathematically: ΣMCW = ΣMACW or ΣM = 0.

Q9. What happens when we shift a force to a parallel position?

When a force F at point A is shifted to a parallel position at point B, a couple must be added to maintain equivalence. The equivalent system becomes: Force F at B (same magnitude and direction) + a couple of moment M = F × d, where d is the perpendicular distance between A and B.

Q10. What is the sign convention for moments?

The standard sign convention is: Anticlockwise (ACW) moment is taken as positive (+) and Clockwise (CW) moment is taken as negative (−). This convention must be stated at the beginning of any problem. Some books use the opposite convention, so always mention which convention you are using.

Long Answer Questions (5–7 Mark Style)

Q1. State and prove Varignon's Theorem. [5–7 Marks] 🔥

Statement: Varignon's Theorem states that the algebraic sum of the moments of a system of coplanar forces about any point in their plane is equal to the moment of their resultant about the same point.

Mathematically: MR = ΣMi

Proof (for two concurrent forces):

Let two forces P and Q act at point A, and their resultant is R (found by parallelogram law). Let O be any point in the plane.

Set up a coordinate system with O as origin. Let point A have coordinates (x, y).

Let P make angle α₁, Q make angle α₂, and R make angle α with the x-axis.

Moment of any force F acting at A about O = F sin θ × x − F cos θ × y (where θ is the angle with x-axis)

Since R is the resultant of P and Q:

R cos α = P cos α₁ + Q cos α₂ ... (i)

R sin α = P sin α₁ + Q sin α₂ ... (ii)

Multiplying (ii) by x: R sin α × x = P sin α₁ × x + Q sin α₂ × x ... (iii)

Multiplying (i) by y: R cos α × y = P cos α₁ × y + Q cos α₂ × y ... (iv)

Subtracting (iv) from (iii):

R(x sin α − y cos α) = P(x sin α₁ − y cos α₁) + Q(x sin α₂ − y cos α₂)

Now, (x sin θ − y cos θ) represents the moment of force at A about O.

Therefore: Moment of R about O = Moment of P about O + Moment of Q about O

This can be extended to any number of forces. Hence proved.

Q2. Define couple. Explain its properties and physical significance. Give three real-life examples. [5–7 Marks] ⭐

Definition: A couple is a system of two equal, parallel, and opposite forces whose lines of action do not coincide. The perpendicular distance between the two forces is called the arm of the couple.

Moment of couple: M = F × d (where F = magnitude of each force, d = arm of couple)

Properties of a Couple:

  1. The algebraic sum of forces forming a couple is zero (ΣF = 0).
  2. The moment of a couple is the same about any point in its plane — it is independent of the moment centre.
  3. A couple cannot be balanced by a single force. It can only be balanced by another couple of equal and opposite moment.
  4. A couple produces pure rotation without any translation.
  5. A couple can be moved anywhere in its plane without changing its effect (it is a free vector).
  6. Two couples are equivalent if they have the same moment (magnitude and direction).

Physical Significance: A couple represents pure rotational effect. Since the two forces cancel each other's translational effect but add up their rotational effect, a couple causes the body to spin without moving linearly.

Examples:

  1. Steering wheel: Two hands push in opposite directions creating a couple to rotate the wheel.
  2. Water tap: Fingers apply opposite forces on the tap to rotate it open or closed.
  3. Screwdriver: The fingers apply a couple to the handle to rotate the screw.

Q3. Explain the resultant of two like parallel forces and two unlike parallel forces. Derive the position of the resultant in each case. [7 Marks] 🔥

Case 1: Two Like Parallel Forces (P and Q, both in same direction)

Let two like parallel forces P and Q act at points A and B respectively. AB = d.

Magnitude of Resultant: R = P + Q

Direction: Same as P and Q.

Position: The resultant R acts at a point C between A and B.

By Varignon's Theorem: Taking moments about C,

Moment of R about C = 0 (since R passes through C)

Therefore: P × AC = Q × BC (moments of P and Q about C must be equal and opposite to cancel)

This gives: AC/BC = Q/P (inverse ratio)

The resultant divides AB internally in the inverse ratio of the forces. It is closer to the larger force.

Case 2: Two Unlike Parallel Forces (P > Q, opposite directions)

Let P act downward at A and Q act upward at B.

Magnitude of Resultant: R = P − Q

Direction: Same as the larger force (P, downward).

Position: The resultant acts at point C outside AB, on the side of the larger force P.

By Varignon's Theorem: P × AC = Q × BC

Since C is outside AB: BC = AC + AB

Solving: AC = (Q × AB)/(P − Q)

The resultant divides AB externally in the inverse ratio of the forces.

Special Case: When P = Q, R = 0 but moment ≠ 0. This forms a couple.

Q4. Prove that the moment of a couple is independent of the moment centre. [5 Marks]

Given: A couple consists of two forces, each of magnitude F, acting at points A and B in opposite directions. The arm of the couple = d (perpendicular distance between their lines of action).

To prove: Moment of the couple about ANY point O = F × d (constant).

Case 1: O is outside AB (to the left of A)

Let OA = x. Then OB = x + d.

Taking moments about O (both forces contribute in same rotational direction):

If F at A goes upward and F at B goes downward:

M = −F × x + F × (x + d) = −Fx + Fx + Fd = Fd ✓

Case 2: O is between A and B

Let OA = x. Then OB = d − x.

Both forces create moment in the same direction about O:

M = F × x + F × (d − x) = Fx + Fd − Fx = Fd ✓

Case 3: O coincides with A

M = F × 0 + F × d = Fd ✓

Conclusion: In all cases, M = F × d = constant, independent of the position of O. Hence proved.

Q5. Explain the process of replacing a force by an equivalent force-couple system at a different point. [5 Marks] ⭐

Concept: A force acting at a point can be replaced by the same force acting at another point, together with a couple whose moment compensates for the change in moment.

Procedure:

Let force F act at point A. We want to shift it to point B (distance d from A).

  1. Step 1: At point B, introduce two equal and opposite forces, each equal to F (one in the same direction as original, one opposite). These cancel each other, so the system is unchanged.
  2. Step 2: Now there are three forces: F at A, F at B (same direction), and F at B (opposite direction).
  3. Step 3: The original F at A and the opposite F at B form a couple with moment M = F × d.
  4. Step 4: The remaining force at B (same direction as original) stays as a single force.
  5. Step 5: Final equivalent system = Force F at B + Couple M = F × d

Key Points:

  • The force magnitude and direction remain unchanged.
  • Only a couple is added to compensate for the change in position.
  • The couple moment equals the moment of the original force about the new point.

M = M₁ + M₂ = −282.8 + 424.2

Quick Revision — Unit Summary

Complete Unit 2 Summary — Read Before Exam!

📌 Moment of a Force

  • Moment = F × d (perpendicular distance)
  • Unit: N·m or kN·m
  • Sign: ACW = +ve, CW = −ve
  • Moment = 0 when force passes through the moment centre
  • For inclined forces: resolve into components and find moment of each
  • Physical significance: Turning tendency / rotational effect

📌 Resultant of Parallel Forces

  • Like (same direction): R = P + Q, acts between them (internal division, inverse ratio), closer to larger force
  • Unlike (opposite): R = P − Q, acts outside (external division, inverse ratio), on side of larger force
  • Position formula: P × AC = Q × BC
  • General: x̄ = Σ(Fᵢxᵢ) / ΣFᵢ
  • If P = Q (unlike), R = 0 → forms a couple

📌 Varignon's Theorem 🔥

  • "Moment of resultant = Sum of moments of individual forces"
  • MR = ΣMi
  • Used to find position of resultant: R × x̄ = Σ(Fᵢ × xᵢ)
  • Valid for any force system about any point
  • Learn both statement AND proof for exam

📌 Couple

  • Two equal, parallel, opposite forces (lines of action don't coincide)
  • Moment of couple M = F × d (arm)
  • Moment is independent of moment centre — ALWAYS = F × d
  • Produces pure rotation (no translation)
  • Resultant force = 0, but resultant moment ≠ 0
  • Can only be balanced by another couple (not by a single force)
  • Is a free vector — can be moved anywhere in its plane

📌 Equivalent Couples

  • Two couples are equivalent if: F₁ × d₁ = F₂ × d₂ AND same rotation sense
  • You can change force or arm, as long as product stays same

📌 Resultant of Coplanar Couples

  • MR = M₁ + M₂ + M₃ + ... (algebraic sum)
  • Result is always a couple (never a single force)
  • If MR = 0 → equilibrium

📌 Force-Couple Replacement

  • Force F at A = Force F at B + Couple (F × d)
  • Force doesn't change in magnitude or direction
  • Only a couple is added to compensate
  • Couple moment = moment of original force about new point
  • Reverse: Force at B + Couple → Single force at new position (d = M/F)
