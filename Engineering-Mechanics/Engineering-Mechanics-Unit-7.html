Unit Overview

What This Unit Covers

This unit studies motion — how a body moves with time. It includes straight-line motion, circular/curved motion, and the basic ideas of work, power, and energy.

This unit is very important because it connects force with motion. It tells us why bodies move, how fast they move, how acceleration works, and how energy is used in motion.

Practical Importance
  • Used in automobile motion and braking
  • Used in design of rotating machinery
  • Helps in understanding fans, wheels, turbines, motors
  • Useful in studying cranes, lifts, and conveyors

Rectilinear Motion

2.1 Meaning of Rectilinear Motion

Rectilinear Motion

Rectilinear motion is the motion of a body along a straight line.

Examples
  • A train moving on a straight track
  • A stone falling vertically
  • A lift moving up or down
  • A car moving on a straight road

2.2 Basic Terms

Distance

Distance is the total path length travelled by a body, irrespective of direction. It is a scalar quantity.

Displacement

Displacement is the shortest distance between initial and final positions of a body, measured in a particular direction. It is a vector quantity.

Speed

Speed is the distance travelled per unit time. It is a scalar quantity.

Velocity

Velocity is the displacement per unit time in a specified direction. It is a vector quantity.

Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with time.

2.3 Displacement–Time Diagram

Meaning of Displacement-Time Graph

A displacement–time graph shows how displacement changes with time.

  • If graph is a straight line with constant slope → body moves with constant velocity
  • If graph is horizontal → body is at rest
  • If slope increases → velocity increases
  • If slope decreases → velocity decreases

Equations of Motion with Deduction 🔥

3.1 Standard Symbols Used

  • u = Initial velocity
  • v = Final velocity
  • a = Acceleration
  • t = Time
  • s = Displacement

3.2 First Equation of Motion

Formula

v = u + at

Deduction

We know, acceleration = change in velocity / time

a = (v - u) / t

Therefore,

at = v - u

v = u + at

3.3 Second Equation of Motion

Formula

s = ut + ½at²

Deduction

For uniformly accelerated motion, average velocity = (u + v)/2

Displacement = average velocity × time

s = [(u + v)/2] × t

Using first equation, v = u + at

s = [(u + u + at)/2] × t

s = [(2u + at)/2] × t

s = ut + ½at²

3.4 Third Equation of Motion

Formula

v² = u² + 2as

Deduction

From first equation:

v = u + at

t = (v - u)/a

Substitute in second equation:

s = [(u + v)/2] × [(v - u)/a]

s = (v² - u²) / 2a

2as = v² - u²

v² = u² + 2as

Very Important

These three equations are valid only for uniform acceleration.

3.5 Solved Examples

Example 7.3

A car starts from rest and accelerates uniformly at 2 m/s² for 5 s. Find the final velocity.

Solution 7.3

Given: u = 0, a = 2 m/s², t = 5 s

Using v = u + at

v = 0 + 2 × 5 = 10 m/s

Final velocity = 10 m/s

Example 7.4

A body with initial velocity 4 m/s moves with acceleration 3 m/s² for 6 s. Find displacement.

Solution 7.4

Given: u = 4 m/s, a = 3 m/s², t = 6 s

Using s = ut + ½at²

s = 4 × 6 + ½ × 3 × 6²

s = 24 + 1.5 × 36

s = 24 + 54 = 78 m

Displacement = 78 m

Example 7.5

A vehicle moving at 10 m/s accelerates at 2 m/s² and covers a distance of 30 m. Find final velocity.

Solution 7.5

Given: u = 10 m/s, a = 2 m/s², s = 30 m

Using v² = u² + 2as

v² = 10² + 2 × 2 × 30

v² = 100 + 120 = 220

v = √220 = 14.83 m/s

Final velocity = 14.83 m/s

Newton’s Second Law, Momentum & Conservation of Momentum

4.1 Newton’s Second Law of Linear Motion 🔥

Newton’s Second Law

Newton’s second law states that the rate of change of momentum of a body is directly proportional to the impressed force and takes place in the direction of that force.

Formula

P = ma

Where:

  • P = Force
  • m = Mass
  • a = Acceleration
Meaning

This means:

  • Greater force produces greater acceleration
  • For same force, lighter body gets more acceleration
  • Force is needed to change velocity

4.2 Momentum

Momentum

Momentum is the quantity of motion possessed by a body. It is equal to the product of mass and velocity.

Formula

Momentum = mv

4.3 Conservation of Momentum

Law of Conservation of Momentum

When no external force acts on a system, the total momentum of the system remains constant.

Formula for Two Bodies

m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂

m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = (m₁ + m₂)v

12 = 5v

v = 12/5 = 2.4 m/s

Common velocity = 2.4 m/s

Curvilinear Motion

Curvilinear motion is the motion of a body along a curved path.

Examples
  • A stone tied to a string and rotated in a circle
  • A car taking a turn on a curved road
  • A fan blade rotating
  • A point on a rotating wheel

5.2 Angular Displacement

Angular Displacement

Angular displacement is the angle through which a rotating body turns about a fixed axis.

Symbol and Unit

Angular displacement is usually denoted by θ (theta).

SI unit: radian (rad)

5.3 Angular Velocity

Angular Velocity

Angular velocity is the rate of change of angular displacement with time.

Formula

ω = θ / t

Where:

  • ω = angular velocity
  • θ = angular displacement
  • t = time

SI unit: rad/s

5.4 Angular Acceleration

Angular Acceleration

Angular acceleration is the rate of change of angular velocity with time.

Formula

α = (ω₂ - ω₁) / t

Or, if initial angular velocity = ω₀ and final = ω:

α = (ω - ω₀)/t

SI unit: rad/s²

5.5 Relation Between Linear and Angular Velocity ⭐

Derivation

Consider a body moving on a circular path of radius r.

In time t, it moves through angle θ.

Arc length travelled:

s = rθ

Now linear velocity:

v = s / t = rθ / t

But θ/t = ω

v = rω

Important Relation

v = rω

This is one of the most commonly asked formulas.

5.6 Relation Between Linear and Angular Acceleration

Derivation

We know:

v = rω

Differentiating with respect to time:

a = rα

a = rα

Important Relation

a = rα

Centripetal and Centrifugal Force 🔥

6.1 Why Circular Motion Needs a Special Force

When a body moves in a circle, its direction changes continuously. Even if speed is constant, velocity changes because velocity has direction. Therefore, acceleration exists even in uniform circular motion.

Key Idea

A body moving in a circle always tries to move tangentially in a straight line. To keep it in circular path, a force must act toward the centre.

6.2 Centripetal Force

Centripetal Force

Centripetal force is the force acting on a body moving in a circular path, directed towards the centre of the circle, which keeps the body in circular motion.

6.3 Derivation of Centripetal Force ⭐

Deduction

For a body of mass m moving with linear velocity v in a circular path of radius r, the centripetal acceleration is:

ac = v²/r

Using Newton’s second law:

F = ma

So,

Fc = m(v²/r)

Fc = mv²/r

Since v = rω, we can also write:

Fc = m(rω)² / r

Fc = mrω²

Final Formulas

Centripetal Force = mv²/r

Centripetal Force = mrω²

6.4 Centrifugal Force

Centrifugal Force

Centrifugal force is the apparent outward force experienced by a body moving in a circular path when seen from the rotating frame. It acts away from the centre and is equal in magnitude to centripetal force.

Important Concept

Centripetal force is the real inward force required for circular motion. Centrifugal force is the apparent outward force felt by the body in a rotating frame.

Real-Life Examples
  • Passengers feel pushed outward when a bus takes a turn
  • Water drops fly outward from a spinning wet umbrella
  • Planets move in nearly circular orbits due to centripetal force
  • Stone tied to a string moves in a circle because of tension in the string

6.5 Solved Examples

Example 7.12

A body of mass 2 kg moves in a circle of radius 0.5 m with speed 4 m/s. Find the centripetal force.

Solution 7.12

F = mv²/r

= 2 × 4² / 0.5

= 2 × 16 / 0.5 = 32 / 0.5 = 64 N

Centripetal force = 64 N

Example 7.13

A mass of 1.5 kg rotates in a circle of radius 0.8 m with angular velocity 5 rad/s. Find centripetal force.

Solution 7.13

F = mrω²

= 1.5 × 0.8 × 5²

= 1.5 × 0.8 × 25

= 30 N

Centripetal force = 30 N

Example 7.14

A stone of mass 0.5 kg tied to a string moves in a circle of radius 1 m. If the tension in string is 18 N, find speed of stone.

Solution 7.14

Here tension provides centripetal force.

F = mv²/r

18 = 0.5 × v² / 1

v² = 18 / 0.5 = 36

v = 6 m/s

Speed = 6 m/s

Work, Power and Energy

8.1 Work

Definition of Work

Work is said to be done when a force acts on a body and the body moves through a certain distance in the direction of the force.

Simple Understanding

If you push a wall but the wall does not move, then no mechanical work is done on the wall. But if you push a trolley and it moves, then work is done.

Formula of Work

Work = Force × Displacement

W = F × s

If force acts at angle θ to displacement:

W = F × s × cos θ

SI Unit: Joule (J)

1 Joule = 1 Newton × 1 metre

Fig. 7.7: Work done by force F causing displacement s, including inclined force case

🔍 Click to view on Google Images
Types of Work
  • Positive work: Force and displacement in same direction
  • Negative work: Force opposite to displacement
  • Zero work: No displacement or force perpendicular to displacement

8.2 Power

Definition of Power

Power is the rate of doing work.

Formula of Power

Power = Work / Time

P = W / t

SI Unit: Watt (W)

1 Watt = 1 Joule/second

1 kilowatt (kW) = 1000 W

8.3 Energy

Definition of Energy

Energy is the capacity of a body to do work.

Common Forms of Mechanical Energy
  • Kinetic Energy: Energy possessed by a body due to its motion
  • Potential Energy: Energy possessed by a body due to its position

8.4 Kinetic Energy

Formula

K.E. = ½mv²

8.5 Potential Energy

Formula

P.E. = mgh

SI Unit of Energy: Joule (J)

Long Answer Questions

Q1. Derive the three equations of motion. [5–7 Marks] 🔥

Let initial velocity = u, final velocity = v, acceleration = a, time = t, displacement = s.

First Equation:

a = (v - u)/t

at = v - u

v = u + at

Second Equation:

Average velocity = (u + v)/2

s = [(u + v)/2] × t

Substitute v = u + at

s = [(u + u + at)/2] × t

s = ut + ½at²

Third Equation:

s = [(u + v)/2] × t and t = (v - u)/a

s = [(u + v)/2] × [(v - u)/a]

2as = v² - u²

v² = u² + 2as

Q2. State Newton’s second law and explain momentum and conservation of momentum. [5 Marks]

Newton’s second law states that force is equal to mass times acceleration, i.e., P = ma.

Momentum: Momentum is the quantity of motion possessed by a body and is equal to mv.

Conservation of Momentum: If no external force acts on a system, the total momentum before collision is equal to the total momentum after collision.

For two bodies:

m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂

Q3. Define angular displacement, angular velocity, and angular acceleration. Derive relation v = rω. [5 Marks] 🔥

Angular displacement: Angle through which a body rotates.

Angular velocity: Rate of change of angular displacement.

Angular acceleration: Rate of change of angular velocity.

Derivation of v = rω:

For a particle moving in a circle of radius r, arc length s = rθ

Linear velocity v = s/t = rθ/t

But θ/t = ω

Therefore, v = rω

Q4. Derive the expression for centripetal force. [5 Marks] ⭐

For a body of mass m moving with velocity v in a circle of radius r, centripetal acceleration is:

a = v²/r

Using Newton’s second law:

F = ma = m(v²/r)

Therefore, centripetal force F = mv²/r

Since v = rω, then:

F = m(rω)²/r = mrω²

Hence, F = mv²/r = mrω²

Q5. Define work, power, and energy. Write their formulas and SI units. [5 Marks]

Work: Work is done when a force causes displacement. Formula: W = Fs. SI unit: Joule.

Power: Power is the rate of doing work. Formula: P = W/t. SI unit: Watt.

Energy: Energy is the capacity to do work. SI unit: Joule.

Mechanical energy includes kinetic energy and potential energy.

K.E. = ½mv² and P.E. = mgh

Q6. Compare speed and velocity, and compare kinetic energy and potential energy. [5 Marks]

Speed vs Velocity:

  • Speed is scalar; velocity is vector.
  • Speed = distance/time; velocity = displacement/time.
  • Speed has no direction; velocity has direction.

Kinetic Energy vs Potential Energy:

  • Kinetic energy is due to motion; potential energy is due to position.
  • K.E. = ½mv²; P.E. = mgh.
  • K.E. depends on velocity; P.E. depends on height.

v = rω = 0.3 × 12 = 3.6 m/s

P6. A body of mass 3 kg moves in a circle of radius 1.5 m at speed 6 m/s. Find centripetal force.

F = mv²/r = 3 × 36 / 1.5 = 108 / 1.5 = 72 N

P7. A force of 20 N moves a body through 15 m in the same direction. Find work done.

W = Fs = 20 × 15 = 300 J

P8. Find the kinetic energy of a body of mass 5 kg moving with speed 4 m/s.

K.E. = ½mv² = ½ × 5 × 16 = 2.5 × 16 = 40 J

Quick Revision / Unit Summary

Last Minute Revision
  • Rectilinear motion = motion in straight line
  • Curvilinear motion = motion in curved path
  • Slope of displacement–time graph = velocity
  • Slope of velocity–time graph = acceleration
  • Area under velocity–time graph = displacement
  • Equations of motion: v = u + at, s = ut + ½at², v² = u² + 2as
  • Newton’s second law: P = ma
  • Momentum = mv
  • Conservation of momentum: total momentum remains constant if no external force acts
  • Angular velocity ω = θ/t
  • Angular acceleration α = change in angular velocity / time
  • Linear and angular relation: v = rω, a = rα
  • Centripetal force: F = mv²/r = mrω²
  • Work = Fs
  • Power = Work/time
  • K.E. = ½mv²
  • P.E. = mgh
Most Important Exam Topics
  • Derivation of three equations of motion
  • Newton’s second law and momentum
  • Relation v = rω and a = rα
  • Centripetal force derivation
  • Work, power, and energy formulas
  • Numericals from motion equations and circular motion
Common Mistakes
  • Do not use equations of motion if acceleration is not uniform.
  • Do not confuse distance with displacement.
  • Do not confuse speed with velocity.
  • Use proper SI units in all answers.
  • In circular motion, centripetal force is inward, not outward.
  • Write work as zero when force is perpendicular to displacement.
